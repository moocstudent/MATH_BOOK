## Core Notes

### What a Quadratic Equation Is

A polynomial equation with **one** unknown whose **highest power is $2$** is a **quadratic equation**. Its **standard form** is

$$ax^2+bx+c=0\quad(a\ne0),$$

where $a$ is the **quadratic coefficient**, $b$ the **linear coefficient**, and $c$ the **constant term**. The **crucial restriction is $a\ne0$** — otherwise it collapses into a linear equation and loses its "quadratic" nature.

A value making the equation true is a **root**. A quadratic has at most **two** real roots. The main solving methods are four: **completing the square, the quadratic formula, factoring** (and the closely related direct square-root method).

**Intuition:** the standard form $ax^2+bx+c$ traces a **parabola**, and the roots are the $x$-coordinates where it meets the $x$-axis — there may be two such points, one, or none, matching the three cases for real roots.

**Pitfall:** to decide whether an equation is quadratic, first **rearrange to standard form** and check whether $a$ is zero; something like $(x-1)(x+2)=x^2$ has its quadratic terms cancel and is in fact linear.

### Completing the Square

**Completing the square** uses the perfect-square identity $x^2+2px+p^2=(x+p)^2$ to turn the left side into a **perfect square**, reducing the equation to one solvable by **direct square roots**. Take $x^2+bx+c=0$:

1. Move the constant to the right: $x^2+bx=-c$.
2. Add to both sides **the square of half the linear coefficient**, $\left(\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2$, making the left a perfect square:
$$x^2+bx+\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2=\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2-c,$$
that is $\left(x+\dfrac{b}{2}\right)^2=\dfrac{b^2-4c}{4}$.
3. When the right side is nonnegative, **take square roots** to get $x+\dfrac{b}{2}=\pm\sqrt{\ \cdot\ }$, then solve for $x$.

If the quadratic coefficient $a\ne1$, first **divide both sides by $a$** to make the leading coefficient $1$, then complete the square.

**Intuition:** "add a piece to make a square" — adding $\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2$ completes $x^2+bx$ into a square of side $x+\frac{b}{2}$.

**Pitfall:** add $\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2$ to **both** sides; when taking roots **do not drop the $\pm$** (keep both signs), or you lose a root.

### The Quadratic Formula and Discriminant

Completing the square on the standard form $ax^2+bx+c=0\ (a\ne0)$ yields the universal **quadratic formula**:

$$x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}.$$

The expression under the root, $\Delta=b^2-4ac$, is the **discriminant**; it tells the nature of the roots **without solving**:

- $\Delta>0$: **two distinct** real roots;
- $\Delta=0$: **two equal** real roots (a repeated root $x=-\dfrac{b}{2a}$);
- $\Delta<0$: **no** real roots (a negative under the root).

Procedure: write the standard form and read off $a,b,c$ → compute $\Delta=b^2-4ac$ → if $\Delta\ge0$, substitute into the formula.

**Intuition:** the discriminant controls how the parabola meets the $x$-axis: $\Delta>0$ crosses (two points), $\Delta=0$ is tangent (one point), $\Delta<0$ floats clear (no point).

**Pitfall:** substitute $a,b,c$ **with their signs**; check the sign of $\Delta$ first — when $\Delta<0$ there is **no real solution**, so do not force a square root.

### Solving by Factoring

If one side of the equation **factors** into a product of two linear factors and the other side is $0$, **factoring** gives the roots quickly, by the principle

$$AB=0 \;\Longrightarrow\; A=0 \text{ or } B=0.$$

Steps:

1. Rearrange to standard form with **$0$ on the right**: $ax^2+bx+c=0$.
2. **Factor** the left side as $(\ \cdots\ )(\ \cdots\ )=0$ (using common factors, cross-multiplication, difference of squares, etc.).
3. Set **each factor equal to $0$**, giving two linear equations.
4. Solve each to obtain the two roots.

For example $x^2-5x+6=0$ factors as $(x-2)(x-3)=0$, so $x=2$ or $x=3$.

**Intuition:** "if a product is zero, a factor must be zero" — split one quadratic into two linear equations.

**Pitfall:** before factoring you **must** move everything so one side is $0$; never **cancel** a factor containing the unknown from both sides (that loses a root).

### Vieta's Formulas and Applications

Let $x_1,x_2$ be the roots of $ax^2+bx+c=0\ (a\ne0)$. The **relations between roots and coefficients (Vieta's formulas)** are

$$x_1+x_2=-\frac{b}{a},\qquad x_1x_2=\frac{c}{a}.$$

**Uses:** without solving, obtain the **sum and product** of the roots, then evaluate symmetric expressions in $x_1,x_2$ (e.g. $x_1^2+x_2^2=(x_1+x_2)^2-2x_1x_2$), judge the signs of the roots, recover an equation from its roots, and set up and solve word problems.

Vieta's formulas also let you **build an equation backwards**: if two numbers have sum $S$ and product $P$, they are the roots of $x^2-Sx+P=0$.

**Intuition:** the sum fixes the axis of symmetry ($x=-\frac{b}{2a}=\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}$), while the product fixes how the parabola "meets" the $x$-axis.

**Pitfall:** mind the signs — the **sum** carries a minus, $-\dfrac{b}{a}$, while the **product** does not, $\dfrac{c}{a}$; before using them, confirm the equation is in standard form with $a\ne0$, and (when real roots are needed) check $\Delta\ge0$.

### Summary

- Standard form $ax^2+bx+c=0\ (a\ne0)$; at most two real roots; four methods: completing the square, the formula, factoring, square roots.
- **Completing the square:** make the leading coefficient $1$ → add $\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2$ to both sides → reach $(x+\frac{b}{2})^2=\dots$ → take roots (with $\pm$).
- **Quadratic formula:** $x=\dfrac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$; the discriminant $\Delta=b^2-4ac$ counts roots: $>0$ two distinct, $=0$ two equal, $<0$ none.
- **Factoring:** make one side $0$ → factor → each factor $=0$; by $AB=0\Rightarrow A=0$ or $B=0$.
- **Vieta's formulas:** $x_1+x_2=-\dfrac{b}{a}$, $x_1x_2=\dfrac{c}{a}$; reconstruct via $x^2-Sx+P=0$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** (Completing the square) Solve $x^2-6x+5=0$.

> **Solution.** Move the constant: $x^2-6x=-5$.
> The square of half of $-6$ is $(-3)^2=9$; add $9$ to both sides:
> $$x^2-6x+9=-5+9 \;\Rightarrow\; (x-3)^2=4.$$
> Take roots: $x-3=\pm2$, so $x-3=2$ or $x-3=-2$.
> Hence $x_1=5,\ x_2=1$.

**Example 2.** (Quadratic formula) Solve $2x^2+3x-2=0$.

> **Solution.** Here $a=2,\ b=3,\ c=-2$. The discriminant is
> $$\Delta=b^2-4ac=3^2-4\cdot2\cdot(-2)=9+16=25>0,$$
> so there are two distinct real roots. Substitute into the formula:
> $$x=\frac{-3\pm\sqrt{25}}{2\cdot2}=\frac{-3\pm5}{4}.$$
> Thus $x_1=\dfrac{-3+5}{4}=\dfrac{1}{2}$ and $x_2=\dfrac{-3-5}{4}=-2$.

**Example 3.** (Factoring) Solve $x^2-5x+6=0$ and $3x^2=12x$.

> **Solution.** For $x^2-5x+6=0$, factor by cross-multiplication: $(x-2)(x-3)=0$, so $x=2$ or $x=3$.
> For $3x^2=12x$, first move everything so one side is zero: $3x^2-12x=0$; take out the common factor $3x(x-4)=0$, so $x=0$ or $x=4$.
> (Note: you **cannot** divide $3x^2=12x$ by $x$ on both sides — that would discard the root $x=0$.)

**Example 4.** (Discriminant and Vieta) The equation $x^2-4x+k=0$ has two distinct real roots $x_1,x_2$. (1) Find the range of $k$; (2) if $x_1^2+x_2^2=10$, find $k$.

> **Solution.**
>
> **(1)** Two distinct real roots require $\Delta>0$:
> $$\Delta=(-4)^2-4\cdot1\cdot k=16-4k>0 \;\Rightarrow\; k<4.$$
>
> **(2)** By Vieta, $x_1+x_2=4$ and $x_1x_2=k$. Then
> $$x_1^2+x_2^2=(x_1+x_2)^2-2x_1x_2=16-2k.$$
> Setting $16-2k=10$ gives $k=3$. Since $3<4$, it lies in the range from (1), so $k=3$.

**Example 5.** (Area application) A rectangular metal sheet is $3$ m longer than it is wide and has area $40$ m$^2$. Find its length and width.

> **Solution.** Let the width be $x$ m ($x>0$); the length is then $(x+3)$ m. The area gives
> $$x(x+3)=40.$$
> Rearrange to standard form: $x^2+3x-40=0$. Factor $(x+8)(x-5)=0$, so $x=-8$ or $x=5$.
> Since the width must be positive, discard $x=-8$ and take $x=5$; the length is $5+3=8$.
> **Answer:** width $5$ m, length $8$ m.

## Exercises

1. Solve $x^2+4x-1=0$ by completing the square.
2. Solve $x^2-2x-1=0$ by the quadratic formula.
3. Solve $2x^2+5x-3=0$ by factoring.
4. Without solving, decide whether $3x^2-2x+1=0$ has real roots, and justify.
5. Let $x_1,x_2$ be the roots of $x^2-7x+10=0$. Without solving, find $x_1+x_2$, $x_1x_2$ and $\dfrac{1}{x_1}+\dfrac{1}{x_2}$.
6. (Application) A product priced at \$100 is discounted twice by the same percentage and then sells for \$81. Find the discount percentage each time.

**Answers.**
1) $x^2+4x=1$; adding $4$ gives $(x+2)^2=5$, so $x=-2\pm\sqrt{5}$.
2) $a=1,b=-2,c=-1$, $\Delta=4+4=8$, $x=\dfrac{2\pm\sqrt{8}}{2}=1\pm\sqrt{2}$.
3) Factor $(2x-1)(x+3)=0$, so $x=\dfrac{1}{2}$ or $x=-3$.
4) $\Delta=(-2)^2-4\cdot3\cdot1=4-12=-8<0$, so there are no real roots.
5) $x_1+x_2=7$, $x_1x_2=10$, $\dfrac{1}{x_1}+\dfrac{1}{x_2}=\dfrac{x_1+x_2}{x_1x_2}=\dfrac{7}{10}$.
6) Let the rate be $x$; then $100(1-x)^2=81$, $(1-x)^2=0.81$, $1-x=0.9$ (positive root), $x=0.1$, a $10\%$ discount each time.
