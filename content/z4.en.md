## Core Notes

### Complex Series and Taylor Expansion

A complex series $\sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n$ converges if its partial sums have a limit; if $\sum|c_n|$ converges, the series **converges absolutely** (hence converges). A **power series**

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(z-z_0)^n$$

has a **radius of convergence** $R$: it converges absolutely on the disc $|z-z_0|<R$ and diverges for $|z-z_0|>R$. One finds $R$ from $\dfrac{1}{R}=\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$ or $\dfrac{1}{R}=\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\left|\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$.

**Taylor's theorem:** if $f$ is analytic on $|z-z_0|<R$, then on that disc it has the unique **Taylor series**

$$f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!}(z-z_0)^n.$$

This echoes the previous chapter's "analytic $\Rightarrow$ infinitely differentiable": locally, an analytic function *is* a convergent power series. Standard expansions (for suitable $|z|$):

$$e^z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{z^n}{n!},\quad \frac{1}{1-z}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n\ (|z|<1),\quad \sin z=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^n z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}.$$

The radius of convergence equals the distance from $z_0$ to the **nearest singularity**.

### Laurent Series

When $f$ is **not analytic** at $z_0$ (has a singularity), the Taylor expansion fails, but if $f$ is analytic on an **annulus** $r<|z-z_0|<R$, it has a **Laurent series** with negative powers,

$$f(z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}c_n(z-z_0)^n,\qquad c_n=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz,$$

where $C$ is a positively oriented loop around $z_0$ in the annulus. Split the series into the **analytic part** $\sum_{n\ge0}$ and the **principal part** $\sum_{n<0}$.

**Practical tip:** one rarely uses the integral for $c_n$; instead, manipulate known expansions. Note that **the same function has different Laurent series in different annuli**. For example $\dfrac{1}{z-1}$ expands as $-\sum_{n\ge0}z^n$ on $|z|<1$, but as $\dfrac{1}{z}\cdot\dfrac{1}{1-1/z}=\sum_{n\ge1}z^{-n}$ on $|z|>1$. The coefficient $c_{-1}$ is especially important — it is the **residue** below.

### Classifying Isolated Singularities

If $f$ is not analytic at $z_0$ but is analytic on some punctured neighbourhood $0<|z-z_0|<\delta$, then $z_0$ is an **isolated singularity**. By the shape of the **principal part** of the Laurent series there are three types:

- **Removable singularity:** the principal part is zero (no negative powers). Defining $f(z_0)=\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)$ (which exists finitely) makes $f$ analytic. E.g. $\dfrac{\sin z}{z}$ at $z=0$.
- **Pole of order $m$:** the principal part has finitely many terms, down to $(z-z_0)^{-m}$ with $c_{-m}\ne0$. Equivalent test: $\lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)^m f(z)$ is a nonzero finite value; then $|f(z)|\to\infty$. E.g. $\dfrac{1}{(z-z_0)^m}$.
- **Essential singularity:** the principal part has infinitely many negative powers. Then $f$ behaves wildly near $z_0$ (**Picard's theorem**: it takes every complex value, with at most one exception). E.g. $e^{1/z}$ at $z=0$, since $e^{1/z}=\sum_{n\ge0}\dfrac{1}{n!\,z^n}$ has infinitely many negative powers.

The order of a pole can also be read from the multiplicity of a zero in the denominator: if $f=\dfrac{P(z)}{Q(z)}$ and $Q$ has a zero of order $m$ at $z_0$ with $P(z_0)\ne0$, then $z_0$ is a pole of order $m$.

### Residues and the Residue Theorem

Let $z_0$ be an isolated singularity of $f$. The **residue** is the coefficient of $(z-z_0)^{-1}$ in the Laurent series:

$$\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_0}f(z)=c_{-1}=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C f(z)\,dz.$$

**Residue theorem:** if $f$ is analytic inside a simple closed curve $C$ except at finitely many isolated singularities $z_1,\dots,z_n$, and $C$ is positively oriented, then

$$\oint_C f(z)\,dz=2\pi i\sum_{k=1}^{n}\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_k}f(z).$$

This **reduces a contour integral to a sum of a few residues** — the ultimate tool for closed-contour integrals. **Computing residues:**

- **Simple pole:** $\displaystyle\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_0}f=\lim_{z\to z_0}(z-z_0)f(z)$.
- **If $f=P/Q$ with $z_0$ a simple zero of $Q$ ($P(z_0)\ne0$):** $\displaystyle\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_0}\frac{P}{Q}=\frac{P(z_0)}{Q'(z_0)}$.
- **Pole of order $m$:** $\displaystyle\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_0}f=\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\lim_{z\to z_0}\frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}\big[(z-z_0)^m f(z)\big]$.

### Real Integrals via Residues

A spectacular use of the residue theorem is evaluating certain **real integrals**. Two common types:

**(i) Trigonometric rationals $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}R(\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\,d\theta$.** Set $z=e^{i\theta}$, so $d\theta=\dfrac{dz}{iz}$, $\cos\theta=\dfrac{z+z^{-1}}{2}$, $\sin\theta=\dfrac{z-z^{-1}}{2i}$; the integral becomes a contour integral over the unit circle $|z|=1$, then apply the residue theorem.

**(ii) Rational integrals over the real line $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\,dx$** (with $\deg Q\ge\deg P+2$ and $Q$ having no real zeros). Take a large **upper semicircle** $C_R$ (the segment $[-R,R]$ plus the upper arc); as $R\to\infty$ the arc integral tends to $0$ (by the ML estimate), so

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\,dx=2\pi i\sum_{\operatorname{Im}z_k>0}\operatorname{Res}_{z=z_k}\frac{P(z)}{Q(z)},$$

i.e. $2\pi i$ times the sum of residues at all poles in the **upper half-plane**. This turns "uncomputable" real integrals into algebra — remarkably powerful.

### Summary

- Power-series radius $R$: $1/R=\lim\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$; Taylor $f(z)=\sum\frac{f^{(n)}(z_0)}{n!}(z-z_0)^n$, with $R=$ distance to the nearest singularity.
- Laurent series $f=\sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty c_n(z-z_0)^n$ on an annulus; the same function expands differently in different annuli; watch $c_{-1}$.
- Three isolated singularities: removable (no principal part) / pole of order $m$ (finite principal part, $|f|\to\infty$) / essential (infinite principal part).
- Residue $\operatorname{Res}_{z_0}f=c_{-1}$; residue theorem $\oint_C f\,dz=2\pi i\sum\operatorname{Res}$. Simple pole $\operatorname{Res}=\lim(z-z_0)f$ or $P(z_0)/Q'(z_0)$; order $m$ uses the $(m-1)$-th derivative formula.
- Real integrals: trigonometric type set $z=e^{i\theta}$; $\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{P}{Q}$ type uses the upper semicircle $=2\pi i\sum_{\operatorname{Im}>0}\operatorname{Res}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Expand $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{1}{z(z-1)}$ as a Laurent series on the annulus $0<|z|<1$.

> **Solution.** Partial fractions: $\dfrac{1}{z(z-1)}=\dfrac{1}{z-1}-\dfrac{1}{z}=-\dfrac{1}{z}-\dfrac{1}{1-z}$. On $|z|<1$, $\dfrac{1}{1-z}=\sum_{n\ge0}z^n$, so
> $$f(z)=-\frac{1}{z}-\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n=-\frac{1}{z}-1-z-z^2-\cdots.$$
> The principal part is $-1/z$, so $z=0$ is a simple pole and $\operatorname{Res}_{z=0}f=-1$.

**Example 2.** Classify the isolated singularity of $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{\sin z}{z}$, $\displaystyle g(z)=\frac{1}{(z-2)^3}$, and $\displaystyle h(z)=e^{1/z}$.

> **Solution.**
> $f$: from $\sin z=z-\dfrac{z^3}{6}+\cdots$, $\dfrac{\sin z}{z}=1-\dfrac{z^2}{6}+\cdots$ has no negative powers, so $z=0$ is **removable** ($\lim_{z\to0}f=1$).
> $g$: $\dfrac{1}{(z-2)^3}$ has principal part only $(z-2)^{-3}$, so $z=2$ is a **pole of order 3**.
> $h$: $e^{1/z}=\sum_{n\ge0}\dfrac{1}{n!\,z^n}$ has infinitely many negative powers, so $z=0$ is an **essential singularity**.

**Example 3.** Find the residues of $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{z}{(z-1)(z+2)}$ at each pole.

> **Solution.** Two simple poles, $z=1$ and $z=-2$. Using $\operatorname{Res}=\lim(z-z_0)f$,
> $$\operatorname{Res}_{z=1}f=\lim_{z\to1}\frac{z}{z+2}=\frac{1}{3},\qquad \operatorname{Res}_{z=-2}f=\lim_{z\to-2}\frac{z}{z-1}=\frac{-2}{-3}=\frac{2}{3}.$$
> Their sum is $\tfrac13+\tfrac23=1$.

**Example 4.** Use the residue theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=3}\frac{e^z}{z(z-1)}\,dz$ (positively oriented).

> **Solution.** The singularities $z=0$ and $z=1$ are both inside $|z|=3$ and are simple poles.
> $$\operatorname{Res}_{z=0}\frac{e^z}{z(z-1)}=\lim_{z\to0}\frac{e^z}{z-1}=\frac{1}{-1}=-1,$$
> $$\operatorname{Res}_{z=1}\frac{e^z}{z(z-1)}=\lim_{z\to1}\frac{e^z}{z}=\frac{e}{1}=e.$$
> By the residue theorem,
> $$\oint_{|z|=3}\frac{e^z}{z(z-1)}\,dz=2\pi i\,(-1+e)=2\pi i\,(e-1).$$

**Example 5.** Use residues to evaluate the real integral $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x^2+1}$.

> **Solution.** Let $f(z)=\dfrac{1}{z^2+1}=\dfrac{1}{(z-i)(z+i)}$. The denominator exceeds the numerator in degree by $2$, satisfying the upper-semicircle conditions. The only pole in the upper half-plane is $z=i$ (simple):
> $$\operatorname{Res}_{z=i}f=\lim_{z\to i}(z-i)\frac{1}{(z-i)(z+i)}=\frac{1}{2i}.$$
> Hence
> $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x^2+1}=2\pi i\cdot\frac{1}{2i}=\pi,$$
> agreeing with the familiar $\arctan x\big|_{-\infty}^{\infty}=\pi$.

## Exercises

1. Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^n}{2^n}$ and its sum function.
2. Expand $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{1}{z-1}$ as a Laurent series on the annulus $|z|>1$.
3. Classify the singularity of $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{1-\cos z}{z^2}$ at $z=0$.
4. Find the residue of $\displaystyle f(z)=\frac{e^z}{(z-1)^2}$ at $z=1$.
5. Use the residue theorem to evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2}\frac{dz}{z^2(z-1)}$ (positively oriented).
6. Use residues to evaluate the real integral $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{dx}{x^2+4}$.

**Answers.**
1) $a_n=2^{-n}$, $1/R=\lim\sqrt[n]{2^{-n}}=\tfrac12$, so $R=2$; on $|z|<2$ the sum is $\dfrac{1}{1-z/2}=\dfrac{2}{2-z}$.
2) $\dfrac{1}{z-1}=\dfrac{1}{z}\cdot\dfrac{1}{1-1/z}=\dfrac{1}{z}\sum_{n\ge0}z^{-n}=\sum_{n=1}^\infty z^{-n}=\dfrac1z+\dfrac1{z^2}+\cdots$ for $|z|>1$.
3) $1-\cos z=\dfrac{z^2}{2}-\dfrac{z^4}{24}+\cdots$, so $\dfrac{1-\cos z}{z^2}=\dfrac12-\dfrac{z^2}{24}+\cdots$ has no negative powers; $z=0$ is **removable** ($\lim=\tfrac12$).
4) $z=1$ is a pole of order 2, so $\operatorname{Res}=\dfrac{1}{1!}\lim_{z\to1}\dfrac{d}{dz}\big[(z-1)^2 f\big]=\lim_{z\to1}\dfrac{d}{dz}e^z=e$.
5) The singularities $z=0$ (order 2) and $z=1$ (simple) are both inside $|z|=2$. $\operatorname{Res}_{z=1}=\lim_{z\to1}\dfrac{1}{z^2}=1$; $\operatorname{Res}_{z=0}=\lim_{z\to0}\dfrac{d}{dz}\dfrac{1}{z-1}=\lim_{z\to0}\dfrac{-1}{(z-1)^2}=-1$. The integral is $2\pi i(1-1)=0$.
6) $f(z)=\dfrac{1}{z^2+4}=\dfrac{1}{(z-2i)(z+2i)}$; the upper-half-plane pole is $z=2i$ with $\operatorname{Res}_{z=2i}=\dfrac{1}{4i}$, so $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty\dfrac{dx}{x^2+4}=2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{1}{4i}=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$.
