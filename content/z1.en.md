## Core Notes

### Complex Numbers and Their Geometry

A **complex number** has the form $z=x+iy$, where $x,y$ are real and $i$ is the **imaginary unit** with $i^2=-1$. We call $x=\operatorname{Re}z$ the **real part** and $y=\operatorname{Im}z$ the **imaginary part**. All complex numbers form the **complex plane** (Argand/Gauss plane): identify $z=x+iy$ with the point $(x,y)$ or the vector $\overrightarrow{Oz}$. The horizontal axis is the **real axis**, the vertical axis the **imaginary axis**.

The **modulus** (absolute value) of $z=x+iy$ is its distance to the origin,

$$|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2},$$

and the **argument** $\arg z$ is the angle (in radians) from the positive real axis to $\overrightarrow{Oz}$, with $\tan(\arg z)=y/x$. The argument is not unique: any two values differing by an integer multiple of $2\pi$ are both arguments, so $\arg z$ is **multivalued**. The unique value in $(-\pi,\pi]$ is the **principal argument**, written $\operatorname{Arg}z$, and

$$\arg z=\operatorname{Arg}z+2k\pi,\qquad k\in\mathbb{Z}.$$

The **complex conjugate** $\bar z=x-iy$ is the mirror image of $z$ across the real axis; it satisfies $z\bar z=|z|^2$, together with $\operatorname{Re}z=\tfrac{z+\bar z}{2}$ and $\operatorname{Im}z=\tfrac{z-\bar z}{2i}$.

Modulus and argument give the **trigonometric form**

$$z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta),\qquad r=|z|,\ \theta=\arg z.$$

Addition and subtraction follow the parallelogram law for vectors; multiplication **multiplies moduli and adds arguments**: if $z_1=r_1(\cos\theta_1+i\sin\theta_1)$ and $z_2=r_2(\cos\theta_2+i\sin\theta_2)$, then

$$z_1z_2=r_1r_2\big[\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2)+i\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2)\big].$$

So "multiplying by a complex number" is geometrically a **rotation followed by a scaling**.

### Euler's Formula and the Complex Exponential

Extending the exponential to purely imaginary arguments gives the celebrated **Euler formula**

$$e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta.$$

It rewrites the trigonometric form compactly as the **exponential form** $z=re^{i\theta}$. Products and quotients then become sums and differences of exponents:

$$z_1z_2=r_1r_2\,e^{i(\theta_1+\theta_2)},\qquad \frac{z_1}{z_2}=\frac{r_1}{r_2}\,e^{i(\theta_1-\theta_2)}.$$

Setting $\theta=\pi$ yields **Euler's identity** $e^{i\pi}+1=0$, linking the five constants $e,i,\pi,1,0$. Euler's formula also expresses the trigonometric functions exponentially,

$$\cos\theta=\frac{e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}}{2},\qquad \sin\theta=\frac{e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}}{2i}.$$

From the exponential form we immediately get **de Moivre's formula** and the **root** formula:

$$z^n=r^n e^{in\theta},\qquad z^{1/n}=r^{1/n}\exp\!\Big(i\,\frac{\theta+2k\pi}{n}\Big),\quad k=0,1,\dots,n-1.$$

Thus a nonzero complex number has exactly $n$ distinct $n$-th roots, equally spaced on the circle of radius $r^{1/n}$ as vertices of a regular $n$-gon.

### Complex Logarithm and Powers

The **complex logarithm** inverts the exponential: $w=\operatorname{Ln}z$ means $e^w=z$ (with $z\ne0$). Writing $z=re^{i\theta}$ and $w=u+iv$ forces $e^u=r$ and $v=\theta$, hence

$$\operatorname{Ln}z=\ln|z|+i\arg z=\ln r+i(\operatorname{Arg}z+2k\pi),\quad k\in\mathbb{Z}.$$

Because $\arg z$ is multivalued, so is the logarithm: adjacent branches differ by $2\pi i$. The branch using the principal argument is the **principal value**,

$$\ln z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z.$$

For instance $\ln(-1)=\ln 1+i\pi=i\pi$, while in general $\operatorname{Ln}(-1)=(2k+1)\pi i$.

**Complex powers** are defined through the logarithm: for a base $a\ne0$,

$$a^{b}=e^{b\operatorname{Ln}a},$$

which is generally multivalued too. A famous example, $i^{i}=e^{i\operatorname{Ln}i}=e^{i\,(i\pi/2+2k\pi i)}=e^{-\pi/2-2k\pi}$, is a family of **real numbers**; its principal value is $e^{-\pi/2}\approx0.2079$.

### Complex Functions and Mappings

Let $D$ be a set of points in the plane. If to each $z$ in $D$ there corresponds a complex number $w$, we obtain a **complex function** $w=f(z)$. With $z=x+iy$ and $w=u+iv$, $f$ is equivalent to a pair of real functions of two variables,

$$u=u(x,y),\qquad v=v(x,y).$$

Unlike a real function, $w=f(z)$ cannot be drawn as a single curve; instead it is a **mapping** from the $z$-plane to the $w$-plane, sending points, curves, and regions of the $z$-plane to points, curves, and regions of the $w$-plane. Studying a complex function means studying this geometric transformation.

For example $w=z^2$ in exponential form is $w=r^2e^{i2\theta}$: it squares the modulus and doubles the argument, mapping the upper half-plane onto the whole plane and circles centred at $0$ to circles. The **Möbius map** $w=\dfrac{1}{z}$ sends circles and lines to circles or lines and is a typical conformal transformation.

### Limits and Continuity

Suppose $f$ is defined on a punctured neighbourhood of $z_0$. If there is a complex number $A$ such that $f(z)$ gets arbitrarily close to $A$ as $z\to z_0$ — i.e. for every $\varepsilon>0$ there is $\delta>0$ with $|f(z)-A|<\varepsilon$ whenever $0<|z-z_0|<\delta$ — we write

$$\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=A.$$

**The crucial point:** here $z\to z_0$ along **any direction and any path in the plane**, and the limit exists only if every path gives the same value. This is far stricter than one-sided limits on the real line. One can show $\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=A$ holds iff the real and imaginary parts have limits separately: $u(x,y)\to\operatorname{Re}A$ and $v(x,y)\to\operatorname{Im}A$.

If $\lim_{z\to z_0}f(z)=f(z_0)$, then $f$ is **continuous** at $z_0$. Sums, differences, products, quotients (nonzero denominator), and compositions of continuous functions are continuous; $f$ is continuous at $z_0$ iff both $u,v$ are continuous at $(x_0,y_0)$.

### Summary

- Complex number $z=x+iy$; modulus $|z|=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$; argument $\arg z$ (multivalued); principal argument $\operatorname{Arg}z\in(-\pi,\pi]$; conjugate $\bar z=x-iy$ with $z\bar z=|z|^2$.
- Trigonometric/exponential form $z=r(\cos\theta+i\sin\theta)=re^{i\theta}$; multiply/divide: moduli multiply/divide, arguments add/subtract.
- Euler's formula $e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta+i\sin\theta$; de Moivre $z^n=r^ne^{in\theta}$; $n$ roots forming a regular $n$-gon.
- Complex log $\operatorname{Ln}z=\ln|z|+i\arg z$ (multivalued); principal value $\ln z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z$; powers $a^b=e^{b\operatorname{Ln}a}$.
- A complex function $w=f(z)$ is a plane-to-plane mapping, $f=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$.
- A complex limit requires the **same value along every path**; $f$ is continuous $\Leftrightarrow$ both $u,v$ are continuous.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the modulus, principal argument, and exponential form of $z=-1+i\sqrt3$.

> **Solution.** The modulus is
> $$|z|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+(\sqrt3)^2}=\sqrt{1+3}=2.$$
> Since $z$ lies in the second quadrant with reference angle $\alpha=\arctan(\sqrt3/1)=\pi/3$, the principal argument is
> $$\operatorname{Arg}z=\pi-\frac{\pi}{3}=\frac{2\pi}{3}.$$
> Hence $z=2e^{i2\pi/3}=2\big(\cos\tfrac{2\pi}{3}+i\sin\tfrac{2\pi}{3}\big)$.

**Example 2.** Use the exponential form to compute $(1+i)^{8}$.

> **Solution.** First, $|1+i|=\sqrt2$ and $\operatorname{Arg}(1+i)=\pi/4$, so $1+i=\sqrt2\,e^{i\pi/4}$. By de Moivre,
> $$(1+i)^8=(\sqrt2)^8\,e^{i\cdot8\cdot\pi/4}=2^4\,e^{i2\pi}=16\cdot1=16.$$
> Thus $(1+i)^8=16$, a real number.

**Example 3.** Find all roots of $z^3=8i$.

> **Solution.** Write $8i=8\,e^{i(\pi/2+2k\pi)}$. Taking cube roots, the modulus is $8^{1/3}=2$ and the argument is divided by $3$:
> $$z_k=2\exp\!\Big(i\,\frac{\pi/2+2k\pi}{3}\Big),\qquad k=0,1,2.$$
> Evaluating each,
> $$\begin{aligned}
> z_0&=2e^{i\pi/6}=2\big(\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}+\tfrac{i}{2}\big)=\sqrt3+i,\\
> z_1&=2e^{i5\pi/6}=-\sqrt3+i,\\
> z_2&=2e^{i3\pi/2}=-2i.
> \end{aligned}$$
> The three roots lie on the circle of radius $2$, spaced $120^\circ$ apart.

**Example 4.** Find the principal values $\ln(-1+i)$ and $(1+i)^{i}$.

> **Solution.** For $z=-1+i$: $|z|=\sqrt2$ and $\operatorname{Arg}z=3\pi/4$ (second quadrant), so
> $$\ln(-1+i)=\ln\sqrt2+i\,\frac{3\pi}{4}=\frac{1}{2}\ln 2+\frac{3\pi}{4}i.$$
> For $(1+i)^i$, take the principal log $\ln(1+i)=\tfrac12\ln2+i\tfrac{\pi}{4}$; then
> $$(1+i)^i=e^{i\ln(1+i)}=e^{i(\frac12\ln2+i\frac{\pi}{4})}=e^{-\pi/4}\,e^{\,i\frac12\ln2}=e^{-\pi/4}\Big[\cos\big(\tfrac12\ln2\big)+i\sin\big(\tfrac12\ln2\big)\Big].$$

**Example 5.** Determine whether $\displaystyle\lim_{z\to0}\frac{\bar z}{z}$ exists.

> **Solution.** Put $z=re^{i\theta}$, so $\bar z=re^{-i\theta}$ and
> $$\frac{\bar z}{z}=\frac{re^{-i\theta}}{re^{i\theta}}=e^{-2i\theta}.$$
> As $z\to0$ along a fixed direction $\theta$ (let $r\to0$), the value $e^{-2i\theta}$ depends on $\theta$: along the real axis ($\theta=0$) it is $1$, along the imaginary axis ($\theta=\pi/2$) it is $e^{-i\pi}=-1$. Different paths give different limits, so
> $$\lim_{z\to0}\frac{\bar z}{z}\ \text{does not exist}.$$

## Exercises

1. Find the modulus $|z|$, principal argument $\operatorname{Arg}z$, and exponential form of $z=\sqrt3-i$.
2. Simplify $\dfrac{2+i}{1-2i}$ into the form $x+iy$.
3. Use de Moivre's formula to compute $(1-i)^{6}$.
4. Find all four roots of $z^4=-16$ and describe their geometric arrangement.
5. Find all values of $\operatorname{Ln}(-i)$ and its principal value $\ln(-i)$.
6. Determine whether $\displaystyle\lim_{z\to0}\frac{\operatorname{Re}z}{z}$ exists, with justification.

**Answers.**
1) $|z|=\sqrt{3+1}=2$; $z$ is in the fourth quadrant, $\operatorname{Arg}z=-\pi/6$; $z=2e^{-i\pi/6}$.
2) Multiply top and bottom by $\overline{1-2i}=1+2i$: $\dfrac{(2+i)(1+2i)}{(1-2i)(1+2i)}=\dfrac{2+4i+i+2i^2}{1+4}=\dfrac{5i}{5}=i$.
3) $1-i=\sqrt2\,e^{-i\pi/4}$, so $(1-i)^6=(\sqrt2)^6e^{-i6\pi/4}=8\,e^{-i3\pi/2}=8\cdot i=8i$.
4) $-16=16e^{i(\pi+2k\pi)}$, $z_k=2\exp\!\big(i\tfrac{\pi+2k\pi}{4}\big)$, $k=0,1,2,3$: $z_0=2e^{i\pi/4}=\sqrt2+\sqrt2\,i$, $z_1=2e^{i3\pi/4}=-\sqrt2+\sqrt2\,i$, $z_2=2e^{i5\pi/4}=-\sqrt2-\sqrt2\,i$, $z_3=2e^{i7\pi/4}=\sqrt2-\sqrt2\,i$; the four roots form a square on the circle of radius $2$.
5) $-i=e^{i(-\pi/2+2k\pi)}$, so $\operatorname{Ln}(-i)=\ln1+i(-\tfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi)=\big(2k-\tfrac12\big)\pi i$; principal value $\ln(-i)=-\tfrac{\pi}{2}i$.
6) With $z=re^{i\theta}$, $\operatorname{Re}z=r\cos\theta$, so $\dfrac{\operatorname{Re}z}{z}=\dfrac{r\cos\theta}{re^{i\theta}}=\cos\theta\,e^{-i\theta}$; along the real axis ($\theta=0$) it is $1$, along the imaginary axis ($\theta=\pi/2$) it is $0$. The values differ, so the limit does not exist.
