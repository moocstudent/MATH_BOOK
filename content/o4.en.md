## Core Notes

### Definition and Existence

Let $f(t)$ be defined for $t\geq 0$. Its **Laplace transform** is the parameter-dependent integral
$$\mathcal{L}\{f\}(s)=F(s)=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt,$$
provided the improper integral converges. This sends a time-domain function $f(t)$ to a complex-frequency-domain function $F(s)$.

**Existence (sufficient conditions)**: if $f$ is **piecewise continuous** on $[0,\infty)$ and of **exponential order** — there are constants $M>0,\ a$ with $|f(t)|\leq Me^{at}$ — then $\mathcal{L}\{f\}$ converges for $s>a$. Polynomials, $e^{at}$, $\sin,\cos$ all qualify.

**Standard transform table** (obtained by integrating the definition):
$$\mathcal{L}\{1\}=\frac1s,\quad \mathcal{L}\{t^n\}=\frac{n!}{s^{n+1}},\quad \mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\frac{1}{s-a},$$
$$\mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}=\frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2},\quad \mathcal{L}\{\cos\omega t\}=\frac{s}{s^2+\omega^2}.$$
For instance $\mathcal{L}\{1\}=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}\,dt=\big[-\tfrac1s e^{-st}\big]_0^\infty=\tfrac1s$ for $s>0$.

### Basic Properties (Linearity, Differentiation, Shift)

**Linearity**: $\mathcal{L}\{af+bg\}=a\mathcal{L}\{f\}+b\mathcal{L}\{g\}$.

**Transform of derivatives** (the heart of solving ODEs):
$$\mathcal{L}\{f'\}=sF(s)-f(0),$$
$$\mathcal{L}\{f''\}=s^2F(s)-sf(0)-f'(0).$$
In general $\mathcal{L}\{f^{(n)}\}=s^nF-s^{n-1}f(0)-\cdots-f^{(n-1)}(0)$. Differentiation in time becomes algebraic multiplication by $s$ — that is its power.

**$s$-shift (frequency shift)**: $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}f(t)\}=F(s-a)$. For example $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\cos\omega t\}=\dfrac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2}$.

**Multiplication by $t$**: $\mathcal{L}\{t f(t)\}=-F'(s)$, and more generally $\mathcal{L}\{t^n f(t)\}=(-1)^nF^{(n)}(s)$.

### Inverse Transform and Partial Fractions

The **inverse Laplace transform** $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{F\}=f$ recovers $f(t)$ from $F(s)$; it is unique wherever $f$ is continuous. In practice we avoid complex contour integration and instead reduce $F(s)$ to standard table forms, inverting term by term.

When $F(s)=\dfrac{P(s)}{Q(s)}$ is a **proper rational function** ($\deg P<\deg Q$), use **partial fractions**:
- a simple real root $s=a$ of $Q$ gives a term $\dfrac{A}{s-a}\ \to\ Ae^{at}$;
- a repeated root $(s-a)^k$ gives $\dfrac{A_1}{s-a}+\cdots+\dfrac{A_k}{(s-a)^k}\ \to$ terms with $t^{j}e^{at}$;
- an irreducible quadratic $(s-\alpha)^2+\omega^2$ gives $\dfrac{B(s-\alpha)+C\omega}{(s-\alpha)^2+\omega^2}\ \to\ e^{\alpha t}(B\cos\omega t+C\sin\omega t)$.

By linearity of $\mathcal{L}^{-1}$, inverting each term gives $f(t)$.

### Solving Initial Value Problems

The Laplace transform turns a **constant-coefficient linear IVP** into an **algebraic equation**, in three steps:

1. **Transform**: apply $\mathcal{L}$ to both sides, using the derivative rules to express $y',y''$ via $Y(s)=\mathcal{L}\{y\}$, $y(0)$, $y'(0)$ — the initial data are **built in automatically**, with no need to find the general solution first.
2. **Solve**: solve for $Y(s)$, usually a rational function.
3. **Invert**: use partial fractions and the table to get $y(t)=\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{Y\}$.

For example $y''+y=0,\ y(0)=0,\ y'(0)=1$: transforming gives $s^2Y-1+Y=0$, so $Y=\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}$, and inverting gives $y=\sin t$.

### Step Functions and the δ Function

The **unit step (Heaviside) function**
$$u(t-a)=\begin{cases}0, & t<a,\\ 1, & t\geq a,\end{cases}\qquad \mathcal{L}\{u(t-a)\}=\frac{e^{-as}}{s}\ (a\geq 0).$$
It models a signal switched on at $t=a$. The **second shift theorem (t-shift)**:
$$\mathcal{L}\{u(t-a)\,f(t-a)\}=e^{-as}F(s),\qquad \mathcal{L}^{-1}\{e^{-as}F(s)\}=u(t-a)f(t-a).$$
This lets us handle piecewise-defined forcing terms.

The **Dirac δ function** $\delta(t-a)$ is an idealized "instantaneous unit impulse", satisfying the sifting property $\int_0^\infty \delta(t-a)g(t)\,dt=g(a)$, with transform
$$\mathcal{L}\{\delta(t-a)\}=e^{-as}\quad(a\geq 0),\qquad \mathcal{L}\{\delta(t)\}=1.$$
δ is the "derivative" of the step: $u'(t-a)=\delta(t-a)$ (in the generalized sense). It models hammer blows, voltage spikes, and other instantaneous actions.

### Summary

- Definition $F(s)=\displaystyle\int_0^\infty e^{-st}f(t)\,dt$; piecewise continuous + exponential order $\Rightarrow$ convergence for $s>a$.
- Table: $\mathcal{L}\{t^n\}=\dfrac{n!}{s^{n+1}}$, $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\dfrac{1}{s-a}$, $\mathcal{L}\{\sin\omega t\}=\dfrac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}$, $\mathcal{L}\{\cos\omega t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+\omega^2}$.
- Differentiation: $\mathcal{L}\{f'\}=sF-f(0)$, $\mathcal{L}\{f''\}=s^2F-sf(0)-f'(0)$; frequency shift $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}f\}=F(s-a)$.
- The inverse uses **partial fractions** + the table; solving an IVP = transform → solve $Y(s)$ → invert.
- Step $\mathcal{L}\{u(t-a)\}=\dfrac{e^{-as}}{s}$, $t$-shift $\mathcal{L}\{u(t-a)f(t-a)\}=e^{-as}F(s)$; impulse $\mathcal{L}\{\delta(t-a)\}=e^{-as}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Compute $\mathcal{L}\{e^{3t}\}$ from the definition and state the region of convergence.

> **Solution.** By definition,
> $$\mathcal{L}\{e^{3t}\}=\int_0^\infty e^{-st}e^{3t}\,dt=\int_0^\infty e^{-(s-3)t}\,dt.$$
> The integral converges when $s-3>0$:
> $$=\left[\frac{-1}{s-3}e^{-(s-3)t}\right]_0^\infty=\frac{1}{s-3}\qquad(s>3).$$
> This matches the table entry $\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}\}=\dfrac{1}{s-a}$.

**Example 2.** Using linearity and the shift theorem, find $\mathcal{L}\{2t^2-3e^{-t}+\cos 4t\}$ and $\mathcal{L}\{e^{2t}\sin 3t\}$.

> **Solution.** By linearity, transform term by term:
> $$\mathcal{L}\{2t^2\}=2\cdot\frac{2!}{s^3}=\frac{4}{s^3},\quad \mathcal{L}\{-3e^{-t}\}=\frac{-3}{s+1},\quad \mathcal{L}\{\cos 4t\}=\frac{s}{s^2+16}.$$
> So the first is $\dfrac{4}{s^3}-\dfrac{3}{s+1}+\dfrac{s}{s^2+16}$.
> For the second, first $\mathcal{L}\{\sin 3t\}=\dfrac{3}{s^2+9}$, then apply the frequency shift $s\to s-2$:
> $$\mathcal{L}\{e^{2t}\sin 3t\}=\frac{3}{(s-2)^2+9}.$$

**Example 3.** Find $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{s+7}{s^2+2s+5}\right\}$.

> **Solution.** Complete the square in the denominator: $s^2+2s+5=(s+1)^2+4$, so $\alpha=-1,\ \omega=2$. Rewrite the numerator in terms of $(s+1)$ and a constant:
> $$\frac{s+7}{(s+1)^2+4}=\frac{(s+1)+6}{(s+1)^2+4}=\frac{s+1}{(s+1)^2+4}+\frac{6}{(s+1)^2+4}.$$
> The first term inverts to $e^{-t}\cos 2t$; the second, written as $3\cdot\dfrac{2}{(s+1)^2+4}$, inverts to $3e^{-t}\sin 2t$. Hence
> $$\mathcal{L}^{-1}=e^{-t}\cos 2t+3e^{-t}\sin 2t.$$

**Example 4.** Use the Laplace transform to solve $y''-y'-2y=0,\ \ y(0)=1,\ y'(0)=0$.

> **Solution.** Write $Y=\mathcal{L}\{y\}$. Transforming and substituting the initial data:
> $$\big(s^2Y-sy(0)-y'(0)\big)-\big(sY-y(0)\big)-2Y=0,$$
> $$\Rightarrow (s^2-s-2)Y-s+1=0\Rightarrow Y=\frac{s-1}{s^2-s-2}=\frac{s-1}{(s-2)(s+1)}.$$
> Partial fractions: $\dfrac{s-1}{(s-2)(s+1)}=\dfrac{A}{s-2}+\dfrac{B}{s+1}$. At $s=2$: $A=\dfrac{1}{3}$; at $s=-1$: $B=\dfrac{-2}{-3}=\dfrac{2}{3}$. Hence
> $$y=\frac13 e^{2t}+\frac23 e^{-t}.$$

**Example 5.** Use the Laplace transform to solve the impulsively forced IVP $y''+y=\delta(t-\pi),\ \ y(0)=0,\ y'(0)=0$.

> **Solution.** Transforming with zero initial data: $(s^2+1)Y=\mathcal{L}\{\delta(t-\pi)\}=e^{-\pi s}$, so
> $$Y=\frac{e^{-\pi s}}{s^2+1}.$$
> Since $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{1}{s^2+1}\right\}=\sin t$, the $t$-shift theorem $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\{e^{-\pi s}F(s)\}=u(t-\pi)f(t-\pi)$ gives
> $$y=u(t-\pi)\sin(t-\pi)=-u(t-\pi)\sin t.$$
> So the system is at rest for $t<\pi$, then begins $\sin$ oscillation after receiving a unit impulse at $t=\pi$ (note $\sin(t-\pi)=-\sin t$).

## Exercises

1. Find $\mathcal{L}\{t^3+2\}$ from the definition or the table.
2. Use the frequency-shift theorem to find $\mathcal{L}\{e^{-2t}\cos 5t\}$.
3. Find $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{1}{(s-1)(s+3)}\right\}$.
4. Find $\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{2s+1}{s^2+4}\right\}$.
5. Use the Laplace transform to solve $y'+3y=e^{-3t},\ y(0)=0$.
6. Use the Laplace transform to solve $y''+4y=0,\ y(0)=2,\ y'(0)=0$.

**Answers.** 1) $\mathcal{L}\{t^3\}=\dfrac{3!}{s^4}=\dfrac{6}{s^4}$ and $\mathcal{L}\{2\}=\dfrac2s$, so $\dfrac{6}{s^4}+\dfrac{2}{s}$; 2) $\mathcal{L}\{\cos5t\}=\dfrac{s}{s^2+25}$, shift $s\to s+2$: $\dfrac{s+2}{(s+2)^2+25}$; 3) partial fractions $\dfrac{1}{(s-1)(s+3)}=\dfrac{1/4}{s-1}-\dfrac{1/4}{s+3}$, inverting to $\dfrac14 e^{t}-\dfrac14 e^{-3t}$; 4) $\dfrac{2s+1}{s^2+4}=2\cdot\dfrac{s}{s^2+4}+\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac{2}{s^2+4}$, inverting to $2\cos 2t+\dfrac12\sin 2t$; 5) $(s+3)Y=\dfrac{1}{s+3}$, so $Y=\dfrac{1}{(s+3)^2}$, inverting to $y=te^{-3t}$; 6) $(s^2+4)Y-2s=0$, $Y=\dfrac{2s}{s^2+4}$, inverting to $y=2\cos 2t$.
