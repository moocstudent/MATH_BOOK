## Core Notes

### Addition, Scalar Multiplication, and Multiplication (why defined this way = composition of maps)

Let $A=(a_{ij})$ and $B=(b_{ij})$ be matrices of the same $m\times n$ size. **Matrix addition** and **scalar multiplication** act entrywise:

$$(A+B)_{ij} = a_{ij}+b_{ij}, \qquad (kA)_{ij} = k\,a_{ij}.$$

These obey the usual commutative, associative, and distributive laws.

**Matrix multiplication** is different: if $A$ is $m\times p$ and $B$ is $p\times n$, then $C=AB$ is $m\times n$ with

$$c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_{ik} b_{kj}.$$

So the $(i,j)$ entry of $C$ is the **dot product** of row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$. **Why this definition?** Because matrices represent **linear transformations**: if $\vec y=B\vec x$ and $\vec z=A\vec y$, then the composite is $\vec z=A(B\vec x)=(AB)\vec x$. Matrix multiplication exactly encodes the **composition** of maps, which is where the formula comes from. It follows that multiplication is **not commutative** ($AB\ne BA$ in general) but is **associative**, $(AB)C=A(BC)$, and distributive. The identity matrix $I$ satisfies $AI=IA=A$, playing the role of the identity map.

### Transpose, Symmetric, and Orthogonal Matrices

The **transpose** $A^{\mathsf T}$ swaps rows and columns: $(A^{\mathsf T})_{ij}=a_{ji}$. Key rules:

$$(A+B)^{\mathsf T}=A^{\mathsf T}+B^{\mathsf T}, \qquad (kA)^{\mathsf T}=kA^{\mathsf T}, \qquad (AB)^{\mathsf T}=B^{\mathsf T}A^{\mathsf T}.$$

Note that the transpose of a product **reverses order**. If $A^{\mathsf T}=A$, then $A$ is **symmetric** (necessarily square, mirrored across the main diagonal); if $A^{\mathsf T}=-A$, it is **skew-symmetric** (zero diagonal).

A square matrix $Q$ satisfying

$$Q^{\mathsf T}Q = QQ^{\mathsf T} = I, \quad\text{i.e.}\quad Q^{-1}=Q^{\mathsf T},$$

is called **orthogonal**. Its columns (rows) form an **orthonormal basis**; it preserves lengths and angles, corresponding geometrically to a rotation or reflection. Its determinant is necessarily $\pm 1$.

### Inverse and Elementary Matrices (computing the inverse)

A square matrix $A$ is **invertible** if there is a $B$ with $AB=BA=I$, written $B=A^{-1}$. $A$ is invertible iff $\det A\ne 0$. Basic rules:

$$(AB)^{-1}=B^{-1}A^{-1}, \qquad (A^{\mathsf T})^{-1}=(A^{-1})^{\mathsf T}, \qquad \det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det A}.$$

**Adjugate method:** $A^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{\det A}A^{*}$, where $A^{*}=(A_{ji})$ is the transpose of the cofactor matrix (the adjugate). For $2\times 2$ matrices there is the handy formula

$$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix}.$$

An **elementary matrix** is obtained by performing one elementary operation on $I$; left-multiplying by it performs the corresponding **row operation**. This gives the **Gauss–Jordan inversion** method: row-reduce the augmented matrix $[A\mid I]$ until the left block becomes $I$; the right block is then $A^{-1}$:

$$[A\mid I] \xrightarrow{\ \text{row ops}\ } [I\mid A^{-1}].$$

### Elementary Row Operations and the Rank of a Matrix

The three **elementary row operations** are: swap two rows, multiply a row by a nonzero scalar, and add a multiple of one row to another. Any matrix can be reduced to **row echelon form**, and further to **reduced row echelon form**.

The **rank** $\operatorname{rank}(A)$ is the number of **nonzero rows** in a row echelon form, equivalently the order of the largest nonzero minor of $A$. Rank measures the dimension of the space spanned by the row (or column) vectors and is the key quantity for analysing solutions of systems. Properties: $\operatorname{rank}(A)=\operatorname{rank}(A^{\mathsf T})$; an $n\times n$ matrix is invertible $\iff \operatorname{rank}(A)=n \iff \det A\ne 0$ (called **full rank**).

### Block Matrices

Partitioning a matrix with horizontal and vertical lines into smaller submatrices (blocks) yields a **block matrix**. When the block shapes are compatible, addition, scalar multiplication, and multiplication proceed block by block as if the blocks were scalars, e.g.

$$\begin{bmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} X \\ Y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} AX+BY \\ CX+DY \end{bmatrix}.$$

For a **block-diagonal matrix** $\operatorname{diag}(A_1,A_2,\dots,A_k)$ the determinant and inverse split blockwise:

$$\det\begin{bmatrix} A & O \\ O & B \end{bmatrix} = \det A\cdot\det B, \qquad \begin{bmatrix} A & O \\ O & B \end{bmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} A^{-1} & O \\ O & B^{-1} \end{bmatrix}.$$

Blocking turns a large problem into smaller ones and is invaluable in both theory and large-scale computation.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Let $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Compute $AB$ and $BA$, and show that multiplication is not commutative.

> **Solution.** Computing entrywise,
> $$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot0+2\cdot1 & 1\cdot1+2\cdot0 \\ 3\cdot0+4\cdot1 & 3\cdot1+4\cdot0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix},$$
> $$BA = \begin{bmatrix} 0\cdot1+1\cdot3 & 0\cdot2+1\cdot4 \\ 1\cdot1+0\cdot3 & 1\cdot2+0\cdot4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}.$$
> Since $AB\ne BA$, matrix multiplication is in general **not commutative**. (Here $B$ is the row-swap elementary matrix, and $BA$ indeed swaps the two rows of $A$.)

**Example 2.** Use Gauss–Jordan elimination to invert $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$.

> **Solution.** Form the augmented matrix and reduce:
> $$\left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 3 & 5 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{R_2 - 3R_1} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -3 & 1 \end{array}\right].$$
> Then $-R_2$ followed by $R_1-2R_2$:
> $$\xrightarrow{\ \ } \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & -1 \end{array}\right] \xrightarrow{R_1 - 2R_2} \left[\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & -5 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 & -1 \end{array}\right].$$
> Hence $A^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix} -5 & 2 \\ 3 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$. Check: $\det A=5-6=-1$, and the $2\times 2$ formula gives $A^{-1}=\frac{1}{-1}\begin{bmatrix}5&-2\\-3&1\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}-5&2\\3&-1\end{bmatrix}$, in agreement.

**Example 3.** Find the rank of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

> **Solution.** Row-reduce to echelon form:
> $$\xrightarrow{R_2 - 2R_1,\ R_3 - R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & -1 & -2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.$$
> There are $2$ nonzero rows, so $\operatorname{rank}(A)=2$. (Row 2 is twice row 1, hence dependent, so the matrix falls short of full rank $3$.)

## Exercises

1. Let $A=\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ and $B=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$. Compute $2A - B$ and $AB$.
2. Decide whether $Q=\dfrac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix} 3 & -4 \\ 4 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ is orthogonal.
3. Find the inverse of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.
4. Find the rank of $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 & 5 \\ 3 & 4 & 7 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Answers.** 1) $2A-B=\begin{bmatrix}3&-4\\3&4\end{bmatrix}$; $AB=\begin{bmatrix}2&8\\-2&10\end{bmatrix}$. 2) $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=\frac{1}{25}\begin{bmatrix}3&4\\-4&3\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}3&-4\\4&3\end{bmatrix}=\frac{1}{25}\begin{bmatrix}25&0\\0&25\end{bmatrix}=I$, so $Q$ is orthogonal. 3) $\det A=1$, so $A^{-1}=\begin{bmatrix}3&-1\\-5&2\end{bmatrix}$. 4) After $R_2-2R_1,\ R_3-3R_1$ we get $\begin{bmatrix}1&1&2\\0&1&1\\0&1&1\end{bmatrix}$; then $R_3-R_2$ produces a zero row, so the rank is $2$.
