## Core Notes

### Monomials, Polynomials, and Integral Expressions

**Monomial:** an expression that is a **product of numbers and letters**, or a single number or letter alone, e.g. $3x^2$, $-5ab$, $7$, $x$. The **numerical factor** is the **coefficient**, and the **sum of the exponents** of all letters is the **degree**. For $-5x^2y^3$ the coefficient is $-5$ and the degree is $2+3=5$.

**Polynomial:** a **sum** of several monomials; each monomial is a **term**, and a term with no letters is the **constant term**. The degree of a polynomial is the **degree of its highest-degree term**. For example $2x^2-3x+1$ is a quadratic trinomial with constant term $1$.

**Integral expression (zhěngshì):** monomials and polynomials together are called **integral expressions**. Their hallmark: **no letter appears in a denominator** (no letter is used as a divisor). Thus $\dfrac{x}{2}$ is integral (denominator is the number $2$), while $\dfrac{2}{x}$ is not (the denominator contains a letter).

**Combining like terms:** terms with **the same letters raised to the same exponents** are like terms. To combine, add the coefficients and keep the letter part:

$$3x^2+5x^2=(3+5)x^2=8x^2.$$

**Pitfall:** $x^2$ and $x^3$ are not like terms and cannot be combined; all constant terms are like terms.

### Laws of Exponents

Let $m,n$ be positive integers and the letters stand for any numbers (the division law also holds when the base is nonzero). There are four **laws of exponents**:

- **Product of powers with the same base:** keep the base, add exponents. $$a^m\cdot a^n=a^{m+n}.$$
- **Quotient of powers with the same base:** keep the base, subtract exponents. $$a^m\div a^n=a^{m-n}\quad(a\ne0).$$
- **Power of a power:** keep the base, multiply exponents. $$(a^m)^n=a^{mn}.$$
- **Power of a product:** raise each factor to the power. $$(ab)^n=a^n b^n.$$

**Intuition:** all of these come from the definition of a power as repeated multiplication. E.g. $a^2\cdot a^3=(aa)(aaa)=a^5$, which is exactly adding the exponents.

The quotient law also yields $a^0=1\ (a\ne0)$, since $a^m\div a^m=a^{m-m}=a^0$, and this is plainly $1$.

**Pitfall:** $a^m\cdot a^n$ **adds** exponents ($a^{m+n}$), while $(a^m)^n$ **multiplies** them ($a^{mn}$) — easily confused. Also $(ab)^n=a^nb^n$, but $(a+b)^n\ne a^n+b^n$!

### Multiplying Integral Expressions and the Special Products

**Monomial times monomial:** multiply coefficients, and multiply powers of the same base. E.g. $3x^2\cdot 4x^3=12x^5$.

**Monomial times polynomial (distributive law):** $a(b+c)=ab+ac$.

**Polynomial times polynomial:** multiply every term of the first by every term of the second, then combine like terms:

$$(a+b)(c+d)=ac+ad+bc+bd.$$

Two indispensable **special products** (memorize them):

- **Difference of squares:** $$(a+b)(a-b)=a^2-b^2.$$
- **Perfect square:** $$(a\pm b)^2=a^2\pm 2ab+b^2.$$

**Intuition:** in the difference of squares, $(a+b)(a-b)=a^2-ab+ab-b^2$ and the two middle terms cancel. In the perfect square, the $2ab$ is the "cross term", the part most often forgotten.

**Pitfall:** $(a+b)^2=a^2+2ab+b^2\ne a^2+b^2$; dropping $2ab$ is the most common mistake.

### Factoring: Taking Out a Common Factor

**Factoring:** rewriting a polynomial as a **product of integral expressions** — the **inverse** of multiplication. E.g. $a^2-b^2=(a+b)(a-b)$.

**Common-factor method:** if every term shares a **common factor**, pull it outside the brackets. The common factor is the **gcd** of the coefficients times the **lowest power** of each shared letter:

$$ma+mb+mc=m(a+b+c).$$

For example $6x^3-9x^2=3x^2(2x-3)$: numerical common factor $3$, letter common factor $x^2$.

**Steps:** (1) find the common factor; (2) pull it out, leaving inside each original term **divided by** the common factor; (3) check whether the bracket factors further.

**Check:** expanding the product should recover the original polynomial.

**Pitfall:** after factoring, the bracket should have the same number of terms as the original; if a whole term **is** the common factor, leave a $1$ in its place, e.g. $2x^2+2x=2x(x+1)$, not $2x(x)$.

### Factoring: Formulas and the Cross Method

**Formula method:** use the special products in reverse.

- **Difference of squares:** $$a^2-b^2=(a+b)(a-b).$$ E.g. $x^2-9=(x+3)(x-3)$.
- **Perfect square:** $$a^2\pm 2ab+b^2=(a\pm b)^2.$$ E.g. $x^2+6x+9=(x+3)^2$.

**Cross method (factoring by inspection):** for a quadratic trinomial $x^2+px+q$, find two numbers $r,s$ with

$$r+s=p,\qquad rs=q,$$

so that $x^2+px+q=(x+r)(x+s)$. For example, to factor $x^2+5x+6$ find two numbers summing to $5$ and multiplying to $6$, namely $2$ and $3$, giving $x^2+5x+6=(x+2)(x+3)$.

**Tip:** look at the sign of $q$ first. If $q>0$, then $r,s$ have the same sign (set by $p$); if $q<0$, they have opposite signs.

**General order of factoring:** **common factor first, formulas second, cross method third**, then check that each factor is fully factored.

### Summary

- Integral expression = monomials + polynomials; coefficient, degree, like terms; integral means **no letter in a denominator**.
- Laws of exponents: $a^m a^n=a^{m+n}$, $a^m\div a^n=a^{m-n}$, $(a^m)^n=a^{mn}$, $(ab)^n=a^nb^n$.
- Special products: difference of squares $(a+b)(a-b)=a^2-b^2$; perfect square $(a\pm b)^2=a^2\pm2ab+b^2$ (do not drop $2ab$).
- Factoring (inverse of multiplication), in order: common factor → formulas → cross method.
- Cross method $x^2+px+q=(x+r)(x+s)$ with $r+s=p$, $rs=q$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Simplify $3x^2-5x+2x^2+4x-1$, and state the degree and constant term of the result.

> **Solution.** Combine like terms: $x^2$ terms $3x^2+2x^2=5x^2$; $x$ terms $-5x+4x=-x$; constant $-1$. Hence
> $$3x^2-5x+2x^2+4x-1 = 5x^2 - x - 1.$$
> This is a quadratic trinomial; the **degree is $2$** and the **constant term is $-1$**.

**Example 2.** Compute $(2x^3)^2\cdot x - x^7\div x^2$.

> **Solution.** Apply the laws of exponents term by term.
> $$(2x^3)^2 = 2^2\cdot(x^3)^2 = 4x^6,\qquad (2x^3)^2\cdot x = 4x^6\cdot x = 4x^{7}.$$
> $$x^7\div x^2 = x^{7-2} = x^5.$$
> Therefore
> $$(2x^3)^2\cdot x - x^7\div x^2 = 4x^7 - x^5.$$
> (Note $4x^7$ and $x^5$ are not like terms and cannot be combined further.)

**Example 3.** Use the special products: (1) $(3x+2)(3x-2)$; (2) $(2a-5)^2$.

> **Solution.** (1) Difference of squares with $a=3x,\ b=2$:
> $$(3x+2)(3x-2) = (3x)^2 - 2^2 = 9x^2 - 4.$$
> (2) Perfect square with $a=2a,\ b=5$, the cross term taking a minus sign:
> $$(2a-5)^2 = (2a)^2 - 2\cdot(2a)\cdot 5 + 5^2 = 4a^2 - 20a + 25.$$
> The key is not to drop the cross term $-20a$.

**Example 4.** Factor: (1) $6a^2b - 9ab^2$; (2) $4x^2 - 25$.

> **Solution.** (1) Common-factor method. The numerical common factor is $3$ and the letter common factor is $ab$:
> $$6a^2b - 9ab^2 = 3ab(2a - 3b).$$
> Check: $3ab\cdot 2a = 6a^2b$ and $3ab\cdot(-3b)=-9ab^2$, correct.
> (2) Difference of squares with $4x^2=(2x)^2$ and $25=5^2$:
> $$4x^2 - 25 = (2x)^2 - 5^2 = (2x+5)(2x-5).$$

**Example 5.** Factor: (1) $x^2 - 7x + 12$; (2) $2x^3 - 12x^2 + 18x$.

> **Solution.** (1) Cross method. Find $r,s$ with $r+s=-7$ and $rs=12$. Since the product is positive and the sum negative, both are negative; $(-3)+(-4)=-7$ and $(-3)(-4)=12$, so
> $$x^2 - 7x + 12 = (x-3)(x-4).$$
> (2) **Common factor first, then formula.** The common factor is $2x$:
> $$2x^3 - 12x^2 + 18x = 2x(x^2 - 6x + 9).$$
> The bracket is a perfect square ($x^2-6x+9=(x-3)^2$), so
> $$2x^3 - 12x^2 + 18x = 2x(x-3)^2.$$
> This illustrates the standard "common factor first, formula second" order.

## Exercises

1. State the coefficient and degree of $-4x^3y^2$, and say whether $\dfrac{5}{x}$ is an integral expression.
2. Compute $a^3\cdot a^4 + (a^2)^3 - a^9\div a^2$.
3. Use the special products to compute $(x+4)(x-4)+(x-3)^2$.
4. Factor $12x^3y - 18x^2y^2$.
5. Factor $9 - 16m^2$.
6. Factor $x^2 + x - 20$.

**Answers.**
1) Coefficient $-4$, degree $3+2=5$; $\dfrac{5}{x}$ has a letter in the denominator, so it is **not** integral.
2) $a^3\cdot a^4=a^7$, $(a^2)^3=a^6$, $a^9\div a^2=a^7$; total $a^7+a^6-a^7=a^6$.
3) $(x+4)(x-4)=x^2-16$, $(x-3)^2=x^2-6x+9$; sum $2x^2-6x-7$.
4) Common factor $6x^2y$: $12x^3y-18x^2y^2=6x^2y(2x-3y)$.
5) Difference of squares: $9-16m^2=3^2-(4m)^2=(3+4m)(3-4m)$.
6) Two numbers summing to $1$ and multiplying to $-20$ are $5$ and $-4$: $x^2+x-20=(x+5)(x-4)$.
