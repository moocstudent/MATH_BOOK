## Core Notes

**Linear programming (LP)** is optimization where both objective and constraints are linear. It is enormously applied (resource allocation, transportation, production planning) and theoretically elegant: the simplest case of convex optimization, with a complete duality theory and efficient algorithms. This chapter covers its standard form, the geometry of the feasible region, the idea behind the simplex method, LP duality, and sensitivity analysis.

### Standard Form of Linear Programming

The **standard form** (used in this chapter) is

$$\min_x\ c^\top x\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ Ax=b,\ \ x\ge 0,$$

with $x\in\mathbb{R}^n$, $A\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$ ($m<n$, full row rank), $c$ the **cost vector**, and $b$ the **resource vector**. Any LP reduces to this form: $\max c^\top x$ becomes $\min(-c^\top x)$; an inequality $a^\top x\le b$ gets a **slack variable** $s\ge 0$, written $a^\top x+s=b$; a free variable $x$ is split as $x=x^+-x^-$ ($x^\pm\ge0$). A uniform standard form makes algorithms convenient. Any $x$ with $Ax=b,\ x\ge0$ is a **feasible solution**.

### Feasible Region (Polyhedron) and Basic Feasible Solutions

The set defined by $Ax=b,\ x\ge0$ is a **polyhedron** (an intersection of finitely many halfspaces and hyperplanes), and it is **convex**. The key structural theorem of LP:

**Theorem.** If a linear objective attains its optimum over a polyhedron, it does so at some **vertex** (extreme point).

So one need only search finitely many vertices. The algebraic description of a vertex is a **basic feasible solution (BFS)**: choose $m$ linearly independent columns of $A$ as a **basis** $B$ (the others are nonbasic, with their **nonbasic variables** set to $0$), solve $B x_B=b$ for $x_B=B^{-1}b$; if $x_B\ge0$ the solution is feasible — a **basic feasible solution**, corresponding exactly to a vertex of the polyhedron. Intuitively a vertex is where constraints intersect "most tightly," with at most $m$ of the $n$ variables nonzero.

### The Idea of the Simplex Method

The **simplex method** (Dantzig) is the classic LP algorithm, with the core idea: **walk along the edges of the polyhedron from vertex to adjacent vertex, decreasing the objective each step, until no further decrease is possible — the optimum.**

Each step is a **pivot**: at the current BFS, compute the **reduced cost** $\bar c_j=c_j-c_B^\top B^{-1}A_j$ of each nonbasic variable (the net change in objective from raising that variable by one unit). If all $\bar c_j\ge 0$, the current vertex is **optimal** (raising any variable cannot decrease the objective); otherwise pick a nonbasic variable with $\bar c_j<0$ to **enter the basis**, and use the **minimum-ratio test** to choose a basic variable to **leave** (preserving feasibility $x\ge0$), update the basis, and move to an adjacent vertex. Iterating, since vertices are finite and the objective decreases monotonically, it terminates in finitely many steps (degeneracy may cause cycling, avoided by Bland's rule). The simplex method is extremely fast in practice, despite worst-case exponential complexity.

### The Dual Problem

Every LP (the **primal**) corresponds to an LP (the **dual**). For a primal in standard inequality form

$$\max\ c^\top x\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ Ax\le b,\ x\ge 0,$$

the **dual** is

$$\min\ b^\top y\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ A^\top y\ge c,\ y\ge 0.$$

The construction rule: each primal **constraint** gives a dual **variable** $y_i\ge0$, and each primal **variable** gives a dual **constraint**; the coefficient matrix is transposed, $b$ and $c$ swap roles, and $\max$ becomes $\min$. The dual variable $y_i$ is interpreted as the **shadow price** of resource $i$.

**Weak duality:** any primal-feasible $x$ and dual-feasible $y$ satisfy $c^\top x\le b^\top y$ (any feasible value of the primal $\max$ is at most any feasible value of the dual $\min$). **Strong duality (the fundamental theorem of LP):** if the primal has a finite optimum, so does the dual, and **their optimal values are equal** $c^\top x^*=b^\top y^*$ with zero duality gap. (LP always satisfies strong duality — no extra Slater condition needed.)

### Complementary Slackness and Sensitivity

Strong duality yields a precise characterization of optimality — **complementary slackness**: $x^*$ (primal) and $y^*$ (dual) are both optimal **if and only if**

$$y_i^*\,(b_i-A_i x^*)=0\ (\forall i),\qquad x_j^*\,(A_j^\top y^*-c_j)=0\ (\forall j).$$

That is: a constraint that is **not tight** (has leftover resource $b_i-A_i x^*>0$) $\Rightarrow$ its shadow price $y_i^*=0$; a variable that is positive $x_j^*>0$ $\Rightarrow$ the corresponding dual constraint holds with equality (that activity "exactly breaks even"). This gives an effective way to recover the dual solution from the primal (and to certify optimality).

**Sensitivity analysis** studies how small data changes affect the optimum. The optimal **shadow price** $y_i^*$ is precisely the rate of change of the optimal value with respect to resource $b_i$: while the optimal basis is unchanged, increasing $b_i$ by one unit changes the optimal objective by about $y_i^*$ — it measures "how much one more unit of that resource is worth," a central quantity in economic decisions. One similarly analyzes the range of cost changes $c_j$ over which the optimum stays the same.

### Summary

- Standard form: $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax=b,\ x\ge0$; inequalities get slack variables, free variables are split into differences.
- The feasible region is a polyhedron (convex); a linear objective is optimized at a **vertex** = a **basic feasible solution** ($x_B=B^{-1}b\ge0$, nonbasic variables $0$).
- Simplex: pivot between vertices; reduced cost $\bar c_j<0$ enters, minimum ratio leaves; all $\bar c_j\ge0$ means optimal.
- Duality: constraints$\leftrightarrow$variables, $A\to A^\top$, $b\leftrightarrow c$, $\max\leftrightarrow\min$; weak duality $c^\top x\le b^\top y$; LP always has strong duality $c^\top x^*=b^\top y^*$.
- Complementary slackness: a non-tight constraint $\Rightarrow y_i^*=0$; a positive variable $\Rightarrow$ its dual constraint is tight. Shadow price $y_i^*$ = sensitivity of the optimum to $b_i$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Convert the LP "$\max\ 3x_1+2x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2\le 4,\ x_1\ge0,\ x_2\ge0$" to standard form $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax=b,\ x\ge0$.

> **Solution.** Negate the objective to minimize: $\min\ -3x_1-2x_2$. Add a slack variable $s\ge0$ to the inequality: $x_1+x_2+s=4$. Then with $(x_1,x_2,s)\ge0$,
> $$c=(-3,-2,0)^\top,\quad A=[\,1\ \ 1\ \ 1\,],\quad b=4.$$
> That is, $\min\ -3x_1-2x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2+s=4,\ x_1,x_2,s\ge0$.

**Example 2.** Solve $\max\ x_1+x_2$ s.t. $x_1+2x_2\le 4,\ 3x_1+2x_2\le 6,\ x_1,x_2\ge0$ by vertex enumeration.

> **Solution.** The feasible region is a quadrilateral; enumerate vertices:
> - $(0,0)$: objective $0$.
> - $(2,0)$ (where $3x_1=6$ meets $x_2=0$): check $x_1+2x_2=2\le4$, objective $2$.
> - $(0,2)$ (where $x_1+2x_2=4$ meets $x_1=0$): check $3x_1+2x_2=4\le6$, objective $2$.
> - Intersection of the two constraints: solve $x_1+2x_2=4,\ 3x_1+2x_2=6$; subtracting, $2x_1=2\Rightarrow x_1=1,\ x_2=1.5$, objective $2.5$.
>
> The maximum is at the vertex $(1,1.5)$, with optimal value $2.5$.

**Example 3.** Write and solve the dual of Example 2, and verify strong duality.

> **Solution.** The primal is $\max\ c^\top x$ ($c=(1,1)$), $Ax\le b$ with $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\3&2\end{bmatrix},\ b=(4,6)^\top$. The dual is
> $$\min\ 4y_1+6y_2\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ y_1+3y_2\ge 1,\ 2y_1+2y_2\ge 1,\ y_1,y_2\ge0.$$
> Set both constraints to equality: $y_1+3y_2=1,\ 2y_1+2y_2=1$. From the first $y_1=1-3y_2$; substituting, $2(1-3y_2)+2y_2=1\Rightarrow 2-4y_2=1\Rightarrow y_2=0.25,\ y_1=0.25$, both $\ge0$. The dual objective is $4(0.25)+6(0.25)=1+1.5=2.5$, equal to the primal optimum $2.5$ from Example 2, so **strong duality holds**.

**Example 4.** Use complementary slackness to verify $x^*=(1,1.5)$ is optimal for Example 2 (given the dual solution $y^*=(0.25,0.25)$).

> **Solution.** Check primal slacks: $b_1-A_1 x^*=4-(1+3)=0$ and $b_2-A_2 x^*=6-(3+3)=0$, so both constraints are **tight**, allowing positive $y_i^*$, consistent with $y^*=(0.25,0.25)>0$. Check dual constraints: $x_1^*=1>0$ requires $y_1+3y_2=1$, and $0.25+0.75=1$✓; $x_2^*=1.5>0$ requires $2y_1+2y_2=1$, and $0.5+0.5=1$✓. All complementary-slackness conditions hold, so $x^*,y^*$ are mutually optimal.

**Example 5.** A plant uses resources $b=(4,6)$ (the two constraints of Example 2) for production, with optimal shadow prices $y^*=(0.25,0.25)$. If the first resource increases from $4$ to $5$ (everything else fixed, optimal basis unchanged), about how much does the optimal profit change?

> **Solution.** The shadow price $y_1^*=0.25$ means each extra unit of the first resource raises the optimal objective (here a $\max$ profit) by about $y_1^*=0.25$. Increasing the resource from $4$ to $5$ is $\Delta b_1=1$, so the optimal profit increases by about $0.25\times1=0.25$, from $2.5$ to about $2.75$ (valid within the sensitivity range where the optimal basis is unchanged).

## Exercises

1. Convert "$\min\ 2x_1+5x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2\ge 3,\ x_1\ge0,\ x_2\ge0$" to standard equality form (using a **surplus variable**).
2. Explain what a basic feasible solution (BFS) is, and its relationship to vertices of the feasible region.
3. By vertex enumeration, find the optimum and optimal value of $\max\ 2x_1+3x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2\le4,\ x_1\le3,\ x_1,x_2\ge0$.
4. Write the dual of the primal $\max\ 5x_1+4x_2$ s.t. $6x_1+4x_2\le 24,\ x_1+2x_2\le 6,\ x\ge0$.
5. State weak and strong duality for LP; does LP strong duality require an extra condition (such as Slater)?
6. A constraint has slack (is not tight) at the optimum. What is its shadow price? Justify via complementary slackness.

**Answers.** 1) $\min\ 2x_1+5x_2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2-s=3,\ x_1,x_2,s\ge0$ (the surplus variable $s\ge0$ is subtracted from the left side of the $\ge$ constraint). 2) A BFS is the feasible solution obtained by choosing $m$ linearly independent columns as a basis, setting nonbasic variables to $0$, and solving $x_B=B^{-1}b\ge0$; it is in **one-to-one correspondence with the vertices (extreme points)** of the polyhedron, and the LP optimum is attained at some BFS/vertex. 3) Vertices: $(0,0)\to0$; $(3,0)\to6$; $(3,1)$ (where $x_1=3$ meets $x_1+x_2=4$) $\to 6+3=9$; $(0,4)\to12$. Check $(0,4)$: $x_1+x_2=4\le4$✓, $x_1=0\le3$✓. The maximum is at $(0,4)$ with optimal value $12$. 4) Dual $\min\ 24y_1+6y_2$ s.t. $6y_1+y_2\ge5,\ 4y_1+2y_2\ge4,\ y_1,y_2\ge0$. 5) Weak duality: any primal-feasible $x$ and dual-feasible $y$ satisfy $c^\top x\le b^\top y$; strong duality: when the optimum is finite, the two optimal values are equal. LP strong duality requires **no** extra condition (it holds whenever the primal or dual has a finite optimum), unlike general convex optimization which needs Slater. 6) The shadow price is $\mathbf{0}$; by complementary slackness $y_i^*(b_i-A_i x^*)=0$, a constraint with slack has $b_i-A_i x^*>0$, forcing $y_i^*=0$ — surplus resource has no value.
