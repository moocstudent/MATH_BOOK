## Core Notes

### Inequalities and Their Properties

An **inequality** is an expression joining two quantities with a sign such as $<,>,\le,\ge,\ne$ to state their **relative size**. A value of the unknown that makes it true is a **solution**; the set of all solutions is the **solution set**.

Inequalities obey three **basic properties**, the basis for all manipulation:

- **Property 1 (add/subtract):** adding (or subtracting) the same number or expression to both sides leaves the direction **unchanged**. If $a>b$, then $a\pm c>b\pm c$.
- **Property 2 (multiply/divide by a positive):** multiplying or dividing both sides by the same **positive** number leaves the direction **unchanged**. If $a>b$ and $c>0$, then $ac>bc$.
- **Property 3 (multiply/divide by a negative):** multiplying or dividing both sides by the same **negative** number **must reverse** the direction. If $a>b$ and $c<0$, then $ac<bc$.

**Property 3 is the crucial and most error-prone one:** on multiplying or dividing by a negative, $>$ becomes $<$, $\le$ becomes $\ge$, and so on.

**Intuition:** picture an inequality as "who sits farther right" on the number line. Adding/subtracting just slides both, so the order holds; scaling by a positive stretches in the same direction, so the order holds; but scaling by a negative **flips** the line, so the order reverses.

**Pitfall:** the commonest mistake is dividing by a coefficient of the unknown **without checking its sign** — keep the direction only when you know the coefficient is positive; if it is negative you must reverse.

### Linear Inequalities

An inequality with one unknown of degree $1$ is a **linear inequality**, rearrangeable to $ax+b>0$ (or $<,\le,\ge$). It is solved almost exactly like a linear equation, replacing "=" with the inequality sign, with one added caution:

After **clearing denominators, removing brackets, transposing and combining**, the last step — **making the coefficient $1$** by dividing both sides by the coefficient $a$ — keeps the direction if $a>0$ but **reverses it if $a<0$**.

For example, to solve $-2x+1>5$: transpose to $-2x>4$; dividing by $-2$ (negative) **reverses** it to $x<-2$.

The solution set is usually drawn on the **number line**: an open dot $\circ$ marks an excluded endpoint ($<,>$), a solid dot $\bullet$ an included one ($\le,\ge$), with a ray toward the solutions.

**Pitfall:** reverse the sign whenever you divide by a negative in the last step; on the number line, the dot's "solid/open" must match whether equality is allowed.

### Systems of Linear Inequalities

Several linear inequalities in the **same** unknown taken together form a **system of linear inequalities**. Its **solution set** is the **common part (intersection)** of the individual solution sets. Method:

1. Solve **each** inequality separately.
2. Draw the solution sets on the **same number line** and take their **overlap** — that overlap is the system's solution set.

A mnemonic (for two solution sets): **both-greater take the greater, both-smaller take the smaller, greater-than/less-than overlapping take the middle, otherwise no solution**. For instance $\begin{cases}x>1\\ x\le4\end{cases}$ overlaps in $1<x\le4$, whereas $\begin{cases}x>4\\ x<1\end{cases}$ has no overlap and is **inconsistent**.

**Intuition:** each inequality marks a stretch of the line, and the system's solution is where those stretches **overlap at once** — drawing and intersecting is the surest method.

**Pitfall:** take the **intersection** (satisfy all), not the union; the mnemonic is only an aid — when unsure, always **draw the number line**.

### Quadratic Inequalities

An inequality of the form $ax^2+bx+c>0$ (or $<,\le,\ge$, with $a\ne0$) is a **quadratic inequality**. It is tied to the quadratic function $y=ax^2+bx+c$ (a parabola): **the solution set is the range of $x$ where the parabola lies above (or below) the $x$-axis**. Take $a>0$ and set $\Delta=b^2-4ac$:

- If $\Delta>0$, the equation has roots $x_1<x_2$ and the parabola opens upward. Then
  - $ax^2+bx+c>0$ has solution $x<x_1$ **or** $x>x_2$ (outside the roots);
  - $ax^2+bx+c<0$ has solution $x_1<x<x_2$ (between the roots).
- If $\Delta=0$, there is a repeated root $x_0$: $>0$ gives $x\ne x_0$, and $<0$ has no solution.
- If $\Delta<0$, the parabola stays entirely above the axis: $>0$ holds always (all reals), and $<0$ has no solution.

**Procedure:** put it in standard form with $a>0$ → find the roots (or the discriminant) → sketch the upward parabola → write the set by "**greater outside, less between**". Factoring with a sign analysis works too.

**Pitfall:** if $a<0$, first multiply both sides by $-1$ to **reverse the sign** into the $a>0$ case; "greater outside, less between" holds only for an upward parabola ($a>0$).

### A First Look at Absolute-Value Inequalities

$|x|$ is the **distance** of $x$ from the origin, so always $|x|\ge0$. From this geometric meaning come two **basic forms** (with $a>0$):

- $|x|<a \iff -a<x<a$ (distance to the origin less than $a$ — squeezed **between** $-a$ and $a$).
- $|x|>a \iff x<-a \text{ or } x>a$ (distance greater than $a$ — lying **outside** on both sides).

If $x$ is replaced by a linear expression (e.g. $|2x-1|$), first make the whole fit the form above, then solve the linear inequality inside. For example $|2x-1|<3 \iff -3<2x-1<3$; adding $1$ gives $-2<2x<4$, and dividing by $2$ gives $-1<x<2$.

**Intuition:** absolute-value inequalities speak the language of **distance**. "Less than" = close = trapped in the middle (one interval); "greater than" = far = split to both sides (two rays).

**Pitfall:** before applying the forms, make sure the right side has $a>0$; the solution of $|x|>a$ is two pieces joined by "**or**" — never write the contradictory $-a>x>a$.

### Summary

- Three properties: add/subtract keeps the direction; multiply/divide by a **positive** keeps it; by a **negative reverses** it (the key trap).
- Linear inequality: solved like an equation, but **reverse the sign when dividing by a negative**; show the set on the number line (open $\circ$ excluded, solid $\bullet$ included).
- System of inequalities: solve each → take the **intersection** on the number line; both-greater take the greater, both-smaller take the smaller, overlapping take the middle, otherwise none.
- Quadratic inequality ($a>0$): use $\Delta$ and the roots, **greater outside, less between**; fix signs via the parabola or factoring.
- Absolute-value inequality ($a>0$): $|x|<a\iff -a<x<a$; $|x|>a\iff x<-a$ or $x>a$ (the language of distance).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** (Linear inequality) Solve $3x-2\le 5x+4$ and show the solution set on the number line.

> **Solution.** Transpose ($5x$ to the left, $-2$ to the right): $3x-5x\le 4+2$, i.e. $-2x\le 6$.
> Divide both sides by $-2$ (**negative — reverse**): $x\ge -3$.
> The solution set is $x\ge -3$. On the number line, place a **solid** dot at $-3$ (endpoint included) and draw a ray to the **right**.

**Example 2.** (Inequality with denominators) Solve $\dfrac{2x-1}{3}>\dfrac{x+1}{2}-1$.

> **Solution.** Multiply both sides by $6$ (clear denominators — positive, direction unchanged):
> $$2(2x-1)>3(x+1)-6.$$
> Remove brackets: $4x-2>3x+3-6$, i.e. $4x-2>3x-3$.
> Transpose and combine: $x>-1$.
> So the solution set is $x>-1$.

**Example 3.** (System of inequalities) Solve $\begin{cases} 2x-1>3, \\ 3x+2\le 14. \end{cases}$

> **Solution.** Solve each inequality:
> from $2x-1>3$, $2x>4$, i.e. $x>2$;
> from $3x+2\le14$, $3x\le12$, i.e. $x\le4$.
> Take the **common part** on the number line: $2<x\le4$.
> So the solution set is $2<x\le4$.

**Example 4.** (Quadratic inequality) Solve $x^2-x-6>0$.

> **Solution.** First solve the equation $x^2-x-6=0$; factoring $(x-3)(x+2)=0$ gives roots $x_1=-2,\ x_2=3$.
> The leading coefficient $a=1>0$, so the parabola opens upward and meets the $x$-axis at $-2$ and $3$.
> For $x^2-x-6>0$ (parabola **above** the axis), "**greater outside**":
> $$x<-2 \;\text{or}\; x>3.$$
> So the solution set is $x<-2$ or $x>3$.

**Example 5.** (Absolute value, application) A part has nominal length $50$ mm, with a machining tolerance of at most $0.2$ mm. Letting the actual length be $x$ mm, express the acceptance condition as an inequality and find the range of $x$.

> **Solution.** "The absolute difference between actual and nominal length is at most $0.2$" means the acceptance condition is
> $$|x-50|\le 0.2.$$
> Since $|x-50|\le0.2 \iff -0.2\le x-50\le0.2$, add $50$ throughout:
> $$49.8\le x\le 50.2.$$
> **Answer:** the acceptance condition is $|x-50|\le0.2$, i.e. the actual length satisfies $49.8\le x\le50.2$ (mm).

## Exercises

1. Solve $5-2x<1$ and show the solution set on the number line.
2. Solve $\dfrac{x-1}{2}\ge \dfrac{2x+1}{3}$.
3. Solve the system $\begin{cases} x+3>1, \\ 2x-1\le 5. \end{cases}$
4. Solve the quadratic inequality $x^2-4\le 0$.
5. Solve the absolute-value inequality $|3x-2|<4$.
6. (Application) On a maths test, Tom has finished the first two big questions for $58$ marks; the last question is worth $40$ marks. If he wants a total of at least $85$, at least how many marks must he score on the last question?

**Answers.**
1) $-2x<-4$; dividing by $-2$ **reverses** to $x>2$; open dot at $2$, ray to the right.
2) Multiply by $6$: $3(x-1)\ge2(2x+1)$, $3x-3\ge4x+2$, $-x\ge5$; dividing by $-1$ **reverses** to $x\le-5$.
3) From $x+3>1$, $x>-2$; from $2x-1\le5$, $x\le3$; the common part is $-2<x\le3$.
4) $x^2\le4$, i.e. $(x-2)(x+2)\le0$; upward parabola, "less between", solution $-2\le x\le2$.
5) $-4<3x-2<4$; adding $2$ gives $-2<3x<6$; dividing by $3$ gives $-\dfrac{2}{3}<x<2$.
6) Let the last score be $x$; $58+x\ge85$, so $x\ge27$; also $x\le40$, hence at least $27$ marks (and no more than the full $40$).
