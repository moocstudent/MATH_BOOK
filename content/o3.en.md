## Core Notes

### First-Order Linear Systems

A set of unknown functions $x_1(t),\dots,x_n(t)$ coupled through first derivatives is a **system of differential equations**. A **linear constant-coefficient homogeneous system** is written in matrix form
$$\frac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}=A\mathbf{x},\qquad \mathbf{x}=\begin{pmatrix}x_1\\ \vdots\\ x_n\end{pmatrix},\quad A\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}.$$
The two-dimensional case is most common:
$$\begin{cases}\dfrac{dx}{dt}=ax+by,\\[4pt] \dfrac{dy}{dt}=cx+dy,\end{cases}\qquad A=\begin{pmatrix}a & b\\ c & d\end{pmatrix}.$$

**Reducing a higher-order equation to a system**: any $n$-th order equation reduces to a first-order system. For $y''+py'+qy=0$, set $x_1=y,\ x_2=y'$:
$$\begin{cases}x_1'=x_2,\\ x_2'=-qx_1-px_2,\end{cases}\qquad A=\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -q & -p\end{pmatrix}.$$
Thus the theory of systems unifies higher-order linear equations. The solution structure is as before: an $n$-dimensional homogeneous system has $n$ linearly independent solutions $\mathbf{x}_1,\dots,\mathbf{x}_n$, with general solution $\mathbf{x}=C_1\mathbf{x}_1+\cdots+C_n\mathbf{x}_n$.

### The Eigenvalue–Eigenvector Method

For $\mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x}$, **try** $\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{v}\,e^{\lambda t}$ with $\mathbf{v}$ a constant vector. Substituting gives $\lambda\mathbf{v}e^{\lambda t}=A\mathbf{v}e^{\lambda t}$, and cancelling $e^{\lambda t}$:
$$A\mathbf{v}=\lambda\mathbf{v},$$
so $\lambda$ is an **eigenvalue** of $A$ and $\mathbf{v}$ a corresponding **eigenvector**. The eigenvalues come from the **characteristic equation**
$$\det(A-\lambda I)=0.$$
In two dimensions this is $\lambda^2-(\operatorname{tr}A)\lambda+\det A=0$, where $\operatorname{tr}A=a+d$ and $\det A=ad-bc$.

**Distinct real eigenvalues** $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ with eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2$ give
$$\mathbf{x}=C_1\mathbf{v}_1e^{\lambda_1 t}+C_2\mathbf{v}_2e^{\lambda_2 t}.$$
For **complex eigenvalues** $\lambda=\alpha\pm\beta i$, take real and imaginary parts of one complex solution $\mathbf{v}e^{\lambda t}$ to obtain two real solutions. For a **repeated eigenvalue** with too few eigenvectors, append a **generalized eigenvector** $\mathbf{w}$ (with $(A-\lambda I)\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{v}$), giving the solution $(\mathbf{w}+t\mathbf{v})e^{\lambda t}$.

### The Phase Plane and Trajectories

Viewing a solution $\big(x(t),y(t)\big)$ as a point moving with $t$ in the $xy$-plane (the **phase plane**), the curve it traces is a **trajectory** (orbit). All trajectories with direction arrows form the **phase portrait**.

A key property of an autonomous system $\mathbf{x}'=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ (right side independent of $t$): trajectories **do not cross** themselves or each other (by uniqueness), and the tangent vector at each point is $\mathbf{f}$. Eliminating $t$ gives the equation satisfied by trajectories, e.g. in two dimensions
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}=\frac{cx+dy}{ax+by}.$$

A point $\mathbf{x}_*$ with $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_*)=\mathbf{0}$ is an **equilibrium point** (critical point, steady state): a solution starting there stays fixed. For a linear system $\mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x}$ with $\det A\neq 0$, the unique equilibrium is the origin.

### Classification of Equilibria and Stability (Node / Saddle / Spiral / Center)

The type of the origin for a linear system is determined by the eigenvalues $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$:

- **Node**: $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ real with the same sign. Both negative → trajectories approach the origin (**stable node**); both positive → recede (**unstable node**).
- **Saddle**: $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ real with opposite signs. One eigen-direction enters and one exits, with hyperbola-like trajectories; **unstable**.
- **Spiral (focus)**: complex eigenvalues $\alpha\pm\beta i$ with $\alpha\neq 0$. Trajectories spiral: $\alpha<0$ inward (**stable spiral**), $\alpha>0$ outward (**unstable spiral**).
- **Center**: purely imaginary eigenvalues $\pm\beta i$ ($\alpha=0$). Trajectories are **closed curves** (ellipses) around the origin; solutions oscillate periodically, **stable but not asymptotically stable**.

Stability concepts: if solutions starting near an equilibrium stay near it, the equilibrium is **(Lyapunov) stable**; if they also approach it as $t\to\infty$, it is **asymptotically stable**. A trace–determinant criterion: with $T=\operatorname{tr}A,\ D=\det A$, $D<0$ gives a saddle; for $D>0$, $T<0$ is asymptotically stable, $T>0$ unstable, $T=0$ a center; the sign of the discriminant $T^2-4D$ distinguishes nodes ($>0$) from spirals ($<0$).

### Linearization and Applications

The local behavior of a nonlinear autonomous system $\mathbf{x}'=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ near an equilibrium $\mathbf{x}_*$ can be studied by **linearization**, using the **Jacobian matrix**
$$J=\left.\frac{\partial \mathbf{f}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right|_{\mathbf{x}_*}=\begin{pmatrix}\partial f_1/\partial x & \partial f_1/\partial y\\ \partial f_2/\partial x & \partial f_2/\partial y\end{pmatrix}_{\mathbf{x}_*}$$
to approximate $\mathbf{x}'\approx J(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}_*)$. The **Hartman–Grobman theorem**: if all eigenvalues of $J$ have nonzero real part (a hyperbolic equilibrium), the phase portrait of the nonlinear system near the equilibrium is **topologically equivalent** to that of its linearization — so the node/saddle/spiral classification carries over directly. Only the **center** is a borderline case where linearization may be unreliable.

Typical applications include the **predator–prey (Lotka–Volterra) model** and the pendulum equation: finding equilibria, computing the Jacobian, and classifying tells us whether the system oscillates, is stable, or destabilizes.

### Summary

- Linear constant-coefficient system $\mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x}$; higher-order equations reduce to first-order systems.
- Method: solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$; distinct real roots $\to C_1\mathbf{v}_1e^{\lambda_1 t}+C_2\mathbf{v}_2e^{\lambda_2 t}$; complex roots use real/imaginary parts; defective repeated roots use a generalized eigenvector $(\mathbf{w}+t\mathbf{v})e^{\lambda t}$.
- Phase plane: trajectories do not cross; equilibria where $\mathbf{f}=\mathbf{0}$; $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{cx+dy}{ax+by}$.
- Classification: same-sign real = node, opposite-sign = saddle, complex with $\alpha\neq0$ = spiral, purely imaginary = center; $D<0$ saddle, $D>0\,\&\,T<0$ asymptotically stable.
- Nonlinear: linearize at an equilibrium via the Jacobian $J$; the type of a hyperbolic equilibrium is determined by $J$ (Hartman–Grobman).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Reduce the second-order equation $y''-3y'+2y=0$ to a first-order linear system and write its coefficient matrix.

> **Solution.** Set $x_1=y,\ x_2=y'$. Then $x_1'=y'=x_2$ and $x_2'=y''=3y'-2y=-2x_1+3x_2$. Hence
> $$\begin{cases}x_1'=x_2,\\ x_2'=-2x_1+3x_2,\end{cases}\qquad A=\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -2 & 3\end{pmatrix}.$$
> One checks $\det(A-\lambda I)=\lambda^2-3\lambda+2$, matching the original characteristic equation.

**Example 2.** Find the general solution of $\mathbf{x}'=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2\\ 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$ and classify the origin.

> **Solution.** The characteristic equation $\det(A-\lambda I)=(1-\lambda)(3-\lambda)=0$ gives $\lambda_1=1,\ \lambda_2=3$.
> For $\lambda_1=1$: $(A-I)\mathbf{v}=0$, i.e. $\begin{pmatrix}0&2\\0&2\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=0$, take $\mathbf{v}_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$.
> For $\lambda_2=3$: $\begin{pmatrix}-2&2\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v}=0$, take $\mathbf{v}_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}$.
> The general solution is
> $$\mathbf{x}=C_1\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}e^{t}+C_2\begin{pmatrix}1\\1\end{pmatrix}e^{3t}.$$
> Both eigenvalues are positive real, so the origin is an **unstable node**.

**Example 3.** Classify the origin and its stability for $\mathbf{x}'=\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\\ 4 & 1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$.

> **Solution.** $T=\operatorname{tr}A=2,\ D=\det A=1\cdot1-1\cdot4=-3$. The characteristic equation $\lambda^2-2\lambda-3=0$ has roots $\lambda=3,\ -1$, of opposite sign.
> Since $D=-3<0$, the origin is a **saddle**, hence **unstable**. The direction for $\lambda=3$ is the unstable manifold (trajectories recede along it); that for $\lambda=-1$ is the stable manifold (they approach along it).

**Example 4.** Find the real general solution of $\mathbf{x}'=\begin{pmatrix}0 & -1\\ 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$ and describe the phase portrait.

> **Solution.** The characteristic equation $\lambda^2+1=0$ has roots $\lambda=\pm i$ ($\alpha=0,\beta=1$). For $\lambda=i$, solve $(A-iI)\mathbf{v}=0$, i.e. $-iv_1-v_2=0$, take $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ -i\end{pmatrix}$. The complex solution
> $$\mathbf{v}e^{it}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\ -i\end{pmatrix}(\cos t+i\sin t)=\begin{pmatrix}\cos t\\ \sin t\end{pmatrix}+i\begin{pmatrix}\sin t\\ -\cos t\end{pmatrix}.$$
> Real and imaginary parts give the real general solution
> $$\mathbf{x}=C_1\begin{pmatrix}\cos t\\ \sin t\end{pmatrix}+C_2\begin{pmatrix}\sin t\\ -\cos t\end{pmatrix}.$$
> With purely imaginary eigenvalues, the origin is a **center**: trajectories are concentric circles and solutions oscillate periodically with constant amplitude.

**Example 5.** Find the equilibria of the nonlinear system $\begin{cases}x'=y,\\ y'=-\sin x\end{cases}$ (undamped pendulum) and classify the origin $(0,0)$ by linearization.

> **Solution.** Equilibria satisfy $y=0$ and $\sin x=0$, i.e. $(x,y)=(k\pi,0),\ k\in\mathbb{Z}$. The Jacobian is
> $$J=\begin{pmatrix}\partial_x f_1 & \partial_y f_1\\ \partial_x f_2 & \partial_y f_2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\\ -\cos x & 0\end{pmatrix}.$$
> At $(0,0)$: $J=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\ -1&0\end{pmatrix}$, with eigenvalues $\lambda=\pm i$. The linearization is a center. This is a borderline case, but energy conservation makes the true origin a center (small oscillations are periodic). At $(\pi,0)$: $J=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\ 1&0\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $\pm1$, a **saddle** (the inverted position, unstable).

## Exercises

1. Reduce $y''+4y=0$ to a first-order system, write $A$, and find its eigenvalues.
2. Find the general solution of $\mathbf{x}'=\begin{pmatrix}2 & 0\\ 0 & -3\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$ and classify the origin.
3. For $A=\begin{pmatrix}-1 & 2\\ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}$, find the general solution of $\mathbf{x}'=A\mathbf{x}$ (hint: repeated eigenvalue, generalized eigenvector needed).
4. For $A=\begin{pmatrix}-2 & 1\\ -1 & -2\end{pmatrix}$, use the trace–determinant criterion to classify the origin and its stability.
5. For a 2D linear system with $\operatorname{tr}A=-4,\ \det A=5$, decide whether the origin is a node or spiral and whether it is stable.
6. Find all equilibria of the nonlinear system $\begin{cases}x'=x-x^2-xy\\ y'=-y+xy\end{cases}$.

**Answers.** 1) Set $x_1=y,x_2=y'$, $A=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\-4&0\end{pmatrix}$, $\det(A-\lambda I)=\lambda^2+4=0$, eigenvalues $\lambda=\pm2i$; 2) $\lambda=2,-3$, $\mathbf{x}=C_1\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}e^{2t}+C_2\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}e^{-3t}$, opposite-sign real → **saddle** (unstable); 3) $\det(A-\lambda I)=(\lambda+1)^2=0$, double root $\lambda=-1$; $(A+I)\mathbf{v}=0$ gives $\mathbf{v}=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ (only one), solving $(A+I)\mathbf{w}=\mathbf{v}$ gives $\mathbf{w}=\begin{pmatrix}0\\ \tfrac12\end{pmatrix}$, so $\mathbf{x}=C_1\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}e^{-t}+C_2\!\left[\begin{pmatrix}0\\ \tfrac12\end{pmatrix}+t\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}\right]e^{-t}$; 4) $T=-4,D=5$, characteristic equation $\lambda^2+4\lambda+5=0$, $\lambda=-2\pm i$, complex with real part $<0$ → **stable spiral**; 5) discriminant $T^2-4D=16-20=-4<0$ → spiral; $T=-4<0$ → **stable spiral**; 6) from $x(1-x-y)=0$ and $y(x-1)=0$: if $x=0$ then $y\cdot(-1)=0\Rightarrow y=0$, giving $(0,0)$; if $1-x-y=0$ and $y=0\Rightarrow x=1$, giving $(1,0)$; if $1-x-y=0$ and $x=1\Rightarrow y=0$, again $(1,0)$. So the equilibria are $(0,0)$ and $(1,0)$.
