## Core Notes

### The Simple Linear Regression Model

Many problems ask how a variable $y$ depends on a variable $x$. **Regression analysis** uses data to build a quantitative relationship between them.

Given $n$ values $x_1,\dots,x_n$ of the predictor with observed responses $y_1,\dots,y_n$, the **simple linear regression model** assumes
$$y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+\varepsilon_i,\qquad i=1,\dots,n,$$
where $\beta_0$ (intercept) and $\beta_1$ (slope) are unknown **regression coefficients**, and $\varepsilon_i$ is the **random error** with
$$E[\varepsilon_i]=0,\quad \operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_i)=\sigma^2,\quad \varepsilon_1,\dots,\varepsilon_n\ \text{mutually independent};$$
for testing one usually assumes further $\varepsilon_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2)$.

Since $E[\varepsilon_i]=0$, we have $E[y_i]=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i$, and (once the coefficients are estimated)
$$\hat y=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x$$
is the **regression line**. Its meaning: a one-unit increase in $x$ changes the mean of $y$ by $\beta_1$ on average. Here $x$ is treated as a controllable, non-random quantity, with all randomness in the error $\varepsilon$.

### Least-Squares Estimation

How do we obtain $\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1$ from data? The **method of least squares** chooses the coefficients minimizing the **residual sum of squares**:
$$Q(\beta_0,\beta_1)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big(y_i-\beta_0-\beta_1 x_i\big)^2\ \longrightarrow\ \min.$$
Setting the partial derivatives in $\beta_0,\beta_1$ to zero gives the normal equations, whose solution is the **least-squares estimator**:
$$\hat\beta_1=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)}{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar x)^2}=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}},\qquad \hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x,$$
where
$$S_{xx}=\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2,\quad S_{yy}=\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2,\quad S_{xy}=\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y).$$
The line always passes through the sample center $(\bar x,\bar y)$. Under normality $\hat\beta_0,\hat\beta_1$ are also the MLEs and are unbiased: $E[\hat\beta_1]=\beta_1$.

**Estimating the error variance.** With **residuals** $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$ and **residual sum of squares** $SSE=\sum e_i^2$,
$$\hat\sigma^2=\frac{SSE}{n-2}$$
is an unbiased estimator of $\sigma^2$ (the denominator $n-2$ because $2$ coefficients were estimated).

### Significance Tests for Regression

Even if $x$ and $y$ are unrelated ($\beta_1=0$), the data still produce a line; we must test whether the slope is **significantly nonzero**.

**Sum-of-squares decomposition.** The total variation splits into regression and residual parts:
$$\underbrace{\sum(y_i-\bar y)^2}_{SST}=\underbrace{\sum(\hat y_i-\bar y)^2}_{SSR}+\underbrace{\sum(y_i-\hat y_i)^2}_{SSE},$$
i.e. $SST=SSR+SSE$, with $SSR=\hat\beta_1 S_{xy}=\hat\beta_1^2 S_{xx}$.

**t test.** Test $H_0:\beta_1=0$ vs $H_1:\beta_1\ne0$. Under normality,
$$T=\frac{\hat\beta_1}{\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}}\overset{H_0}{\sim}t(n-2),$$
with rejection region $|T|>t_{\alpha/2}(n-2)$, where $\hat\sigma=\sqrt{SSE/(n-2)}$ is the residual standard deviation.

**F test (equivalent to t).**
$$F=\frac{SSR/1}{SSE/(n-2)}\overset{H_0}{\sim}F(1,n-2),\qquad F=T^2,$$
with rejection region $F>F_\alpha(1,n-2)$. The two tests give the same conclusion.

### Prediction and Correlation

**Prediction.** At a new point $x_0$, the regression gives the **point prediction** $\hat y_0=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x_0$. It serves both as an estimate of $E[y\mid x_0]$ and as a prediction of a single new response $y_0$; the latter has a wider prediction interval (it also carries one share of individual error $\sigma^2$). Extrapolation ($x_0$ far outside the sample range) is risky and should be done cautiously.

**Correlation coefficient.** The **sample correlation coefficient** measures the strength and direction of the linear association between $x$ and $y$:
$$r=\frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}},\qquad -1\le r\le 1.$$
$r>0$ is positive correlation, $r<0$ negative; the closer $|r|$ is to $1$, the stronger the linear relation, while $r=0$ means linearly uncorrelated.

**Link to regression.** It shares the sign of the slope: $\hat\beta_1=r\sqrt{S_{yy}/S_{xx}}$; its square
$$r^2=\frac{SSR}{SST}$$
is the **coefficient of determination**, the proportion of total variation explained by the regression (e.g. $r^2=0.9$ means $90\%$ of the variation is explained by $x$).

### One-Way ANOVA

**Analysis of variance** (ANOVA) tests whether several population means are equal. Let a single factor $A$ have $k$ levels, with $n_i$ observations $y_{ij}$ at level $i$ and total size $n=\sum n_i$. The model
$$y_{ij}=\mu_i+\varepsilon_{ij},\qquad \varepsilon_{ij}\sim N(0,\sigma^2)\ \text{independent},$$
tests $H_0:\mu_1=\mu_2=\dots=\mu_k$ vs $H_1:$ the $\mu_i$ are not all equal.

**Sum-of-squares decomposition.** With grand mean $\bar y$ and group means $\bar y_i$, the total variation splits into **between-group** (due to factor $A$) and **within-group** (random error):
$$\underbrace{\sum_{i,j}(y_{ij}-\bar y)^2}_{SST}=\underbrace{\sum_i n_i(\bar y_i-\bar y)^2}_{SSA}+\underbrace{\sum_{i,j}(y_{ij}-\bar y_i)^2}_{SSE},$$
i.e. $SST=SSA+SSE$, with degrees of freedom $n-1=(k-1)+(n-k)$.

**F test.** With mean squares $MSA=\dfrac{SSA}{k-1}$ and $MSE=\dfrac{SSE}{n-k}$, under $H_0$
$$F=\frac{MSA}{MSE}=\frac{SSA/(k-1)}{SSE/(n-k)}\overset{H_0}{\sim}F(k-1,\,n-k),$$
with rejection region $F>F_\alpha(k-1,n-k)$. Intuition: a large $F$ means the between-group differences far exceed the within-group random scatter, contradicting "all means equal". Results are tabulated in an **ANOVA table** (source, sum of squares, df, mean square, $F$).

### Summary

- Model $y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+\varepsilon_i$, with $E[\varepsilon_i]=0$, $\operatorname{Var}=\sigma^2$, independent (normal for testing).
- Least squares: $\hat\beta_1=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}$, $\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x$; passes through $(\bar x,\bar y)$; $\hat\sigma^2=\dfrac{SSE}{n-2}$.
- Significance: $SST=SSR+SSE$; $T=\dfrac{\hat\beta_1}{\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}}\sim t(n-2)$, $F=\dfrac{SSR}{SSE/(n-2)}\sim F(1,n-2)=T^2$.
- Prediction $\hat y_0=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x_0$ (wider interval for a single $y_0$); extrapolate cautiously.
- Correlation $r=\dfrac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}S_{yy}}}\in[-1,1]$; $\hat\beta_1=r\sqrt{S_{yy}/S_{xx}}$; determination $r^2=SSR/SST$.
- One-way ANOVA: $SST=SSA+SSE$, $n-1=(k-1)+(n-k)$; $F=\dfrac{SSA/(k-1)}{SSE/(n-k)}\sim F(k-1,n-k)$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** (Least-squares line) For output $x$ (hundreds of units) and cost $y$ (thousand yuan), the $5$ data points are $x:1,2,3,4,5$; $y:6,5,7,10,12$. Find the regression line $\hat y=\hat\beta_0+\hat\beta_1 x$.

> **Solution.** Means: $\bar x=3$, $\bar y=\dfrac{6+5+7+10+12}{5}=8$.
> Deviations: $x_i-\bar x=-2,-1,0,1,2$; $y_i-\bar y=-2,-3,-1,2,4$.
> $$S_{xx}=\sum(x_i-\bar x)^2=4+1+0+1+4=10,$$
> $$S_{xy}=\sum(x_i-\bar x)(y_i-\bar y)=(-2)(-2)+(-1)(-3)+0+(1)(2)+(2)(4)=4+3+0+2+8=17.$$
> Hence
> $$\hat\beta_1=\frac{S_{xy}}{S_{xx}}=\frac{17}{10}=1.7,\qquad \hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x=8-1.7\times3=2.9.$$
> The regression line is $\hat y=2.9+1.7x$.

**Example 2.** (Error variance) Continuing Example 1, with $x:1,2,3,4,5$, $y:6,5,7,10,12$, $\hat y=2.9+1.7x$. Find the residual sum of squares $SSE$ and $\hat\sigma^2$.

> **Solution.** Fitted values $\hat y_i=2.9+1.7x_i$: $x=1\!:\!4.6$, $x=2\!:\!6.3$, $x=3\!:\!8.0$, $x=4\!:\!9.7$, $x=5\!:\!11.4$. Residuals $e_i=y_i-\hat y_i$:
> $$6-4.6=1.4,\quad 5-6.3=-1.3,\quad 7-8.0=-1.0,\quad 10-9.7=0.3,\quad 12-11.4=0.6.$$
> $$SSE=\sum e_i^2=1.96+1.69+1.00+0.09+0.36=5.10.$$
> Hence $\hat\sigma^2=\dfrac{SSE}{n-2}=\dfrac{5.10}{5-2}=1.70$, with residual standard deviation $\hat\sigma=\sqrt{1.70}\approx1.304$.

**Example 3.** (Significance t test) Continuing Examples 1–2: $S_{xx}=10$, $\hat\beta_1=1.7$, $\hat\sigma^2=1.70$, $n=5$. At $\alpha=0.05$ test whether the regression is significant ($H_0:\beta_1=0$). (Use $t_{0.025}(3)=3.182$.)

> **Solution.** $\hat\sigma=\sqrt{1.70}\approx1.304$. The standard error of $\hat\beta_1$ is
> $$\frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{S_{xx}}}=\frac{1.304}{\sqrt{10}}=\frac{1.304}{3.162}\approx0.4124.$$
> The test statistic
> $$T=\frac{\hat\beta_1}{\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}}=\frac{1.7}{0.4124}\approx4.12\overset{H_0}{\sim}t(3).$$
> Rejection region $|T|>t_{0.025}(3)=3.182$. Since $4.12>3.182$, reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ the regression is significant, i.e. cost varies linearly with output significantly.

**Example 4.** (Correlation and determination) Continuing Example 1: $S_{xx}=10$, $S_{xy}=17$, and $y:6,5,7,10,12$. Find the sample correlation $r$ and the coefficient of determination $r^2$, and interpret them.

> **Solution.** First $S_{yy}$. With $y_i-\bar y=-2,-3,-1,2,4$,
> $$S_{yy}=4+9+1+4+16=34.$$
> The correlation coefficient
> $$r=\frac{S_{xy}}{\sqrt{S_{xx}\,S_{yy}}}=\frac{17}{\sqrt{10\times34}}=\frac{17}{\sqrt{340}}=\frac{17}{18.44}\approx0.922.$$
> The determination coefficient $r^2\approx0.850$. Interpretation: $x$ and $y$ are strongly positively correlated; about $85\%$ of the total variation in cost is explained by output (the regression), the remaining $15\%$ being random error.

**Example 5.** (One-way ANOVA) A factor $A$ has $3$ levels with $4$ observations each; we have $SSA=48$, $SSE=36$, total $n=12$. Build the ANOVA table and at $\alpha=0.05$ test whether the level means are equal. (Use $F_{0.05}(2,9)=4.26$.)

> **Solution.** $k=3$, $n=12$. Degrees of freedom: between $k-1=2$, within $n-k=9$. Mean squares
> $$MSA=\frac{SSA}{k-1}=\frac{48}{2}=24,\qquad MSE=\frac{SSE}{n-k}=\frac{36}{9}=4.$$
> The test statistic
> $$F=\frac{MSA}{MSE}=\frac{24}{4}=6\overset{H_0}{\sim}F(2,9).$$
>
> | Source | Sum of squares | df | Mean square | $F$ |
> |---|---|---|---|---|
> | Between ($A$) | $48$ | $2$ | $24$ | $6$ |
> | Within (error) | $36$ | $9$ | $4$ | |
> | Total | $84$ | $11$ | | |
>
> Rejection region $F>F_{0.05}(2,9)=4.26$. Since $6>4.26$, reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ the level means differ significantly.

## Exercises

1. Write the simple linear regression model and the standard assumptions on the errors $\varepsilon_i$, and explain the practical meaning of the coefficient $\beta_1$.
2. Given $\bar x=10$, $\bar y=20$, $S_{xx}=40$, $S_{xy}=100$. Find the least-squares estimates $\hat\beta_1,\hat\beta_0$, write the regression line, and predict $\hat y_0$ at $x_0=12$.
3. A regression has $SST=200$ and $SSE=50$. Find $SSR$ and the determination coefficient $r^2$; if $S_{xy}>0$, find the correlation $r$.
4. A regression with $n=10$ gives $\hat\beta_1=2.5$, $S_{xx}=16$, $SSE=72$. At $\alpha=0.05$ test $H_0:\beta_1=0$. (Hint: $\hat\sigma^2=SSE/(n-2)$; use $t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$.)
5. Write the sum-of-squares decomposition and the degrees of freedom for one-way ANOVA, and explain why the $F$ statistic reflects "whether the between-group difference is significantly larger than the within-group random scatter".
6. A one-way ANOVA has $k=4$ levels, total $n=24$, with $SST=300$ and $SSE=144$. Find $SSA$, complete the ANOVA table (mean squares and $F$), and test at $\alpha=0.05$. (Use $F_{0.05}(3,20)=3.10$.)

**Answers.**
1) $y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1 x_i+\varepsilon_i$; assumptions $E[\varepsilon_i]=0$, $\operatorname{Var}(\varepsilon_i)=\sigma^2$ (equal variance), the $\varepsilon_i$ independent (and normal for testing); $\beta_1$ is the average change in the mean of $y$ per one-unit increase in $x$.
2) $\hat\beta_1=S_{xy}/S_{xx}=100/40=2.5$; $\hat\beta_0=\bar y-\hat\beta_1\bar x=20-2.5\times10=-5$; line $\hat y=-5+2.5x$; $\hat y_0=-5+2.5\times12=25$.
3) $SSR=SST-SSE=200-50=150$; $r^2=SSR/SST=150/200=0.75$; since $S_{xy}>0$, $r=+\sqrt{0.75}\approx0.866$.
4) $\hat\sigma^2=72/8=9$, $\hat\sigma=3$; standard error $\hat\sigma/\sqrt{S_{xx}}=3/\sqrt{16}=0.75$; $T=2.5/0.75\approx3.33$; $|T|>t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$, reject $H_0$, regression significant.
5) $SST=SSA+SSE$, df $n-1=(k-1)+(n-k)$; $F=\dfrac{SSA/(k-1)}{SSE/(n-k)}$, the numerator estimates the between-group variance carrying the factor effect and the denominator the purely random within-group variance; under $H_0$ both estimate $\sigma^2$ so $F\approx1$, while large between-group differences inflate the numerator, making $F$ significantly larger than $1$ and leading to rejection.
6) $SSA=SST-SSE=300-144=156$; df between $k-1=3$, within $n-k=20$; $MSA=156/3=52$, $MSE=144/20=7.2$; $F=52/7.2\approx7.22$; since $7.22>F_{0.05}(3,20)=3.10$, reject $H_0$, the level means differ significantly.
