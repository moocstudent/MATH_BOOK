## Core Notes

### Complex Integrals: Definition and Properties

Let $C$ be a **directed smooth curve** in the plane along which $f(z)$ is defined. Partition $C$ into small arcs, pick sample points $\zeta_k$, and form the sum $\sum_k f(\zeta_k)\,\Delta z_k$. If the limit exists as the partition is refined indefinitely, it defines the **complex integral**

$$\int_C f(z)\,dz=\lim_{\|\Delta\|\to0}\sum_k f(\zeta_k)\,\Delta z_k.$$

Expanding $f=u+iv$ and $dz=dx+i\,dy$, the complex integral splits into two real line integrals:

$$\int_C f\,dz=\int_C(u\,dx-v\,dy)+i\int_C(v\,dx+u\,dy).$$

**Parametric evaluation (most common):** if $C$ is given by $z=z(t)$ ($a\le t\le b$), then

$$\int_C f(z)\,dz=\int_a^b f\big(z(t)\big)\,z'(t)\,dt.$$

Complex integration is **linear** and reverses sign under reversal, $\int_{-C}=-\int_C$. It also obeys the key **length estimate (ML inequality)**

$$\left|\int_C f\,dz\right|\le \max_{z\in C}|f(z)|\cdot L,$$

where $L$ is the arc length of $C$. In general a complex integral **depends on the path**; for analytic functions, the next theorem changes this completely.

### The Cauchy–Goursat Theorem

**Cauchy–Goursat theorem:** if $f$ is analytic on a **simply connected** region $D$, then for every **simple closed curve** $C$ (piecewise smooth) in $D$,

$$\oint_C f(z)\,dz=0.$$

This is the cornerstone of complex analysis. Intuitively, analyticity ("source-free and curl-free") forces the "circulation" around any closed loop to vanish.

**Two equivalent consequences:**

1. **Path independence:** in a simply connected analytic region, $\int_C f\,dz$ depends only on the endpoints, not the path.
2. **Existence of an antiderivative:** then $f$ has an **antiderivative** $F$ ($F'=f$), and the **fundamental theorem** holds:
$$\int_{z_1}^{z_2}f(z)\,dz=F(z_2)-F(z_1).$$

For example $\oint_C z^n\,dz=0$ ($n\ge0$, any closed $C$), since $z^n$ is entire.

### Composite Contours and Deformation

When the region is **not simply connected** (has "holes") or $f$ has singularities, the basic theorem does not apply directly, but the **deformation principle** does: within the analytic region of $f$, **continuously deforming** the contour (without crossing a singularity) leaves the integral unchanged.

**Composite-contour theorem:** let $C$ be a large closed curve and $C_1,\dots,C_n$ smaller mutually exterior closed curves inside it, each enclosing one "hole". If $f$ is analytic on the **multiply connected** region they bound (and its boundary), then, taking a consistent counterclockwise orientation,

$$\oint_C f\,dz=\sum_{k=1}^{n}\oint_{C_k} f\,dz.$$

The most important **basic integral**: for a positively oriented circle $C$ centred at $z_0$ of any radius,

$$\oint_C \frac{dz}{(z-z_0)^n}=\begin{cases}2\pi i,& n=1,\\[2pt]0,& n\ne1\ (n\in\mathbb{Z}).\end{cases}$$

This is the seed of the Cauchy integral formula and residue theory below: only the $\dfrac{1}{z-z_0}$ term "survives".

### The Cauchy Integral Formula

**Cauchy integral formula:** let $f$ be analytic on the closed region bounded by a simple closed curve $C$, with $z_0$ **inside** $C$ and $C$ positively oriented. Then

$$f(z_0)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-z_0}\,dz.$$

**A striking meaning:** the value of an analytic function at any **interior** point is completely determined by its values on the **boundary** — the essence of analytic rigidity, with no real-variable analogue. It also gives a ready algorithm for a class of singular integrals: for $\oint_C\dfrac{g(z)}{z-z_0}\,dz$ with $g$ analytic and $z_0$ inside, the value is $2\pi i\,g(z_0)$.

It further yields the **mean-value property**: $f(z_0)$ equals the average of $f$ over any circle centred at $z_0$; and the **maximum-modulus principle**: a nonconstant analytic function attains no interior maximum of its modulus.

### Infinite Differentiability of Analytic Functions

Differentiating the Cauchy formula repeatedly in $z_0$ (under the integral sign) gives the **higher-derivative formula**: an analytic function is **infinitely differentiable** at every interior point, and

$$f^{(n)}(z_0)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz,\qquad n=0,1,2,\dots$$

**A profound conclusion:** being once complex-differentiable (analytic) implies being **infinitely differentiable**! This is the fundamental divide between complex and real analysis — a real function differentiable once need not be twice differentiable, whereas an analytic function is automatically smooth to all orders and expandable in a power series (next chapter).

The higher-derivative formula is again a **computational tool**: for $\oint_C\dfrac{g(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz$ with $g$ analytic and $z_0$ inside, the value is $\dfrac{2\pi i}{n!}g^{(n)}(z_0)$.

### Summary

- Complex integral $\int_C f\,dz$: parametric $=\int_a^b f(z(t))z'(t)\,dt$; linear, sign-reversing; ML estimate $|\int_C f\,dz|\le \max|f|\cdot L$.
- Cauchy–Goursat: on a simply connected analytic region $\oint_C f\,dz=0$ $\Rightarrow$ path independence + antiderivative $\Rightarrow$ $\int_{z_1}^{z_2}f=F(z_2)-F(z_1)$.
- Composite contours: $\oint_C f=\sum_k\oint_{C_k}f$; basic integral $\oint_C\frac{dz}{(z-z_0)^n}=2\pi i\,(n=1)$, else $0$.
- Cauchy integral formula $f(z_0)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-z_0}\,dz$: interior values from the boundary; $\oint\frac{g}{z-z_0}=2\pi i\,g(z_0)$.
- Higher derivatives $f^{(n)}(z_0)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-z_0)^{n+1}}\,dz$; analytic $\Rightarrow$ **infinitely differentiable**.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_C z\,dz$ where $C$ is the line segment from $0$ to $1+i$.

> **Solution.** Parametrise $z(t)=(1+i)t$, $0\le t\le1$, $z'(t)=1+i$. Then
> $$\int_C z\,dz=\int_0^1(1+i)t\cdot(1+i)\,dt=(1+i)^2\int_0^1 t\,dt=(1+i)^2\cdot\frac12.$$
> Since $(1+i)^2=2i$, we get $\int_C z\,dz=\dfrac{2i}{2}=i$. Using the antiderivative $F(z)=\tfrac12 z^2$: $F(1+i)-F(0)=\tfrac12(2i)=i$, in agreement.

**Example 2.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{dz}{z-2}$ where $C$ is (a) $|z|=1$; (b) $|z|=3$, both positively oriented.

> **Solution.** The integrand has its only singularity at $z=2$.
> (a) $z=2$ is **outside** $|z|=1$, so $\dfrac{1}{z-2}$ is analytic on the closed disc and the integral is $0$ by Cauchy–Goursat.
> (b) $z=2$ is **inside** $|z|=3$, so by the basic integral $\oint_C\dfrac{dz}{z-z_0}=2\pi i$, the integral is $2\pi i$.

**Example 3.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{e^z}{z}\,dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=1$ positively oriented.

> **Solution.** Take $f(z)=e^z$ (entire) and $z_0=0$ inside $C$. By the Cauchy integral formula,
> $$\oint_C\frac{e^z}{z}\,dz=\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-0}\,dz=2\pi i\,f(0)=2\pi i\cdot e^0=2\pi i.$$

**Example 4.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{\cos z}{(z-\pi)^2}\,dz$ where $C$ is $|z-\pi|=1$ positively oriented.

> **Solution.** Take $f(z)=\cos z$ with $z_0=\pi$ inside $C$; the denominator $(z-z_0)^{2}$ corresponds to $n=1$. By the higher-derivative formula,
> $$\oint_C\frac{\cos z}{(z-\pi)^2}\,dz=\frac{2\pi i}{1!}f'(\pi).$$
> Since $f'(z)=-\sin z$ and $f'(\pi)=-\sin\pi=0$, the integral is
> $$2\pi i\cdot 0=0.$$

**Example 5.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{dz}{z^2+1}$ where $C$ is $|z|=2$ positively oriented.

> **Solution.** The denominator $z^2+1=(z-i)(z+i)$ has both singularities $z=\pm i$ **inside** $|z|=2$. By the composite-contour theorem, surround each with a small circle $C_1$ (around $i$) and $C_2$ (around $-i$):
> $$\oint_C=\oint_{C_1}+\oint_{C_2}.$$
> On $C_1$, write $\dfrac{1}{z^2+1}=\dfrac{1/(z+i)}{z-i}$ with $f_1(z)=\dfrac{1}{z+i}$; Cauchy's formula gives $2\pi i\,f_1(i)=2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{1}{2i}=\pi$.
> On $C_2$, $f_2(z)=\dfrac{1}{z-i}$ gives $2\pi i\,f_2(-i)=2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{1}{-2i}=-\pi$.
> Hence $\oint_C\dfrac{dz}{z^2+1}=\pi+(-\pi)=0$.

## Exercises

1. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_C \bar z\,dz$ where $C$ is the segment from $0$ to $1+i$, and state whether it is path-independent.
2. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_C (z^2+1)\,dz$ along any path from $0$ to $2i$.
3. Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{dz}{z+1}$ where $C$ is $|z|=2$ positively oriented.
4. Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{\sin z}{z}\,dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=1$ positively oriented.
5. Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{e^z}{(z-1)^3}\,dz$ where $C$ is $|z-1|=1$ positively oriented.
6. Evaluate $\displaystyle\oint_C\frac{z}{(z-1)(z-2)}\,dz$ where $C$ is $|z|=3$ positively oriented.

**Answers.**
1) $z(t)=(1+i)t$, $\bar z=(1-i)t$, $dz=(1+i)dt$, so $\int_0^1(1-i)(1+i)t\,dt=(1-i)(1+i)\cdot\tfrac12=2\cdot\tfrac12=1$. Since $\bar z$ is not analytic, the integral **depends on the path** (other paths give different values).
2) $z^2+1$ is entire, so the integral is path-independent; with $F(z)=\tfrac13 z^3+z$, $F(2i)-F(0)=\tfrac13(2i)^3+2i=\tfrac13(-8i)+2i=-\tfrac83 i+2i=-\tfrac23 i$.
3) The singularity $z=-1$ is inside $|z|=2$, so $\oint_C\dfrac{dz}{z+1}=2\pi i$.
4) Take $f(z)=\sin z$, $z_0=0$ inside; $=2\pi i\,f(0)=2\pi i\cdot\sin0=0$.
5) Take $f(z)=e^z$, $z_0=1$ inside; the denominator $(z-1)^3$ corresponds to $n=2$, so $=\dfrac{2\pi i}{2!}f''(1)=\pi i\,e^{1}=\pi e\,i$.
6) Both singularities $z=1,2$ lie inside $|z|=3$. Around $z=1$: $f_1=\dfrac{z}{z-2}$, $2\pi i\,f_1(1)=2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{1}{-1}=-2\pi i$; around $z=2$: $f_2=\dfrac{z}{z-1}$, $2\pi i\,f_2(2)=2\pi i\cdot\dfrac{2}{1}=4\pi i$. Total $-2\pi i+4\pi i=2\pi i$.
