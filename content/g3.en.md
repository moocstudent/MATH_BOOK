## Core Notes

Integration wears two faces. One is "the inverse of differentiation" (the indefinite integral); the other is "the limiting sum of infinitely many small pieces" (the definite integral). The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus reveals that these two faces are really the same thing — one of the deepest results in all of calculus, and the key that reduces an "accumulation problem" to "finding an antiderivative".

### Indefinite integrals and the basic table

**Definition.** If $F'(x) = f(x)$, then $F$ is an **antiderivative** of $f$. The whole family $F(x)+C$ of antiderivatives is the **indefinite integral** of $f$, written
$$\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C,$$
where $C$ is an arbitrary constant (since the derivative of a constant is zero, antiderivatives are unique only up to such a constant). The Mean Value Theorem shows that any two antiderivatives of the same function differ by a constant, so the "$+C$" captures *all* of them.

Because integration undoes differentiation, every differentiation formula, read backwards, becomes an integration formula. The **basic table** (memorize it):
$$\int x^a\,dx = \frac{x^{a+1}}{a+1} + C\ (a\ne -1), \qquad \int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C,$$
$$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C, \qquad \int \sin x\,dx = -\cos x + C, \qquad \int \cos x\,dx = \sin x + C,$$
$$\int \frac{1}{1+x^2}\,dx = \arctan x + C, \qquad \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\,dx = \arcsin x + C.$$

Linearity: $\displaystyle\int [a f(x) + b g(x)]\,dx = a\int f\,dx + b\int g\,dx$. Note the absolute value in $\int\frac1x\,dx$ — it keeps the formula valid for $x<0$ as well.

### Integration by substitution

When the integrand is not in the basic table, the most common simplification is **substitution**.

**Substitution of the first kind (recognizing a differential).** The idea is to recognize the integrand as $f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = f(u)\,du$:
$$\int f(g(x))\,g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du \quad (u = g(x)).$$
For example, in $\int 2x\,e^{x^2}\,dx$, since $d(x^2)=2x\,dx$, the substitution $u=x^2$ gives $\int e^u\,du = e^{x^2}+C$. This is the chain rule run backwards; the skill is *spotting* which part is the differential of an inner function.

**Substitution of the second kind (change of variable).** Here you deliberately set $x=\varphi(t)$, trading $x$ for a new variable $t$ — useful for radicals. Facing $\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}$, set $x = a\sin t$ and use $1-\sin^2 t=\cos^2 t$ to eliminate the root; facing $\sqrt{a^2+x^2}$, set $x=a\tan t$. After substituting, do not forget to **convert back** to the original variable.

### Integration by parts

Reversing the product rule $(uv)' = u'v + uv'$ yields the key tool for products of two different kinds of functions:
$$\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du.$$

Success hinges on **choosing $u$ and $dv$ well**: pick $u$ so that $du$ is simpler, and $dv$ so that $v$ is easy to find. A useful priority order for $u$ is **LIATE** (Logarithmic, Inverse-trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential). It shines on integrals like $\int x e^x dx$, $\int x\sin x\,dx$, and $\int \ln x\,dx$; sometimes you apply it twice, or a "loop" appears (as in $\int e^x\sin x\,dx$) and you solve for the integral algebraically.

### Definite integrals and Riemann sums

The definite integral starts from "find the area under a curve". Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ small pieces, pick a sample point $\xi_i$ in each, and approximate with a row of thin rectangles:

**Definition (definite integral).**
$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{\lambda \to 0}\sum_{i=1}^{n} f(\xi_i)\,\Delta x_i,$$
where $\Delta x_i$ is the width of the $i$-th piece and $\lambda$ the largest width. This sum is a **Riemann sum**; when the limit exists, $f$ is **integrable** on $[a,b]$. Every function continuous on a closed interval is integrable.

Two key points: the result is a **number** (no $+C$), and its geometric meaning is the **signed area** between $f$ and the $x$-axis (area below the axis counts as negative). Basic properties include linearity, additivity over intervals $\int_a^b=\int_a^c+\int_c^b$, and sign preservation ($f\ge 0\Rightarrow\int_a^b f\ge 0$).

### The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This theorem builds a bridge between "summing up" and "differentiating".

**Newton–Leibniz formula.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$, then
$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a).$$

Its power: instead of evaluating a fearsomely complicated limit (the Riemann sum), you just find one antiderivative and subtract its values at the endpoints. The "summing" task of finding area is reduced to "undoing a derivative".

Its companion is **differentiation of an integral with variable upper limit**: if $\Phi(x)=\displaystyle\int_a^x f(t)\,dt$, then $\Phi'(x) = f(x)$ — saying that integrating and then differentiating returns the original function, and proving that every continuous function has an antiderivative. When substituting or integrating by parts in a definite integral, **change the limits too**; the parts formula reads $\int_a^b u\,dv = [uv]_a^b - \int_a^b v\,du$.

### Applications and improper integrals

The essence of the definite integral is "partition, approximate, sum, take the limit". Any quantity that can be written in this accumulated form can be computed with it.

**Area of a planar region.** The area bounded by $y=f(x)$, $y=g(x)$ (with $f\ge g$), and $x=a,x=b$:
$$A = \int_a^b [f(x) - g(x)]\,dx.$$

**Volume of a solid of revolution.** Rotating the curve $y=f(x)$ about the $x$-axis gives (disk method):
$$V = \pi\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx.$$
The reasoning: a thin slice at $x$ is approximately a small cylinder of radius $f(x)$ and thickness $dx$, of volume $\pi[f(x)]^2\,dx$, which we then accumulate along $x$.

**Improper integrals.** When the interval is infinite (e.g. $\int_1^{\infty}$) or the integrand is unbounded at an endpoint, the definite integral is extended as a limit: $\displaystyle\int_a^{\infty} f\,dx = \lim_{b\to\infty}\int_a^b f\,dx$. If the limit exists the integral **converges**, otherwise it **diverges**. For instance $\int_1^{\infty}\frac{1}{x^p}\,dx$ converges for $p>1$ and diverges for $p\le 1$.

### Summary

- Antiderivative: $F'=f$; the indefinite integral $\int f\,dx=F+C$ captures all of them. Memorize the basic table and linearity first.
- Three methods: recognizing a differential (chain rule reversed), second-kind substitution ($x=\varphi(t)$ to kill a radical — convert back), integration by parts $\int u\,dv=uv-\int v\,du$ (choose $u$ by LIATE).
- The definite integral $\int_a^b f\,dx$ is a limit of Riemann sums — a number — geometrically the signed area; continuous ⇒ integrable.
- Newton–Leibniz $\int_a^b f\,dx=F(b)-F(a)$ turns accumulation into antidifferentiation; variable-limit rule $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t)\,dt=f(x)$.
- Applications: area $\int_a^b[f-g]\,dx$, volume of revolution $\pi\int_a^b f^2\,dx$.
- Improper integrals are defined by a limit; $\int_1^{\infty}x^{-p}\,dx$ converges for $p>1$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find $\displaystyle\int (3x^2 - 2\cos x)\,dx$.

> **Solution.** Split by linearity and apply the basic table term by term:
> $$\int (3x^2 - 2\cos x)\,dx = 3\cdot\frac{x^3}{3} - 2\sin x + C = x^3 - 2\sin x + C.$$

**Example 2.** Find $\displaystyle\int x\,e^{x}\,dx$.

> **Solution.** A power times an exponential — use integration by parts. By LIATE choose $u = x$ (its derivative is simpler) and $dv = e^x\,dx$, so $du = dx$ and $v = e^x$:
> $$\int x e^x\,dx = x e^x - \int e^x\,dx = x e^x - e^x + C = (x-1)e^x + C.$$

**Example 3.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^2\,dx$ and interpret it geometrically.

> **Solution.** First find the antiderivative $F(x)=\dfrac{x^3}{3}$, then apply Newton–Leibniz:
> $$\int_0^1 x^2\,dx = \left.\frac{x^3}{3}\right|_0^1 = \frac{1^3}{3} - \frac{0^3}{3} = \frac{1}{3}.$$
> Geometrically, this is the area of the region bounded by $y=x^2$, the $x$-axis, and the line $x=1$, equal to $\dfrac13$.

**Example 4.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{a}\sqrt{a^2 - x^2}\,dx$ (with $a>0$).

> **Solution.** The radical $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$ calls for a second-kind (trigonometric) substitution. Set $x = a\sin t$, so $dx = a\cos t\,dt$ and $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}=a\cos t$ (with $\cos t\ge0$ on $t\in[0,\frac{\pi}{2}]$). Change the limits: $x=0\Rightarrow t=0$ and $x=a\Rightarrow t=\frac{\pi}{2}$. Then
> $$\int_0^a\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\,dx = \int_0^{\pi/2} a\cos t\cdot a\cos t\,dt = a^2\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^2 t\,dt.$$
> Use the power-reduction identity $\cos^2 t = \dfrac{1+\cos 2t}{2}$:
> $$= a^2\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{1+\cos 2t}{2}\,dt = \frac{a^2}{2}\left[t + \frac{\sin 2t}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{a^2}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi a^2}{4}.$$
> This is exactly the area of a quarter circle, matching geometric intuition.

**Example 5.** Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by $y = \sqrt{x}$, the $x$-axis, and $x=1$ about the $x$-axis.

> **Solution.** Use the disk method $V=\pi\displaystyle\int_a^b [f(x)]^2\,dx$ with $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,1]$:
> $$V = \pi\int_0^1 (\sqrt{x})^2\,dx = \pi\int_0^1 x\,dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{2}.$$

## Exercises

1. Find the indefinite integral $\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}}\,dx$.
2. Use substitution to find $\displaystyle\int \cos(2x)\,dx$.
3. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} \sin x\,dx$.
4. Find the area of the region bounded by $y=x^2$ and the line $y=x$.
5. Use integration by parts to find $\displaystyle\int \ln x\,dx$.
6. Decide whether the improper integral $\displaystyle\int_1^{\infty}\frac{1}{x^2}\,dx$ converges, and if so find its value.

**Answers.** 1) $\int x^{-1/2}dx = 2x^{1/2}+C = 2\sqrt{x}+C$; 2) let $u=2x$, $du=2dx$, giving $\frac12\sin 2x + C$; 3) $\big[-\cos x\big]_0^{\pi} = -\cos\pi-(-\cos 0)= 1+1 = 2$; 4) the curves meet at $x=0,1$ with $x\ge x^2$ on $(0,1)$, so the area is $\int_0^1 (x - x^2)\,dx = \frac12 - \frac13 = \frac16$; 5) take $u=\ln x$, $dv=dx$, so $du=\frac1x dx$, $v=x$, giving $\int\ln x\,dx = x\ln x - \int x\cdot\frac1x dx = x\ln x - x + C$; 6) $\int_1^{b}x^{-2}dx = \big[-\frac1x\big]_1^b = 1-\frac1b\to 1$ as $b\to\infty$, so it **converges** with value $1$.
