## Core Notes

### Extending the Number System: Integers → Rationals → Reals

Our idea of "number" grew step by step, each extension solving a problem the previous numbers could not answer.

- The **natural numbers** $0,1,2,3,\dots$ (the whole set is written $\mathbb{N}$) count objects. But $3-5$ has no answer among them.
- To make subtraction always work, we introduce **negative numbers** and obtain the **integers** $\dots,-2,-1,0,1,2,\dots$, written $\mathbb{Z}$. Now temperature, altitude, and profit/loss can all be expressed.
- Dividing integers often leaves a remainder, e.g. $1\div 3$. To make division (by a nonzero divisor) always work, we introduce **fractions** and obtain the **rational numbers**: any number expressible as $\dfrac{p}{q}$ (with integers $p,q$ and $q\ne 0$) is rational, written $\mathbb{Q}$.
- Yet some numbers, such as $\pi$ and $\sqrt 2$, **cannot** be written as a ratio of two integers; these are **irrational numbers**. The rationals together with the irrationals form the **real numbers** $\mathbb{R}$.

**Notation for the common number sets (worth memorizing):**

| Symbol | Name | What it is | Where the letter comes from |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\mathbb{N}$ | natural numbers | $0,1,2,3,\dots$ | Natural |
| $\mathbb{Z}$ | integers | $\dots,-2,-1,0,1,2,\dots$ | German *Zahlen* (numbers) |
| $\mathbb{Q}$ | rational numbers | numbers of the form $\frac{p}{q}$ ($q\ne 0$) | Quotient |
| $\mathbb{R}$ | real numbers | every point on the number line | Real |
| $\mathbb{C}$ | complex numbers | the form $a+b\mathrm{i}$ (see module M6) | Complex |

These hollow bold letters are **blackboard bold**, used specifically for number sets; you read them as the letter itself ("N, Z, Q, R"). They nest: $\mathbb{N}\subset\mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}\subset\mathbb{C}$. A "$+$" superscript marks the positive part, e.g. $\mathbb{Z}^{+}$ or $\mathbb{N}^{*}$ for positive integers and $\mathbb{R}^{+}$ for positive reals.

A key fact: **a rational number = a terminating or repeating decimal; an irrational number = a non-terminating, non-repeating decimal**. For instance $0.75=\dfrac34$ and $0.333\dots=\dfrac13$ are rational, while $\sqrt2=1.41421356\dots$ never repeats and is irrational.

**Inclusions:** $\mathbb{N}\subset\mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}$. Each set is larger than the one before.

**Common pitfall:** integers are also rational (e.g. $5=\dfrac51$); $0$ is an integer and a rational, but it is neither positive nor negative.

### Opposites and Absolute Value

**Opposite (additive inverse):** two numbers that differ only in sign are opposites. The opposite of $a$ is $-a$, e.g. $5$ and $-5$, or $-\dfrac23$ and $\dfrac23$. **The opposite of $0$ is itself.** On the number line, opposites sit symmetrically about the origin.

**Absolute value:** the **distance** from the point for $a$ to the origin, written $|a|$; it is **always non-negative**. By definition,

$$|a|=\begin{cases} a, & a\ge 0,\\ -a, & a<0.\end{cases}$$

For example $|7|=7$, $|-7|=7$, $|0|=0$. Intuition: absolute value "drops the sign and keeps the size".

**Useful properties:**

- $|a|\ge 0$, and $|a|=0$ exactly when $a=0$.
- $|-a|=|a|$: opposites have equal absolute value.
- $|a\times b|=|a|\times|b|$.
- The **distance** between $a$ and $b$ on the number line is $|a-b|$.

**Pitfall:** $-a$ is not always negative! When $a<0$, $-a$ is in fact positive. That is why $|a|=-a$ is correct precisely when $a<0$.

### The Four Operations on Rational Numbers

**Addition:**

- Same signs: keep the common sign, add the absolute values. E.g. $(-3)+(-5)=-8$.
- Opposite signs: take the sign of the larger absolute value, subtract the smaller from the larger. E.g. $(-7)+4=-3$.
- Adding $0$ leaves a number unchanged.

**Subtraction:** subtracting a number equals adding its opposite: $a-b=a+(-b)$. E.g. $3-(-5)=3+5=8$.

**Multiplication and division (signs):** **like signs give a positive, unlike signs give a negative**, then multiply (divide) the absolute values. E.g. $(-4)\times(-3)=12$, $(-12)\div 4=-3$. Any number times $0$ is $0$; **$0$ may never be a divisor**.

**Order of operations:** do **brackets** first, then **powers**, then **multiplication/division**, and finally **addition/subtraction**; operations of the same rank go left to right. For example,

$$2-3\times(-4)^2 = 2-3\times 16 = 2-48 = -46.$$

**Pitfall:** $-4^2$ and $(-4)^2$ differ. $-4^2=-(4\times4)=-16$ (power first, then negate); $(-4)^2=16$.

### Powers and Scientific Notation

**Powers:** the product of $n$ equal factors $a$ is written $a^n$, read "$a$ to the $n$-th power", where $a$ is the **base** and $n$ the **exponent**:

$$a^n=\underbrace{a\times a\times\cdots\times a}_{n\text{ factors}}.$$

**Sign rule:** a negative number raised to an **even** power is positive, to an **odd** power is negative. E.g. $(-2)^4=16$, $(-2)^3=-8$. Any nonzero number to the $0$-th power equals $1$, i.e. $a^0=1\ (a\ne0)$.

**Scientific notation:** writing a number as $a\times 10^{n}$ with $1\le |a|<10$ and integer $n$. This compactly represents very large or very small numbers.

- Large: $300000=3\times 10^{5}$; the count of zeros matches the exponent.
- Small: $0.0006=6\times 10^{-4}$.

**Finding $n$:** for a number greater than $10$, $n$ = (number of digits in the integer part) $-1$; for a positive decimal less than $1$, $n$ is negative and $|n|$ = the number of zeros before the first nonzero digit (counting after the decimal point).

### Real Numbers, the Number Line, and Estimation

**Number line:** a straight line with a chosen **origin, positive direction, and unit length**. **The real numbers correspond one-to-one with points on the line** — every real number has a unique point, and vice versa. This is exactly why irrational numbers also "live" on the line.

**Estimating an irrational number:** for an irrational such as $\sqrt2$, **squeezing** pins down its range. Since $1^2=1<2<4=2^2$, we get $1<\sqrt2<2$; and since $1.4^2=1.96<2<2.25=1.5^2$, we get $1.4<\sqrt2<1.5$. Refining further gives $\sqrt2\approx1.414$.

**Comparing size:** on the number line **the number farther right is always larger**. So positives exceed $0$, and $0$ exceeds negatives; between two negatives, the one with the larger absolute value is actually smaller, e.g. $-5<-3$.

### Summary

- Extensions: $\mathbb{N}\subset\mathbb{Z}\subset\mathbb{Q}\subset\mathbb{R}$; rational = terminating/repeating decimal, irrational = non-terminating non-repeating decimal.
- Opposite: $a$ and $-a$; absolute value $|a|$ = distance to origin, $|a-b|$ = distance between two points.
- Four operations: addition adds (like signs) or subtracts (unlike); subtraction becomes adding the opposite; multiplication/division follow **like = positive, unlike = negative**; never divide by $0$.
- Order: brackets → powers → ×÷ → +−; note $-a^2\ne(-a)^2$.
- Scientific notation $a\times10^{n}$ with $1\le|a|<10$.
- Reals correspond one-to-one with points on the line; estimate irrationals by squeezing; farther right means larger.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Classify each of $-3,\ \dfrac27,\ 0,\ \sqrt2,\ -1.5,\ \pi,\ 4$: which are integers, which are rational, which are irrational?

> **Solution.** Check each in turn.
> Integers: $-3,\ 0,\ 4$ (whole numbers, no denominator needed).
> Rationals: $-3,\ \dfrac27,\ 0,\ -1.5,\ 4$ (all expressible as $\dfrac{p}{q}$; note $-1.5=-\dfrac32$, and integers are rational too).
> Irrationals: $\sqrt2,\ \pi$ (non-terminating non-repeating decimals, not a ratio of two integers).

**Example 2.** Compute $|-3|+|5|-|-2|$, and find the distance between $-4$ and $7$ on the number line.

> **Solution.** Remove absolute values first: $|-3|=3,\ |5|=5,\ |-2|=2$. Then
> $$|-3|+|5|-|-2| = 3+5-2 = 6.$$
> The distance between two points is $|a-b|$, so the distance between $-4$ and $7$ is
> $$|-4-7| = |-11| = 11.$$

**Example 3.** Compute $(-6)+9+(-4)-(-3)$.

> **Solution.** Turn every subtraction into adding the opposite:
> $$(-6)+9+(-4)-(-3) = (-6)+9+(-4)+3.$$
> For convenience, group by sign first: positives $9+3=12$, negatives $(-6)+(-4)=-10$. Then add (unlike signs, take the larger absolute value's sign):
> $$12+(-10) = 2.$$

**Example 4.** Compute $-2^2+(-3)^2\times\left(\dfrac12\right)-12\div(-4)$.

> **Solution.** Follow "powers → ×÷ → +−", minding the difference between $-2^2$ and $(-3)^2$.
> $$\begin{aligned} -2^2+(-3)^2\times\tfrac12-12\div(-4) &= -4 + 9\times\tfrac12 - (-3)\\ &= -4 + \tfrac92 + 3\\ &= -1 + 4.5 \\ &= 3.5. \end{aligned}$$
> So the result is $\dfrac72$. Here $-2^2=-4$ (square first, then negate), $(-3)^2=9$, and $12\div(-4)=-3$.

**Example 5.** Write in scientific notation: (1) the speed of light, about $300000000$ m/s; (2) an atom's diameter, about $0.0000000001$ m. Then find which two consecutive integers $\sqrt5$ lies between.

> **Solution.**
>
> **(1)** $300000000$ has $9$ digits; moving the decimal point $8$ places to just after the $3$ gives $300000000 = 3\times 10^{8}$, i.e. about $3\times10^{8}$ m/s.
>
> **(2)** In $0.0000000001$ the first nonzero digit $1$ sits in the $10$th decimal place, so the exponent is $-10$: $0.0000000001 = 1\times 10^{-10}$ m.
>
> **(3)** Estimate $\sqrt5$: since $2^2=4<5<9=3^2$, we have $2<\sqrt5<3$, so $\sqrt5$ lies between the consecutive integers $2$ and $3$ (more precisely $\sqrt5\approx2.236$).

## Exercises

1. Among $-7,\ 0.\overline{6},\ \sqrt3,\ \dfrac{22}{7},\ -\sqrt9,\ 0$, state which are rational and which are irrational.
2. Write the opposite of $-\dfrac35$, and compute $\left|-\dfrac35\right|$ and $\left|\dfrac35\right|$.
3. Compute $(-8)+5-(-3)+(-2)$.
4. Compute $(-2)^3\div 4 + (-1)^{2026}\times(-5)$.
5. Write $0.00045$ and $52000$ in scientific notation, and write $7.2\times 10^{3}$ in ordinary notation.
6. Without a calculator, find which two consecutive integers $\sqrt{10}$ lies between, and decide which is larger, $\sqrt{10}$ or $3.2$.

**Answers.**
1) Rational: $-7,\ 0.\overline{6}\,(=\tfrac23),\ \dfrac{22}{7},\ -\sqrt9\,(=-3),\ 0$; irrational: $\sqrt3$.
2) The opposite is $\dfrac35$; $\left|-\dfrac35\right|=\dfrac35$ and $\left|\dfrac35\right|=\dfrac35$, which are equal.
3) $(-8)+5+3+(-2)=(5+3)+(-8-2)=8-10=-2$.
4) $(-2)^3=-8$, $-8\div4=-2$; $(-1)^{2026}=1$ (even power), $1\times(-5)=-5$; total $-2+(-5)=-7$.
5) $0.00045=4.5\times10^{-4}$; $52000=5.2\times10^{4}$; $7.2\times10^{3}=7200$.
6) Since $3^2=9<10<16=4^2$, we have $3<\sqrt{10}<4$; and $3.2^2=10.24>10$, so $\sqrt{10}<3.2$.
