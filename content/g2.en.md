## Core Notes

If a limit answers "what happens as we approach", the derivative answers "how fast is it changing". It unifies three seemingly different ideas into one — the instantaneous rate of change, the slope of the tangent line, and the local linearization of a function — and from it grows an entire differential-calculus toolkit for studying where a function rises, falls, bends, and reaches extrema.

### Definition and geometric meaning

**Definition (derivative).** Let $y=f(x)$ be defined near $x_0$. If the limit
$$f'(x_0) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{f(x_0 + \Delta x) - f(x_0)}{\Delta x}$$
exists, then $f$ is **differentiable** at $x_0$, and that limit is the **derivative** of $f$ at $x_0$, also written $\dfrac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{x_0}$ or $y'(x_0)$.

The quotient $\dfrac{f(x_0+\Delta x)-f(x_0)}{\Delta x}$ is the **average rate of change** — the slope of a secant line; letting $\Delta x\to 0$ turns the secant into the tangent. An equivalent form (set $x=x_0+\Delta x$) is $f'(x_0)=\lim_{x\to x_0}\dfrac{f(x)-f(x_0)}{x-x_0}$.

**Geometric meaning.** $f'(x_0)$ is the **slope of the tangent line** to $y=f(x)$ at the point $(x_0, f(x_0))$. The tangent line is
$$y - f(x_0) = f'(x_0)\,(x - x_0),$$
and the perpendicular **normal line** has slope $-\dfrac{1}{f'(x_0)}$ (when $f'(x_0)\ne 0$).

**Differentiability vs. continuity.** Differentiable implies continuous, but not conversely. The classic counterexample is $f(x)=|x|$ at $x=0$: it has a corner there (left derivative $-1$, right derivative $+1$, which disagree). So differentiability is a *stronger* smoothness demand than continuity: the graph must not only be unbroken but also have a single well-defined tangent direction.

### Rules: arithmetic and the chain rule

Differentiating from the definition each time is far too slow; real computation relies on rules. For differentiable $u,v$ and a constant $C$:

$$(Cu)' = Cu', \qquad (u\pm v)' = u' \pm v',$$
$$(uv)' = u'v + uv', \qquad \left(\frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}\ (v\ne 0).$$

Note that the product rule is *not* $u'v'$ — that is the single most common error. It follows from the definition: write the numerator $u(x+\Delta x)v(x+\Delta x)-u(x)v(x)$, add and subtract $u(x+\Delta x)v(x)$ to split it into two pieces, and take the limit of each.

**The chain rule (composite functions).** This is the most important tool in differentiation. If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx} = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x).$$
Think "outside-in, multiplying layer by layer". Several nested layers just multiply several factors — the uniform method for expressions like $\sin(x^2)$, $e^{\cos x}$, or $\ln\sqrt{1+x^2}$.

Standard derivatives to memorize:
$$(x^a)' = a x^{a-1},\quad (e^x)' = e^x,\quad (a^x)'=a^x\ln a,\quad (\ln x)' = \frac{1}{x},\quad (\sin x)' = \cos x,\quad (\cos x)' = -\sin x,$$
$$(\tan x)' = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2 x,\qquad (\arcsin x)' = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}},\qquad (\arctan x)' = \frac{1}{1 + x^2}.$$

### Implicit and parametric differentiation

**Implicit differentiation.** When $y$ is defined implicitly by an equation $F(x,y)=0$ that is hard to solve for $y$, simply differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ (so any term containing $y$ picks up a factor $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$), then solve for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

For instance, differentiating $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$ gives $2x + 2y\,y' = 0$, so $y' = -\dfrac{x}{y}$. For a power-tower $y=u(x)^{v(x)}$, use **logarithmic differentiation**: take $\ln$ of both sides to turn products into sums and powers into products, then differentiate — a major simplification.

**Parametric differentiation.** If a curve is given by $\begin{cases} x = \varphi(t) \\ y = \psi(t)\end{cases}$, then
$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \frac{\psi'(t)}{\varphi'(t)}\quad (\varphi'(t)\ne 0).$$

### The Mean Value Theorem and higher-order derivatives

**Lagrange's Mean Value Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists $\xi\in(a,b)$ with
$$f(b) - f(a) = f'(\xi)\,(b - a).$$
It links the *overall* average rate of change to the *instantaneous* rate at one point, and it is the theoretical basis for reading monotonicity off the sign of the derivative: $f$ is strictly increasing where $f'>0$ and strictly decreasing where $f'<0$. The special case $f(a)=f(b)$ is **Rolle's theorem**.

Differentiating $f'(x)$ again gives the **second derivative** $f''(x)=\dfrac{d^2 y}{dx^2}$; repeating yields the $n$-th derivative $f^{(n)}(x)$. The second derivative carries clear physical and geometric meaning: if $s(t)$ is displacement, then $s'(t)$ is velocity and $s''(t)$ is acceleration; geometrically, $f''>0$ means the curve is **concave up** and $f''<0$ means **concave down**, with an **inflection point** where concavity changes sign.

Two useful higher-order formulas:
$$(e^x)^{(n)} = e^x, \qquad (\sin x)^{(n)} = \sin\left(x + \frac{n\pi}{2}\right).$$

### Differentials and linear approximation

**Definition (differential).** If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, the increment of the function can be written
$$\Delta y = f'(x_0)\,\Delta x + o(\Delta x),$$
and the linear leading part $f'(x_0)\,\Delta x$ is the **differential** of $f$ at $x_0$, written $dy = f'(x_0)\,dx$.

The key idea of the differential is **replacing a curve by its tangent line**: when $\Delta x$ is small, the increment $dy$ along the tangent approximates the true increment $\Delta y$. This gives the **linear approximation formula**:
$$f(x_0 + \Delta x) \approx f(x_0) + f'(x_0)\,\Delta x.$$

It is the first-order version of the Taylor expansion to come, and the everyday tool for quick estimates and error analysis in engineering (for example, a measurement error $\Delta x$ propagates to a function error $|dy|=|f'(x_0)|\,|\Delta x|$).

### Summary

- The derivative is the limit of a difference quotient, $f'(x_0)=\lim_{\Delta x\to0}\frac{f(x_0+\Delta x)-f(x_0)}{\Delta x}$; geometrically it is the tangent slope. **Differentiable ⇒ continuous**, but not conversely.
- Arithmetic rules: $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$, $\left(\frac uv\right)'=\frac{u'v-uv'}{v^2}$; the product rule is **not** $u'v'$.
- Chain rule: $\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}$, multiplying outside-in layer by layer.
- Implicit: differentiate in $x$, multiply $y$-terms by $y'$, then solve; parametric $\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{\psi'(t)}{\varphi'(t)}$.
- Mean Value Theorem $f(b)-f(a)=f'(\xi)(b-a)$; $f'>0$ increasing, $f'<0$ decreasing, $f''>0$ concave up, $f''<0$ concave down.
- The differential $dy=f'(x_0)\,dx$; linear approximation $f(x_0+\Delta x)\approx f(x_0)+f'(x_0)\Delta x$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Differentiate $y = x^2 \sin x$.

> **Solution.** This is a product, so use $(uv)'=u'v+uv'$ with $u=x^2$, $v=\sin x$:
> $$y' = (x^2)'\sin x + x^2(\sin x)' = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x.$$

**Example 2.** Differentiate $y = \ln(\cos x)$.

> **Solution.** A composite function: outer $\ln u$, inner $u=\cos x$. By the chain rule, $\dfrac{dy}{du}=\dfrac1u$ and $\dfrac{du}{dx}=-\sin x$:
> $$y' = \frac{1}{\cos x}\cdot(-\sin x) = -\frac{\sin x}{\cos x} = -\tan x.$$

**Example 3.** Use a differential to approximate $\sqrt{4.02}$.

> **Solution.** Let $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$, so $f'(x)=\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$. Choose the convenient base point $x_0=4$ with increment $\Delta x = 0.02$. By the linear approximation:
> $$f(4.02) \approx f(4) + f'(4)\,\Delta x = 2 + \frac{1}{2\cdot 2}\times 0.02 = 2 + 0.005 = 2.005.$$
> (The true value is about $2.004994$, so the error is tiny — linear approximation is quite accurate for small increments.)

**Example 4.** Differentiate the power-tower $y = x^{x}$ (with $x>0$).

> **Solution.** Both the base and the exponent contain $x$, so this is neither a power nor an exponential function — use **logarithmic differentiation**. Take natural logs of both sides:
> $$\ln y = x\ln x.$$
> Differentiate both sides in $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ on the left (factor $\dfrac{y'}{y}$) and using the product rule on the right:
> $$\frac{y'}{y} = 1\cdot\ln x + x\cdot\frac1x = \ln x + 1.$$
> Solve for $y'$ and substitute $y=x^x$:
> $$y' = y\,(\ln x + 1) = x^{x}\,(\ln x + 1).$$

**Example 5.** Find the intervals of monotonicity and the extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$.

> **Solution.** An application of differentiation: read the rise/fall off the sign of the first derivative. Differentiate:
> $$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x-1)(x+1).$$
> Setting $f'(x)=0$ gives the critical points $x=-1,\ 1$, which split the line into three pieces:
> - $x<-1$: $f'>0$, so $f$ is increasing;
> - $-1<x<1$: $f'<0$, so $f$ is decreasing;
> - $x>1$: $f'>0$, so $f$ is increasing.
>
> Hence at $x=-1$ the function switches from increasing to decreasing, giving a **local maximum** $f(-1)=-1+3=2$; at $x=1$ it switches from decreasing to increasing, giving a **local minimum** $f(1)=1-3=-2$.

## Exercises

1. Using the definition, compute $f'(1)$ for $f(x)=x^2$.
2. Differentiate $y = \dfrac{x}{1+x^2}$.
3. Given $x^3 + y^3 = 6xy$, find $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ by implicit differentiation.
4. Find the tangent line to $y = e^x$ at the point $(0,1)$.
5. Differentiate $y = \arctan(x^2)$.
6. Find the intervals of monotonicity of $f(x) = 2x^3 - 6x^2 + 1$.

**Answers.** 1) $\frac{(1+\Delta x)^2-1}{\Delta x}=2+\Delta x\to 2$, so $f'(1)=2$; 2) by the quotient rule, $y'=\dfrac{(1+x^2)-x\cdot 2x}{(1+x^2)^2}=\dfrac{1-x^2}{(1+x^2)^2}$; 3) differentiating gives $3x^2+3y^2 y'=6y+6x y'$, so $y'=\dfrac{2y-x^2}{y^2-2x}$; 4) since $y'=e^x$ has slope $1$ at $x=0$, the tangent line is $y = x + 1$; 5) by the chain rule, $y'=\dfrac{1}{1+(x^2)^2}\cdot 2x=\dfrac{2x}{1+x^4}$; 6) $f'(x)=6x^2-12x=6x(x-2)$, so $f$ increases on $(-\infty,0)$ and $(2,+\infty)$ and decreases on $(0,2)$.
