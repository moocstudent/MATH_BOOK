## Core Notes

### Propositions and Connectives

A **proposition** is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both. We denote propositional variables by $p, q, r, \dots$ and truth values by **T** (true) or **F** (false). Questions, commands, paradoxes (e.g. "this sentence is false"), and open sentences with free variables (e.g. "$x>3$") are not propositions.

Propositions built from simpler ones using **connectives** are called **compound propositions**. The five basic connectives are:

- **Negation** $\neg p$: "not $p$"; flips the truth value.
- **Conjunction** $p \land q$: "$p$ and $q$"; true only when both are true.
- **Disjunction** $p \lor q$: "$p$ or $q$" (inclusive); true when at least one is true.
- **Implication** $p \to q$: "if $p$ then $q$". It is false **only** when the antecedent is true and the consequent false; hence $\text{F}\to q$ is always true (**vacuously true**). Here $p$ is the antecedent and $q$ the consequent.
- **Biconditional** $p \leftrightarrow q$: "$p$ if and only if $q$"; true exactly when both sides share the same truth value.

Around an implication $p\to q$, three related forms must be distinguished: the **converse** $q\to p$, the **inverse** $\neg p\to\neg q$, and the **contrapositive** $\neg q\to\neg p$. Only the contrapositive is always equivalent to the original; the converse and inverse are equivalent to each other but not to the original.

Precedence, highest to lowest: $\neg,\ \land,\ \lor,\ \to,\ \leftrightarrow$. For instance $\neg p \lor q \to r$ means $\big((\neg p)\lor q\big)\to r$. Equal precedence usually groups left to right, but parentheses are encouraged for clarity.

**Logic-symbol cheat sheet:**

| Symbol | Read as / name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $\neg$ | not / negation | $\neg p$: flips the truth value of $p$ |
| $\land$ | and / conjunction | $p\land q$: true only if both are true |
| $\lor$ | or / disjunction | $p\lor q$: true if at least one is (inclusive) |
| $\to$ | if…then / implication | $p\to q$: false only when $p$ true, $q$ false |
| $\leftrightarrow$ | iff / biconditional | $p\leftrightarrow q$: true when both sides match |
| $\oplus$ | xor / exclusive or | $p\oplus q$: true when exactly one is true |
| $\equiv$ | logically equivalent | $p\equiv q$: identical truth tables |
| $\forall$ | for all / universal | $\forall x\,P(x)$: holds for every $x$ |
| $\exists$ | there exists / existential | $\exists x\,P(x)$: holds for some $x$ |
| $\text{T}$ / $\text{F}$ | true / false | the two truth values |

(The quantifiers $\forall,\exists$ are covered in "Predicates and Quantifiers" below.)

### Truth Tables and Logical Equivalence

A **truth table** lists the value of a compound proposition under every assignment. With $n$ variables there are $2^n$ rows. The five connectives:

| $p$ | $q$ | $\neg p$ | $p\land q$ | $p\lor q$ | $p\to q$ | $p\leftrightarrow q$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| T | T | F | T | T | T | T |
| T | F | F | F | T | F | F |
| F | T | T | F | T | T | F |
| F | F | T | F | F | T | T |

A formula true under every assignment is a **tautology**, written $\top$; one false under every assignment is a **contradiction**, written $\bot$; one true under at least one assignment is **satisfiable**.

Two formulas $A, B$ with identical truth values under all assignments are **logically equivalent**, written $A \equiv B$. Equivalently, $A \leftrightarrow B$ is a tautology. Key laws:

- **Double negation**: $\neg\neg p \equiv p$.
- **Commutative / associative / distributive**: $p\land q\equiv q\land p$; $(p\lor q)\lor r\equiv p\lor(q\lor r)$; $p\land(q\lor r)\equiv(p\land q)\lor(p\land r)$.
- **De Morgan's laws**: $\neg(p\land q)\equiv \neg p\lor \neg q$, $\quad\neg(p\lor q)\equiv \neg p\land \neg q$.
- **Absorption / idempotent**: $p\lor(p\land q)\equiv p$; $p\lor p\equiv p$.
- **Implication rewrite**: $p\to q\equiv \neg p\lor q$.
- **Contrapositive**: $p\to q\equiv \neg q\to \neg p$.
- **Identity / domination**: $p\land\top\equiv p$, $p\lor\bot\equiv p$, $p\lor\top\equiv\top$, $p\land\bot\equiv\bot$.

The table below verifies De Morgan's law $\neg(p\land q)\equiv \neg p\lor \neg q$:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p\land q$ | $\neg(p\land q)$ | $\neg p$ | $\neg q$ | $\neg p\lor\neg q$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| T | T | T | F | F | F | F |
| T | F | F | T | F | T | T |
| F | T | F | T | T | F | T |
| F | F | F | T | T | T | T |

Columns 4 and 7 match exactly, so the equivalence holds. There are two routes to prove an equivalence: compare truth tables row by row, or rewrite step by step using known laws.

### Normal Forms (DNF / CNF)

A **literal** is a propositional variable or its negation (e.g. $p$, $\neg q$).

A **conjunctive clause** is a conjunction of literals (e.g. $p\land\neg q$); a **disjunctive clause** is a disjunction of literals (e.g. $\neg p\lor q$).

- **Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)**: a disjunction of conjunctive clauses, shaped $(\dots\land\dots)\lor(\dots\land\dots)$.
- **Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)**: a conjunction of disjunctive clauses, shaped $(\dots\lor\dots)\land(\dots\lor\dots)$.

**Every formula has an equivalent DNF and an equivalent CNF.** The procedure has three steps: eliminate $\to,\leftrightarrow$ via implication rewrite; push negations **inward** to the literals with De Morgan and double negation; finally expand with distributivity into the desired shape.

If every variable appears exactly once (plain or negated) in each conjunctive clause, we obtain the **full DNF**; each such clause is a **minterm** and corresponds directly to a row where the formula is **T**. Dually, the **full CNF** is built from **maxterms**, one per row where it is F. The full normal forms are **unique** up to reordering, making them the standard tool for deciding whether two formulas are equivalent.

### Predicates and Quantifiers

Propositional logic cannot express "all" or "some". A **predicate** $P(x)$ is a statement with a variable; substituting an individual yields a proposition. E.g. $P(x):$ "$x$ is even" makes $P(4)$ true. The range of the variable is the **domain** (universe); the truth of a quantified statement depends on it.

The two **quantifiers**:

- **Universal** $\forall x\, P(x)$: "for all $x$, $P(x)$ holds". One counterexample makes it false.
- **Existential** $\exists x\, P(x)$: "there exists an $x$ for which $P(x)$ holds". One witness makes it true.

Negating quantifiers (quantifier De Morgan):
$$\neg\,\forall x\, P(x)\equiv \exists x\,\neg P(x),\qquad \neg\,\exists x\, P(x)\equiv \forall x\,\neg P(x).$$
Mnemonic: when a negation passes a quantifier, $\forall$ and $\exists$ swap and the negation moves inside.

The **order of nested quantifiers matters**: $\forall x\,\exists y\,P(x,y)$ and $\exists y\,\forall x\,P(x,y)$ are generally not equivalent. With $P(x,y):$ "$x<y$" over $\mathbb{Z}$, the first is true (every $x$ has a larger $y$) but the second is false (no $y$ exceeds every integer). Adjacent quantifiers of the same kind do commute: $\forall x\,\forall y\equiv\forall y\,\forall x$.

### Inference Rules and Proof Methods

An **argument** consists of premises $H_1,\dots,H_n$ and a conclusion $C$. It is **valid** iff $(H_1\land\cdots\land H_n)\to C$ is a tautology; validity is purely formal and independent of whether the premises are actually true. Common rules of inference:

- **Modus Ponens**: from $p\to q$ and $p$, infer $q$.
- **Modus Tollens**: from $p\to q$ and $\neg q$, infer $\neg p$.
- **Hypothetical Syllogism**: from $p\to q$ and $q\to r$, infer $p\to r$.
- **Disjunctive Syllogism**: from $p\lor q$ and $\neg p$, infer $q$.
- **Simplification / Conjunction**: from $p\land q$ infer $p$; from $p$ and $q$ infer $p\land q$.

Two common **fallacies** to avoid: **affirming the consequent** (wrongly inferring $p$ from $p\to q$ and $q$) and **denying the antecedent** (wrongly inferring $\neg q$ from $p\to q$ and $\neg p$); neither is valid.

Common proof methods:

- **Direct proof**: derive the conclusion step by step from the premises.
- **Proof by contradiction**: to prove $C$, assume $\neg C$ and derive a contradiction $\bot$, hence $C$ holds.
- **Contrapositive proof**: to prove $p\to q$, prove the equivalent $\neg q\to \neg p$.
- **Proof by cases**: if a premise is $p_1\lor\cdots\lor p_k$, prove $C$ from each $p_i$ separately.

### Summary

- Among the five connectives only $\to$ is error-prone: **false only when antecedent true and consequent false**, true otherwise.
- Three classes: tautology $\top$, contradiction $\bot$, satisfiable; $A\equiv B \iff A\leftrightarrow B$ is a tautology.
- Memorize: double negation, De Morgan, distributivity, implication rewrite $p\to q\equiv\neg p\lor q$, contrapositive $p\to q\equiv\neg q\to\neg p$.
- Every formula has an equivalent DNF / CNF; full normal forms are unique and decide equivalence.
- Quantifier negation: $\neg\forall x\,P\equiv\exists x\,\neg P$, $\neg\exists x\,P\equiv\forall x\,\neg P$; $\forall\exists$ and $\exists\forall$ do not commute.
- Valid rules: MP, MT, hypothetical & disjunctive syllogism; fallacies: affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Use a truth table to decide whether $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)$ is logically equivalent to $p\leftrightarrow q$.

> **Solution.** Build the table:
>
> | $p$ | $q$ | $p\to q$ | $q\to p$ | $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)$ | $p\leftrightarrow q$ |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | T | T | T | T | T | T |
> | T | F | F | T | F | F |
> | F | T | T | F | F | F |
> | F | F | T | T | T | T |
>
> Columns five and six are identical, so $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)\equiv p\leftrightarrow q$. This is precisely the meaning of "if and only if".

**Example 2.** Find a disjunctive normal form of $\neg(p\to q)\lor r$.

> **Solution.** Simplify step by step:
> $$\neg(p\to q)\lor r \equiv \neg(\neg p\lor q)\lor r$$
> Apply De Morgan to the inner part:
> $$\equiv (\neg\neg p\land \neg q)\lor r \equiv (p\land\neg q)\lor r.$$
> The result $(p\land\neg q)\lor r$ is already a disjunction of two conjunctive clauses ($p\land\neg q$ and the single literal $r$), giving the required **DNF**.

**Example 3.** Premises: "if it rains, the ground is wet ($p\to q$)" and "the ground is not wet ($\neg q$)". Prove formally that "it did not rain ($\neg p$)".

> **Solution.** Write the proof with justifications:
>
> 1. $p\to q$ — premise
> 2. $\neg q$ — premise
> 3. $\neg p$ — from 1, 2 by **Modus Tollens**
>
> Hence $\neg p$ follows and the argument is valid. Equivalently, take the contrapositive of 1 to get $\neg q\to\neg p$, then apply Modus Ponens with 2 to reach $\neg p$.

**Example 4.** Without a full truth table, show that $p\to(q\to p)$ is a tautology.

> **Solution.** Simplify with equivalence laws. By implication rewrite, the inner $q\to p\equiv\neg q\lor p$, so
> $$p\to(q\to p)\equiv p\to(\neg q\lor p)\equiv \neg p\lor(\neg q\lor p).$$
> Reorder using associativity and commutativity:
> $$\equiv (\neg p\lor p)\lor\neg q\equiv \top\lor\neg q\equiv \top.$$
> Since $\neg p\lor p\equiv\top$ (excluded middle), the whole formula is always $\top$, so $p\to(q\to p)$ is a **tautology**. Intuitively: whatever $q$ is, if $p$ is true then $q\to p$ is true, and if $p$ is false then the outer antecedent is false, making the implication true.

**Example 5.** Let the domain be all integers, $P(x):$ "$x$ is even" and $Q(x):$ "$x$ is a multiple of $3$". Write "there is an integer that is both even and a multiple of $3$" in quantifier form, then write its negation in a form where negation applies only to predicates, and finally decide the truth of the original.

> **Solution.** The original statement:
> $$\exists x\,\big(P(x)\land Q(x)\big).$$
> Negate using quantifier De Morgan, then De Morgan on the conjunction:
> $$\neg\,\exists x\,(P(x)\land Q(x))\equiv \forall x\,\neg(P(x)\land Q(x))\equiv \forall x\,(\neg P(x)\lor\neg Q(x)).$$
> That is, "every integer is either not even or not a multiple of $3$". The original is **true**: take $x=6$, which is even and a multiple of $3$, providing a witness.

## Exercises

1. Let $p$ be true, $q$ false, $r$ true. Find the truth value of $(\neg p\lor q)\to(r\land\neg q)$.
2. Using De Morgan and implication rewrite, convert $\neg(p\land(q\to r))$ into an equivalent formula using only $\neg,\land,\lor$.
3. Rewrite $\neg\,\forall x\,(P(x)\to Q(x))$ so that the negation applies only to predicates.
4. Decide whether this argument is valid and justify: premises $p\lor q$, $\neg p$; conclusion $q$.
5. Using a truth table or equivalence laws, decide whether $(p\land q)\to p$ is a tautology.
6. Write the converse, inverse, and contrapositive of "if $n$ is divisible by $4$, then $n$ is even", and state which are equivalent to the original.

**Answers.** 1) $\neg p\lor q$ is $\text{F}\lor\text{F}=\text{F}$, so the antecedent is false and the implication is **T**; 2) $\neg(p\land(q\to r))\equiv \neg p\lor\neg(q\to r)\equiv \neg p\lor\neg(\neg q\lor r)\equiv \neg p\lor(q\land\neg r)$; 3) $\neg\,\forall x\,(P(x)\to Q(x))\equiv \exists x\,\neg(P(x)\to Q(x))\equiv \exists x\,(P(x)\land\neg Q(x))$; 4) **Valid** — this is **Disjunctive Syllogism**: $p\lor q$ with $\neg p$ forces $q$; 5) **Tautology** — $(p\land q)\to p\equiv\neg(p\land q)\lor p\equiv(\neg p\lor\neg q)\lor p\equiv(\neg p\lor p)\lor\neg q\equiv\top$; 6) let $p:$ "$n$ divisible by $4$", $q:$ "$n$ even". Converse $q\to p$: "if $n$ is even, then $n$ is divisible by $4$"; inverse $\neg p\to\neg q$: "if $n$ is not divisible by $4$, then $n$ is not even"; contrapositive $\neg q\to\neg p$: "if $n$ is not even, then $n$ is not divisible by $4$". The **contrapositive is equivalent to the original** (both true), while the converse and inverse are equivalent to each other but not to the original (both false, e.g. $n=6$).
