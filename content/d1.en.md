## Core Notes

### Propositions and Connectives

A **proposition** is a declarative sentence that is either true or false, but not both. We denote propositional variables by $p, q, r, \dots$ and truth values by **T** (true) or **F** (false). Questions, commands, and paradoxes (e.g. "this sentence is false") are not propositions.

Propositions built from simpler ones using **connectives** are called **compound propositions**. The five basic connectives are:

- **Negation** $\neg p$: "not $p$".
- **Conjunction** $p \land q$: "$p$ and $q$"; true only when both are true.
- **Disjunction** $p \lor q$: "$p$ or $q$" (inclusive); true when at least one is true.
- **Implication** $p \to q$: "if $p$ then $q$". It is false **only** when the antecedent is true and the consequent false; hence $\text{F}\to q$ is always true (**vacuously true**).
- **Biconditional** $p \leftrightarrow q$: "$p$ if and only if $q$"; true exactly when both sides share the same truth value.

Precedence, highest to lowest: $\neg,\ \land,\ \lor,\ \to,\ \leftrightarrow$. For instance $\neg p \lor q \to r$ means $\big((\neg p)\lor q\big)\to r$.

### Truth Tables and Logical Equivalence

A **truth table** lists the value of a compound proposition under every assignment. With $n$ variables there are $2^n$ rows. The five connectives:

| $p$ | $q$ | $\neg p$ | $p\land q$ | $p\lor q$ | $p\to q$ | $p\leftrightarrow q$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| T | T | F | T | T | T | T |
| T | F | F | F | T | F | F |
| F | T | T | F | T | T | F |
| F | F | T | F | F | T | T |

A formula true under every assignment is a **tautology**, written $\top$; one false under every assignment is a **contradiction**, written $\bot$.

Two formulas $A, B$ with identical truth values under all assignments are **logically equivalent**, written $A \equiv B$. Equivalently, $A \leftrightarrow B$ is a tautology. Key laws:

- **Double negation**: $\neg\neg p \equiv p$.
- **De Morgan's laws**: $\neg(p\land q)\equiv \neg p\lor \neg q$, $\quad\neg(p\lor q)\equiv \neg p\land \neg q$.
- **Distributivity**: $p\land(q\lor r)\equiv(p\land q)\lor(p\land r)$.
- **Implication rewrite**: $p\to q\equiv \neg p\lor q$.
- **Contrapositive**: $p\to q\equiv \neg q\to \neg p$.

The table below verifies De Morgan's law $\neg(p\land q)\equiv \neg p\lor \neg q$:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p\land q$ | $\neg(p\land q)$ | $\neg p\lor\neg q$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| T | T | T | F | F |
| T | F | F | T | T |
| F | T | F | T | T |
| F | F | F | T | T |

The last two columns match, so the equivalence holds.

### Normal Forms (DNF / CNF)

A **literal** is a propositional variable or its negation (e.g. $p$, $\neg q$).

A **conjunctive clause** is a conjunction of literals (e.g. $p\land\neg q$); a **disjunctive clause** is a disjunction of literals (e.g. $\neg p\lor q$).

- **Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF)**: a disjunction of conjunctive clauses, shaped $(\dots\land\dots)\lor(\dots\land\dots)$.
- **Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF)**: a conjunction of disjunctive clauses, shaped $(\dots\lor\dots)\land(\dots\lor\dots)$.

**Every formula has an equivalent DNF and an equivalent CNF.** Procedure: eliminate $\to,\leftrightarrow$ via implication rewrite, push negations inward to the literals with De Morgan's laws, then expand using distributivity.

If every variable appears exactly once (plain or negated) in each conjunctive clause, we obtain the **full DNF**, which corresponds directly to the rows of the truth table where the formula is T. Dually, the full CNF corresponds to the rows where it is F.

### Predicates and Quantifiers

Propositional logic cannot express "all" or "some". A **predicate** $P(x)$ is a statement with a variable; substituting an individual yields a proposition. E.g. $P(x):$ "$x$ is even" makes $P(4)$ true.

The two **quantifiers**:

- **Universal** $\forall x\, P(x)$: "for all $x$, $P(x)$ holds".
- **Existential** $\exists x\, P(x)$: "there exists an $x$ for which $P(x)$ holds".

Negating quantifiers (quantifier De Morgan):
$$\neg\,\forall x\, P(x)\equiv \exists x\,\neg P(x),\qquad \neg\,\exists x\, P(x)\equiv \forall x\,\neg P(x).$$

The **order of nested quantifiers matters**: $\forall x\,\exists y\,P(x,y)$ and $\exists y\,\forall x\,P(x,y)$ are generally not equivalent. With $P(x,y):$ "$x<y$" over $\mathbb{Z}$, the first is true (every $x$ has a larger $y$) but the second is false (no $y$ exceeds every integer).

### Inference Rules and Proof Methods

An **argument** consists of premises $H_1,\dots,H_n$ and a conclusion $C$. It is **valid** iff $(H_1\land\cdots\land H_n)\to C$ is a tautology. Common rules of inference:

- **Modus Ponens**: from $p\to q$ and $p$, infer $q$.
- **Modus Tollens**: from $p\to q$ and $\neg q$, infer $\neg p$.
- **Hypothetical Syllogism**: from $p\to q$ and $q\to r$, infer $p\to r$.
- **Disjunctive Syllogism**: from $p\lor q$ and $\neg p$, infer $q$.

Common proof methods:

- **Direct proof**: derive the conclusion step by step from the premises.
- **Proof by contradiction**: to prove $C$, assume $\neg C$ and derive a contradiction $\bot$, hence $C$ holds.
- **Contrapositive proof**: to prove $p\to q$, prove the equivalent $\neg q\to \neg p$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Use a truth table to decide whether $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)$ is logically equivalent to $p\leftrightarrow q$.

> **Solution.** Build the table:
>
> | $p$ | $q$ | $p\to q$ | $q\to p$ | $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)$ | $p\leftrightarrow q$ |
> |---|---|---|---|---|---|
> | T | T | T | T | T | T |
> | T | F | F | T | F | F |
> | F | T | T | F | F | F |
> | F | F | T | T | T | T |
>
> Columns five and six are identical, so $(p\to q)\land(q\to p)\equiv p\leftrightarrow q$. This is precisely the meaning of "if and only if".

**Example 2.** Find a disjunctive normal form of $\neg(p\to q)\lor r$.

> **Solution.** Simplify step by step:
> $$\neg(p\to q)\lor r \equiv \neg(\neg p\lor q)\lor r$$
> Apply De Morgan to the inner part:
> $$\equiv (\neg\neg p\land \neg q)\lor r \equiv (p\land\neg q)\lor r.$$
> The result $(p\land\neg q)\lor r$ is already a disjunction of two conjunctive clauses ($p\land\neg q$ and the single literal $r$), giving the required **DNF**.

**Example 3.** Premises: "if it rains, the ground is wet ($p\to q$)" and "the ground is not wet ($\neg q$)". Prove formally that "it did not rain ($\neg p$)".

> **Solution.** Write the proof with justifications:
>
> 1. $p\to q$ — premise
> 2. $\neg q$ — premise
> 3. $\neg p$ — from 1, 2 by **Modus Tollens**
>
> Hence $\neg p$ follows and the argument is valid. Equivalently, take the contrapositive of 1 to get $\neg q\to\neg p$, then apply Modus Ponens with 2 to reach $\neg p$.

## Exercises

1. Let $p$ be true, $q$ false, $r$ true. Find the truth value of $(\neg p\lor q)\to(r\land\neg q)$.
2. Using De Morgan and implication rewrite, convert $\neg(p\land(q\to r))$ into an equivalent formula using only $\neg,\land,\lor$.
3. Rewrite $\neg\,\forall x\,(P(x)\to Q(x))$ so that the negation applies only to predicates.
4. Decide whether this argument is valid and justify: premises $p\lor q$, $\neg p$; conclusion $q$.

**Answers.** 1) $\neg p\lor q$ is $\text{F}\lor\text{F}=\text{F}$, so the antecedent is false and the implication is **T**; 2) $\neg(p\land(q\to r))\equiv \neg p\lor\neg(q\to r)\equiv \neg p\lor\neg(\neg q\lor r)\equiv \neg p\lor(q\land\neg r)$; 3) $\neg\,\forall x\,(P(x)\to Q(x))\equiv \exists x\,\neg(P(x)\to Q(x))\equiv \exists x\,(P(x)\land\neg Q(x))$; 4) **Valid** — this is **Disjunctive Syllogism**: $p\lor q$ with $\neg p$ forces $q$.
