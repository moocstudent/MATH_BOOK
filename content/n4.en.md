## Core Notes

### Algebraic Fractions and Their Fundamental Property

**Algebraic fraction:** an expression of the form $\dfrac{A}{B}$ where $A,B$ are integral expressions and **the denominator $B$ contains a letter**. Examples: $\dfrac{1}{x}$, $\dfrac{x+1}{x-2}$, $\dfrac{a}{a^2+1}$.

**Condition for meaning:** the **denominator may not be zero**, so using a fraction requires $B\ne0$. For instance $\dfrac{x+1}{x-2}$ is undefined when $x=2$.

**When the value is zero:** we need the **numerator zero and the denominator nonzero**, i.e. $A=0$ and $B\ne0$. For example $\dfrac{x-3}{x+1}=0$ requires $x=3$ (then the denominator $4\ne0$).

**Fundamental property of fractions:** multiplying (or dividing) numerator and denominator by the **same nonzero integral expression** leaves the value unchanged:

$$\frac{A}{B}=\frac{A\times M}{B\times M}=\frac{A\div M}{B\div M}\quad(M\ne0).$$

This matches the fundamental property of numerical fractions and underlies **cancellation** and **finding a common denominator**.

**Pitfall:** the thing multiplied/divided must be the **entire** numerator and **entire** denominator, not just one term; and it must be nonzero.

### Operations on Fractions

**Cancellation (reduction):** cancel the **common factors** of numerator and denominator to reach a **fraction in lowest terms** (no common factor). Usually **factor** numerator and denominator first, then cancel:

$$\frac{x^2-1}{x^2+2x+1}=\frac{(x+1)(x-1)}{(x+1)^2}=\frac{x-1}{x+1}.$$

**Common denominator:** turn several fractions with **different denominators** into ones with the **same denominator**, taking the **least common denominator** (the product of each factor to its highest power).

**Multiplication and division:**

- Multiplication: numerator times numerator, denominator times denominator, cancelling where possible. $$\frac{A}{B}\cdot\frac{C}{D}=\frac{AC}{BD}.$$
- Division: dividing by a fraction equals multiplying by its **reciprocal**. $$\frac{A}{B}\div\frac{C}{D}=\frac{A}{B}\cdot\frac{D}{C}=\frac{AD}{BC}.$$

**Addition and subtraction:** with a common denominator, add/subtract numerators and keep the denominator; with different denominators, find a common denominator first:

$$\frac{a}{c}\pm\frac{b}{c}=\frac{a\pm b}{c},\qquad \frac{A}{B}\pm\frac{C}{D}=\frac{AD\pm BC}{BD}.$$

**Pitfall:** when subtracting fractions, the minus sign acts on the **whole numerator**; after finding a common denominator, use brackets so no term in the second numerator is left unsubtracted.

### Square Roots (Radicals) and Simplification

**Square root (radical):** an expression $\sqrt{a}$ with $a\ge0$; here $a$ is the **radicand**. **Negative numbers have no square root**, so the radicand must be **non-negative**: $\sqrt a$ is defined only when $a\ge0$.

**Key identities:** distinguish

$$(\sqrt a)^2=a\ (a\ge0),\qquad \sqrt{a^2}=|a|.$$

In the second, because the principal square root is **non-negative**, $\sqrt{a^2}$ equals the **absolute value** of $a$: it is $a$ when $a\ge0$ and $-a$ when $a<0$. E.g. $\sqrt{(-5)^2}=\sqrt{25}=5=|-5|$.

**Two operating rules** (for simplifying, with $a\ge0,b\ge0$):

$$\sqrt{ab}=\sqrt a\cdot\sqrt b,\qquad \sqrt{\frac{a}{b}}=\frac{\sqrt a}{\sqrt b}\ (b>0).$$

**Simplest (reduced) radical:** two conditions — (1) the radicand has **no factor that comes out evenly** (no perfect-square factor); (2) the radicand contains **no denominator**. Simplifying means pulling perfect-square factors out from under the root, e.g.

$$\sqrt{12}=\sqrt{4\times3}=\sqrt4\cdot\sqrt3=2\sqrt3.$$

### Operations with Radicals

**Adding and subtracting radicals:** only **like radicals** (the same radicand after simplifying) may be combined; add/subtract the outside coefficients and keep the root, just like combining like terms:

$$2\sqrt3+5\sqrt3=7\sqrt3.$$

Often you must **simplify first**. E.g. $\sqrt{12}+\sqrt{27}=2\sqrt3+3\sqrt3=5\sqrt3$.

**Multiplying and dividing radicals:** use $\sqrt a\cdot\sqrt b=\sqrt{ab}$ and $\dfrac{\sqrt a}{\sqrt b}=\sqrt{\dfrac{a}{b}}$, multiplying/dividing coefficients with coefficients and radicands with radicands:

$$2\sqrt3\cdot 3\sqrt5=(2\times3)\sqrt{3\times5}=6\sqrt{15}.$$

In mixed expressions the special products still apply, e.g. $(\sqrt a+\sqrt b)(\sqrt a-\sqrt b)=a-b$.

**Pitfall:** $\sqrt a+\sqrt b\ne\sqrt{a+b}$! For instance $\sqrt9+\sqrt{16}=3+4=7$, whereas $\sqrt{25}=5$; the two are not equal.

### Rationalizing the Denominator

**Rationalizing the denominator:** removing radicals from the denominator. Using the fundamental property, multiply numerator and denominator by a suitable expression.

**Case 1: a single radical** $\sqrt b$ in the denominator. Multiply numerator and denominator by $\sqrt b$:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt2}=\frac{1\times\sqrt2}{\sqrt2\times\sqrt2}=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}.$$

**Case 2: a two-term denominator** (containing a radical). Use the difference of squares: multiply by the **rationalizing (conjugate) factor**. For a denominator $\sqrt a-\sqrt b$, multiply by $\sqrt a+\sqrt b$:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt3-\sqrt2}=\frac{\sqrt3+\sqrt2}{(\sqrt3-\sqrt2)(\sqrt3+\sqrt2)}=\frac{\sqrt3+\sqrt2}{3-2}=\sqrt3+\sqrt2.$$

**Purpose:** after rationalizing, the denominator is rational, which eases numerical estimation and further computation, and is the standard form expected for radicals.

**Pitfall:** multiply numerator and denominator **together** by the rationalizing factor; for a two-term denominator use the **conjugate** (flip only the middle sign), not a copy of the same two terms.

### Summary

- Fraction $\dfrac{A}{B}$ ($B$ contains a letter): defined when $B\ne0$; value zero when $A=0$ and $B\ne0$.
- Fundamental property: scaling numerator and denominator by a nonzero expression keeps the value → cancellation (lowest terms), common denominator (least common denominator).
- Operations: multiply $\dfrac{AC}{BD}$, divide by multiplying the reciprocal, add/subtract after a common denominator; the minus sign acts on the whole numerator.
- Radicals: defined only for $a\ge0$; $\sqrt{a^2}=|a|$; $\sqrt{ab}=\sqrt a\sqrt b$; simplest radical has no perfect-square factor and no denominator.
- Only like radicals combine; $\sqrt a+\sqrt b\ne\sqrt{a+b}$; rationalizing: single root times $\sqrt b$, two-term denominator times its conjugate.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** For which $x$ is the fraction $\dfrac{x-3}{x+2}$ (1) defined? (2) equal to zero?

> **Solution.** (1) Being defined requires a **nonzero denominator**: $x+2\ne0$, i.e. $x\ne-2$.
> (2) Being zero requires the **numerator zero and the denominator nonzero**:
> $$x-3=0\ \text{and}\ x+2\ne0.$$
> From $x-3=0$ we get $x=3$, and then the denominator $3+2=5\ne0$, which is fine. So $x=3$.

**Example 2.** Simplify $\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-4x+4}$.

> **Solution.** Factor numerator and denominator, then cancel.
> Numerator: $x^2-4=(x+2)(x-2)$ (difference of squares); denominator: $x^2-4x+4=(x-2)^2$ (perfect square). Hence
> $$\frac{x^2-4}{x^2-4x+4}=\frac{(x+2)(x-2)}{(x-2)^2}=\frac{x+2}{x-2}\quad(x\ne2).$$
> Cancelling the common factor $(x-2)$ gives the reduced form.

**Example 3.** Compute $\dfrac{a}{a-1}-\dfrac{1}{a-1}+\dfrac{2}{a+1}$.

> **Solution.** The first two terms share a denominator; combine them first:
> $$\frac{a}{a-1}-\frac{1}{a-1}=\frac{a-1}{a-1}=1.$$
> So the expression is $1+\dfrac{2}{a+1}$; find a common denominator:
> $$1+\frac{2}{a+1}=\frac{a+1}{a+1}+\frac{2}{a+1}=\frac{a+1+2}{a+1}=\frac{a+3}{a+1}\quad(a\ne\pm1).$$

**Example 4.** Simplify $\sqrt{12}+\sqrt{75}-\sqrt{48}$.

> **Solution.** Reduce each radical, pulling out perfect-square factors.
> $$\sqrt{12}=\sqrt{4\times3}=2\sqrt3,\quad \sqrt{75}=\sqrt{25\times3}=5\sqrt3,\quad \sqrt{48}=\sqrt{16\times3}=4\sqrt3.$$
> These are all like radicals of $\sqrt3$; combine the coefficients:
> $$2\sqrt3+5\sqrt3-4\sqrt3=(2+5-4)\sqrt3=3\sqrt3.$$

**Example 5.** Rationalize the denominator of $\dfrac{2}{\sqrt5-\sqrt3}$.

> **Solution.** The denominator has two terms; multiply by its conjugate $\sqrt5+\sqrt3$ (flip only the middle sign) and use the difference of squares:
> $$\frac{2}{\sqrt5-\sqrt3}=\frac{2(\sqrt5+\sqrt3)}{(\sqrt5-\sqrt3)(\sqrt5+\sqrt3)}=\frac{2(\sqrt5+\sqrt3)}{(\sqrt5)^2-(\sqrt3)^2}=\frac{2(\sqrt5+\sqrt3)}{5-3}.$$
> The denominator becomes the rational number $2$; cancel:
> $$\frac{2(\sqrt5+\sqrt3)}{2}=\sqrt5+\sqrt3.$$

## Exercises

1. For which $x$ is the fraction $\dfrac{x+1}{2x-6}$ defined? For which $x$ is its value zero?
2. Simplify $\dfrac{6x^2y}{9xy^2}$.
3. Simplify $\dfrac{x^2-9}{x^2+6x+9}$.
4. Compute $\dfrac{2}{x-1}+\dfrac{1}{x+1}$.
5. Simplify $\sqrt{18}+\sqrt{8}-\sqrt{50}$.
6. Rationalize the denominators of $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt6}$ and $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt7+\sqrt5}$.

**Answers.**
1) Defined requires $2x-6\ne0$, i.e. $x\ne3$; the value is zero when the numerator $x+1=0$ and the denominator is nonzero, so $x=-1$.
2) Cancel the common factor $3xy$: $\dfrac{6x^2y}{9xy^2}=\dfrac{2x}{3y}$.
3) $\dfrac{x^2-9}{x^2+6x+9}=\dfrac{(x+3)(x-3)}{(x+3)^2}=\dfrac{x-3}{x+3}\ (x\ne-3)$.
4) Common denominator: $\dfrac{2(x+1)+1\cdot(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+1)}=\dfrac{2x+2+x-1}{x^2-1}=\dfrac{3x+1}{x^2-1}$.
5) $\sqrt{18}=3\sqrt2,\ \sqrt8=2\sqrt2,\ \sqrt{50}=5\sqrt2$; $3\sqrt2+2\sqrt2-5\sqrt2=0$.
6) $\dfrac{3}{\sqrt6}=\dfrac{3\sqrt6}{6}=\dfrac{\sqrt6}{2}$; $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt7+\sqrt5}=\dfrac{\sqrt7-\sqrt5}{(\sqrt7)^2-(\sqrt5)^2}=\dfrac{\sqrt7-\sqrt5}{2}$.
