## Core Notes

### Angles and Radian Measure

In middle school we measure angles in **degrees**; high school introduces **radian measure**, which is better suited to analysis. By definition, the central angle subtended by an arc whose length equals the radius is **$1$ radian** ($1\ \text{rad}$). Thus if an arc of length $l$ lies on a circle of radius $r$, the radian measure of the central angle it subtends is

$$\alpha=\frac{l}{r}.$$

So the full circumference $2\pi r$ corresponds to a full angle of $\dfrac{2\pi r}{r}=2\pi$ radians, giving the **conversion between degrees and radians**:

$$360^\circ=2\pi,\qquad 180^\circ=\pi,\qquad 1^\circ=\frac{\pi}{180},\qquad 1\ \text{rad}=\frac{180^\circ}{\pi}\approx 57.3^\circ.$$

Common special angles: $30^\circ=\dfrac{\pi}{6}$, $45^\circ=\dfrac{\pi}{4}$, $60^\circ=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$, $90^\circ=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$. Radian measure yields two **clean formulas**: **arc length** $l=\alpha r$ and **sector area** $S=\dfrac12\alpha r^2=\dfrac12 l r$.

We also extend angles to **general angles**: starting from the positive $x$-axis, a counterclockwise rotation gives a **positive angle** and a clockwise one a **negative angle**. All angles sharing the terminal side of $\alpha$ form the set $\{\,\beta\mid\beta=\alpha+2k\pi,\ k\in\mathbb{Z}\,\}$, called **coterminal angles**.

### Trigonometric Functions of a General Angle (Unit Circle)

Let the terminal side of $\alpha$ meet the **unit circle** (radius $1$, centred at the origin) at $P(x,y)$. Define

$$\sin\alpha=y,\qquad \cos\alpha=x,\qquad \tan\alpha=\frac{y}{x}\ (x\ne 0).$$

**Intuition:** on the unit circle the $y$-coordinate is the sine, the $x$-coordinate is the cosine, and the tangent is their ratio (also the slope of the terminal side). This definition works for any angle and unifies the acute-angle ratios. Since $P$ lies on the unit circle, $x^2+y^2=1$, giving the fundamental identity $\sin^2\alpha+\cos^2\alpha=1$.

The **signs are decided by the quadrant** of the terminal side ("All, Sine, Tangent, Cosine" positive in quadrants I–IV):

$$\begin{aligned}
&\text{Quadrant I}:\ \sin>0,\cos>0,\tan>0; &&\text{Quadrant II}:\ \sin>0,\cos<0,\tan<0;\\
&\text{Quadrant III}:\ \sin<0,\cos<0,\tan>0; &&\text{Quadrant IV}:\ \sin<0,\cos>0,\tan<0.
\end{aligned}$$

### Same-Angle Relations and Reduction Formulas

For **one and the same angle** $\alpha$ there are two core relations:

$$\sin^2\alpha+\cos^2\alpha=1\quad(\text{Pythagorean}),\qquad \tan\alpha=\frac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}\quad(\text{quotient}).$$

From the Pythagorean relation we can "find two from one": given $\sin\alpha$ (or $\cos\alpha$) and the quadrant of $\alpha$, we recover the other (**the sign of the square root is fixed by the quadrant**).

**Reduction formulas** rewrite the trig functions of any angle in terms of an acute angle; the guiding rule is **"odd changes the name, even keeps it; the sign follows the quadrant"**: for $\dfrac{k\pi}{2}\pm\alpha$, the function name changes ($\sin\leftrightarrow\cos$) when $k$ is odd and stays the same when $k$ is even, with the sign taken by treating $\alpha$ as acute and reading the original angle's quadrant. Common ones include:

$$\sin(-\alpha)=-\sin\alpha,\quad \cos(-\alpha)=\cos\alpha,\quad \sin(\pi-\alpha)=\sin\alpha,\quad \cos(\pi-\alpha)=-\cos\alpha,$$

$$\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\alpha\right)=\cos\alpha,\qquad \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\alpha\right)=\sin\alpha,\qquad \sin(\pi+\alpha)=-\sin\alpha.$$

### Graphs and Properties of Sine, Cosine, and Tangent

Unrolling the moving point on the unit circle into the coordinate plane gives three basic curves, whose **periodicity, parity, and monotonicity** are:

- **$y=\sin x$:** domain $\mathbb{R}$, range $[-1,1]$; **odd** ($\sin(-x)=-\sin x$, symmetric about the origin); **smallest positive period** $2\pi$; increasing on $\left[-\dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi,\ \dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi\right]$.
- **$y=\cos x$:** domain $\mathbb{R}$, range $[-1,1]$; **even** ($\cos(-x)=\cos x$, symmetric about the $y$-axis); **smallest positive period** $2\pi$; increasing on $[-\pi+2k\pi,\ 2k\pi]$.
- **$y=\tan x$:** domain $\left\{x\mid x\ne\dfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi\right\}$, range $\mathbb{R}$; **odd**; **smallest positive period** $\pi$; increasing on each interval $\left(-\dfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi,\ \dfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi\right)$, with vertical asymptotes.

**Extrema** of sine/cosine: $\sin x=1$ at $x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi$; $\cos x=1$ at $x=2k\pi$. Shifting the sine graph left by $\dfrac{\pi}{2}$ yields the cosine graph, so $\cos x=\sin\!\left(x+\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)$.

### The Function $y=A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$: Graph and Transformations

The general sinusoid

$$y=A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)+k\qquad(A>0,\ \omega>0)$$

has parameters with clear geometric meaning: **$A$ is the amplitude** (range $[k-A,\ k+A]$), **$\omega$ sets the period** $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega}$, **$\varphi$ is the initial phase** (horizontal shift), and $k$ is the vertical shift. Starting from $y=\sin x$, we reach it by **three transformations** (taking $k=0$):

1. **Phase shift (horizontal translation):** $y=\sin x\to y=\sin(x+\varphi)$; left by $\varphi$ if $\varphi>0$, right by $|\varphi|$ if $\varphi<0$.
2. **Period change (horizontal scaling):** $y=\sin(x+\varphi)\to y=\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$; the $x$-coordinates shrink to $\dfrac{1}{\omega}$ of their former values.
3. **Amplitude change (vertical scaling):** $y=\sin(\omega x+\varphi)\to y=A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$; the $y$-coordinates scale by $A$.

**Order pitfall:** if you scale before translating, the shift gets "diluted" by $\omega$. The safe move is to write the bracket as $\omega\!\left(x+\dfrac{\varphi}{\omega}\right)$, which shows the **actual horizontal shift is $\dfrac{\varphi}{\omega}$**, not $\varphi$. For an **axis of symmetry** set $\omega x+\varphi=\dfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi$; for a **centre of symmetry** set $\omega x+\varphi=k\pi$.

### Summary

- Radians: $180^\circ=\pi$; arc length $l=\alpha r$, sector area $S=\dfrac12\alpha r^2=\dfrac12 lr$; coterminal angles $\alpha+2k\pi$.
- Unit-circle definitions: $\sin\alpha=y$, $\cos\alpha=x$, $\tan\alpha=\dfrac yx$; signs "All/Sine/Tangent/Cosine" by quadrant.
- Same-angle relations $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$, $\tan=\dfrac{\sin}{\cos}$; reduction rule "odd changes the name, even keeps it; sign by quadrant".
- $\sin,\cos$ have period $2\pi$, range $[-1,1]$ ($\sin$ odd, $\cos$ even); $\tan$ has period $\pi$, range $\mathbb{R}$, odd.
- $y=A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$: amplitude $A$, period $\dfrac{2\pi}{\omega}$, shift $\dfrac{\varphi}{\omega}$; axis $\omega x+\varphi=\dfrac\pi2+k\pi$, centre $\omega x+\varphi=k\pi$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** A sector has radius $6$ and central angle $\dfrac{\pi}{3}$. Find its arc length and area.

> **Solution.** Use the radian formulas directly. The arc length is
> $$l=\alpha r=\frac{\pi}{3}\times 6=2\pi.$$
> The area is
> $$S=\frac12\alpha r^2=\frac12\cdot\frac{\pi}{3}\cdot 6^2=\frac12\cdot\frac{\pi}{3}\cdot 36=6\pi.$$
> So the arc length is $2\pi$ and the area is $6\pi$.

**Example 2.** Given $\sin\alpha=\dfrac{3}{5}$ with $\alpha$ in the second quadrant, find $\cos\alpha$ and $\tan\alpha$.

> **Solution.** From the Pythagorean relation, $\cos^2\alpha=1-\sin^2\alpha=1-\dfrac{9}{25}=\dfrac{16}{25}$, so $\cos\alpha=\pm\dfrac45$.
> Since $\alpha$ is in the second quadrant, $\cos\alpha<0$, so
> $$\cos\alpha=-\frac{4}{5}.$$
> Then by the quotient relation,
> $$\tan\alpha=\frac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}=\frac{3/5}{-4/5}=-\frac34.$$
> Thus $\cos\alpha=-\dfrac45$ and $\tan\alpha=-\dfrac34$.

**Example 3.** Simplify $\dfrac{\sin(\pi-\alpha)\cos(-\alpha)}{\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\alpha\right)}$.

> **Solution.** Apply reduction formulas one by one: $\sin(\pi-\alpha)=\sin\alpha$, $\cos(-\alpha)=\cos\alpha$, $\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}-\alpha\right)=\sin\alpha$. Substituting,
> $$\frac{\sin(\pi-\alpha)\cos(-\alpha)}{\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\alpha\right)}=\frac{\sin\alpha\cdot\cos\alpha}{\sin\alpha}=\cos\alpha\quad(\sin\alpha\ne 0).$$
> So the expression equals $\cos\alpha$.

**Example 4.** For $y=2\sin\!\left(2x-\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right)$, find the amplitude, the smallest positive period, and the $x$ at which the maximum occurs.

> **Solution.** Matching $y=A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$ gives $A=2,\ \omega=2,\ \varphi=-\dfrac{\pi}{3}$.
> The amplitude is $A=2$, and the smallest positive period is
> $$T=\frac{2\pi}{\omega}=\frac{2\pi}{2}=\pi.$$
> The maximum occurs when $\sin=1$, i.e. $2x-\dfrac{\pi}{3}=\dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi$, giving
> $$x=\frac{5\pi}{12}+k\pi\quad(k\in\mathbb{Z}),$$
> at which the maximum value is $2$.

**Example 5.** Shift the graph of $y=\sin x$ right by $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$, then shrink the $x$-coordinates to half (keeping $y$ fixed). Find the resulting equation.

> **Solution.** Step 1, shift right by $\dfrac{\pi}{6}$ by replacing $x$ with $x-\dfrac{\pi}{6}$:
> $$y=\sin\!\left(x-\frac{\pi}{6}\right).$$
> Step 2, halve the $x$-coordinates by replacing $x$ with $2x$ (halving corresponds to $\omega=2$):
> $$y=\sin\!\left(2x-\frac{\pi}{6}\right).$$
> So the required equation is $y=\sin\!\left(2x-\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)$.

## Exercises

1. Convert $75^\circ$ to radians and $\dfrac{5\pi}{6}$ to degrees.
2. A sector of radius $3$ has area $\dfrac{3\pi}{2}$. Find its central angle (in radians) and its arc length.
3. Given $\cos\alpha=-\dfrac{12}{13}$ with $\alpha\in\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2},\pi\right)$, find $\sin\alpha$ and $\tan\alpha$.
4. Simplify $\dfrac{\sin(\pi+\alpha)+\cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}+\alpha\right)}{\cos(-\alpha)}$.
5. For $y=3\sin\!\left(\dfrac{1}{2}x+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)$, find the amplitude, smallest positive period, and one axis of symmetry.
6. State the increasing intervals of $y=\sin x$ and determine the parity of $y=\cos x$.

**Answers.**
1) $75^\circ=75\cdot\dfrac{\pi}{180}=\dfrac{5\pi}{12}$; $\dfrac{5\pi}{6}=\dfrac{5\pi}{6}\cdot\dfrac{180^\circ}{\pi}=150^\circ$.
2) From $S=\dfrac12\alpha r^2$, $\dfrac{3\pi}{2}=\dfrac12\alpha\cdot 9$, so $\alpha=\dfrac{\pi}{3}$; arc length $l=\alpha r=\dfrac{\pi}{3}\cdot 3=\pi$.
3) $\sin^2\alpha=1-\dfrac{144}{169}=\dfrac{25}{169}$; since $\alpha$ is in the second quadrant $\sin\alpha>0$, so $\sin\alpha=\dfrac{5}{13}$; $\tan\alpha=\dfrac{5/13}{-12/13}=-\dfrac{5}{12}$.
4) $\sin(\pi+\alpha)=-\sin\alpha$, $\cos\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}+\alpha\right)=-\sin\alpha$, $\cos(-\alpha)=\cos\alpha$; the expression is $\dfrac{-\sin\alpha-\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}=\dfrac{-2\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}=-2\tan\alpha$.
5) Amplitude $3$; period $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{1/2}=4\pi$; setting $\dfrac12 x+\dfrac{\pi}{4}=\dfrac{\pi}{2}+k\pi$ gives axes $x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi$ (e.g. $x=\dfrac{\pi}{2}$).
6) Increasing on $\left[-\dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi,\ \dfrac{\pi}{2}+2k\pi\right]$ ($k\in\mathbb{Z}$); $y=\cos x$ is even.
