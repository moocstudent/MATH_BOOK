## Core Notes

### Ratios and Simplifying Ratios

**Ratio:** dividing one number by another is also called their **ratio**. The ratio of $a$ to $b$ (with $b\ne0$) is written $a:b$ or $\dfrac{a}{b}$, read "$a$ to $b$". Here $a$ is the **first term**, $b$ the **second term**, and their quotient $\dfrac{a}{b}$ is the **value of the ratio**.

For example, the value of $6:8$ is $\dfrac68=\dfrac34$. A ratio expresses a **multiplicative relationship** between two quantities, not their actual sizes.

**Fundamental property of ratios:** multiplying or dividing both terms by the **same nonzero number** leaves the value unchanged:

$$a:b=(a\times k):(b\times k)=(a\div k):(b\div k)\quad(k\ne0).$$

This matches the fundamental property of fractions exactly (a ratio is just another way of writing a fraction).

**Simplifying a ratio:** reduce it to a **lowest-terms integer ratio** (the two terms are coprime integers). Methods:

- Integer ratio: divide both terms by their **greatest common divisor**. E.g. $12:18=(12\div6):(18\div6)=2:3$.
- Fractional ratio: clear denominators first. E.g. $\dfrac12:\dfrac34$, multiply both by $4$ to get $2:3$.
- Decimal ratio: clear decimal points first. E.g. $0.6:0.9=6:9=2:3$.

**Pitfall:** the **value** of a ratio is a single **number** (possibly a fraction or decimal), while the **ratio** is a **relationship** between two numbers (the form $a:b$). The three objects $6:8$, $\dfrac34$, $0.75$ are different faces of the same ratio.

### Proportions and Their Fundamental Property

**Proportion:** a statement that **two ratios are equal**, such as $\dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d}$, also written $a:b=c:d$. Here $a,d$ are the **extremes** (outer terms) and $b,c$ the **means** (inner terms).

**Fundamental property of proportions (cross-multiplication):** in a proportion, **the product of the extremes equals the product of the means**:

$$\frac{a}{b}=\frac{c}{d}\ \Longrightarrow\ ad=bc.$$

Intuition: multiplying both sides of $\dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d}$ by $bd$ gives $ad=bc$. Conversely, if $ad=bc$ (with $b,d\ne0$) then $\dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d}$.

This property is enormously useful: whenever one quantity in a proportion is unknown, cross-multiplication solves for it. For example, $\dfrac{x}{6}=\dfrac{5}{3}$ gives $3x=30$, so $x=10$.

**Pitfall:** cross-multiplication requires a genuine **proportion (two equal ratios)**. The proportion form for $ad=bc$ is not unique; e.g. $ad=bc$ also means $\dfrac{a}{c}=\dfrac{b}{d}$.

### Percentages and Percentage Points

**Percentage:** how many **hundredths** one number is of another, i.e. a ratio with denominator $100$. The symbol $\%$ means $\dfrac{1}{100}$, so

$$p\% = \frac{p}{100}.$$

For example $75\%=\dfrac{75}{100}=0.75=\dfrac34$. Percentages, decimals, and fractions convert into one another: **decimal → percentage**, multiply by $100$ and append $\%$ ($0.2=20\%$); **percentage → decimal**, drop $\%$ and divide by $100$ ($35\%=0.35$).

**Finding a percentage of a number:** "$p\%$ of $A$" $=A\times p\%$. E.g. $25\%$ of $80$ is $80\times0.25=20$.

**Percentage points:** when comparing two percentages, their **difference** is stated in "percentage points", not "percent". If an interest rate rises from $3\%$ to $5\%$, that is a rise of $2$ **percentage points** (absolute difference), but the relative increase is $\dfrac{5-3}{3}\approx66.7\%$.

**Pitfall:** "an increase of $2$ percentage points" (from $3\%$ to $5\%$) is entirely different from "an increase of $2\%$" (from $3\%$ to $3\%\times1.02=3.06\%$); do not confuse them.

### Proportional Distribution

**Proportional distribution:** splitting a total into parts according to a given ratio. The core method is **first find the total number of shares, then find one share**.

To split a total $S$ between two parts in the ratio $m:n$, the total shares is $m+n$, **one share** $=\dfrac{S}{m+n}$, hence

$$\text{first part}=S\times\frac{m}{m+n},\qquad \text{second part}=S\times\frac{n}{m+n}.$$

Three or more parts follow the same idea: splitting in the ratio $a:b:c$, the parts take fractions $\dfrac{a}{a+b+c}$, $\dfrac{b}{a+b+c}$, $\dfrac{c}{a+b+c}$ of the total.

**Check:** the parts should sum to the total, and their ratio should equal the given ratio.

### Applications: Interest, Discount, Concentration

Percentages and proportions appear everywhere in daily life; the key is to **identify the "unit 1" (the base quantity)**. The basic relation:

$$\text{part} = \text{base}\times\text{rate},\qquad \text{rate}=\frac{\text{part}}{\text{base}}.$$

**Discount:** in Chinese retail a "$x$-fold discount" means the sale price is $\dfrac{x}{10}$ of the original (so an "8-fold discount" means $80\%$). If a $200$-yuan item is sold at $80\%$, the price is $200\times80\%=160$ yuan, saving $200-160=40$ yuan.

**Interest:** **interest = principal × rate × periods**. With principal $1000$ yuan at annual rate $3\%$ for $2$ years, the simple interest is $1000\times3\%\times2=60$ yuan, and the maturity total is $1060$ yuan.

**Concentration:** **concentration $=\dfrac{\text{mass of solute}}{\text{mass of solution}}\times100\%$**, where solution = solute + solvent. Many concentration problems need a **simple equation**: let the unknown be $x$ and set up an equation from "conservation of solute".

For example, $200$ g of $10\%$ salt water contains $200\times10\%=20$ g of salt; adding $x$ g of salt to raise the concentration to $20\%$ gives, by conservation, $\dfrac{20+x}{200+x}=20\%$, so $x=25$ g.

### Summary

- Ratio $a:b$, value $\dfrac{a}{b}$; fundamental property: scaling both terms by a nonzero number keeps the value; reduce to a lowest-terms integer ratio.
- Proportion $\dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d}$, fundamental property $ad=bc$ (cross-multiplication), used to solve for an unknown.
- Percentage $p\%=\dfrac{p}{100}$; convert with decimals and fractions; percentage points = the **difference** of two percentages.
- Proportional distribution: one share $=\dfrac{S}{m+n}$, each part takes its fraction of the total.
- Three applications: discount (price = original × rate), interest (principal × rate × periods), concentration (solute/solution), often via a simple equation.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Simplify each ratio: (1) $24:36$; (2) $\dfrac23:\dfrac56$; (3) $1.2:0.8$.

> **Solution.** Reduce to a lowest-terms integer ratio.
>
> **(1)** The gcd of $24$ and $36$ is $12$: $24:36=(24\div12):(36\div12)=2:3$.
>
> **(2)** Multiply both terms by $6$ to clear denominators: $\dfrac23:\dfrac56=\left(\dfrac23\times6\right):\left(\dfrac56\times6\right)=4:5$.
>
> **(3)** Multiply both terms by $10$ to clear decimals: $1.2:0.8=12:8=3:2$.

**Example 2.** Solve the proportion $\dfrac{x}{8}=\dfrac{15}{12}$.

> **Solution.** By the fundamental property, the product of the extremes equals the product of the means:
> $$12x = 8\times 15 = 120.$$
> Divide both sides by $12$:
> $$x = \frac{120}{12} = 10.$$
> Check: $\dfrac{10}{8}=\dfrac54$ and $\dfrac{15}{12}=\dfrac54$ are equal, so $x=10$.

**Example 3.** An item priced at $250$ yuan is first sold at $80\%$, then reduced a further $10\%$ off the discounted price. Find the final price and what percentage of the original it represents.

> **Solution.** After the $80\%$ sale the price is
> $$250\times 80\% = 200\ (\text{yuan}).$$
> A further $10\%$ off means paying $90\%$:
> $$200\times(1-10\%) = 200\times 0.9 = 180\ (\text{yuan}).$$
> As a percentage of the original,
> $$\frac{180}{250}\times100\% = 72\%.$$
> So the final price is $180$ yuan, which is $72\%$ of the original. (Note: successive discounts multiply, $80\%\times90\%=72\%$.)

**Example 4.** Split a $600$-yuan bonus among A, B, C in the ratio $2:3:5$. How much does each receive?

> **Solution.** The total shares is $2+3+5=10$, so one share is
> $$\frac{600}{10}=60\ (\text{yuan}).$$
> Therefore
> $$\begin{aligned} A &= 600\times\frac{2}{10}=120\ (\text{yuan}),\\ B &= 600\times\frac{3}{10}=180\ (\text{yuan}),\\ C &= 600\times\frac{5}{10}=300\ (\text{yuan}). \end{aligned}$$
> Check: $120+180+300=600$ and $120:180:300=2:3:5$, correct.

**Example 5.** You have $400$ g of $15\%$ salt water. How much water must be added to dilute it to $10\%$?

> **Solution.** The key is that the **mass of salt (solute) stays fixed**. The salt present is
> $$400\times 15\% = 60\ (\text{g}).$$
> Let $x$ g of water be added; the solution becomes $(400+x)$ g while the salt is still $60$ g. By the definition of concentration,
> $$\frac{60}{400+x}=10\%=0.1.$$
> Cross-multiply: $60 = 0.1\times(400+x)=40+0.1x$, so $0.1x=20$, giving
> $$x = 200\ (\text{g}).$$
> So $200$ g of water is needed. Check: $\dfrac{60}{400+200}=\dfrac{60}{600}=10\%$, correct.

## Exercises

1. Simplify the ratio $45:60$ and state its value.
2. Solve the proportion $\dfrac{6}{x}=\dfrac{9}{15}$.
3. At a school the number of boys is $120\%$ of the number of girls. If there are $300$ girls, find the number of boys and the total enrolment.
4. Cut an $84$-cm wire into two pieces in the ratio $3:4$; find the length of each piece.
5. With principal $5000$ yuan at annual rate $4\%$, simple interest for $3$ years: find the interest and the maturity total.
6. You have $300$ g of $20\%$ sugar water. How much sugar must be added to raise the concentration to $25\%$?

**Answers.**
1) The gcd of $45$ and $60$ is $15$, so $45:60=3:4$, value $\dfrac34$.
2) Cross-multiply: $9x=6\times15=90$, so $x=10$.
3) Boys $=300\times120\%=360$; total $=300+360=660$.
4) Total shares $3+4=7$, one share $84\div7=12$; the pieces are $12\times3=36$ cm and $12\times4=48$ cm.
5) Interest $=5000\times4\%\times3=600$ yuan; total $=5000+600=5600$ yuan.
6) Initial sugar $300\times20\%=60$ g. Let $x$ g be added: $\dfrac{60+x}{300+x}=25\%$, i.e. $60+x=0.25(300+x)=75+0.25x$, so $0.75x=15$, $x=20$ g.
