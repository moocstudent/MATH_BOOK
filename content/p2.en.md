## Core Notes

### Random Variables and the Distribution Function $F(x)$

Let $\Omega$ be the sample space of a random experiment. If to each sample point $\omega$ there corresponds a unique real number $X(\omega)$, then $X=X(\omega)$ is a **random variable**. It quantifies random outcomes and is written with a capital letter $X,Y$, while its values are written with lowercase $x,y$.

The **distribution function** of $X$ is
$$F(x)=P(X\le x),\qquad -\infty<x<\infty.$$
It completely characterizes the distribution of $X$ and has these basic properties:

- **Non-decreasing**: $x_1<x_2\Rightarrow F(x_1)\le F(x_2)$.
- **Bounded and normalized**: $0\le F(x)\le 1$, with $\lim_{x\to-\infty}F(x)=0$ and $\lim_{x\to+\infty}F(x)=1$.
- **Right-continuous**: $F(x+0)=F(x)$.

Conversely, any function with these three properties is the distribution function of some random variable. Interval probabilities are obtained from $F$:
$$P(a<X\le b)=F(b)-F(a),\qquad P(X>a)=1-F(a),\qquad P(X=a)=F(a)-F(a-0).$$
The last identity shows that the **jump height** of $F$ at $a$ is exactly $P(X=a)$. So a discrete $F$ is a step function (jumping at the mass points), while a continuous $F$ is everywhere continuous (no jumps).

### Discrete Distributions (Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson)

If $X$ takes only finitely or countably many values $x_1,x_2,\dots$, it is a **discrete random variable** with **probability mass function**
$$p_k=P(X=x_k),\qquad p_k\ge 0,\quad \sum_k p_k=1.$$
Then $F(x)=\sum_{x_k\le x}p_k$ is a step function. The three most important discrete laws:

- **Bernoulli (0-1) distribution** $X\sim B(1,p)$: $P(X=1)=p,\ P(X=0)=1-p$. The model of a single "success/failure" trial.
- **Binomial distribution** $X\sim B(n,p)$: the number of "successes" in $n$ independent repeated trials,
$$P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{\,n-k},\qquad k=0,1,\dots,n.$$
Here $\binom{n}{k}$ counts which $k$ trials succeed, and $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ is the probability of one specified outcome sequence; the binomial theorem gives $\sum_k P(X=k)=(p+(1-p))^n=1$.
- **Poisson distribution** $X\sim P(\lambda)$ ($\lambda>0$): counts of rare events per unit time/space,
$$P(X=k)=\frac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda},\qquad k=0,1,2,\dots$$
Since $e^\lambda=\sum_k \lambda^k/k!$, the probabilities sum to $1$. When $n$ is large, $p$ small, and $np=\lambda$ moderate, the binomial is approximated by the Poisson (**Poisson's theorem**): $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\approx \dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}$, making Poisson a convenient model for "rare events in large samples".

In addition, the **geometric distribution** $P(X=k)=(1-p)^{k-1}p\ (k=1,2,\dots)$ models the number of trials until the first success and has the discrete memoryless property.

### Continuous Distributions and Density Functions (Uniform, Exponential, Normal)

If there is a non-negative function $f(x)$ with
$$F(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{x}f(t)\,dt\quad\text{for all }x,$$
then $X$ is a **continuous random variable** and $f(x)$ is its **probability density function**. The density satisfies $f(x)\ge 0$ and $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)\,dx=1$; at points of continuity $F'(x)=f(x)$, and
$$P(a<X\le b)=\int_a^b f(x)\,dx,\qquad P(X=a)=0.$$
Note that $f(x)$ is **not a probability** (it may exceed $1$); only its integral over an interval is. Heuristically $f(x)\,dx$ is the probability that $X$ falls in $[x,x+dx]$.

The three most important continuous laws:

- **Uniform distribution** $X\sim U(a,b)$: "equally likely" on $[a,b]$,
$$f(x)=\begin{cases}\dfrac{1}{b-a},& a\le x\le b,\\[4pt] 0,&\text{otherwise}.\end{cases}$$
- **Exponential distribution** $X\sim \mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ ($\lambda>0$), used for lifetimes/waiting times:
$$f(x)=\begin{cases}\lambda e^{-\lambda x},& x\ge 0,\\ 0,& x<0,\end{cases}\qquad F(x)=1-e^{-\lambda x}\ (x\ge0).$$
It is **memoryless**: $P(X>s+t\mid X>s)=P(X>t)$ — having "survived $s$ hours" does not change the chance of "lasting $t$ more hours".
- **Normal (Gaussian) distribution** $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$:
$$f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\,e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}},\qquad -\infty<x<\infty.$$
Here $\mu$ is the location parameter and $\sigma>0$ the scale parameter; the density is "bell-shaped", symmetric about $x=\mu$, with inflection points at $\mu\pm\sigma$.

### Standard Normal and Summary of Distributions

For the normal law, setting $Z=\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$ gives the **standard normal distribution** $Z\sim N(0,1)$, with density $\varphi(z)$ and distribution function $\Phi(z)$. Standardization is the key to computing normal probabilities:
$$P(a<X\le b)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right)-\Phi\!\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma}\right),\qquad \Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z).$$
Symmetry also gives the useful "$3\sigma$ rule": $P(|X-\mu|<\sigma)\approx 0.683$, $P(|X-\mu|<2\sigma)\approx 0.954$, $P(|X-\mu|<3\sigma)\approx 0.997$.

A summary of values and parameters:

| Distribution | Notation | Values | Key quantity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bernoulli | $B(1,p)$ | $0,1$ | $E[X]=p$ |
| Binomial | $B(n,p)$ | $0,\dots,n$ | $E[X]=np$ |
| Poisson | $P(\lambda)$ | $0,1,\dots$ | $E[X]=\lambda$ |
| Uniform | $U(a,b)$ | $[a,b]$ | $E[X]=\frac{a+b}{2}$ |
| Exponential | $\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ | $[0,\infty)$ | $E[X]=\frac{1}{\lambda}$ |
| Normal | $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ | $\mathbb R$ | $E[X]=\mu$ |

### Distribution of a Function $Y=g(X)$

Given the distribution of $X$, find the distribution of $Y=g(X)$.

- **Discrete case**: for each value $y$ of $Y$, sum the probabilities over all $x_k$ with $g(x_k)=y$: $P(Y=y)=\sum_{g(x_k)=y}P(X=x_k)$.
- **Continuous case — distribution-function method** (general): first compute $F_Y(y)=P(g(X)\le y)=\int_{\{x:\,g(x)\le y\}}f_X(x)\,dx$, then differentiate to get the density. For non-monotone $g$ this is the only option; the key is writing the set $\{x:g(x)\le y\}$ correctly.
- **Continuous case — formula method**: if $y=g(x)$ is monotone and differentiable on the range of $X$ with differentiable inverse $x=h(y)$, then
$$f_Y(y)=f_X\big(h(y)\big)\,\big|h'(y)\big|.$$

**Key derivation (formula method).** If $g$ is strictly increasing, $F_Y(y)=P(g(X)\le y)=P(X\le h(y))=F_X(h(y))$; differentiating in $y$ with the chain rule gives $f_Y(y)=f_X(h(y))h'(y)$. A strictly decreasing $g$ reverses the direction, and the absolute value yields the general formula. An important fact: if $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, then a linear transform $Y=aX+b\ (a\ne 0)$ is again normal, $Y\sim N(a\mu+b,\,a^2\sigma^2)$.

### Summary

- Distribution function $F(x)=P(X\le x)$: non-decreasing, right-continuous, $F(-\infty)=0,F(+\infty)=1$; jump height $=P(X=a)$.
- Three discrete laws: $B(n,p)$ with $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$, $P(\lambda)$ with $\dfrac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}$, $B(1,p)$; Poisson's theorem $B(n,p)\approx P(np)$.
- Three continuous laws: $U(a,b)$, $\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ (memoryless), $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$.
- Density facts: $f\ge0$, $\int f=1$, $P(a<X\le b)=\int_a^b f$, $P(X=a)=0$.
- Normal standardization $Z=\dfrac{X-\mu}{\sigma}$, $P(a<X\le b)=\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\big)-\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{a-\mu}{\sigma}\big)$, $\Phi(-z)=1-\Phi(z)$.
- Function distributions: discrete summing; continuous via distribution-function or formula method $f_Y(y)=f_X(h(y))|h'(y)|$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Let $X\sim B(5,0.2)$. Find $P(X=2)$ and $P(X\ge 1)$.

> **Solution.** By the binomial formula,
> $$P(X=2)=\binom{5}{2}(0.2)^2(0.8)^3=10\cdot 0.04\cdot 0.512=0.2048.$$
> Use the complement for $P(X\ge 1)$:
> $$P(X\ge 1)=1-P(X=0)=1-(0.8)^5=1-0.32768=0.67232.$$

**Example 2.** Let $X\sim N(160,\,25)$ (units: cm), i.e. $\mu=160,\ \sigma=5$. Find $P(155<X<170)$. (Use $\Phi(1)=0.8413,\ \Phi(2)=0.9772$.)

> **Solution.** Standardize $Z=\dfrac{X-160}{5}\sim N(0,1)$:
> $$P(155<X<170)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{170-160}{5}\right)-\Phi\!\left(\frac{155-160}{5}\right)=\Phi(2)-\Phi(-1).$$
> Since $\Phi(-1)=1-\Phi(1)=1-0.8413=0.1587$,
> $$P=0.9772-0.1587=0.8185.$$

**Example 3.** Let $X\sim U(0,1)$ and set $Y=-2\ln X$. Find the density $f_Y(y)$.

> **Solution.** Use the distribution-function method. For $y\le 0$, $F_Y(y)=0$. For $y>0$, $Y=-2\ln X\le y$ gives $X\ge e^{-y/2}$, and since $X\sim U(0,1)$ with $0<e^{-y/2}<1$,
> $$F_Y(y)=P\!\left(X\ge e^{-y/2}\right)=1-e^{-y/2}.$$
> Differentiating,
> $$f_Y(y)=\begin{cases}\dfrac12 e^{-y/2},& y>0,\\[4pt]0,&y\le 0,\end{cases}$$
> i.e. $Y\sim \mathrm{Exp}\!\left(\tfrac12\right)$.

**Example 4.** (From density to distribution function and probability) Let $X$ have density
$$f(x)=\begin{cases}kx^2,& 0\le x\le 3,\\ 0,&\text{otherwise}.\end{cases}$$
Find the constant $k$, the distribution function $F(x)$, and $P(1<X\le 2)$.

> **Solution.** Normalization $\int_0^3 kx^2\,dx=k\cdot\dfrac{x^3}{3}\Big|_0^3=9k=1$ gives $k=\dfrac19$.
> Integrate piecewise for $F$: for $0\le x\le 3$,
> $$F(x)=\int_0^x \frac19 t^2\,dt=\frac{x^3}{27};$$
> so $F(x)=0\ (x<0)$, $F(x)=\dfrac{x^3}{27}\ (0\le x\le 3)$, $F(x)=1\ (x>3)$. Therefore
> $$P(1<X\le 2)=F(2)-F(1)=\frac{8}{27}-\frac{1}{27}=\frac{7}{27}\approx 0.259.$$

**Example 5.** (Formula method, linear and exponential transforms) Let $X\sim N(0,1)$. (a) Find the density of $Y=3X-2$; (b) use the formula method to find the density of $W=e^{X}$ (the log-normal distribution).

> **Solution.** (a) The linear transform $Y=3X-2$ is normal by the linearity rule, $Y\sim N(-2,\,9)$, i.e.
> $$f_Y(y)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\cdot 3}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(y+2)^2}{18}\right).$$
> (b) $w=e^{x}$ is strictly increasing on $\mathbb R$ with inverse $x=h(w)=\ln w\ (w>0)$ and $h'(w)=\dfrac1w$. By the formula method,
> $$f_W(w)=f_X(\ln w)\,\Big|\frac1w\Big|=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,w}\,e^{-\frac{(\ln w)^2}{2}},\qquad w>0,$$
> with $f_W(w)=0$ for $w\le 0$. This is the standard **log-normal** density.

## Exercises

1. A discrete random variable $X$ has mass function $P(X=k)=ca^{k}\ (k=0,1,2,\dots)$ with $0<a<1$. Find the constant $c$.
2. The number of calls per minute at a switchboard is $X\sim P(3)$. Find the probability of "exactly $2$ calls in a minute" and of "at least $1$ call".
3. Let $X$ have density $f(x)=\begin{cases}cx,&0\le x\le 2,\\0,&\text{otherwise}.\end{cases}$ Find $c$, the distribution function $F(x)$, and $P(X>1)$.
4. Let $X\sim N(0,1)$. Find the density of $Y=X^2$.
5. Let $X\sim \mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ with mean lifetime $\dfrac1\lambda=500$ hours. Find the probability the component works for more than $600$ hours; and verify $P(X>1100\mid X>500)=P(X>600)$.
6. A batch of bolts has length $X\sim N(10,\,0.04)$ (cm), i.e. $\sigma=0.2$. A bolt passes if its length lies in $[9.6,10.4]$. Find the pass rate. (Use $\Phi(2)=0.9772$.)

**Answers.**
1) From $\sum_{k\ge0}ca^k=\dfrac{c}{1-a}=1$ we get $c=1-a$ (geometric distribution).
2) $P(X=2)=\dfrac{3^2}{2!}e^{-3}=4.5e^{-3}\approx 0.224$; $P(X\ge1)=1-e^{-3}\approx 0.950$.
3) From $\int_0^2 cx\,dx=2c=1$, $c=\dfrac12$; $F(x)=0\ (x<0)$, $F(x)=\dfrac{x^2}{4}\ (0\le x\le2)$, $F(x)=1\ (x>2)$; $P(X>1)=1-F(1)=\dfrac34$.
4) For $y>0$, $F_Y(y)=P(-\sqrt y\le X\le\sqrt y)=2\Phi(\sqrt y)-1$; differentiating gives $f_Y(y)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}}\,e^{-y/2}\ (y>0)$, else $0$ (the $\chi^2(1)$ distribution).
5) $\lambda=\dfrac{1}{500}$, so $P(X>600)=e^{-600/500}=e^{-1.2}\approx 0.301$; by memorylessness $P(X>1100\mid X>500)=P(X>600)=e^{-1.2}\approx 0.301$, which are equal.
6) Standardize $Z=\dfrac{X-10}{0.2}$: $P(9.6\le X\le 10.4)=\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{0.4}{0.2}\big)-\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{-0.4}{0.2}\big)=\Phi(2)-\Phi(-2)=2\Phi(2)-1=2(0.9772)-1=0.9544$, a pass rate of about $95.4\%$.
