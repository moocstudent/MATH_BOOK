## Core Notes

### Convergence of Sequences of Random Variables (in Probability)

The laws of large numbers and the central limit theorem study the limiting behavior of a sequence $X_1,X_2,\dots$, so we first make precise what "convergence" means.

Let $\{Y_n\}$ be a sequence of random variables and $a$ a constant. If for every $\varepsilon>0$,
$$\lim_{n\to\infty}P\big(|Y_n-a|\ge \varepsilon\big)=0,$$
then $Y_n$ **converges in probability** to $a$, written $Y_n\xrightarrow{\ P\ } a$. Intuitively, for large $n$ the chance that $Y_n$ deviates from $a$ is arbitrarily small (though occasional large deviations are not ruled out).

A weaker mode is **convergence in distribution**: if the distribution functions satisfy $F_n(x)\to F(x)$ at every continuity point of $F$, we write $Y_n\xrightarrow{\ d\ } Y$. The central limit theorem is exactly a statement of convergence in distribution. In general, convergence in probability implies convergence in distribution; the converse also holds when the limit is a constant. This distinction is the key to telling apart the LLN (convergence to a constant) and the CLT (convergence to a distribution) below.

### Chebyshev's and Khinchin's Laws of Large Numbers

A **law of large numbers** asserts that the arithmetic mean of many random variables stabilizes near its expectation. Write $\bar X_n=\dfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i$.

**Chebyshev's LLN.** Let $X_1,X_2,\dots$ be pairwise uncorrelated with existing means $E[X_i]=\mu_i$ and uniformly bounded variances $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)\le C$. Then
$$\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i-\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mu_i\ \xrightarrow{\ P\ }\ 0.$$
In particular, if the $X_i$ share a common mean $\mu$ and have bounded variance, then $\bar X_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$.

**Proof idea.** By (un)correlatedness, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\dfrac{1}{n^2}\sum_i\operatorname{Var}(X_i)\le \dfrac{C}{n}$; applying Chebyshev's inequality to $\bar X_n$,
$$P\big(|\bar X_n-E[\bar X_n]|\ge\varepsilon\big)\le \frac{\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)}{\varepsilon^2}\le\frac{C}{n\varepsilon^2}\to 0.$$
Intuitively, averaging "smooths out" individual fluctuations: the variance decays to $0$ at rate $1/n$, so $\bar X_n$ concentrates ever more tightly.

**Khinchin's LLN.** Let $X_1,X_2,\dots$ be **independent and identically distributed** with existing mean $E[X_i]=\mu$ (no variance assumption needed). Then
$$\bar X_n=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i\ \xrightarrow{\ P\ }\ \mu.$$
This is the theoretical basis for estimating a population mean by the sample mean, and it underlies the consistency of methods such as Monte Carlo integration.

### Bernoulli's LLN (Frequency $\to$ Probability)

**Bernoulli's LLN** is a special case of Chebyshev's law that rigorously expresses "frequency stabilizes at probability".

Suppose event $A$ occurs $n_A$ times in $n$ independent repeated trials, with probability $p$ each time. Then the **relative frequency** $\dfrac{n_A}{n}$ converges in probability to $p$: for every $\varepsilon>0$,
$$\lim_{n\to\infty}P\!\left(\left|\frac{n_A}{n}-p\right|\ge\varepsilon\right)=0.$$

**Remark.** Let $X_i$ be the indicator of trial $i$ ($1$ if $A$ occurs, else $0$); then $X_i\sim B(1,p)$ i.i.d., $\dfrac{n_A}{n}=\bar X_n$, and $E[X_i]=p$. The LLN yields the result. This explains theoretically why an unknown probability can be estimated by the frequency over many repetitions — the mathematical basis of the "frequency interpretation" of probability.

### The Central Limit Theorem (i.i.d., Lindeberg–Lévy)

The LLN says the mean converges to a constant; the **central limit theorem (CLT)** further describes the **shape of its fluctuations** — a sum of many independent random effects is approximately normal.

**Lindeberg–Lévy CLT.** Let $X_1,X_2,\dots$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2\in(0,\infty)$. With $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i$, the standardized sum converges in distribution to the standard normal:
$$Z_n=\frac{S_n-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\ \xrightarrow{\ d\ }\ N(0,1),$$
i.e. for every real $x$,
$$\lim_{n\to\infty}P\!\left(\frac{S_n-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt n}\le x\right)=\Phi(x).$$
In terms of the sample mean, $\dfrac{\bar X_n-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$. The striking point: whatever the original distribution of the $X_i$ (provided mean and variance are finite), the standardized sum tends to the same normal law. **Proof sketch** (MGF method): the MGF of the standardized $\tfrac{X_i-\mu}{\sigma}$ expands near $0$ as $1+\tfrac{t^2}{2}+o(t^2)$, so the MGF of $Z_n$ is $\big(1+\tfrac{t^2}{2n}+o(\tfrac1n)\big)^n\to e^{t^2/2}$, which is exactly the MGF of $N(0,1)$; uniqueness gives the convergence in distribution.

Practical approximation: for large $n$,
$$S_n\ \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}\ N\big(n\mu,\ n\sigma^2\big),\qquad \bar X_n\ \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}\ N\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).$$
Note: the LLN tells us $\bar X_n\to\mu$ (a degenerate limit); the CLT magnifies the fluctuations by the scale $\sqrt n$ to obtain a non-degenerate normal limit — the two are complementary.

### Application: Normal Approximation (Binomial Approximation)

A binomial $X\sim B(n,p)$ is a sum of $n$ independent $B(1,p)$ variables, so for large $n$ it admits a normal approximation (**De Moivre–Laplace theorem**):
$$\frac{X-np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\ \xrightarrow{\ d\ }\ N(0,1).$$
Hence
$$P(a\le X\le b)\approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{b-np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)-\Phi\!\left(\frac{a-np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right).$$

Because the binomial is **discrete** while the normal is **continuous**, one usually applies a **continuity correction**: replace the upper limit $b$ by $b+0.5$ and the lower limit $a$ by $a-0.5$, which notably improves accuracy. As a rule of thumb the normal approximation is good when $np\ge 5$ and $n(1-p)\ge 5$; if $p$ is small with moderate $np$, the Poisson approximation is preferable. These two approximations correspond respectively to the CLT and Poisson's theorem; choose by whether $p$ is near $0/1$.

### Summary

- Two modes of convergence: in probability $Y_n\xrightarrow{P}a$ ($P(|Y_n-a|\ge\varepsilon)\to0$); in distribution $F_n\to F$ (used for the CLT).
- LLN: Khinchin (i.i.d., existing mean) gives $\bar X_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$; Chebyshev (uncorrelated, bounded variance) gives the same; Bernoulli (frequency $\to$ probability) is a special case.
- Key mechanism: $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\to0$ + Chebyshev's inequality $\Rightarrow$ LLN.
- CLT: $\dfrac{S_n-n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt n}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$; approximations $S_n\sim N(n\mu,n\sigma^2)$, $\bar X_n\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)$.
- De Moivre–Laplace: $B(n,p)\approx N(np,np(1-p))$ for large $n$; use a continuity correction ($\pm0.5$) for the discrete count.
- Choice: normal approximation when $p$ is not extreme; Poisson approximation when $p$ small with moderate $np$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** An insurer has $10000$ similar policyholders, each filing a claim within a year with probability $p=0.006$, independently. Let $X$ be the number of claims; use the normal approximation to estimate $P(X\le 70)$. (Use $\Phi(1.30)=0.9032$.)

> **Solution.** $X\sim B(10000,0.006)$, so
> $$np=60,\qquad np(1-p)=60\times 0.994=59.64,\qquad \sqrt{59.64}\approx 7.72.$$
> With continuity correction, take the upper limit $70.5$:
> $$P(X\le 70)\approx \Phi\!\left(\frac{70.5-60}{7.72}\right)=\Phi(1.36)\approx 0.913.$$
> (Without correction, $\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{70-60}{7.72}\big)=\Phi(1.30)=0.9032$, which is close.)

**Example 2.** A quantity is measured $100$ times independently; the errors $X_i$ are i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=0$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=0.25$. Find the approximate probability that the average error $\bar X=\dfrac1{100}\sum X_i$ satisfies $|\bar X|<0.1$. (Use $\Phi(2)=0.9772$.)

> **Solution.** By the CLT, $\bar X\overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}N\!\left(0,\dfrac{0.25}{100}\right)=N(0,0.0025)$, with standard deviation $\sigma_{\bar X}=0.05$.
> $$P(|\bar X|<0.1)=P\!\left(\left|\frac{\bar X-0}{0.05}\right|<\frac{0.1}{0.05}\right)=P(|Z|<2)=2\Phi(2)-1.$$
> Thus
> $$P=2\times 0.9772-1=0.9544.$$

**Example 3.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_{n}$ be i.i.d. $U(0,1)$ with $E[X_i]=\tfrac12$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\tfrac1{12}$. How large must $n$ be so that Chebyshev's inequality guarantees $P\big(|\bar X_n-\tfrac12|<0.05\big)\ge 0.95$?

> **Solution.** $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\dfrac{1}{12n}$; take $\varepsilon=0.05$. By Chebyshev's inequality,
> $$P\big(|\bar X_n-\tfrac12|<0.05\big)\ge 1-\frac{\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)}{\varepsilon^2}=1-\frac{1}{12n\cdot 0.0025}.$$
> Requiring this lower bound $\ge 0.95$ means $\dfrac{1}{0.03n}\le 0.05$, so
> $$n\ge \frac{1}{0.03\times 0.05}=\frac{1}{0.0015}\approx 666.7,\quad\text{hence } n\ge 667.$$
> (Note: Chebyshev gives a conservative bound; using the CLT instead would require a far smaller $n$.)

**Example 4.** (CLT for a sum rather than a mean) Parts have i.i.d. weights $X_i$ with $E[X_i]=50$ g and $\sigma=5$ g. A crate holds $100$ parts; let the crate weight be $S=\sum_{i=1}^{100}X_i$. Estimate $P(S>5100)$. (Use $\Phi(2)=0.9772$.)

> **Solution.** By the CLT, $S\overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}N\big(100\cdot50,\ 100\cdot5^2\big)=N(5000,\,2500)$, so the standard deviation is $\sigma_S=\sqrt{2500}=50$. Standardize:
> $$P(S>5100)=P\!\left(\frac{S-5000}{50}>\frac{5100-5000}{50}\right)=P(Z>2)=1-\Phi(2).$$
> Hence
> $$P=1-0.9772=0.0228.$$
> So a crate exceeds $5100$ g with probability about $2.3\%$. Here the CLT is applied directly to the **sum** $S_n$, without converting to a mean.

**Example 5.** (Sample size for a target precision, via the CLT) Estimate an event probability $p$ by the frequency $\dfrac{n_A}{n}$. How large must $n$ be so that $P\big(|\tfrac{n_A}{n}-p|<0.01\big)\ge 0.95$? (For unknown $p$ take the worst case $p(1-p)\le\tfrac14$; use $\Phi(1.96)=0.975$.)

> **Solution.** By De Moivre–Laplace, $\dfrac{n_A}{n}\overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}N\!\left(p,\dfrac{p(1-p)}{n}\right)$ with standard deviation $\sigma=\sqrt{\tfrac{p(1-p)}{n}}$. The target event is $\Big|\dfrac{n_A/n-p}{\sigma}\Big|<\dfrac{0.01}{\sigma}$, so
> $$P\!\left(\Big|\frac{n_A}{n}-p\Big|<0.01\right)\approx 2\Phi\!\left(\frac{0.01}{\sigma}\right)-1\ge 0.95\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{0.01}{\sigma}\ge 1.96.$$
> That is $0.01\ge 1.96\sqrt{\tfrac{p(1-p)}{n}}$, giving
> $$n\ge \frac{1.96^2\,p(1-p)}{0.01^2}\le \frac{1.96^2\cdot \tfrac14}{0.0001}=\frac{0.9604}{0.0001}=9604.$$
> So $n\ge 9604$ suffices for all $p$. (Compared with the Chebyshev bound of Example 3, the CLT needs an order of magnitude fewer samples.)

## Exercises

1. In words: how do Khinchin's LLN and the Lindeberg–Lévy CLT differ in their hypotheses and their conclusions?
2. Toss a fair coin $400$ times. Use the normal approximation to estimate the probability that the number of heads $X$ lies between $190$ and $210$. (Use $\Phi(1)=0.8413$; apply a continuity correction.)
3. Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$. Write down the mean and variance of $\bar X_n$, and explain how the variance as $n\to\infty$ "supports" the law of large numbers.
4. A product has defect rate $p=0.1$; sample $n=100$ items and let $X$ be the number defective. Use the normal approximation (with continuity correction) to estimate $P(X\le 15)$. (Use $\Phi(1.83)=0.9664$.)
5. A website's hits per second $X_i$ are i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=20$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=20$ (approximately Poisson). Estimate the probability that the total over $60$ seconds, $S=\sum_{i=1}^{60}X_i$, exceeds $1260$. (Use $\Phi(1.73)=0.9582$.)
6. Let $X_1,\dots,X_{n}$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2=4$. Use the CLT to estimate the smallest $n$ for which $P\big(|\bar X_n-\mu|<0.5\big)\ge 0.95$. (Use $\Phi(1.96)=0.975$.)

**Answers.**
1) LLN: i.i.d. with existing mean; conclusion is that the sample mean converges **in probability** to the constant $\mu$ (it gives only the limit value). CLT: additionally needs finite variance; conclusion is that the standardized sum converges **in distribution** to $N(0,1)$ (describing the fluctuation of the mean about $\mu$).
2) $X\sim B(400,0.5)$, $np=200$, $\sqrt{np(1-p)}=\sqrt{100}=10$. With correction, $P(190\le X\le210)\approx\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{210.5-200}{10}\big)-\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{189.5-200}{10}\big)=2\Phi(1.05)-1\approx 2(0.8531)-1\approx 0.706$; a rough estimate with $\Phi(1)$ gives $2\Phi(1)-1\approx 0.683$.
3) $E[\bar X_n]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X_n)=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$; a variance tending to $0$ means $\bar X_n$ concentrates ever more tightly at $\mu$, hence $\bar X_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$.
4) $X\sim B(100,0.1)$, $np=10$, $\sqrt{np(1-p)}=\sqrt{9}=3$; with correction, $P(X\le15)\approx\Phi\!\big(\tfrac{15.5-10}{3}\big)=\Phi(1.83)\approx 0.9664$.
5) By the CLT, $S\overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}N(60\cdot20,\,60\cdot20)=N(1200,1200)$, $\sigma_S=\sqrt{1200}\approx 34.64$; $P(S>1260)=P\!\big(Z>\tfrac{1260-1200}{34.64}\big)=P(Z>1.73)=1-\Phi(1.73)\approx 1-0.9582=0.0418$.
6) By the CLT, $\bar X_n\overset{\text{approx}}{\sim}N\!\big(\mu,\tfrac{4}{n}\big)$, $\sigma_{\bar X}=\tfrac{2}{\sqrt n}$; requiring $\dfrac{0.5}{2/\sqrt n}\ge 1.96$ gives $\sqrt n\ge \dfrac{1.96\cdot2}{0.5}=7.84$, so $n\ge 61.5$, hence $n\ge 62$.
