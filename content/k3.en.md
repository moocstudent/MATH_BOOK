## Core Notes

Real problems carry constraints: budgets, capacities, physical feasibility. This chapter studies **constrained optimization**, whose central tools are **Lagrange multipliers** and the **KKT conditions** — turning "optimize subject to constraints" into a solvable system of equations and inequalities — and, via **duality**, giving a lower bound on the optimal value from the other side. We write problems in standard minimization form.

### Equality Constraints and Lagrange Multipliers

Start with equality constraints only:

$$\min_x\ f(x)\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ h_j(x)=0\ (j=1,\dots,p).$$

Introduce **Lagrange multipliers** $\lambda_j$ and form the **Lagrangian**

$$L(x,\lambda)=f(x)+\sum_{j=1}^{p}\lambda_j h_j(x).$$

**Lagrange's theorem.** Under a regularity condition (the $\nabla h_j(x^*)$ are linearly independent), if $x^*$ is a local extremum then there exists $\lambda^*$ with

$$\nabla_x L(x^*,\lambda^*)=\nabla f(x^*)+\sum_j\lambda_j^*\nabla h_j(x^*)=0,\qquad h_j(x^*)=0.$$

Geometric intuition: at an extremum the objective gradient $\nabla f$ must lie in the span of the constraint gradients $\{\nabla h_j\}$ (otherwise one could descend along the constraint surface), i.e. $\nabla f=-\sum_j\lambda_j\nabla h_j$. This determines the $n+p$ unknowns $(x,\lambda)$ from $n+p$ equations.

### Inequality Constraints

A general problem has both inequality and equality constraints, in standard form

$$\min_x\ f(x)\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ g_i(x)\le 0\ (i=1,\dots,m),\ \ h_j(x)=0\ (j=1,\dots,p).$$

Attach a multiplier $\mu_i\ge 0$ to each inequality, giving the generalized Lagrangian

$$L(x,\mu,\lambda)=f(x)+\sum_{i=1}^{m}\mu_i g_i(x)+\sum_{j=1}^{p}\lambda_j h_j(x).$$

The key notion is an **active constraint**: if $g_i(x^*)=0$ the constraint is **active** (pressed against its boundary); otherwise $g_i(x^*)<0$ is **inactive** (slack). Intuitively an inactive constraint does not affect the local optimum, so its multiplier should be zero — exactly the source of complementary slackness below. Note the **sign requirement** $\mu_i\ge 0$ for inequality multipliers, whereas the equality multipliers $\lambda_j$ are free in sign.

### The KKT Conditions

Combining the above gives the first-order necessary conditions for constrained optimization — the **KKT (Karush–Kuhn–Tucker) conditions**. Under suitable regularity, if $x^*$ is locally optimal there exist $\mu^*\ge 0,\lambda^*$ such that:

- **Stationarity:** $\nabla f(x^*)+\sum_i\mu_i^*\nabla g_i(x^*)+\sum_j\lambda_j^*\nabla h_j(x^*)=0$;
- **Primal feasibility:** $g_i(x^*)\le 0,\ \ h_j(x^*)=0$;
- **Dual feasibility:** $\mu_i^*\ge 0$;
- **Complementary slackness:** $\mu_i^*\,g_i(x^*)=0\ (\forall i)$.

**Complementary slackness** $\mu_i^* g_i(x^*)=0$ is central: each constraint is either active ($g_i=0$) or has zero multiplier ($\mu_i=0$) — both cannot be "slack" at once. A common solution strategy is to **enumerate the active set**: guess which constraints are active, set the corresponding $g_i=0$ and the remaining $\mu_i=0$, solve stationarity and feasibility, then check $\mu_i\ge 0$ and $g_i\le 0$. For a **convex** problem (satisfying Slater's condition below), the KKT conditions are **necessary and sufficient**, and any KKT point is globally optimal.

### Lagrangian Duality

Define the **dual function** as the infimum of the Lagrangian over $x$:

$$d(\mu,\lambda)=\inf_x\,L(x,\mu,\lambda).$$

Whether or not the primal is convex, $d$ is always **concave** (a pointwise infimum of affine functions of $(\mu,\lambda)$). **Weak duality:** for any $\mu\ge 0$ and any $\lambda$,

$$d(\mu,\lambda)\le p^*,$$

where $p^*$ is the primal optimal value. So the dual function gives a **lower bound** on $p^*$. Maximizing over all such bounds gives the **dual problem**

$$\max_{\mu\ge 0,\ \lambda}\ d(\mu,\lambda),$$

which is **always a convex problem** (maximizing a concave function over the convex set $\mu\ge 0$), even when the primal is nonconvex — making duality a powerful tool for bounds and algorithms. Writing the dual optimal value $d^*$, we always have $d^*\le p^*$.

### Duality Gap and Strong Duality (Slater)

The difference $p^*-d^*\ge 0$ is the **duality gap**. If $p^*=d^*$ (zero gap), **strong duality** holds and one can recover the primal optimum by solving the (often easier) dual. Strong duality need not hold in general, but for convex problems there is a checkable sufficient condition:

**Slater's condition.** If the primal is convex and there is a **strictly feasible point** (some $\bar x$ with all inequalities strict, $g_i(\bar x)<0$, and the equalities satisfied), then **strong duality holds** and the dual optimum is attained at some $(\mu^*,\lambda^*)$. Then $(x^*,\mu^*,\lambda^*)$ satisfies the KKT conditions, and primal and dual solutions determine each other. Intuition: strong duality means "the best lower bound exactly reaches the optimal value," so duality loses no information.

### Summary

- Equality constraints: $L=f+\sum_j\lambda_j h_j$; at an extremum $\nabla_x L=0,\ h_j=0$ ($\nabla f$ lies in the span of the constraint gradients).
- Inequality constraints: $\mu_i\ge 0$; active $g_i=0$ vs. inactive $g_i<0$.
- KKT: stationarity, primal feasibility ($g_i\le0,h_j=0$), dual feasibility ($\mu_i\ge0$), complementary slackness ($\mu_i g_i=0$); necessary and sufficient under convexity + Slater.
- Dual function $d(\mu,\lambda)=\inf_x L$ is always concave; weak duality $d(\mu,\lambda)\le p^*$; the dual $\max_{\mu\ge0,\lambda}d$ is always convex.
- Duality gap $p^*-d^*\ge0$; Slater (convex + strictly feasible) $\Rightarrow$ strong duality $p^*=d^*$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Use Lagrange multipliers to solve $\min\ x_1^2+x_2^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2=1$.

> **Solution.** $L=x_1^2+x_2^2+\lambda(x_1+x_2-1)$. Stationarity: $\partial_{x_1}L=2x_1+\lambda=0$ and $\partial_{x_2}L=2x_2+\lambda=0$, so $x_1=x_2=-\lambda/2$. Substituting into $x_1+x_2=1$: $-\lambda=1\Rightarrow\lambda=-1$, hence $x^*=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$ with minimum $f^*=\tfrac14+\tfrac14=\tfrac12$. (Geometrically, the closest point on the line $x_1+x_2=1$ to the origin.)

**Example 2.** Solve $\min\ x_1^2+x_2^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2\ge 1$, writing and solving the KKT conditions.

> **Solution.** In standard form $g(x)=1-x_1-x_2\le 0$, with $L=x_1^2+x_2^2+\mu(1-x_1-x_2)$. KKT:
> stationarity $2x_1-\mu=0,\ 2x_2-\mu=0$; dual feasibility $\mu\ge0$; complementary slackness $\mu(1-x_1-x_2)=0$.
> If the constraint is active ($x_1+x_2=1$): the first two give $x_1=x_2=\mu/2$, and substituting gives $\mu=1\ge0$, so $x^*=(\tfrac12,\tfrac12)$, $\mu^*=1$, with $g(x^*)=0\le0$. The minimum is $\tfrac12$. (If instead the constraint were inactive with $\mu=0$, then $x=(0,0)$, but $g(0,0)=1>0$ violates feasibility, so this is rejected.)

**Example 3.** Solve $\min\ x_1^2+x_2^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2\ge 4$ and $x_1\ge 0$; determine which constraints are active.

> **Solution.** Guess $x_1+x_2=4$ is active and $x_1\ge0$ is inactive. As in Example 2, $x_1=x_2=2$, satisfying $x_1=2>0$ (the second constraint is indeed slack, with multiplier $0$), and the multiplier of the first is $\mu_1=4\ge0$. Check: $g_1=4-x_1-x_2=0$, $g_2=-x_1=-2\le0$, with complementary slackness and dual feasibility holding. So $x^*=(2,2)$, minimum $8$, and only the first constraint is active.

**Example 4.** Find the dual function and dual problem of $\min\ x_1^2+x_2^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2=1$, and verify strong duality.

> **Solution.** $L=x_1^2+x_2^2+\lambda(x_1+x_2-1)$. Minimizing over $x$: $\partial_{x_i}L=2x_i+\lambda=0\Rightarrow x_i=-\lambda/2$. Back-substituting,
> $$d(\lambda)=2\cdot\frac{\lambda^2}{4}+\lambda\Big(2\cdot(-\tfrac{\lambda}{2})-1\Big)=\frac{\lambda^2}{2}-\lambda^2-\lambda=-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}-\lambda.$$
> The dual is $\max_\lambda\big(-\tfrac{\lambda^2}{2}-\lambda\big)$: setting the derivative $-\lambda-1=0\Rightarrow\lambda^*=-1$, giving $d^*=-\tfrac12+1=\tfrac12$. This equals $p^*=\tfrac12$ from Example 1, so **strong duality holds** (convex objective, affine constraint, so Slater is automatic).

**Example 5.** Solve $\min\ x$ subject to $x\ge 1$ (i.e. $g(x)=1-x\le0$) via KKT, and state complementary slackness.

> **Solution.** $L=x+\mu(1-x)$, stationarity $1-\mu=0\Rightarrow\mu=1\ge0$. Complementary slackness $\mu(1-x)=0$: since $\mu=1\ne0$ we must have $1-x=0$, i.e. $x^*=1$, so the constraint is **active**. Feasibility $g(1)=0\le0$ and dual feasibility $\mu=1\ge0$ both hold, giving optimum $x^*=1$.

## Exercises

1. Use Lagrange multipliers to solve $\min\ x_1^2+4x_2^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2=3$; give $x^*$, $\lambda^*$, and the minimum.
2. Write all KKT conditions (four groups) for the general problem $\min f$ s.t. $g_i\le0,\ h_j=0$, and identify which group encodes "active vs. inactive."
3. Solve $\min\ (x_1-2)^2+(x_2-2)^2$ subject to $x_1+x_2\le 2$; decide whether the constraint is active and find $x^*$.
4. Solve $\max\ x_1x_2$ subject to $x_1+x_2=10$ (hint: convert to a $\min$) via Lagrange multipliers.
5. Explain the meaning of weak duality $d(\mu,\lambda)\le p^*$; why is the dual problem always convex (even when the primal is not)?
6. State Slater's condition and explain what it guarantees for a convex problem (strong duality? KKT sufficiency?).

**Answers.** 1) $L=x_1^2+4x_2^2+\lambda(x_1+x_2-3)$; $2x_1+\lambda=0,\ 8x_2+\lambda=0\Rightarrow x_1=-\lambda/2,\ x_2=-\lambda/8$, substituting $-\lambda/2-\lambda/8=3\Rightarrow -5\lambda/8=3\Rightarrow\lambda=-24/5$; so $x_1=12/5,\ x_2=3/5$, minimum $(12/5)^2+4(3/5)^2=144/25+36/25=180/25=36/5=7.2$. 2) Stationarity $\nabla f+\sum\mu_i\nabla g_i+\sum\lambda_j\nabla h_j=0$; primal feasibility $g_i\le0,h_j=0$; dual feasibility $\mu_i\ge0$; complementary slackness $\mu_i g_i=0$ — **complementary slackness** encodes active ($g_i=0$) vs. inactive ($\mu_i=0$). 3) The unconstrained minimum is at $(2,2)$, where $g=x_1+x_2-2=2>0$ is infeasible, so the constraint is **active**, $x_1+x_2=2$. By symmetry $x_1=x_2=1$, so $x^*=(1,1)$ with multiplier $\mu=2\ge0$ and minimum $(1-2)^2+(1-2)^2=2$. 4) Convert to $\min\ -x_1x_2$; $L=-x_1x_2+\lambda(x_1+x_2-10)$, $-x_2+\lambda=0,\ -x_1+\lambda=0\Rightarrow x_1=x_2=\lambda$, substituting $2\lambda=10\Rightarrow\lambda=5$, so $x^*=(5,5)$ with maximum $x_1x_2=25$. 5) Weak duality says the dual function at any feasible multipliers is a **lower bound** on the primal optimum $p^*$; the dual maximizes $d=\inf_x L$, an infimum of affine functions, hence **always concave**, over the convex set $\mu\ge0$, so the dual is always a convex problem regardless of the primal. 6) Slater: the primal is convex and a strictly feasible point exists ($g_i(\bar x)<0$, equalities satisfied); it guarantees **strong duality** $p^*=d^*$ and makes the KKT conditions **sufficient (and necessary)** for global optimality.
