## Core Notes

### Population and Sample

The job of mathematical statistics is to **infer the whole from a part**: starting from observable data, draw conclusions about the laws governing a random phenomenon.

The totality of objects under study is the **population**, and a numerical attribute of each individual is described by a random variable $X$; the distribution of $X$ is the **population distribution**. For instance, the lifetimes of a batch of bulbs form a population, with $X$ following some unknown distribution.

To learn about the population we draw $n$ individuals "at random", obtaining $X_1,X_2,\dots,X_n$, called a **sample** of size $n$. The most common is a **simple random sample**, which requires:

- **Independence**: $X_1,\dots,X_n$ are mutually independent;
- **Identical distribution**: each $X_i$ has the same distribution as the population $X$.

Thus a simple random sample is a collection of **independent and identically distributed** (i.i.d.) random variables. Its joint distribution (or joint density) factors into the marginals: if the population density is $f(x)$, then
$$f(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i).$$
Once the sample is realized, the concrete numbers $x_1,\dots,x_n$ are the **sample values**. The word "sample" denotes both the random vector and its realization; tell them apart from context.

### Statistics and the Empirical Distribution

A function $T=T(X_1,\dots,X_n)$ of the sample alone that **contains no unknown parameter** is a **statistic**. A statistic is itself a random variable, and its distribution is called a **sampling distribution**. Common statistics include:

the **sample mean** $\displaystyle \bar X=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i$;

the **sample variance** $\displaystyle S^2=\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(X_i-\bar X)^2$, whose square root $S$ is the **sample standard deviation**;

the **sample $k$-th moment** $\displaystyle A_k=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i^{k}$.

Note that $S^2$ divides by $n-1$, not $n$; this makes it an **unbiased estimator** of the population variance (next chapter). If the population mean is $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, then always
$$E[\bar X]=\mu,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\frac{\sigma^2}{n},\qquad E[S^2]=\sigma^2.$$
The first two follow from independence; the third is the reason for the choice $n-1$.

Sorting the sample values as $x_{(1)}\le\dots\le x_{(n)}$, define the **empirical distribution function**
$$F_n(x)=\frac1n\#\{i: x_i\le x\}=\frac{\text{number of sample points} \le x}{n}.$$
$F_n(x)$ estimates the population distribution function $F(x)$; the Glivenko theorem states that $F_n\to F$ uniformly as $n\to\infty$, the cornerstone of inferring a population from a sample.

### The χ² Distribution

Interval estimation and hypothesis testing rely on three distributions derived from a normal population. We start with the $\chi^2$ distribution.

**Definition.** If $X_1,\dots,X_n$ are independent and each follows the standard normal $N(0,1)$, then
$$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i^{2}$$
follows the **$\chi^2$ distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom**, written $\chi^2\sim\chi^2(n)$. It takes only nonnegative values and has a right-skewed, single-peaked density.

**Moments.** If $\chi^2\sim\chi^2(n)$, then
$$E[\chi^2]=n,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(\chi^2)=2n.$$

**Additivity.** If $Y_1\sim\chi^2(m)$ and $Y_2\sim\chi^2(n)$ are independent, then
$$Y_1+Y_2\sim\chi^2(m+n).$$
The sum of independent $\chi^2$ variables is again $\chi^2$, with degrees of freedom adding; this is immediate from the definition (a sum of squared standard normals).

Write $\chi^2_\alpha(n)$ for the **upper $\alpha$ quantile** satisfying $P\big(\chi^2(n)>\chi^2_\alpha(n)\big)=\alpha$, obtained from a table.

### The t and F Distributions

**The t distribution.** If $X\sim N(0,1)$ and $Y\sim\chi^2(n)$ are independent, then
$$t=\frac{X}{\sqrt{Y/n}}$$
follows the **$t$ distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom**, $t\sim t(n)$. Its density is symmetric about $0$, shaped like a normal but with heavier tails; as $n\to\infty$, $t(n)$ tends to $N(0,1)$. With upper $\alpha$ quantile $t_\alpha(n)$, symmetry gives $t_{1-\alpha}(n)=-t_\alpha(n)$.

**The F distribution.** If $U\sim\chi^2(m)$ and $V\sim\chi^2(n)$ are independent, then
$$F=\frac{U/m}{V/n}$$
follows the **$F$ distribution with degrees of freedom $(m,n)$**, $F\sim F(m,n)$. It takes only positive values. Two useful facts:
$$\frac1F\sim F(n,m),\qquad F_{1-\alpha}(m,n)=\frac{1}{F_\alpha(n,m)}.$$
Also $t$ and $F$ are linked: if $t\sim t(n)$, then $t^2\sim F(1,n)$.

### Sampling Distributions for a Normal Population

Let the population be $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with simple random sample $X_1,\dots,X_n$. The **sampling theorems** below hold.

**(1) Sample mean.** Since a linear combination of normals is normal,
$$\bar X\sim N\!\left(\mu,\ \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right),\qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \frac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\sim N(0,1).$$

**(2) Sample variance.**
$$\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2(n-1),$$
and $\bar X$ and $S^2$ are **independent**. The degrees of freedom are $n-1$: because $\sum(X_i-\bar X)=0$, only $n-1$ of the $n$ deviations are "free".

**(3) Studentization (unknown variance).** Replacing $\sigma$ in (1) by $S$, by the independence in (1)–(2) and the definition of $t$,
$$\frac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t(n-1).$$

**(4) Ratio for two normal populations.** For two independent samples from $N(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)$ and $N(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)$ with sample variances $S_1^2,S_2^2$,
$$\frac{S_1^2/\sigma_1^2}{S_2^2/\sigma_2^2}\sim F(n_1-1,\,n_2-1).$$
These four results are the starting point for every normal-population inference in the next two chapters.

### Summary

- Simple random sample = i.i.d.; joint density $\prod f(x_i)$.
- A statistic contains no unknown parameter; $\bar X=\frac1n\sum X_i$, $S^2=\frac1{n-1}\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2$.
- Basic identities: $E[\bar X]=\mu$, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2/n$, $E[S^2]=\sigma^2$.
- $\chi^2(n)=\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2$ ($X_i$ i.i.d. $N(0,1)$); $E=n$, $\operatorname{Var}=2n$; additivity adds the degrees of freedom.
- $t(n)=\dfrac{N(0,1)}{\sqrt{\chi^2(n)/n}}$ (symmetric, heavy-tailed); $F(m,n)=\dfrac{\chi^2(m)/m}{\chi^2(n)/n}$, $1/F\sim F(n,m)$.
- Normal population: $\bar X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)$; $\dfrac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2(n-1)$; $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t(n-1)$; $\bar X\perp S^2$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be a sample from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. Find the distribution of $\bar X$; then with $\mu=10,\ \sigma^2=4,\ n=16$ find $P(\bar X>11)$. (Use $z_{0.025}=1.96$, $\Phi(2)=0.9772$.)

> **Solution.** By the sampling theorem, $\bar X\sim N\!\big(\mu,\sigma^2/n\big)$. Substituting,
> $$\bar X\sim N\!\left(10,\ \frac{4}{16}\right)=N(10,\,0.25),\qquad \sigma_{\bar X}=0.5.$$
> Standardize:
> $$P(\bar X>11)=P\!\left(\frac{\bar X-10}{0.5}>\frac{11-10}{0.5}\right)=P(Z>2)=1-\Phi(2).$$
> Hence $P(\bar X>11)=1-0.9772=0.0228$.

**Example 2.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_5$ be independent $N(0,1)$. Identify the distribution of each statistic:
(a) $Y=\sum_{i=1}^{5}X_i^2$; (b) $T=\dfrac{X_1}{\sqrt{(X_2^2+X_3^2+X_4^2+X_5^2)/4}}$.

> **Solution.** (a) By the $\chi^2$ definition, a sum of $5$ independent squared standard normals has $5$ degrees of freedom:
> $$Y=\sum_{i=1}^{5}X_i^2\sim\chi^2(5).$$
> (b) The numerator $X_1\sim N(0,1)$; inside the root, $X_2^2+X_3^2+X_4^2+X_5^2\sim\chi^2(4)$, independent of $X_1$. By the definition $\dfrac{N(0,1)}{\sqrt{\chi^2(4)/4}}$,
> $$T\sim t(4).$$

**Example 3.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ come from a population $X$ with $E[X]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\sigma^2$ (not necessarily normal). Prove $E[S^2]=\sigma^2$.

> **Solution.** Use the identity $\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2=\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2-n\bar X^2$ and take expectations.
> For a single term: $E[X_i^2]=\operatorname{Var}(X_i)+(E[X_i])^2=\sigma^2+\mu^2$, so $E\big[\sum X_i^2\big]=n(\sigma^2+\mu^2)$.
> For $\bar X$: $E[\bar X^2]=\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)+(E[\bar X])^2=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}+\mu^2$, so $E[n\bar X^2]=\sigma^2+n\mu^2$.
> Subtracting,
> $$E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar X)^2\right]=n(\sigma^2+\mu^2)-(\sigma^2+n\mu^2)=(n-1)\sigma^2.$$
> Dividing by $n-1$ gives $E[S^2]=\sigma^2$. This is exactly why the sample variance uses $n-1$ in the denominator.

**Example 4.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_{10}$ be a sample from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with sample variance $S^2$. Express $P\!\left(\dfrac{S^2}{\sigma^2}\le 1.88\right)$, and using $\chi^2_{0.05}(9)=16.92$ decide whether $\dfrac{S^2}{\sigma^2}\le 1.88$ is a high-probability event.

> **Solution.** By the sampling theorem $\dfrac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2(n-1)$; here $n=10$, so $\dfrac{9S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2(9)$.
> $$P\!\left(\frac{S^2}{\sigma^2}\le 1.88\right)=P\!\left(\frac{9S^2}{\sigma^2}\le 9\times 1.88\right)=P\big(\chi^2(9)\le 16.92\big).$$
> Since $\chi^2_{0.05}(9)=16.92$ means $P\big(\chi^2(9)>16.92\big)=0.05$,
> $$P\big(\chi^2(9)\le 16.92\big)=1-0.05=0.95.$$
> The event has probability $0.95$, so it is a high-probability event.

**Example 5.** Let $X_1,\dots,X_9$ be from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with sample mean $\bar X$ and sample standard deviation $S$. Find a constant $c$ such that $P\big(|\bar X-\mu|<cS\big)=0.95$. (Use $t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$.)

> **Solution.** With unknown variance, use the Studentized statistic $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t(n-1)$; here $n=9$, df $8$. Then
> $$|\bar X-\mu|<cS\iff \left|\frac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt 9}\right|<\frac{cS}{S/3}=3c.$$
> So $P\big(|t(8)|<3c\big)=0.95$, i.e. $3c=t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$.
> $$c=\frac{2.306}{3}\approx 0.769.$$

## Exercises

1. State the two requirements for a "simple random sample" and write the joint density of $(X_1,\dots,X_n)$ when the population density is $f(x)$.
2. A population $X$ has mean $\mu=5$ and variance $\sigma^2=9$; take a sample of size $n=36$. Find $E[\bar X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)$, and (via the CLT) estimate $P(\bar X>5.5)$. (Use $\Phi(1)=0.8413$.)
3. Let $X_1,\dots,X_6$ be independent $N(0,1)$. What is the distribution of $\dfrac{X_1^2+X_2^2+X_3^2}{X_4^2+X_5^2+X_6^2}$?
4. Let $\chi^2\sim\chi^2(10)$. Using $E[\chi^2]=n$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\chi^2)=2n$, write its mean and variance; and explain the relation between $\chi^2(10)$ and the sum of two independent $\chi^2(4)$ and $\chi^2(6)$.
5. Let $X_1,\dots,X_{16}$ be from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. Give the distribution of $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt{16}}$, and using $t_{0.025}(15)=2.131$ find $a$ with $P\big(|\bar X-\mu|<a\,S/4\big)=0.95$.
6. Two independent samples come from $N(\mu_1,\sigma^2)$ and $N(\mu_2,\sigma^2)$ (equal variances), each of size $n_1=n_2=10$, with sample variances $S_1^2,S_2^2$. What is the distribution of $\dfrac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}$? Around what value does it tend to concentrate?

**Answers.**
1) Two requirements: independence (the $X_i$ are mutually independent) and identical distribution (each $X_i$ has the population's distribution); joint density $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)=\prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i)$.
2) $E[\bar X]=\mu=5$, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2/n=9/36=0.25$, $\sigma_{\bar X}=0.5$; $P(\bar X>5.5)\approx P\big(Z>\tfrac{5.5-5}{0.5}\big)=P(Z>1)=1-\Phi(1)=1-0.8413=0.1587$.
3) Numerator $\sim\chi^2(3)$, denominator $\sim\chi^2(3)$ and independent, so $\dfrac{(\cdot)/3}{(\cdot)/3}\sim F(3,3)$; the given ratio (the two $3$'s cancel) follows $F(3,3)$.
4) $E[\chi^2]=10$, $\operatorname{Var}(\chi^2)=20$; by additivity the independent sum $\chi^2(4)+\chi^2(6)\sim\chi^2(10)$, so they share the same distribution.
5) $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt{16}}\sim t(15)$; $|\bar X-\mu|<a S/4\iff\big|\tfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/4}\big|<a$, so $a=t_{0.025}(15)=2.131$.
6) Since $\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$, $\dfrac{S_1^2/\sigma^2}{S_2^2/\sigma^2}=\dfrac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}\sim F(9,9)$; its values concentrate near $1$ (numerator and denominator are identically distributed, so the ratio is about $1$).
