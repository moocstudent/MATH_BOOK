## Core Notes

### Exponents and Fractional Powers

We extend integer exponents step by step to **rational exponents**. For $a>0$ we first set $a^0=1$ and $a^{-n}=\dfrac{1}{a^n}$ ($n$ a positive integer); then we build the **bridge between radicals and fractional powers**:

$$a^{1/n}=\sqrt[n]{a},\qquad a^{m/n}=\sqrt[n]{a^m}=\left(\sqrt[n]{a}\right)^m\quad(a>0,\ n\in\mathbb{N}^*,\ m\in\mathbb{Z}).$$

Requiring the **base $a>0$** keeps every radical meaningful and positive, avoiding troublesome expressions like $(-8)^{1/2}$. For instance $8^{2/3}=\sqrt[3]{8^2}=\sqrt[3]{64}=4$, which also equals $(\sqrt[3]{8})^2=2^2=4$ — both orders give the same value.

Once extended, the familiar **laws of exponents** hold for all rational (indeed real) exponents. For $a>0,\ b>0$ and rational $r,s$:

$$a^r a^s=a^{r+s},\qquad \frac{a^r}{a^s}=a^{r-s},\qquad (a^r)^s=a^{rs},\qquad (ab)^r=a^r b^r,\qquad \left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^r=\frac{a^r}{b^r}.$$

**Intuition:** adding exponents corresponds to multiplying powers — this is what turns multiplication into addition, and it is the very seed of the logarithm. The standard way to simplify a radical expression is to **convert everything to fractional powers** first, combine using the laws, and convert back to radicals only if needed. For example $\dfrac{\sqrt{a}\cdot\sqrt[3]{a}}{\sqrt[6]{a}}=a^{1/2+1/3-1/6}=a^{2/3}=\sqrt[3]{a^2}$.

### The Exponential Function $y=a^x$: Graph and Properties

A function of the form $y=a^x$ (with $a>0$ and $a\ne 1$) is an **exponential function**. We require $a>0,a\ne1$ because $a^x$ is undefined for many $x$ when $a\le0$, while $a=1$ gives the constant $1$ and is uninteresting. Its key properties are:

- **Domain** $\mathbb{R}$, **range** $(0,+\infty)$. So $a^x>0$ always, and the graph stays above the $x$-axis without touching it (the $x$-axis is a horizontal asymptote).
- **Always passes through** $(0,1)$, since $a^0=1$; and $y=a$ when $x=1$.
- **Monotonicity:** for $a>1$ the function is **increasing** on $\mathbb{R}$; for $0<a<1$ it is **decreasing**.
- Two exponential functions with reciprocal bases are symmetric about the **$y$-axis**: $y=a^x$ and $y=(1/a)^x=a^{-x}$ mirror each other across the $y$-axis.

**Using monotonicity:** comparing $a^{x_1}$ with $a^{x_2}$ reduces to comparing the exponents $x_1,x_2$ — but the direction depends on the base. If $a>1$, then $a^{x_1}>a^{x_2}\iff x_1>x_2$; if $0<a<1$, the inequality **reverses**. A common base is the natural base $e\approx 2.718$, and $y=e^x$ is the most natural growth function.

### Logarithms and the Laws of Logarithms

If $a^b=N$ (with $a>0,a\ne1,N>0$), then $b$ is called the **logarithm** of $N$ to base $a$, written $b=\log_a N$. In other words, **the logarithm is the inverse of exponentiation**:

$$a^b=N\iff b=\log_a N.$$

The definition immediately gives two **identities**, $a^{\log_a N}=N$ and $\log_a a^b=b$, and two **special values**, $\log_a 1=0$ and $\log_a a=1$. Common notation: base $10$ is the **common logarithm** $\lg N=\log_{10}N$, and base $e$ is the **natural logarithm** $\ln N=\log_e N$.

For $a>0,a\ne1$ and $M>0,N>0$, the **laws of logarithms** are:

$$\log_a(MN)=\log_a M+\log_a N,\qquad \log_a\frac{M}{N}=\log_a M-\log_a N,\qquad \log_a M^k=k\log_a M.$$

**Intuition:** logarithms turn multiplication/division into addition/subtraction and powers into multiplication — exactly the principle behind log tables and slide rules. To change the base, use the **change-of-base formula**:

$$\log_a N=\frac{\log_c N}{\log_c a}\quad(c>0,c\ne1),$$

which yields the handy corollaries $\log_a b=\dfrac{1}{\log_b a}$ and $\log_{a^m}N^k=\dfrac{k}{m}\log_a N$. Remember: the laws hold only when the **arguments are positive**; $\log_a(MN)$ and $\log_a M+\log_a N$ agree only on that domain.

### The Logarithmic Function $y=\log_a x$ (Inverse of the Exponential)

A function $y=\log_a x$ (with $a>0,a\ne1$) is a **logarithmic function**. It is the **inverse** of the exponential $y=a^x$, so the two graphs are symmetric about the line **$y=x$**. Reflecting the exponential's properties across $y=x$ gives the logarithm's properties:

- **Domain** $(0,+\infty)$, **range** $\mathbb{R}$. The argument must be positive, and the $y$-axis is a vertical asymptote.
- **Always passes through** $(1,0)$ (mirroring the exponential through $(0,1)$); and $y=1$ when $x=a$.
- **Monotonicity:** for $a>1$ it is **increasing** on $(0,+\infty)$; for $0<a<1$ it is **decreasing**.
- Being inverses means $\log_a(a^x)=x$ (for $x\in\mathbb{R}$) and $a^{\log_a x}=x$ (for $x>0$), and the two functions share the same direction of monotonicity (if $a>1$, both increase).

**Why the inverse viewpoint helps:** to solve $\log_a x=c$, just write $x=a^c$; to decide the sign of a log, compare its argument with $1$ — when $a>1$, $x>1\Rightarrow\log_a x>0$ and $0<x<1\Rightarrow\log_a x<0$.

### Exponential and Logarithmic Equations and Inequalities

The basic method for an **exponential equation** is to **match bases**: rewrite it as $a^{f(x)}=a^{g(x)}$, then use injectivity of the exponential to get $f(x)=g(x)$. If bases cannot be matched, **take logarithms of both sides**. For an equation of the form $a^{2x}+p\,a^x+q=0$, substitute $t=a^x>0$ to get a quadratic in $t$; after solving you **must check $t>0$**.

The basic method for a **logarithmic equation** is again to **match bases** and equate arguments: $\log_a f(x)=\log_a g(x)\Rightarrow f(x)=g(x)$ — but you **must substitute back to check the domain** ($f(x)>0,g(x)>0$) to discard extraneous roots.

For **inequalities**, keep monotonicity central and **split into cases by the base**:

$$a>1:\quad a^{f(x)}>a^{g(x)}\iff f(x)>g(x);\qquad 0<a<1:\quad a^{f(x)}>a^{g(x)}\iff f(x)<g(x).$$

The same applies to $\log_a f(x)>\log_a g(x)$: for $a>1$ it is equivalent to $f(x)>g(x)>0$; for $0<a<1$ it is equivalent to $0<f(x)<g(x)$. **Key pitfall: when the base is below $1$ the inequality reverses, and a logarithmic inequality always carries positivity constraints on the arguments.**

### Summary

- Fractional powers: $a^{m/n}=\sqrt[n]{a^m}$ ($a>0$); laws $a^ra^s=a^{r+s}$, $(a^r)^s=a^{rs}$, $(ab)^r=a^rb^r$.
- Exponential $y=a^x$: domain $\mathbb{R}$, range $(0,+\infty)$, through $(0,1)$; increasing if $a>1$, decreasing if $0<a<1$.
- Logarithm $a^b=N\iff b=\log_aN$; laws $\log_a(MN)=\log_aM+\log_aN$, $\log_a\frac MN=\log_aM-\log_aN$, $\log_aM^k=k\log_aM$; change of base $\log_aN=\dfrac{\log_cN}{\log_ca}$.
- Logarithm $y=\log_ax$ is the inverse of $y=a^x$; graphs symmetric about $y=x$; through $(1,0)$, range $\mathbb{R}$.
- Equations: match bases; substitute $t=a^x>0$; inequalities split into $a>1$ vs $0<a<1$ (the latter **reverses**); log problems **check arguments are positive**.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Simplify $\left(\dfrac{27}{8}\right)^{-2/3}+\sqrt[3]{(-2)^3}-\left(\sqrt{2}\right)^{4}$.

> **Solution.** Compute term by term. Write the first base as $\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^3$:
> $$\left(\frac{27}{8}\right)^{-2/3}=\left[\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^3\right]^{-2/3}=\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^{-2}=\frac{4}{9}.$$
> The second term $\sqrt[3]{(-2)^3}=-2$ (an odd root accepts negatives). The third term $(\sqrt2)^4=(2^{1/2})^4=2^2=4$.
> Hence the expression equals $\dfrac{4}{9}+(-2)-4=\dfrac{4}{9}-6=-\dfrac{50}{9}$.

**Example 2.** Given $\lg 2=0.301$ and $\lg 3=0.477$, find $\lg 12$ and $\lg 5$.

> **Solution.** Factor each argument into powers of $2$ and $3$, then use the laws.
> $$\lg 12=\lg(2^2\cdot 3)=2\lg 2+\lg 3=2(0.301)+0.477=1.079.$$
> For $\lg 5$, note $5=\dfrac{10}{2}$:
> $$\lg 5=\lg\frac{10}{2}=\lg 10-\lg 2=1-0.301=0.699.$$
> So $\lg 12\approx 1.079$ and $\lg 5\approx 0.699$.

**Example 3.** Order the three numbers $a=2^{0.3}$, $b=0.3^{2}$, $c=\log_2 0.3$.

> **Solution.** Locate each in an interval.
> Since $2^x$ is increasing with $2^0=1$ and $0.3>0$, we have $a=2^{0.3}>2^0=1$, so $a>1$.
> $b=0.3^2=0.09$, so clearly $0<b<1$.
> Since $\log_2 x$ is increasing with $\log_2 1=0$ and $0<0.3<1$, we have $c=\log_2 0.3<0$.
> Therefore $c<0<b<1<a$, i.e. $c<b<a$.

**Example 4.** Solve $4^{x}-3\cdot 2^{x}-4=0$.

> **Solution.** Since $4^x=(2^2)^x=(2^x)^2$, let $t=2^x>0$; the equation becomes
> $$t^2-3t-4=0\ \Rightarrow\ (t-4)(t+1)=0\ \Rightarrow\ t=4\ \text{or}\ t=-1.$$
> As $t=2^x>0$, discard $t=-1$ and keep $t=4$. Then $2^x=4=2^2$, and injectivity gives $x=2$.
> Checking confirms $x=2$ satisfies the original equation, so $x=2$.

**Example 5.** Solve the inequality $\log_{1/2}(2x-1)>\log_{1/2}(x+3)$.

> **Solution.** The base $a=\dfrac12\in(0,1)$, so the logarithm is **decreasing** and the inequality **reverses** when the logs are removed; meanwhile both arguments must be positive:
> $$\begin{cases} 2x-1>0,\\ x+3>0,\\ 2x-1<x+3.\end{cases}$$
> Solving each: $x>\dfrac12$ from the first, $x>-3$ from the second, $x<4$ from the third. Intersecting gives
> $$\frac12<x<4.$$
> Hence the solution set is $\left(\dfrac12,\,4\right)$.

## Exercises

1. Simplify $16^{3/4}-\left(\dfrac18\right)^{-1/3}+\sqrt[3]{27}$.
2. Write $\dfrac{a^2\sqrt{b}}{\sqrt[3]{a}\,b^{1/2}}$ (with $a>0,b>0$) using only positive fractional exponents.
3. Given $\log_a 2=m$ and $\log_a 3=n$, express $\log_a 18$ and $\log_a \dfrac{9}{2}$ in terms of $m,n$.
4. Find the domain of $y=\log_3(x-1)$ and describe how its graph is obtained from $y=\log_3 x$ by translation.
5. Solve $9^{x}-4\cdot 3^{x}+3=0$.
6. Solve the inequality $2^{x^2-x}<8$.

**Answers.**
1) $16^{3/4}=(2^4)^{3/4}=2^3=8$; $\left(\tfrac18\right)^{-1/3}=(2^{-3})^{-1/3}=2$; $\sqrt[3]{27}=3$. The expression is $8-2+3=9$.
2) $\dfrac{a^2 b^{1/2}}{a^{1/3} b^{1/2}}=a^{2-1/3}b^{1/2-1/2}=a^{5/3}$.
3) $\log_a 18=\log_a(2\cdot 3^2)=m+2n$; $\log_a\dfrac{9}{2}=\log_a 3^2-\log_a 2=2n-m$.
4) Need $x-1>0$, so the domain is $(1,+\infty)$; the graph is $y=\log_3 x$ **shifted right by $1$ unit**.
5) Let $t=3^x>0$: $t^2-4t+3=0\Rightarrow(t-1)(t-3)=0\Rightarrow t=1$ or $t=3$, both positive. From $3^x=1$, $x=0$; from $3^x=3$, $x=1$. So $x=0$ or $x=1$.
6) $8=2^3$ and base $2>1$ is increasing, so the inequality is equivalent to $x^2-x<3$, i.e. $x^2-x-3<0$, giving $\dfrac{1-\sqrt{13}}{2}<x<\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}$.
