## Core Notes

Limits are the logical starting point of all of calculus. A derivative is one kind of limit, a definite integral is another, and the sum of an infinite series is a limit too. Once we make the idea of "getting arbitrarily close" precise, everything that follows stands on solid ground.

### Sequences and convergence

A **sequence** $\{a_n\}$ is just a list of numbers indexed by the positive integers, $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. The question is whether, as $n$ grows, the terms $a_n$ settle down toward some fixed number.

**Definition (limit of a sequence).** A constant $A$ is the limit of $\{a_n\}$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there is an integer $N$ such that whenever $n > N$,
$$|a_n - A| < \varepsilon.$$
We then say $\{a_n\}$ **converges** to $A$ and write $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = A$. Otherwise the sequence **diverges**.

Read it as a game: no matter how narrow you make the error band $(A-\varepsilon,\ A+\varepsilon)$, from some index $N$ onward *all* the terms fall inside it. The $\varepsilon$ is the challenge; the $N$ is your response. Being able to respond to every challenge is exactly what convergence means.

Two basic facts: the limit of a convergent sequence is **unique**, and a convergent sequence is **bounded** (the converse fails — $a_n=(-1)^n$ is bounded but diverges).

### The ε–δ definition of a function limit

A sequence only lives on the integers; a function varies over a whole interval, so "$n\to\infty$" is replaced by "$x\to x_0$".

**Definition (limit of a function).** Suppose $f$ is defined on a punctured neighborhood of $x_0$. A constant $A$ is the limit of $f$ as $x\to x_0$ if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that whenever $0 < |x - x_0| < \delta$,
$$|f(x) - A| < \varepsilon.$$
We write $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x) = A$.

The condition $0 < |x-x_0|$ is the whole point: a limit cares only about the behavior of $f$ *near* $x_0$, never about the value $f(x_0)$ itself — indeed $f$ need not even be defined there. This is precisely what separates "take a limit" from "just plug in".

**One-sided limits.** Letting $x$ approach only from the right gives $\lim_{x\to x_0^+}f(x)$; from the left, $\lim_{x\to x_0^-}f(x)$. The two-sided limit exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and agree.

### Limit laws

If $\lim f = A$ and $\lim g = B$ (under the same limiting process), then

$$\lim (f \pm g) = A \pm B, \qquad \lim (f\cdot g) = A\cdot B, \qquad \lim \frac{f}{g} = \frac{A}{B}\ (B \ne 0).$$

These laws let you break a complicated expression into simple pieces and take the limit of each. But beware of **indeterminate forms**: when you meet $\frac{0}{0}$, $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, $\infty-\infty$, or $0\cdot\infty$, the laws do not apply directly — you must first rewrite the expression (cancel, combine fractions, rationalize, or extract the leading term).

**Squeeze theorem.** If $g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)$ near $x_0$ and $\lim g(x) = \lim h(x) = A$, then $\lim f(x) = A$. It is the tool of choice when a limit is "pinned" by an oscillating factor.

### The two standard limits

These two limits are the source of a great many later formulas, so commit them to memory.

**The first standard limit:**
$$\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1.$$
It says $\sin x \approx x$ for small $x$ and is the cornerstone of differentiating every trigonometric function. The proof squeezes areas on the unit circle: $\sin x < x < \tan x$.

**The second standard limit:**
$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^{x} = e, \qquad \text{equivalently}\quad \lim_{t\to 0}(1+t)^{1/t} = e.$$
This is where the constant $e\approx 2.71828$ comes from; almost every "$1^{\infty}$" indeterminate form is handled by reducing it to this shape.

### Infinitesimals and equivalents

**Definition.** If $\lim_{x\to x_0}\alpha(x) = 0$, then $\alpha$ is an **infinitesimal** under that process. An infinitesimal is not "a tiny number" but a *variable tending to zero*.

Two infinitesimals may vanish at different rates. For infinitesimals $\alpha,\beta$, if $\lim\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=1$ we call them **equivalent** and write $\alpha\sim\beta$. Equivalent infinitesimals may be substituted for one another inside **multiplicative factors**, which greatly simplifies computation — but substitution inside a sum or difference is dangerous and should be avoided.

As $x\to 0$, the standard equivalents are:
$$\sin x \sim x,\quad \tan x \sim x,\quad 1-\cos x \sim \frac{x^2}{2},\quad \ln(1+x)\sim x,\quad e^x - 1 \sim x,\quad (1+x)^a - 1 \sim a x.$$

### Continuity and discontinuities

**Definition (continuity).** $f$ is **continuous** at $x_0$ if $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x) = f(x_0)$. This single equation packs in three requirements at once: $f(x_0)$ is defined, the limit exists, and the two are equal.

Intuitively, continuity means the graph does not break at that point — you could draw it without lifting your pen. A function continuous on a closed interval enjoys two powerful properties: the **Extreme Value Theorem** (it attains a maximum and a minimum) and the **Intermediate Value Theorem** (it takes every value between its endpoint values).

If $f$ fails to be continuous at $x_0$, that point is a **discontinuity**, of one of two kinds:

- **First kind**: both one-sided limits exist. If they are equal (but differ from $f(x_0)$, or $f$ is undefined there) it is a **removable** discontinuity; if they differ it is a **jump** discontinuity.
- **Second kind**: at least one one-sided limit fails to exist — e.g. an **infinite** discontinuity (a limit of $\infty$) or an **oscillating** one like $\sin\frac1x$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

> **Solution.** Substituting gives $\frac{0}{0}$, an indeterminate form, so factor first. Since $x\to 3$ means $x\ne 3$, the factor $x-3\ne 0$ cancels:
> $$\frac{x^2-9}{x-3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3.$$
> Hence
> $$\lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2-9}{x-3} = \lim_{x\to 3}(x+3) = 6.$$

**Example 2.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x}$.

> **Solution.** Use the equivalents $\sin 5x \sim 5x$ and $\tan 3x \sim 3x$ as $x\to 0$ (both are multiplicative factors, so substitution is legal):
> $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{5x}{3x} = \frac{5}{3}.$$
> Without equivalents, build up the first standard limit instead:
> $$\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x} = \frac{\sin 5x}{5x}\cdot\frac{3x}{\tan 3x}\cdot\frac{5}{3} \to 1\cdot 1 \cdot \frac{5}{3} = \frac{5}{3}.$$

**Example 3.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1 + \frac{2}{x}\right)^{x}$.

> **Solution.** This is a $1^{\infty}$ form; reshape it into the second standard limit. Set $u = \frac{x}{2}$, so $x = 2u$ and $u\to\infty$ as $x\to\infty$:
> $$\left(1+\frac{2}{x}\right)^{x} = \left(1+\frac{1}{u}\right)^{2u} = \left[\left(1+\frac{1}{u}\right)^{u}\right]^{2}.$$
> The inner bracket tends to $e$, so
> $$\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{2}{x}\right)^{x} = e^{2}.$$

## Exercises

1. Using the $\varepsilon$–$N$ idea, show that $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}=0$: given $\varepsilon>0$, how large should $N$ be?
2. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2 - 4}{x^2 - 3x + 2}$.
3. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$.
4. Discuss the continuity of $f(x)=\dfrac{|x|}{x}$ at $x=0$ and classify the discontinuity.

**Answers.** 1) From $\left|\frac1n-0\right|=\frac1n<\varepsilon$ we need $n>\frac1\varepsilon$, so take $N=\left[\frac1\varepsilon\right]$ (integer part); 2) factor top and bottom: $\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x-1)(x-2)}\to\frac{x+2}{x-1}\big|_{x=2}=4$; 3) using $1-\cos x\sim\frac{x^2}{2}$, the limit is $\frac12$; 4) the left limit is $-1$ and the right limit is $+1$ — both exist but differ, so $x=0$ is a **jump** discontinuity (first kind) and $f$ is not continuous there.
