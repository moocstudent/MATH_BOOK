## Core Notes

Limits are the logical starting point of all of calculus. A derivative is one kind of limit, a definite integral is another, and the sum of an infinite series is a limit too. Once we make the idea of "getting arbitrarily close" precise, everything that follows stands on solid ground. The central task of this chapter is to translate the intuitive "getting nearer and nearer" into the airtight language of inequalities.

### Sequences and convergence

A **sequence** $\{a_n\}$ is just a list of numbers indexed by the positive integers, $a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots$. The question is whether, as $n$ grows, the terms $a_n$ settle down toward some fixed number.

**Definition (limit of a sequence).** A constant $A$ is the limit of $\{a_n\}$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there is an integer $N$ such that whenever $n > N$,
$$|a_n - A| < \varepsilon.$$
We then say $\{a_n\}$ **converges** to $A$ and write $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = A$. Otherwise the sequence **diverges**.

Read it as a game: no matter how narrow you make the error band $(A-\varepsilon,\ A+\varepsilon)$, from some index $N$ onward *all* the terms fall inside it. The $\varepsilon$ is the challenge; the $N$ is your response. The point is that $N$ is **allowed to depend** on $\varepsilon$: a smaller $\varepsilon$ usually forces you further out (a larger $N$), but as long as some $N$ always works, you win.

Two basic facts: the limit of a convergent sequence is **unique** (if it converged to both $A$ and $B$, taking $\varepsilon=\frac{|A-B|}{2}$ yields a contradiction) and a convergent sequence is **bounded** (the converse fails — $a_n=(-1)^n$ is bounded but diverges). A handy sufficient condition is the **monotone-bounded criterion**: an increasing sequence with an upper bound must converge — this is exactly what guarantees that $e$ exists in the second standard limit.

### The ε–δ definition of a function limit

A sequence only lives on the integers; a function varies over a whole interval, so "$n\to\infty$" is replaced by "$x\to x_0$".

**Definition (limit of a function).** Suppose $f$ is defined on a punctured neighborhood of $x_0$. A constant $A$ is the limit of $f$ as $x\to x_0$ if for every $\varepsilon>0$ there exists $\delta>0$ such that whenever $0 < |x - x_0| < \delta$,
$$|f(x) - A| < \varepsilon.$$
We write $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x) = A$.

The condition $0 < |x-x_0|$ is the whole point: a limit cares only about the behavior of $f$ *near* $x_0$, never about the value $f(x_0)$ itself — indeed $f$ need not even be defined there. This is precisely what separates "take a limit" from "just plug in". Here $\delta$ plays the role of $N$: it measures "how close $x$ must be to $x_0$", and it too may depend on $\varepsilon$.

**One-sided limits.** Letting $x$ approach only from the right gives $\lim_{x\to x_0^+}f(x)$; from the left, $\lim_{x\to x_0^-}f(x)$. The two-sided limit exists if and only if both one-sided limits exist and agree. This criterion is the standard tool for limits at the seams of piecewise functions and for functions involving $|x|$.

### Limit laws

If $\lim f = A$ and $\lim g = B$ (under the same limiting process), then

$$\lim (f \pm g) = A \pm B, \qquad \lim (f\cdot g) = A\cdot B, \qquad \lim \frac{f}{g} = \frac{A}{B}\ (B \ne 0).$$

These laws let you break a complicated expression into simple pieces and take the limit of each. But beware of **indeterminate forms**: when you meet $\frac{0}{0}$, $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, $\infty-\infty$, or $0\cdot\infty$, the laws do not apply directly — you must first rewrite the expression (cancel, combine fractions, rationalize, or extract the leading term). For a rational expression as $x\to\infty$, the standard trick is to divide top and bottom by the highest power in the denominator, crushing the unimportant terms into infinitesimals.

**Squeeze theorem.** If $g(x) \le f(x) \le h(x)$ near $x_0$ and $\lim g(x) = \lim h(x) = A$, then $\lim f(x) = A$. It is the tool of choice when a limit is "pinned" by an oscillating factor — for instance $x\sin\frac1x$: the bound $-|x|\le x\sin\frac1x\le|x|$ squeezes the limit to $0$.

### The two standard limits

These two limits are the source of a great many later formulas, so commit them to memory.

**The first standard limit:**
$$\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1.$$
It says $\sin x \approx x$ for small $x$ and is the cornerstone of differentiating every trigonometric function. The proof squeezes areas on the unit circle: in the first quadrant $\sin x < x < \tan x$; dividing through by $\sin x$ gives $1 < \dfrac{x}{\sin x} < \dfrac{1}{\cos x}$, and letting $x\to 0$ squeezes the middle to $1$.

**The second standard limit:**
$$\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^{x} = e, \qquad \text{equivalently}\quad \lim_{t\to 0}(1+t)^{1/t} = e.$$
This is where the constant $e\approx 2.71828$ comes from; almost every "$1^{\infty}$" indeterminate form is handled by reducing it to this shape — reshape the base into $1+\frac1u$ and the exponent into a constant multiple of $u$, or take logarithms to turn it into a $\frac{0}{0}$ form.

### Infinitesimals and equivalents

**Definition.** If $\lim_{x\to x_0}\alpha(x) = 0$, then $\alpha$ is an **infinitesimal** under that process. An infinitesimal is not "a tiny number" but a *variable tending to zero*. Dually, a variable whose absolute value grows without bound is **infinite**; the two are linked: the reciprocal of a nonzero infinitesimal is infinite, and vice versa.

Two infinitesimals may vanish at different rates. For infinitesimals $\alpha,\beta$, if $\lim\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=1$ we call them **equivalent** and write $\alpha\sim\beta$; if $\lim\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=0$ then $\alpha$ is of **higher order** than $\beta$, written $\alpha=o(\beta)$. Equivalent infinitesimals may be substituted for one another inside **multiplicative factors**, which greatly simplifies computation — but substitution inside a sum or difference is dangerous and may discard the leading part, giving a wrong answer.

As $x\to 0$, the standard equivalents are:
$$\sin x \sim x,\quad \tan x \sim x,\quad \arcsin x \sim x,\quad 1-\cos x \sim \frac{x^2}{2},\quad \ln(1+x)\sim x,\quad e^x - 1 \sim x,\quad (1+x)^a - 1 \sim a x.$$

### Continuity and discontinuities

**Definition (continuity).** $f$ is **continuous** at $x_0$ if $\lim_{x\to x_0} f(x) = f(x_0)$. This single equation packs in three requirements at once: $f(x_0)$ is defined, the limit exists, and the two are equal. Equivalently, writing the increment $\Delta y=f(x_0+\Delta x)-f(x_0)$, continuity says $\lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\Delta y=0$.

Intuitively, continuity means the graph does not break at that point — you could draw it without lifting your pen. Every elementary function (polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, and their compositions) is continuous throughout its domain, which is what makes "plug in to take the limit" legitimate. A function continuous on a closed interval enjoys two powerful properties: the **Extreme Value Theorem** (it attains a maximum and a minimum) and the **Intermediate Value Theorem** (it takes every value between its endpoint values). The IVT is the theoretical basis for the *existence* of roots: if $f$ is continuous and $f(a)f(b)<0$, then $f$ has a zero in $(a,b)$.

If $f$ fails to be continuous at $x_0$, that point is a **discontinuity**, of one of two kinds:

- **First kind**: both one-sided limits exist. If they are equal (but differ from $f(x_0)$, or $f$ is undefined there) it is a **removable** discontinuity; if they differ it is a **jump** discontinuity.
- **Second kind**: at least one one-sided limit fails to exist — e.g. an **infinite** discontinuity (a limit of $\infty$) or an **oscillating** one like $\sin\frac1x$.

### Summary

- The precise language of limits: $\varepsilon$–$N$ for sequences, $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ for functions; the core is "for every $\varepsilon$, there exists a matching $N$ or $\delta$".
- A convergent sequence is **unique and bounded**; **monotone + bounded ⇒ convergent**. A two-sided limit exists $\Leftrightarrow$ both one-sided limits exist and agree.
- The arithmetic laws hold only when both limits exist (and the denominator is nonzero); rewrite indeterminate forms first.
- The two standard limits: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin x}{x}=1$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1+\frac1x\right)^x=e$.
- Standard equivalents ($x\to0$): $\sin x\sim x$, $\tan x\sim x$, $1-\cos x\sim\frac{x^2}{2}$, $\ln(1+x)\sim x$, $e^x-1\sim x$, $(1+x)^a-1\sim ax$; substitute only inside multiplicative factors.
- Continuity $\Leftrightarrow\lim_{x\to x_0}f(x)=f(x_0)$; classify discontinuities by whether the one-sided limits exist — first kind (removable/jump) or second kind (infinite/oscillating).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

> **Solution.** Substituting gives $\frac{0}{0}$, an indeterminate form, so factor first. Since $x\to 3$ means $x\ne 3$, the factor $x-3\ne 0$ cancels:
> $$\frac{x^2-9}{x-3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x + 3.$$
> Hence
> $$\lim_{x\to 3}\frac{x^2-9}{x-3} = \lim_{x\to 3}(x+3) = 6.$$

**Example 2.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x}$.

> **Solution.** Use the equivalents $\sin 5x \sim 5x$ and $\tan 3x \sim 3x$ as $x\to 0$ (both are multiplicative factors, so substitution is legal):
> $$\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{5x}{3x} = \frac{5}{3}.$$
> Without equivalents, build up the first standard limit instead:
> $$\frac{\sin 5x}{\tan 3x} = \frac{\sin 5x}{5x}\cdot\frac{3x}{\tan 3x}\cdot\frac{5}{3} \to 1\cdot 1 \cdot \frac{5}{3} = \frac{5}{3}.$$

**Example 3.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1 + \frac{2}{x}\right)^{x}$.

> **Solution.** This is a $1^{\infty}$ form; reshape it into the second standard limit. Set $u = \frac{x}{2}$, so $x = 2u$ and $u\to\infty$ as $x\to\infty$:
> $$\left(1+\frac{2}{x}\right)^{x} = \left(1+\frac{1}{u}\right)^{2u} = \left[\left(1+\frac{1}{u}\right)^{u}\right]^{2}.$$
> The inner bracket tends to $e$, so
> $$\lim_{x\to\infty}\left(1+\frac{2}{x}\right)^{x} = e^{2}.$$

**Example 4.** Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to +\infty}\left(\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x\right)$.

> **Solution.** This is an $\infty - \infty$ form; rewrite it to remove the cancellation. Rationalize by multiplying by the conjugate $\sqrt{x^2+x}+x$:
> $$\sqrt{x^2+x}-x = \frac{(x^2+x) - x^2}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x} = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x}.$$
> Divide top and bottom by $x$ (using $\sqrt{x^2}=x$ for $x>0$):
> $$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\frac1x}+1}\ \longrightarrow\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+0}+1} = \frac{1}{2}.$$
> So the limit is $\dfrac12$.

**Example 5.** Let $f(x)=\begin{cases} \dfrac{\sin x}{x}, & x < 0,\\[4pt] a, & x = 0,\\[4pt] x + b, & x > 0.\end{cases}$ For which $a,b$ is $f$ continuous at $x=0$?

> **Solution.** Continuity requires the left limit, the right limit, and the value to all agree.
> Left limit: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0^-}\frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$ (the first standard limit).
> Right limit: $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0^+}(x+b) = b$.
> Value: $f(0) = a$.
> For continuity at $x=0$ we need all three equal: $1 = b = a$. Hence $f$ is continuous at $x=0$ precisely when $a = 1$ and $b = 1$.

## Exercises

1. Using the $\varepsilon$–$N$ idea, show that $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}=0$: given $\varepsilon>0$, how large should $N$ be?
2. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 2}\frac{x^2 - 4}{x^2 - 3x + 2}$.
3. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{1-\cos x}{x^2}$.
4. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3x^2 - x + 1}{2x^2 + 5}$.
5. Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\ln(1+2x)}{e^{3x}-1}$.
6. Discuss the continuity of $f(x)=\dfrac{|x|}{x}$ at $x=0$ and classify the discontinuity.

**Answers.** 1) From $\left|\frac1n-0\right|=\frac1n<\varepsilon$ we need $n>\frac1\varepsilon$, so take $N=\left[\frac1\varepsilon\right]$ (integer part); 2) factor top and bottom: $\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x-1)(x-2)}\to\frac{x+2}{x-1}\big|_{x=2}=4$; 3) using $1-\cos x\sim\frac{x^2}{2}$, the limit is $\frac12$; 4) divide top and bottom by $x^2$: $\frac{3-1/x+1/x^2}{2+5/x^2}\to\frac32$; 5) by the equivalents $\ln(1+2x)\sim 2x$ and $e^{3x}-1\sim 3x$, the limit is $\lim\frac{2x}{3x}=\frac23$; 6) the left limit is $-1$ and the right limit is $+1$ — both exist but differ, so $x=0$ is a **jump** discontinuity (first kind) and $f$ is not continuous there.
