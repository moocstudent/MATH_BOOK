## Core Notes

### Sequences and the General Term

A list of numbers $a_1,a_2,a_3,\dots,a_n,\dots$ arranged in a definite order is a **sequence**, and its $n$-th term $a_n$ is the **general term**. Expressing $a_n$ as a formula in $n$, $a_n=f(n)$, gives the **general-term formula**. A sequence is essentially a **function whose domain is the positive integers $\mathbb{N}^*$** (or a finite subset), so its graph is a set of isolated points.

A sequence is tied to its **partial sum** $S_n=a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n$. The two are **interconvertible**:

$$a_n=\begin{cases} S_1, & n=1,\\ S_n-S_{n-1}, & n\ge 2.\end{cases}$$

**Intuition:** knowing every term lets us add up to $S_n$; conversely, knowing $S_n$ lets us recover $a_n$ by **subtracting consecutive sums**. **Always check $n=1$ separately** — the derivation of $S_n-S_{n-1}$ assumes $n\ge2$, so $a_1$ must be computed as $S_1$ and then compared with the general formula.

A sequence may also be defined by a **recurrence**, e.g. $a_1=1,\ a_{n+1}=a_n+2$, fixing each term from its neighbours. Turning a recurrence into a general-term formula is the core skill of this chapter.

### Arithmetic Sequences

If from the second term on, each term minus the previous one is a **constant $d$** (the **common difference**), i.e. $a_{n+1}-a_n=d$, the sequence is **arithmetic**. Its general term is

$$a_n=a_1+(n-1)d.$$

**Intuition:** an arithmetic sequence grows linearly — $a_n$ is a first-degree function of $n$, and the common difference $d$ is the slope of that line. More generally, any two terms satisfy $a_n=a_m+(n-m)d$.

**Core properties** of arithmetic sequences:

- **Arithmetic mean:** if $a,b,c$ are in arithmetic progression, then $2b=a+c$, i.e. $b=\dfrac{a+c}{2}$.
- **Index-sum property:** if $m+n=p+q$, then $a_m+a_n=a_p+a_q$; in particular $a_1+a_n=a_2+a_{n-1}=\cdots$.
- **Tests:** $\{a_n\}$ is arithmetic $\iff a_{n+1}-a_n$ is constant $\iff a_n$ is linear in $n$ $\iff S_n$ is quadratic in $n$ with no constant term.

### Summing an Arithmetic Sequence

Let $S_n$ be the partial sum of an arithmetic sequence. By **reverse-and-add** — adding $S_n=a_1+a_2+\cdots+a_n$ to its reversal and using $a_1+a_n=a_2+a_{n-1}=\cdots$ — we get

$$S_n=\frac{n(a_1+a_n)}{2}.$$

Substituting $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$ gives the form in terms of **first term and common difference only**:

$$S_n=na_1+\frac{n(n-1)}{2}d.$$

Thus $S_n=\dfrac{d}{2}n^2+\left(a_1-\dfrac d2\right)n$ is a **quadratic in $n$ with no constant term**, so its extremum can be found via the parabola's vertex or by locating where terms change sign — when $d<0,a_1>0$, $S_n$ is maximal at the last term with $a_n\ge0$.

### Geometric Sequences and Their Sums

If from the second term on, each term divided by the previous one is a **constant $q$** ($q\ne0$, the **common ratio**), i.e. $\dfrac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}=q$, the sequence is **geometric** (all terms nonzero). Its general term is

$$a_n=a_1 q^{\,n-1}.$$

**Core properties:** geometric mean $b^2=ac$ (same sign); index-product property $a_m a_n=a_p a_q$ (when $m+n=p+q$).

The **partial sum** comes from **multiply-and-subtract** (subtracting $qS_n$ from $S_n$) and requires **splitting on the ratio**:

$$S_n=\begin{cases} na_1, & q=1,\\[4pt] \dfrac{a_1(1-q^n)}{1-q}=\dfrac{a_1-a_nq}{1-q}, & q\ne 1.\end{cases}$$

**Key pitfall:** **always discuss $q=1$** before using the formula (there the sequence is constant and the sum is $na_1$). When $|q|<1$, $q^n\to0$ and the infinite sum is $S=\dfrac{a_1}{1-q}$ (infinite geometric series).

### Common Summation Techniques (Multiply-and-Subtract, Telescoping)

For sequences that are neither arithmetic nor geometric, two methods are standard:

**Multiply-and-subtract** suits the "**arithmetic $\times$ geometric**" type, i.e. $a_n=(\text{arithmetic})\cdot(\text{geometric})$, such as $a_n=n\cdot 2^n$. Method: write $S_n$, multiply both sides by the ratio $q$ to get $qS_n$, **shift by one position and subtract**, reducing the middle to a geometric sum.

**Telescoping** suits a general term that splits as a **difference of two pieces**, so adjacent terms cancel head-to-tail. The most common templates:

$$\frac{1}{n(n+1)}=\frac1n-\frac1{n+1},\qquad \frac{1}{n(n+2)}=\frac12\!\left(\frac1n-\frac1{n+2}\right),$$

$$\frac{1}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}=\frac12\!\left(\frac1{2n-1}-\frac1{2n+1}\right).$$

**Intuition:** after splitting, the sum behaves like falling dominoes — interior terms cancel in pairs, leaving only a **few boundary terms**. Also useful are **grouping** (splitting the term into easily summed sequences) and the **closed forms** $\sum_{k=1}^n k=\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}$, $\sum_{k=1}^n k^2=\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$.

### Summary

- $a_n$ and $S_n$: $a_1=S_1$, $a_n=S_n-S_{n-1}\,(n\ge2)$, and check $n=1$.
- Arithmetic: $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$, $S_n=\dfrac{n(a_1+a_n)}{2}=na_1+\dfrac{n(n-1)}{2}d$; mean $2b=a+c$.
- Geometric: $a_n=a_1q^{n-1}$, $S_n=\dfrac{a_1(1-q^n)}{1-q}\,(q\ne1)$, and $S_n=na_1$ when $q=1$; mean $b^2=ac$.
- Multiply-and-subtract: arithmetic $\times$ geometric; telescoping: $\dfrac{1}{n(n+1)}=\dfrac1n-\dfrac1{n+1}$.
- Closed forms: $\sum k=\dfrac{n(n+1)}2$, $\sum k^2=\dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}6$; infinite sum $\dfrac{a_1}{1-q}$ when $|q|<1$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Given the partial sum $S_n=n^2+2n$, find the general term $a_n$.

> **Solution.** First use $a_n=S_n-S_{n-1}\,(n\ge2)$:
> $$a_n=(n^2+2n)-\big[(n-1)^2+2(n-1)\big]=(n^2+2n)-(n^2-1)=2n+1.$$
> Now check $n=1$: $a_1=S_1=1+2=3$, and the formula gives $2\cdot1+1=3$, which matches.
> So $a_n=2n+1$ for all $n\ge1$ (an arithmetic sequence with first term $3$ and common difference $2$).

**Example 2.** In an arithmetic sequence $\{a_n\}$, $a_3=7$ and $a_7=19$. Find $a_1$, $d$, and the sum $S_{10}$.

> **Solution.** Using $a_n=a_1+(n-1)d$, set up the system
> $$\begin{cases} a_1+2d=7,\\ a_1+6d=19.\end{cases}$$
> Subtracting gives $4d=12$, so $d=3$; back-substituting, $a_1=7-2\cdot3=1$. Then $a_{10}=a_1+9d=1+27=28$, and
> $$S_{10}=\frac{10(a_1+a_{10})}{2}=\frac{10(1+28)}{2}=5\cdot29=145.$$
> So $a_1=1,\ d=3,\ S_{10}=145$.

**Example 3.** In a geometric sequence $\{a_n\}$, $a_1=2$ and $a_4=16$. Find the common ratio $q$ and the sum $S_5$.

> **Solution.** From $a_4=a_1 q^3$, $16=2q^3$, so $q^3=8$ and $q=2$. Since $q\ne1$, use the sum formula:
> $$S_5=\frac{a_1(1-q^5)}{1-q}=\frac{2(1-2^5)}{1-2}=\frac{2(1-32)}{-1}=\frac{2\cdot(-31)}{-1}=62.$$
> So $q=2$ and $S_5=62$.

**Example 4.** Evaluate $S_n=\dfrac{1}{1\cdot2}+\dfrac{1}{2\cdot3}+\cdots+\dfrac{1}{n(n+1)}$.

> **Solution.** Telescope using $\dfrac{1}{k(k+1)}=\dfrac1k-\dfrac1{k+1}$:
> $$S_n=\left(1-\frac12\right)+\left(\frac12-\frac13\right)+\cdots+\left(\frac1n-\frac1{n+1}\right).$$
> The interior terms cancel in pairs, leaving only the ends:
> $$S_n=1-\frac1{n+1}=\frac{n}{n+1}.$$
> So $S_n=\dfrac{n}{n+1}$.

**Example 5.** Evaluate $S_n=1\cdot2+2\cdot2^2+3\cdot2^3+\cdots+n\cdot2^n$.

> **Solution.** The term $a_k=k\cdot2^k$ is "arithmetic $\times$ geometric", so use **multiply-and-subtract**. Write
> $$S_n=1\cdot2+2\cdot2^2+\cdots+n\cdot2^n,$$
> and multiply both sides by the ratio $2$:
> $$2S_n=1\cdot2^2+2\cdot2^3+\cdots+n\cdot2^{n+1}.$$
> Subtract the second from the first (shifted by one position):
> $$S_n-2S_n=\big(2+2^2+2^3+\cdots+2^n\big)-n\cdot2^{n+1}.$$
> The bracket is a geometric sum $\dfrac{2(2^n-1)}{2-1}=2^{n+1}-2$, so
> $$-S_n=(2^{n+1}-2)-n\cdot2^{n+1}=(1-n)2^{n+1}-2.$$
> Hence $S_n=(n-1)2^{n+1}+2$.

## Exercises

1. Given $S_n=2n^2-n$, find the general term $a_n$ and state whether $\{a_n\}$ is arithmetic.
2. In an arithmetic sequence $a_2+a_8=16$; find $a_5$. If also $a_1=1$, find the common difference $d$.
3. A geometric sequence has first term $a_1=3$ and ratio $q=\dfrac12$; find its infinite sum $S$.
4. Evaluate $\dfrac{1}{1\cdot3}+\dfrac{1}{3\cdot5}+\cdots+\dfrac{1}{(2n-1)(2n+1)}$.
5. In an arithmetic sequence $\{a_n\}$ with $a_1=20$ and $d=-3$, for which $n$ is the partial sum $S_n$ largest, and what is the maximum?
6. Evaluate $S_n=\sum_{k=1}^{n}(2k-1)$ and verify with a closed form.

**Answers.**
1) For $n\ge2$, $a_n=S_n-S_{n-1}=(2n^2-n)-\big[2(n-1)^2-(n-1)\big]=4n-3$; for $n=1$, $a_1=S_1=1=4\cdot1-3$ matches. So $a_n=4n-3$, common difference $4$ — yes, arithmetic.
2) By the index-sum property $a_2+a_8=2a_5=16$, so $a_5=8$; and $a_5=a_1+4d=1+4d=8$ gives $d=\dfrac74$.
3) Since $|q|=\dfrac12<1$, the infinite sum is $S=\dfrac{a_1}{1-q}=\dfrac{3}{1-\frac12}=6$.
4) Telescope $\dfrac{1}{(2k-1)(2k+1)}=\dfrac12\!\left(\dfrac1{2k-1}-\dfrac1{2k+1}\right)$; the sum is $\dfrac12\!\left(1-\dfrac1{2n+1}\right)=\dfrac{n}{2n+1}$.
5) $a_n=20-3(n-1)=23-3n\ge0\Rightarrow n\le\dfrac{23}{3}\approx7.67$, so the first $7$ terms are nonnegative and $S_7$ is largest; $S_7=\dfrac{7(20+a_7)}{2}=\dfrac{7(20+2)}{2}=77$.
6) $S_n=\sum_{k=1}^n(2k-1)=2\sum_{k=1}^n k-n=2\cdot\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}-n=n^2+n-n=n^2$. (The sum of the first $n$ odd numbers is $n^2$.)
