## Core Notes

### Point Estimation: Method of Moments

Suppose the population distribution contains an unknown parameter $\theta$ (possibly a vector). From a sample $X_1,\dots,X_n$ we build a statistic $\hat\theta=\hat\theta(X_1,\dots,X_n)$ to estimate $\theta$; this is **point estimation**, and $\hat\theta$ is the **estimator** (its value at the data is the **estimate**).

The idea of the **method of moments** is to match **sample moments** with **population moments**. Write the population $k$-th moment $\mu_k=E[X^k]$ (a function of $\theta$) and the sample $k$-th moment $A_k=\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^k$. Set up as many equations as there are parameters:
$$\mu_k(\theta)=A_k,\qquad k=1,2,\dots,$$
and solve for $\hat\theta$, the moment estimator.

Intuition: by the law of large numbers $A_k\xrightarrow{P}\mu_k$, so equating the two is sensible. The most common first-order case is $E[X]=\bar X$. For example, if the mean $E[X]=\mu$ is the parameter, then $\hat\mu=\bar X$; if the variance $\sigma^2$ is involved, use $E[X^2]=A_2$, i.e. $\sigma^2+\mu^2=\frac1n\sum X_i^2$, giving $\widehat{\sigma^2}=\frac1n\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2$.

The method is simple and needs no specific distributional form (only that moments exist); its drawback is that it ignores distributional information, so it is often less efficient than maximum likelihood below.

### Maximum Likelihood Estimation

The idea of **maximum likelihood estimation** (MLE): since the sample has already occurred, choose the parameter value that makes "this sample most likely to occur".

Let the population have density (or mass function) $f(x;\theta)$. The joint density of the observations $x_1,\dots,x_n$, viewed as a function of $\theta$, is the **likelihood function**:
$$L(\theta)=\prod_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i;\theta).$$
The $\hat\theta$ maximizing $L(\theta)$ is the **maximum likelihood estimate**. Because differentiating a product is awkward, one usually maximizes the **log-likelihood** $\ell(\theta)=\ln L(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\ln f(x_i;\theta)$, solving the **likelihood equation**
$$\frac{\partial \ell(\theta)}{\partial \theta}=0.$$

**Steps.** Write $L$ → take the log $\ell$ → differentiate in each parameter and set to $0$ → solve → verify it is a maximum if needed.

For large samples the MLE is consistent, asymptotically normal, and asymptotically efficient — the single most important estimation method. Caution: when the likelihood is maximized at a boundary (the derivative is never zero), analyze the monotonicity of $L$ directly; e.g. for $U(0,\theta)$ the MLE is $\hat\theta=\max_i X_i$.

### Criteria for Estimators

A parameter may have several estimators, so we need criteria to compare them.

**Unbiasedness.** If $E[\hat\theta]=\theta$ for all $\theta$, then $\hat\theta$ is an **unbiased estimator** of $\theta$ — there is no systematic error. For example $\bar X$ is unbiased for $\mu$; $S^2$ (dividing by $n-1$) is unbiased for $\sigma^2$, whereas $\frac1n\sum(X_i-\bar X)^2$ is biased (its mean is $\frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2$).

**Efficiency.** Among unbiased estimators, **smaller variance is better**. If $\hat\theta_1,\hat\theta_2$ are both unbiased and $\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta_1)\le\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta_2)$, then $\hat\theta_1$ is **more efficient** than $\hat\theta_2$. Smaller variance means a more stable estimate, more tightly concentrated about the true value.

**Consistency.** If $\hat\theta_n\xrightarrow{P}\theta$ as $n\to\infty$, then $\hat\theta_n$ is a **consistent estimator** — the most basic requirement: more data, more accuracy. A sufficient condition is $E[\hat\theta_n]\to\theta$ together with $\operatorname{Var}(\hat\theta_n)\to0$.

### Interval Estimation and Confidence Intervals

A point estimate gives a single number but says nothing about precision. **Interval estimation** gives a random interval together with a degree of confidence.

Let $\theta$ be an unknown parameter and $0<\alpha<1$. If two statistics $\underline\theta,\ \overline\theta$ from the sample satisfy
$$P\big(\underline\theta<\theta<\overline\theta\big)=1-\alpha,$$
then the random interval $(\underline\theta,\overline\theta)$ is a **confidence interval for $\theta$ at confidence level $1-\alpha$**; $1-\alpha$ is the **confidence level** (often $0.95$ or $0.99$) and $\alpha$ the significance level.

**Correct reading**: it is the endpoints that are random, not $\theta$. The meaning of $1-\alpha$ is: if we resample many times and build such an interval each time, about $100(1-\alpha)\%$ of them will "cover" the true value $\theta$. The **pivotal-quantity method** is the general construction: find a **pivot** $G(X_1,\dots,X_n;\theta)$ that involves $\theta$ but has a known distribution (free of unknown parameters), bound it by its quantiles, and solve for $\theta$.

### Confidence Intervals for Normal-Population Parameters

Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ at confidence level $1-\alpha$. The sampling theorems of the previous chapter give the standard results.

**(1) Mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$ known.** Pivot $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\sim N(0,1)$, interval
$$\left(\bar X-z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n},\ \ \bar X+z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\right).$$

**(2) Mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$ unknown.** Replace $\sigma$ by $S$; pivot $\dfrac{\bar X-\mu}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t(n-1)$, interval
$$\left(\bar X-t_{\alpha/2}(n-1)\frac{S}{\sqrt n},\ \ \bar X+t_{\alpha/2}(n-1)\frac{S}{\sqrt n}\right).$$

**(3) Variance $\sigma^2$ ($\mu$ unknown).** Pivot $\dfrac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}\sim\chi^2(n-1)$, interval
$$\left(\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2}(n-1)},\ \ \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}(n-1)}\right).$$

The interval length reflects precision: other things equal, a higher confidence level (a "safer" interval) gives a longer interval, while a larger sample size $n$ gives a shorter one. In practice one often solves for the minimum $n$ from a requirement like "error at most $d$".

### Summary

- Point estimation: build $\hat\theta$ from the sample to estimate $\theta$.
- Method of moments: set population moment = sample moment, $\mu_k(\theta)=A_k$, and solve; first order is $E[X]=\bar X$.
- MLE: $L(\theta)=\prod f(x_i;\theta)$, solve $\frac{d}{d\theta}\ln L=0$; at a boundary, examine monotonicity directly.
- Criteria: unbiased $E[\hat\theta]=\theta$; efficient (smaller variance among unbiased); consistent $\hat\theta_n\xrightarrow{P}\theta$.
- Confidence interval: $P(\underline\theta<\theta<\overline\theta)=1-\alpha$; the endpoints are random; build it via a pivot.
- Normal population: $\sigma$ known use $z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$; $\sigma$ unknown use $t_{\alpha/2}(n-1)\frac{S}{\sqrt n}$; variance via a $\chi^2$ interval.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** (Method of moments) Let the population $X\sim U(0,\theta)$ with $\theta>0$ unknown and sample $X_1,\dots,X_n$. Find the moment estimator of $\theta$.

> **Solution.** The first moment of $U(0,\theta)$ is
> $$E[X]=\frac{\theta}{2}.$$
> Equate population to sample moment, $E[X]=\bar X$, i.e. $\dfrac{\theta}{2}=\bar X$, giving
> $$\hat\theta=2\bar X.$$
> Intuition: the mean of $(0,\theta)$ is $\theta/2$, so use twice the sample mean to estimate the upper end.

**Example 2.** (MLE) Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be from the exponential density $f(x;\lambda)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x}\ (x>0)$ with $\lambda>0$ unknown. Find the MLE of $\lambda$.

> **Solution.** The likelihood is
> $$L(\lambda)=\prod_{i=1}^{n}\lambda e^{-\lambda x_i}=\lambda^{n}\exp\!\Big(-\lambda\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\Big).$$
> Take the log: $\ell(\lambda)=n\ln\lambda-\lambda\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$. Differentiate and set to zero:
> $$\frac{d\ell}{d\lambda}=\frac{n}{\lambda}-\sum_{i=1}^n x_i=0\ \Longrightarrow\ \hat\lambda=\frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}=\frac{1}{\bar X}.$$
> The second derivative $-n/\lambda^2<0$, so it is a maximum. Hence $\hat\lambda=1/\bar X$.

**Example 3.** (Unbiasedness and efficiency) Let $X_1,X_2,X_3$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$. Compare $\hat\mu_1=\bar X=\frac13(X_1+X_2+X_3)$ with $\hat\mu_2=\frac14 X_1+\frac12 X_2+\frac14 X_3$: are they unbiased? Which is more efficient?

> **Solution.** **Unbiasedness.** $E[\hat\mu_1]=\frac13(\mu+\mu+\mu)=\mu$; $E[\hat\mu_2]=(\frac14+\frac12+\frac14)\mu=\mu$. Both are unbiased.
> **Variance.** By independence,
> $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\mu_1)=\Big(\tfrac13\Big)^2\cdot 3\sigma^2=\frac{\sigma^2}{3}\approx 0.333\sigma^2,$$
> $$\operatorname{Var}(\hat\mu_2)=\Big(\tfrac14\Big)^2\sigma^2+\Big(\tfrac12\Big)^2\sigma^2+\Big(\tfrac14\Big)^2\sigma^2=\Big(\tfrac1{16}+\tfrac14+\tfrac1{16}\Big)\sigma^2=\frac{3}{8}\sigma^2=0.375\sigma^2.$$
> Since $\frac{\sigma^2}{3}<\frac{3\sigma^2}{8}$, $\hat\mu_1=\bar X$ is more efficient. The equal-weight average (sample mean) has the smallest variance, beating any unequal weighting.

**Example 4.** ($\sigma$ known, mean interval) A part length $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma=0.6$ cm known. A sample of $n=9$ gives $\bar X=20.1$ cm. Find a $95\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$. (Use $z_{0.025}=1.96$.)

> **Solution.** With $\sigma$ known, use the normal pivot; the interval is $\bar X\pm z_{\alpha/2}\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}$. Here $1-\alpha=0.95$, $z_{0.025}=1.96$, $\sigma=0.6$, $n=9$:
> $$z_{0.025}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}=1.96\times\frac{0.6}{\sqrt 9}=1.96\times 0.2=0.392.$$
> So the interval is
> $$\big(20.1-0.392,\ 20.1+0.392\big)=(19.708,\ 20.492)\ \text{cm}.$$

**Example 5.** ($\sigma$ unknown, mean interval) A solution's concentration $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma$ unknown. From $n=10$ measurements, $\bar X=4.84$, $S=0.10$. Find a $95\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$. (Use $t_{0.025}(9)=2.262$.)

> **Solution.** With $\sigma$ unknown, use the $t$ pivot; the interval is $\bar X\pm t_{\alpha/2}(n-1)\dfrac{S}{\sqrt n}$. Here df $n-1=9$, $t_{0.025}(9)=2.262$, $S=0.10$, $n=10$:
> $$t_{0.025}(9)\frac{S}{\sqrt n}=2.262\times\frac{0.10}{\sqrt{10}}=2.262\times 0.03162\approx 0.0715.$$
> So the interval is
> $$\big(4.84-0.0715,\ 4.84+0.0715\big)\approx(4.768,\ 4.912).$$

## Exercises

1. State in one sentence each the idea of the method of moments and of maximum likelihood, and note the difference in information they require.
2. Let $X\sim P(\lambda)$ (Poisson) with $E[X]=\lambda$ and sample $X_1,\dots,X_n$. Find the moment estimator of $\lambda$; then write the log-likelihood $\ell(\lambda)$ and find the MLE of $\lambda$.
3. Let $X_1,\dots,X_n$ be from $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. Show $\bar X$ is an unbiased and consistent estimator of $\mu$ (give $E[\bar X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)$ and argue from them).
4. Let $X_1,X_2$ be i.i.d. with $E[X_i]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2$. For $T_a=aX_1+(1-a)X_2$, find the condition for unbiasedness and the value of $a$ minimizing the variance.
5. A shell's muzzle speed $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma=2$ m/s known. A sample of $n=16$ gives $\bar X=985$ m/s. Find a $95\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$. (Use $z_{0.025}=1.96$.) And: to shrink the half-width to $0.5$ m/s, what is the minimum sample size $n$?
6. Let $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\mu$ unknown. A sample of $n=10$ gives $S^2=0.072$. Find a $95\%$ confidence interval for $\sigma^2$. (Use $\chi^2_{0.025}(9)=19.02$, $\chi^2_{0.975}(9)=2.70$.)

**Answers.**
1) Method of moments: set population moments equal to sample moments and solve — needs only that moments exist, not the distributional form; MLE: choose the parameter making the sample most likely — needs the explicit density/mass function.
2) Moments: $E[X]=\lambda=\bar X$, so $\hat\lambda=\bar X$. Log-likelihood $\ell(\lambda)=\sum_i(x_i\ln\lambda-\lambda-\ln x_i!)=(\sum x_i)\ln\lambda-n\lambda-\sum\ln x_i!$; $\ell'(\lambda)=\frac{\sum x_i}{\lambda}-n=0\Rightarrow\hat\lambda=\bar X$ (moments and MLE coincide).
3) $E[\bar X]=\mu$ (unbiased); $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X)=\sigma^2/n\to0$ as $n\to\infty$, and the mean stays $\mu$, so $\bar X\xrightarrow{P}\mu$, i.e. consistent.
4) Unbiasedness needs $E[T_a]=a\mu+(1-a)\mu=\mu$, which holds for every $a$; the variance $\operatorname{Var}(T_a)=[a^2+(1-a)^2]\sigma^2$ is minimized at $a=\tfrac12$, the equal-weight average.
5) Half-width $1.96\times\frac{2}{\sqrt{16}}=1.96\times0.5=0.98$, interval $(985-0.98,985+0.98)=(984.02,985.98)$; for half-width $\le0.5$ need $1.96\times\frac{2}{\sqrt n}\le0.5\Rightarrow\sqrt n\ge\frac{1.96\times2}{0.5}=7.84\Rightarrow n\ge61.5$, so $n\ge62$.
6) The $\sigma^2$ interval is $\Big(\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{0.025}(9)},\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\chi^2_{0.975}(9)}\Big)$; with $(n-1)S^2=9\times0.072=0.648$, $\big(\frac{0.648}{19.02},\frac{0.648}{2.70}\big)=(0.0341,\,0.240)$.
