## Core Notes

### Sum and Difference Formulas

From rotations on the unit circle (or vector dot products) we obtain the **sum and difference formulas**, the cornerstone of all trigonometric identity work:

$$\cos(\alpha\pm\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta\mp\sin\alpha\sin\beta,$$

$$\sin(\alpha\pm\beta)=\sin\alpha\cos\beta\pm\cos\alpha\sin\beta,$$

$$\tan(\alpha\pm\beta)=\frac{\tan\alpha\pm\tan\beta}{1\mp\tan\alpha\tan\beta}.$$

**Memory aids:** the cosine expansion uses "same names multiplied, opposite sign" (the $\cos\cos$ and $\sin\sin$ terms take opposite signs); the sine expansion uses "different names multiplied, same sign". These let us **evaluate non-special angles**, e.g. writing $15^\circ=45^\circ-30^\circ$:

$$\cos 15^\circ=\cos(45^\circ-30^\circ)=\cos45^\circ\cos30^\circ+\sin45^\circ\sin30^\circ=\frac{\sqrt6+\sqrt2}{4}.$$

A key consequence is the **auxiliary-angle formula**: $a\sin x+b\cos x=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\,\sin(x+\varphi)$ with $\tan\varphi=\dfrac ba$. It merges two trig terms into one, which is invaluable for finding extrema and periods.

### Double-Angle Formulas

Setting $\beta=\alpha$ in the sum formulas yields the **double-angle formulas**:

$$\sin 2\alpha=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha,\qquad \tan 2\alpha=\frac{2\tan\alpha}{1-\tan^2\alpha},$$

$$\cos 2\alpha=\cos^2\alpha-\sin^2\alpha=2\cos^2\alpha-1=1-2\sin^2\alpha.$$

The three forms of cosine each have their uses. Solving the last two for $\cos^2\alpha,\sin^2\alpha$ gives the heavily used **power-reduction formulas**:

$$\cos^2\alpha=\frac{1+\cos 2\alpha}{2},\qquad \sin^2\alpha=\frac{1-\cos 2\alpha}{2}.$$

**Intuition:** double-angle "raises the angle, lowers the degree" for simplifying compound angles, while power reduction "lowers the degree, raises the angle" to linearise squared terms (indispensable when turning $y=a\sin^2x+\dots$ into $A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$).

### Simplifying and Evaluating Trigonometric Expressions

A general strategy is the **"three looks"**: look at the **angle** (sum/difference, double/half, complementary relations), the **name** (aim for one function, e.g. tangent to sine-cosine), and the **form** (completing the square, common denominators, factoring). Common tools:

- **Tangent to sine-cosine:** $\tan\alpha=\dfrac{\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha}$, then combine over sine and cosine.
- **Power reduction and auxiliary angle:** rewrite $\sin^2,\cos^2,\sin\cos$ via reduction and double-angle into a single sinusoid $A\sin(2x+\varphi)+B$.
- **Using "$1$" cleverly:** $1=\sin^2\alpha+\cos^2\alpha=\tan45^\circ$, useful for creating a Pythagorean relation or building angles.
- **Building angles:** write the target as a sum/difference of known angles, e.g. $\alpha=(\alpha+\beta)-\beta$ or $2\alpha=(\alpha+\beta)+(\alpha-\beta)$.

**Two types of evaluation:** computing a value from a concrete angle (apply formulas directly), or finding one expression from given trig values (fix the quadrant signs first, then build and substitute).

### Law of Sines and Law of Cosines

Let $\triangle ABC$ have angles $A,B,C$ opposite sides $a,b,c$, with circumradius $R$.

**Law of Sines:**

$$\frac{a}{\sin A}=\frac{b}{\sin B}=\frac{c}{\sin C}=2R.$$

It expresses "larger side opposite larger angle" and enables **side-angle conversion**: $a=2R\sin A$, or $\dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{\sin A}{\sin B}$. It applies to **two angles and a side (AAS/ASA)** or **two sides and a non-included angle (SSA)** (the latter may have two solutions — check).

**Law of Cosines:**

$$c^2=a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C,\qquad \cos C=\frac{a^2+b^2-c^2}{2ab}.$$

It generalises the Pythagorean theorem (reducing to $c^2=a^2+b^2$ when $C=90^\circ$). It applies to **two sides and the included angle (SAS)** to find the third side, or **three sides (SSS)** to find an angle. It also **classifies a triangle's shape**: if $a^2+b^2<c^2$ then $C$ is obtuse, and so on.

### Solving Triangles and Area

To **solve a triangle** is to find the remaining sides and angles from the given data. Basic workflow: **choose the law first** — use the Law of Sines when two angles are involved, the Law of Cosines when an included angle or all three sides are involved; alternate between them as needed. Recall $A+B+C=\pi$, so the third angle follows from two.

The **area formulas** (two sides and the included angle):

$$S=\frac12 ab\sin C=\frac12 bc\sin A=\frac12 ca\sin B.$$

**Intuition:** the area is "half the product of two adjacent sides times the sine of the included angle"; when that angle is $90^\circ$ this is $\dfrac12\times$ base $\times$ height. Combined with $c=2R\sin C$ it also gives $S=\dfrac{abc}{4R}$. **SSA case count:** given $a,b$ and $A$, find $\sin B$ from $\dfrac{a}{\sin A}=\dfrac{b}{\sin B}$; if $\sin B>1$ there is no solution, $=1$ one solution, and $<1$ then compare $a$ with $b$ for one or two solutions.

### Summary

- Sum/difference: $\sin(\alpha\pm\beta)=\sin\alpha\cos\beta\pm\cos\alpha\sin\beta$; $\cos(\alpha\pm\beta)=\cos\alpha\cos\beta\mp\sin\alpha\sin\beta$; $\tan(\alpha\pm\beta)=\dfrac{\tan\alpha\pm\tan\beta}{1\mp\tan\alpha\tan\beta}$.
- Auxiliary angle $a\sin x+b\cos x=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\sin(x+\varphi)$.
- Double-angle: $\sin2\alpha=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha$, $\cos2\alpha=2\cos^2\alpha-1=1-2\sin^2\alpha$; power reduction $\sin^2\alpha=\dfrac{1-\cos2\alpha}{2}$, $\cos^2\alpha=\dfrac{1+\cos2\alpha}{2}$.
- Law of Sines $\dfrac{a}{\sin A}=2R$; Law of Cosines $c^2=a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C$.
- Area $S=\dfrac12 ab\sin C=\dfrac{abc}{4R}$; simplification mantra "three looks": angle, name, form.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the exact value of $\sin 75^\circ$.

> **Solution.** Write $75^\circ=45^\circ+30^\circ$ and use the sine sum formula:
> $$\sin 75^\circ=\sin(45^\circ+30^\circ)=\sin45^\circ\cos30^\circ+\cos45^\circ\sin30^\circ.$$
> Substituting $\sin45^\circ=\cos45^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt2}{2}$, $\cos30^\circ=\dfrac{\sqrt3}{2}$, $\sin30^\circ=\dfrac12$:
> $$\sin 75^\circ=\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{2}+\frac{\sqrt2}{2}\cdot\frac12=\frac{\sqrt6+\sqrt2}{4}.$$

**Example 2.** Given $\sin\alpha=\dfrac35$ with $\alpha\in\left(0,\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)$, find $\sin 2\alpha$ and $\cos 2\alpha$.

> **Solution.** Since $\alpha$ is acute, $\cos\alpha=\sqrt{1-\sin^2\alpha}=\sqrt{1-\dfrac{9}{25}}=\dfrac45>0$. By the double-angle formulas:
> $$\sin 2\alpha=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha=2\cdot\frac35\cdot\frac45=\frac{24}{25}.$$
> $$\cos 2\alpha=1-2\sin^2\alpha=1-2\cdot\frac{9}{25}=1-\frac{18}{25}=\frac{7}{25}.$$
> So $\sin 2\alpha=\dfrac{24}{25}$ and $\cos 2\alpha=\dfrac{7}{25}$.

**Example 3.** Simplify $y=\sin^2 x+\sqrt3\sin x\cos x$, then find its smallest positive period and maximum value.

> **Solution.** Linearise the square and product using power reduction and the double-angle formula:
> $$y=\frac{1-\cos 2x}{2}+\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\sin 2x=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}\sin 2x-\frac12\cos 2x+\frac12.$$
> Merge with the auxiliary-angle formula ($\sqrt{(\sqrt3/2)^2+(1/2)^2}=1$):
> $$y=\sin\!\left(2x-\frac{\pi}{6}\right)+\frac12.$$
> So the smallest positive period is $T=\dfrac{2\pi}{2}=\pi$; the maximum value $1+\dfrac12=\dfrac32$ occurs when $\sin\!\left(2x-\dfrac{\pi}{6}\right)=1$.

**Example 4.** In $\triangle ABC$, $a=2$, $b=\sqrt6$, $A=45^\circ$. Find angle $B$.

> **Solution.** This is SSA (two sides and a non-included angle), so use the Law of Sines:
> $$\frac{a}{\sin A}=\frac{b}{\sin B}\ \Rightarrow\ \sin B=\frac{b\sin A}{a}=\frac{\sqrt6\cdot\frac{\sqrt2}{2}}{2}=\frac{\sqrt{12}}{4}=\frac{\sqrt3}{2}.$$
> So $B=60^\circ$ or $B=120^\circ$. Since $b>a$ we need $B>A=45^\circ$, which both satisfy; and $A+B<180^\circ$ holds for both $45^\circ+60^\circ$ and $45^\circ+120^\circ$. Hence $B=60^\circ$ or $120^\circ$ (**two solutions**).

**Example 5.** In $\triangle ABC$, $b=3$, $c=4$, $A=60^\circ$. Find side $a$ and the area of $\triangle ABC$.

> **Solution.** This is SAS, so find the third side by the Law of Cosines:
> $$a^2=b^2+c^2-2bc\cos A=9+16-2\cdot3\cdot4\cdot\cos60^\circ=25-24\cdot\frac12=13,$$
> so $a=\sqrt{13}$. The area uses two sides and the included angle:
> $$S=\frac12 bc\sin A=\frac12\cdot3\cdot4\cdot\sin60^\circ=6\cdot\frac{\sqrt3}{2}=3\sqrt3.$$
> Thus $a=\sqrt{13}$ and the area is $3\sqrt3$.

## Exercises

1. Find the exact value of $\tan 15^\circ$ (hint: $15^\circ=45^\circ-30^\circ$).
2. Given $\cos\alpha=\dfrac{4}{5}$ with $\alpha\in\left(-\dfrac{\pi}{2},0\right)$, find $\sin 2\alpha$.
3. Simplify $\dfrac{\sin 2\alpha}{1+\cos 2\alpha}$.
4. Rewrite $y=\cos^2 x-\sin^2 x+2\sin x\cos x$ in the form $A\sin(\omega x+\varphi)$.
5. In $\triangle ABC$, $a=7$, $b=5$, $c=3$; find the cosine of the largest interior angle.
6. In $\triangle ABC$, $A=30^\circ$, $b=4$, area $S=2\sqrt3$; find side $c$.

**Answers.**
1) $\tan 15^\circ=\dfrac{\tan45^\circ-\tan30^\circ}{1+\tan45^\circ\tan30^\circ}=\dfrac{1-\frac{\sqrt3}{3}}{1+\frac{\sqrt3}{3}}=\dfrac{3-\sqrt3}{3+\sqrt3}=2-\sqrt3$.
2) $\alpha$ is in the fourth quadrant, so $\sin\alpha=-\sqrt{1-\frac{16}{25}}=-\dfrac35$; $\sin2\alpha=2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha=2\cdot(-\tfrac35)\cdot\tfrac45=-\dfrac{24}{25}$.
3) $\dfrac{\sin2\alpha}{1+\cos2\alpha}=\dfrac{2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha}{2\cos^2\alpha}=\tan\alpha$ (for $\cos\alpha\ne0$).
4) $y=\cos2x+\sin2x=\sqrt2\sin\!\left(2x+\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right)$.
5) The largest angle is opposite the largest side $a=7$: $\cos A=\dfrac{b^2+c^2-a^2}{2bc}=\dfrac{25+9-49}{2\cdot5\cdot3}=\dfrac{-15}{30}=-\dfrac12$ (so $A=120^\circ$).
6) $S=\dfrac12 bc\sin A=\dfrac12\cdot4\cdot c\cdot\sin30^\circ=c$; from $c=2\sqrt3$, we get $c=2\sqrt3$.
