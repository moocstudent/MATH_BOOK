## Core Notes

If a linear function graphs as a perfectly straight line, a **quadratic function** graphs as a graceful curve — the **parabola**. From the path of a thrown ball to the arc of a fountain, from the shape of an arched bridge to how profit varies with price, parabolas are everywhere. This chapter works out what this curve looks like, where its highest (or lowest) point lies, where its axis of symmetry is, and how it connects to quadratic equations and inequalities.

### The graph: a parabola

**Definition.** A function of the form
$$y=ax^2+bx+c \quad (a\ne 0)$$
is a **quadratic function**, with constants $a,b,c$; $a$ is the **leading coefficient** (it must be nonzero, otherwise the function degenerates to linear).

The simplest quadratic is $y=ax^2$, whose graph is a parabola with the $y$-axis as its axis of symmetry and vertex at the origin. The general $y=ax^2+bx+c$ graphs as a parabola of the same shape, obtained from $y=ax^2$ by a translation. Some shared features:

- the graph is an **axially symmetric** curve, with a vertical line of symmetry;
- the parabola has one most special point — the **vertex**, the intersection of the axis with the curve, and the highest or lowest point of the curve;
- parabolas with the same $|a|$ have identical shape (the same "width"); larger $|a|$ means a narrower opening.

### Vertex form and the axis of symmetry

To read off the vertex at a glance, rewrite the formula in **vertex form**.

**Vertex form.** A quadratic can be written
$$y=a(x-h)^2+k \quad (a\ne 0),$$
in which the **vertex** is $(h,k)$ and the **axis of symmetry** is the line $x=h$. The reason is $(x-h)^2\ge 0$: at $x=h$ the value $y=k$ is the extreme; the farther $x$ is from $h$, the more $y$ departs from $k$, and $x=h\pm t$ give the same $y$, so the curve is symmetric about $x=h$.

**From general form to vertex form (completing the square).** Completing the square in $y=ax^2+bx+c$:
$$y=a\left(x+\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2+\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}.$$
Matching the vertex form gives the formulas for the axis and vertex:
$$x=-\frac{b}{2a}, \qquad \text{vertex}\;\left(-\frac{b}{2a},\,\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}\right).$$
The key thing to remember is the axis $x=-\frac{b}{2a}$; the $y$-coordinate can always be found by substituting this $x$ back into the formula, so there is no need to memorize it.

### Opening, monotonicity and extremum

The sign of the leading coefficient $a$ fixes which way the parabola opens, and hence whether the function has a maximum or a minimum.

- **Opening direction.** When $a>0$ the parabola **opens upward** like a bowl, and the vertex is the lowest point; when $a<0$ it **opens downward** like a hill, and the vertex is the highest point.
- **Monotonicity.** The axis $x=-\frac{b}{2a}$ is the dividing line: when $a>0$, $y$ decreases with $x$ to the left of the axis and increases to the right; when $a<0$ it is the reverse (increasing on the left, decreasing on the right).
- **Extremum.** The $y$-coordinate of the vertex is the extreme value. When $a>0$ the function has a **minimum** $y_{\min}=\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}$ (attained at $x=-\frac{b}{2a}$); when $a<0$ it has a **maximum** by the same formula. Many "maximum profit" or "minimum material" problems come down to finding the extremum of a quadratic.

### Determining the coefficients

To recover the formula from given conditions, **choose the right form first**, then apply undetermined coefficients.

- Given **three points** on the graph, use the general form $y=ax^2+bx+c$ and substitute to get a system in $a,b,c$;
- Given the **vertex** (or the axis plus one point), use the vertex form $y=a(x-h)^2+k$, fill in $h,k$ directly, and fix $a$ from one more point;
- Given the **two $x$-intercepts** $(x_1,0),(x_2,0)$, use the factored form $y=a(x-x_1)(x-x_2)$ and fix $a$ from one more point.

Choosing the right form cuts the work sharply: if the vertex is known, do not use the general form.

### Links to equations and inequalities

The quadratic $y=ax^2+bx+c$ is tightly bound to the quadratic equation and inequality.

**Quadratic equation.** Setting $y=0$, the roots of $ax^2+bx+c=0$ are exactly the **$x$-coordinates where the parabola meets the $x$-axis**. By the discriminant $\Delta=b^2-4ac$:

- $\Delta>0$: the parabola meets the $x$-axis at two points; the equation has two distinct real roots;
- $\Delta=0$: the parabola is **tangent** to the $x$-axis (the vertex sits on it); the equation has a repeated real root;
- $\Delta<0$: the parabola misses the $x$-axis; the equation has no real root.

**Quadratic inequality.** Take $a>0$ with $\Delta>0$ (roots $x_1<x_2$) as an example; read the solution set from whether the graph is above or below the $x$-axis:

- $ax^2+bx+c>0$ (graph above the $x$-axis) has solution $x<x_1$ or $x>x_2$;
- $ax^2+bx+c<0$ (graph below the $x$-axis) has solution $x_1<x<x_2$.

The mnemonic "greater-than takes the outside, less-than takes the inside" (for an upward opening) turns an algebraic inequality into "reading the graph".

### Summary

- Quadratic $y=ax^2+bx+c\,(a\ne0)$ graphs as a parabola; larger $|a|$ gives a narrower opening.
- Vertex form $y=a(x-h)^2+k$: vertex $(h,k)$, axis $x=h$; completing the square gives axis $x=-\frac{b}{2a}$, vertex $\left(-\frac{b}{2a},\frac{4ac-b^2}{4a}\right)$.
- $a>0$ opens upward with a minimum; $a<0$ opens downward with a maximum; the extremum is the vertex's $y$, and the axis separates increase from decrease.
- Coefficients: three points → general form, vertex known → vertex form, two intercepts → factored form.
- Equation: roots of $ax^2+bx+c=0$ are the $x$-intercepts, with their count set by $\Delta=b^2-4ac$; inequality: read the solution from where the graph is above/below the $x$-axis.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Complete the square to write $y=x^2-4x+3$ in vertex form, and give the vertex and axis of symmetry.

> **Solution.** Completing the square:
> $$y=x^2-4x+3=(x^2-4x+4)-4+3=(x-2)^2-1.$$
> So the vertex form is $y=(x-2)^2-1$, the **vertex** is $(2,-1)$, and the **axis** is the line $x=2$.

**Example 2.** Find the maximum or minimum of $y=-2x^2+8x-5$ and where it occurs.

> **Solution.** Here $a=-2<0$, so the parabola opens downward and has a **maximum**. The axis is
> $$x=-\frac{b}{2a}=-\frac{8}{2\times(-2)}=2.$$
> Substituting $x=2$: $y=-2\times 4+8\times 2-5=-8+16-5=3$. So the maximum value $3$ is attained at $x=2$.

**Example 3.** A parabola has vertex $(1,-4)$ and passes through $(3,0)$. Find its formula.

> **Solution.** Since the vertex is known, use vertex form $y=a(x-1)^2-4$. Substituting $(3,0)$:
> $$0=a(3-1)^2-4=4a-4 \;\Rightarrow\; a=1.$$
> So $y=(x-1)^2-4$, which expands to $y=x^2-2x-3$.

**Example 4.** Without drawing, decide whether $y=x^2-2x+3$ meets the $x$-axis; then find where $y=x^2-2x-3$ meets the $x$-axis.

> **Solution.** For $y=x^2-2x+3$: $\Delta=(-2)^2-4\times1\times3=4-12=-8<0$, so it has **no** $x$-intercept.
> For $y=x^2-2x-3$: set $y=0$ and solve $x^2-2x-3=0$; factoring $(x-3)(x+1)=0$ gives $x=3$ or $x=-1$. So it meets the $x$-axis at $(3,0)$ and $(-1,0)$.

**Example 5.** For $y=x^2-2x-3$, use the graph to solve the inequality $x^2-2x-3<0$.

> **Solution.** From Example 4 the parabola opens upward ($a=1>0$) and meets the $x$-axis at $x=-1$ and $x=3$. The inequality $x^2-2x-3<0$ asks where the graph lies **below** the $x$-axis, i.e. **between** the two intercepts:
> $$-1<x<3.$$
> (Mnemonic: opening upward, less-than takes the inside.)

## Exercises

1. Write $y=x^2+6x+5$ in vertex form, and give its vertex and axis.
2. Find the minimum of $y=2x^2-8x+1$ and the $x$ where it occurs.
3. The parabola $y=ax^2$ passes through $(2,8)$. Find $a$ and state the opening direction.
4. A quadratic passes through $(0,3)$, $(1,0)$, $(-1,4)$. Find its formula.
5. Determine the number of $x$-intercepts of $y=2x^2-4x+5$ (use the discriminant).
6. For $y=x^2-x-6$, solve the inequality $x^2-x-6>0$.

**Answers.** 1) $y=(x+3)^2-4$, vertex $(-3,-4)$, axis $x=-3$; 2) $a=2>0$ gives a minimum; axis $x=-\frac{-8}{2\times2}=2$, and substituting $y=2\times4-16+1=-7$, so the minimum is $-7$ at $x=2$; 3) substituting, $8=a\times4$ so $a=2$; $a>0$ opens upward; 4) set $y=ax^2+bx+c$; from $(0,3)$, $c=3$; from $(1,0)$, $a+b+3=0$; from $(-1,4)$, $a-b+3=4$; combining gives $2a+6=4$, $a=-1$, then $b=-2$, so $y=-x^2-2x+3$; 5) $\Delta=(-4)^2-4\times2\times5=16-40=-24<0$, no intercepts; 6) solving $x^2-x-6=0$ gives $(x-3)(x+2)=0$, $x=3$ or $x=-2$; opening upward, greater-than takes the outside, so $x<-2$ or $x>3$.
