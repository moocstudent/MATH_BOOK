## Core Notes

### Definition and Geometric Meaning

Let $A$ be an $n\times n$ matrix. If there is a scalar $\lambda$ and a **nonzero** vector $\vec v$ such that

$$A\vec v = \lambda\vec v,$$

then $\lambda$ is an **eigenvalue** of $A$ and $\vec v$ is an **eigenvector** belonging to $\lambda$.

**Geometric meaning:** in general a linear map $A$ changes the direction of a vector; eigenvectors are the special vectors whose **direction is preserved** — $A$ merely **scales** them by $\lambda$ ($\lambda>1$ stretches, $0<\lambda<1$ shrinks, $\lambda<0$ flips, $\lambda=0$ collapses to the zero vector). These "fixed directions" expose the intrinsic structure of the transformation and are the key to understanding and simplifying a matrix. All eigenvectors for one eigenvalue, together with the zero vector, form a subspace called the **eigenspace** — precisely the solution space of the homogeneous system $(A-\lambda I)\vec v=\vec 0$. Note: an eigenvector must be **nonzero**, but an eigenvalue may be $0$ (in which case $A$ is singular).

### The Characteristic Polynomial $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$

Rewrite $A\vec v=\lambda\vec v$ as $(A-\lambda I)\vec v=\vec 0$. This homogeneous system has a **nonzero solution** $\vec v$ only when its coefficient determinant vanishes:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0.$$

The left side expands to a degree-$n$ polynomial in $\lambda$, the **characteristic polynomial** $p(\lambda)$, whose roots are all the eigenvalues (with multiplicity). **Procedure:** (1) write $\det(A-\lambda I)$, factor it and find the roots $\lambda_i$; (2) for each $\lambda_i$, solve the homogeneous system $(A-\lambda_i I)\vec v=\vec 0$ for a fundamental system, giving the eigenvectors. Two useful relations (roots and coefficients):

$$\sum_i \lambda_i = \operatorname{tr}(A), \qquad \prod_i \lambda_i = \det A,$$

i.e. the sum of the eigenvalues equals the **trace** (sum of diagonal entries) and their product equals the **determinant** — handy as checks. For a $2\times 2$ matrix, $p(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\operatorname{tr}(A)\,\lambda+\det A$ can be used directly.

### Similarity and Diagonalization (when is it possible?)

If there is an invertible $P$ with $B=P^{-1}AP$, then $A$ and $B$ are **similar**. Similar matrices share the **same** characteristic polynomial, eigenvalues, trace, and determinant (they represent one linear map in different bases).

A matrix $A$ is **diagonalizable** if it is similar to a diagonal matrix $\Lambda$, i.e. there is an invertible $P$ with

$$P^{-1}AP = \Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n).$$

The columns of $P$ are then the corresponding **linearly independent eigenvectors** (their order matches the diagonal entries of $\Lambda$). The **necessary and sufficient condition** for diagonalizability is that $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors; equivalently, the **geometric multiplicity** (eigenspace dimension $=n-\operatorname{rank}(A-\lambda I)$) of every eigenvalue equals its **algebraic multiplicity** (multiplicity as a root). **Sufficient condition:** if $A$ has $n$ **distinct** eigenvalues, it is automatically diagonalizable (eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are independent). If some eigenvalue has geometric multiplicity $<$ algebraic multiplicity, $A$ is **not diagonalizable** (a defective matrix).

### Orthogonal Diagonalization of Real Symmetric Matrices

Real symmetric matrices ($A^{\mathsf T}=A$) have especially elegant properties:

1. all eigenvalues are **real**;
2. eigenvectors for **distinct eigenvalues** are automatically **orthogonal**;
3. they are always diagonalizable, and can be diagonalized by an **orthogonal matrix** (even with repeated roots).

**Spectral theorem:** for any real symmetric $A$ there is an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (with $Q^{\mathsf T}Q=I$) such that

$$Q^{\mathsf T}AQ = Q^{-1}AQ = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n).$$

**Steps for orthogonal diagonalization:** (1) find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors; (2) apply Gram–Schmidt to the several vectors of a **repeated** eigenvalue (those for distinct eigenvalues are already orthogonal), then **normalize** every eigenvector; (3) assemble these orthonormal eigenvectors as the columns of $Q$.

### Application: Matrix Powers and Recurrences

The greatest payoff of diagonalization is fast computation of **matrix powers**. If $A=P\Lambda P^{-1}$, then

$$A^k = P\Lambda^k P^{-1}, \qquad \Lambda^k = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1^k,\dots,\lambda_n^k).$$

Since powers of a diagonal matrix are taken entrywise, $A^k$ follows immediately without repeated matrix products ($k$ multiplications become one diagonal power plus two matrix products). This solves **linear recurrences**: write the recurrence $\vec u_{k+1}=A\vec u_k$ in matrix form, so $\vec u_k = A^k\vec u_0$, then diagonalize to get a closed formula. The famous **Fibonacci sequence** arises from $\begin{bmatrix}F_{k+1}\\F_k\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}F_k\\F_{k-1}\end{bmatrix}$, whose diagonalization yields Binet's formula.

### Summary

- Definition: $A\vec v=\lambda\vec v$ ($\vec v\ne\vec0$); the eigenspace is the solution space of $(A-\lambda I)\vec v=\vec0$.
- Method: solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ for $\lambda_i$, then $(A-\lambda_i I)\vec v=\vec0$ for eigenvectors; check $\sum\lambda_i=\operatorname{tr}A$, $\prod\lambda_i=\det A$.
- Order 2: $p(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\operatorname{tr}(A)\lambda+\det A$.
- Diagonalizable $\iff$ $n$ independent eigenvectors $\iff$ geometric = algebraic multiplicity for every $\lambda$; $n$ distinct eigenvalues $\Rightarrow$ diagonalizable.
- Real symmetric matrices are orthogonally diagonalizable: $Q^{\mathsf T}AQ=\Lambda$ with $Q$ built from orthonormal eigenvectors.
- $A=P\Lambda P^{-1}\Rightarrow A^k=P\Lambda^k P^{-1}$, with $\Lambda^k$ taken entrywise; this solves linear recurrences.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$.

> **Solution.** The characteristic polynomial is
> $$\det(A-\lambda I)=\begin{vmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{vmatrix} = (2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda-1)(\lambda-3),$$
> so $\lambda_1=1,\ \lambda_2=3$.
> For $\lambda_1=1$: solve $(A-I)\vec v=\vec 0$, i.e. $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{bmatrix}\vec v=\vec 0$, giving $v_1+v_2=0$, eigenvector $\vec v_1=(1,-1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> For $\lambda_2=3$: solve $\begin{bmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}\vec v=\vec 0$, giving $v_1=v_2$, eigenvector $\vec v_2=(1,1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> Check: $\lambda_1+\lambda_2=4=\operatorname{tr}(A)$ and $\lambda_1\lambda_2=3=\det A$.

**Example 2.** Diagonalize $A=\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$ and use it to compute $A^k$.

> **Solution.** By Example 1, $A$ has two distinct eigenvalues $1,3$, so it is diagonalizable. Take
> $$P=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \Lambda=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix},$$
> so $\det P=2$ and $P^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Then
> $$A^k = P\Lambda^k P^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3^k \end{bmatrix}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 1+3^k & 3^k-1 \\ 3^k-1 & 1+3^k \end{bmatrix}.$$
> Setting $k=1$ recovers $A$, confirming the result.

**Example 3.** For the real symmetric matrix $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, find an orthogonal $Q$ making $Q^{\mathsf T}AQ$ diagonal.

> **Solution.** The characteristic polynomial $(1-\lambda)^2-4=\lambda^2-2\lambda-3=(\lambda-3)(\lambda+1)$ gives $\lambda_1=3,\ \lambda_2=-1$.
> For $\lambda_1=3$: $(A-3I)\vec v=\vec 0$ gives $v_1=v_2$, take $\vec v_1=(1,1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> For $\lambda_2=-1$: $(A+I)\vec v=\vec 0$ gives $v_1=-v_2$, take $\vec v_2=(1,-1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> The two vectors are orthogonal (dot product $1\cdot1+1\cdot(-1)=0$); normalizing gives
> $$Q = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad Q^{\mathsf T}AQ = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.$$

**Example 4.** Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$ and decide whether it is diagonalizable.

> **Solution.** $A$ is lower triangular, so the eigenvalues are the diagonal entries: $\lambda_1=2,\ \lambda_2=3,\ \lambda_3=4$. They are distinct, so $A$ **is diagonalizable**. Find each eigenvector:
> - $\lambda=2$: $(A-2I)=\begin{bmatrix}0&0&0\\1&1&0\\1&1&2\end{bmatrix}$ gives $v_3=0,\ v_1=-v_2$, take $\vec v_1=(1,-1,0)^{\mathsf T}$.
> - $\lambda=3$: $(A-3I)=\begin{bmatrix}-1&0&0\\1&0&0\\1&1&1\end{bmatrix}$ gives $v_1=0,\ v_2=-v_3$, take $\vec v_2=(0,1,-1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> - $\lambda=4$: $(A-4I)=\begin{bmatrix}-2&0&0\\1&-1&0\\1&1&0\end{bmatrix}$ gives $v_1=0,\ v_2=0$, take $\vec v_3=(0,0,1)^{\mathsf T}$.
>
> Taking $P=\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\\-1&1&0\\0&-1&1\end{bmatrix}$ gives $P^{-1}AP=\operatorname{diag}(2,3,4)$. Check $\operatorname{tr}(A)=9=2+3+4$ and $\det A=24=2\cdot3\cdot4$.

**Example 5.** Let $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Find its eigenvalues and give a closed form for $A^k$ via diagonalization.

> **Solution.** The characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda)=\lambda^2-\operatorname{tr}(A)\lambda+\det A=\lambda^2-\lambda-2=(\lambda-2)(\lambda+1)$ gives $\lambda_1=2,\ \lambda_2=-1$.
> Eigenvectors: for $\lambda=2$, solve $\begin{bmatrix}-1&2\\1&-2\end{bmatrix}\vec v=\vec0$ to get $\vec v_1=(2,1)^{\mathsf T}$; for $\lambda=-1$, solve $\begin{bmatrix}2&2\\1&1\end{bmatrix}\vec v=\vec0$ to get $\vec v_2=(1,-1)^{\mathsf T}$.
> Take $P=\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}$, $\det P=-3$, $P^{-1}=\dfrac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-2\end{bmatrix}$. Then
> $$A^k = P\begin{bmatrix}2^k&0\\0&(-1)^k\end{bmatrix}P^{-1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix} 2^{k+1}+(-1)^k & 2^{k+1}-2(-1)^k \\ 2^{k}-(-1)^k & 2^{k}+2(-1)^k \end{bmatrix}.$$
> Check $k=2$: $\frac13\begin{bmatrix}8+1&8-2\\4-1&4+2\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}3&2\\1&2\end{bmatrix}=A^2$.

## Exercises

1. Find the eigenvalues of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 8 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$.
2. Find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $A=\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$.
3. Decide whether $A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ is diagonalizable, and explain.
4. Given that $A=\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $2$ and $-1$, use diagonalization to compute $A^k$.
5. For the real symmetric matrix $A=\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$, find all eigenvalues and give an orthogonal basis of the eigenspace of the repeated eigenvalue.
6. A $3\times 3$ matrix $A$ has eigenvalues $1,2,3$. Find $\operatorname{tr}(A)$, $\det A$, and $\operatorname{tr}(A^2)$; state whether $A$ is invertible and whether it is diagonalizable.

**Answers.**
1) For a triangular matrix the eigenvalues are the diagonal entries: $\lambda_1=3,\ \lambda_2=-1$.
2) $\det(A-\lambda I)=(4-\lambda)(3-\lambda)-2=\lambda^2-7\lambda+10=(\lambda-2)(\lambda-5)$, so $\lambda=2$ has eigenvector $\vec v=(1,-2)^{\mathsf T}$ and $\lambda=5$ has $\vec v=(1,1)^{\mathsf T}$.
3) The only eigenvalue is $\lambda=1$ (double), but $(A-I)=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{bmatrix}$ has rank $1$, so the geometric multiplicity is $2-1=1 <2=$ the algebraic multiplicity; hence $A$ is **not diagonalizable**.
4) Eigenvectors: $(1,1)^{\mathsf T}$ for $\lambda=2$ and $(2,-1)^{\mathsf T}$ for $\lambda=-1$. Take $P=\begin{bmatrix}1&2\\1&-1\end{bmatrix}$; then $A^k=P\begin{bmatrix}2^k&0\\0&(-1)^k\end{bmatrix}P^{-1}=\frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix}2^{k}+2(-1)^k & 2^{k+1}-2(-1)^k \\ 2^k-(-1)^k & 2^{k+1}+(-1)^k\end{bmatrix}$.
5) The characteristic polynomial is $(\lambda-2)^2(\lambda-5)$, so $\lambda=5$ (simple) and $\lambda=2$ (double). The eigenvector for $\lambda=5$ is $(1,1,1)^{\mathsf T}$. The eigenspace for $\lambda=2$ is $v_1+v_2+v_3=0$; take $\vec u_1=(1,-1,0)^{\mathsf T}$, then Gram–Schmidt gives $\vec u_2=(1,1,-2)^{\mathsf T}$ (with $\vec u_1\cdot\vec u_2=0$), an orthogonal basis of that eigenspace.
6) $\operatorname{tr}(A)=1+2+3=6$; $\det A=1\cdot2\cdot3=6\ne0$, so $A$ is **invertible**. The eigenvalues of $A^2$ are $1,4,9$, so $\operatorname{tr}(A^2)=1+4+9=14$. The three eigenvalues are distinct, so $A$ is **diagonalizable**.
