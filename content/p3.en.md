## Core Notes

### Mathematical Expectation $E[X]$

The **expectation** (mean) of a random variable describes the "center" or long-run average of its values.

- Discrete case: $X$ takes values $x_k$ with probabilities $p_k$; if $\sum_k|x_k|p_k<\infty$, then
$$E[X]=\sum_k x_k\,p_k.$$
- Continuous case: with density $f(x)$, if $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|x|f(x)\,dx<\infty$, then
$$E[X]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x\,f(x)\,dx.$$

(Absolute convergence is required so that $E[X]$ is independent of the order of summation/integration.)

**Expectation of a function (law of the unconscious statistician)**: for $Y=g(X)$, no need to find the distribution of $Y$ first,
$$E[g(X)]=\sum_k g(x_k)\,p_k\quad\text{or}\quad E[g(X)]=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}g(x)\,f(x)\,dx.$$

Basic properties ($a,b$ constants):

- **Linearity**: $E[aX+b]=aE[X]+b$ and $E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]$ (independence not required). This extends to any finite sum: $E\big[\sum_i a_iX_i\big]=\sum_i a_iE[X_i]$.
- **Product under independence**: if $X,Y$ are independent, then $E[XY]=E[X]\,E[Y]$.

Linearity is the most powerful tool for computing expectations: **decompose a complicated variable into a sum of indicators**, take the expectation termwise, and add — often bypassing a messy mass function (see Example 4).

### Variance and Standard Deviation $\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$

The **variance** measures the spread of values about the mean:
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[(X-E[X])^2\big].$$
Expanding and using linearity gives the most-used **computational formula**:
$$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E\big[X^2-2XE[X]+(E[X])^2\big]=E[X^2]-2(E[X])^2+(E[X])^2=E[X^2]-\big(E[X]\big)^2.$$
The **standard deviation** is $\sigma_X=\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}$, in the same units as $X$ and easier to interpret.

Main properties:

- $\operatorname{Var}(X)\ge 0$; $\operatorname{Var}(X)=0$ iff $X$ is almost surely constant.
- $\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$ (a shift leaves the variance unchanged, scaling acts as $a^2$).
- In general $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$; if $X,Y$ are independent, $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)$.

Means and variances of common laws: $B(n,p)$ has $E=np,\ \operatorname{Var}=np(1-p)$; $P(\lambda)$ has $E=\operatorname{Var}=\lambda$; $U(a,b)$ has $E=\frac{a+b}{2},\ \operatorname{Var}=\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$; $\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$ has $E=\frac1\lambda,\ \operatorname{Var}=\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$; $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ has $E=\mu,\ \operatorname{Var}=\sigma^2$.

### Covariance and Correlation Coefficient $\rho$

The **covariance** measures the direction and strength of linear association between two random variables:
$$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E\big[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])\big]=E[XY]-E[X]\,E[Y].$$

Properties: $\operatorname{Cov}(X,X)=\operatorname{Var}(X)$; symmetry $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\operatorname{Cov}(Y,X)$; bilinearity $\operatorname{Cov}(aX+b,\,cY+d)=ac\,\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$. Bilinearity gives the variance of a sum: $\operatorname{Var}\big(\sum_i X_i\big)=\sum_i\operatorname{Var}(X_i)+2\sum_{i<j}\operatorname{Cov}(X_i,X_j)$. If $X,Y$ are independent then $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=0$ (the converse need **not** hold).

To remove units, define the **correlation coefficient**:
$$\rho_{XY}=\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)}\,\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(Y)}}.$$
It satisfies $|\rho_{XY}|\le 1$ (by the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality); $\rho_{XY}=\pm1$ iff $X,Y$ are almost surely linearly related. If $\rho=0$, $X,Y$ are **uncorrelated** (no *linear* association, though nonlinear dependence may remain, as in Example 2). Thus "independent $\Rightarrow$ uncorrelated", but "uncorrelated $\not\Rightarrow$ independent".

### Moments and the Moment-Generating Function

The $k$-th **raw moment** of $X$ is $\alpha_k=E[X^k]$, and the $k$-th **central moment** is $\mu_k=E\big[(X-E[X])^k\big]$. Thus $E[X]=\alpha_1$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\mu_2$. The (standardized) third and fourth central moments measure **skewness** and **kurtosis**.

The **moment-generating function (MGF)** is
$$M_X(t)=E\!\big[e^{tX}\big].$$
When it exists near $t=0$, expanding $e^{tX}$ as the power series $\sum_k \tfrac{t^k}{k!}X^k$ and taking expectations termwise gives $M_X(t)=\sum_k \tfrac{t^k}{k!}E[X^k]$, so its derivatives at the origin give the moments:
$$M_X^{(k)}(0)=E[X^k],\qquad\text{in particular } M_X'(0)=E[X],\ \ M_X''(0)=E[X^2].$$
Two key uses of the MGF: it **uniquely determines the distribution** (when it exists); and for independent sums $M_{X+Y}(t)=M_X(t)\,M_Y(t)$, which makes finding the law of a sum of independent variables easy.

### Chebyshev's Inequality

If $X$ has mean $E[X]=\mu$ and variance $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\sigma^2$, then for any $\varepsilon>0$,
$$P\big(|X-\mu|\ge \varepsilon\big)\le \frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2},$$
equivalently $P\big(|X-\mu|<\varepsilon\big)\ge 1-\dfrac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}$. Setting $\varepsilon=k\sigma$ gives $P(|X-\mu|\ge k\sigma)\le \dfrac{1}{k^2}$.

**Key derivation.** From the more basic **Markov inequality**: for a non-negative random variable $Z\ge0$ and $a>0$, $P(Z\ge a)\le \dfrac{E[Z]}{a}$. Take $Z=(X-\mu)^2$ and $a=\varepsilon^2$; then $P(|X-\mu|\ge\varepsilon)=P(Z\ge\varepsilon^2)\le \dfrac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2}=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}$.

Chebyshev's inequality needs only the mean and variance — not the specific distribution — so its bound is loose, but it is the key tool for proving the **law of large numbers**.

### Summary

- Expectation: discrete $\sum_k x_kp_k$, continuous $\int xf\,dx$; linearity $E[aX+bY]=aE[X]+bE[Y]$ (no independence); independence gives $E[XY]=E[X]E[Y]$.
- Variance: $\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$, $\operatorname{Var}(aX+b)=a^2\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
- Covariance: $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]$; $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$.
- Correlation $\rho=\dfrac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y}$, $|\rho|\le1$; independent $\Rightarrow$ uncorrelated, not conversely.
- Common $E$ and $\operatorname{Var}$: $B(n,p)\to np,\ np(1-p)$; $P(\lambda)\to\lambda,\lambda$; $U(a,b)\to\frac{a+b}{2},\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$; $\mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)\to\frac1\lambda,\frac1{\lambda^2}$; $N(\mu,\sigma^2)\to\mu,\sigma^2$.
- MGF $M_X(t)=E[e^{tX}]$, $M_X^{(k)}(0)=E[X^k]$, multiplies over independent sums.
- Chebyshev $P(|X-\mu|\ge\varepsilon)\le\dfrac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Let $X\sim P(\lambda)$. Verify $E[X]=\lambda$ from the definition and find $\operatorname{Var}(X)$.

> **Solution.** By definition,
> $$E[X]=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}k\,\frac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}=\lambda e^{-\lambda}\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{\lambda^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}=\lambda e^{-\lambda}e^{\lambda}=\lambda.$$
> First compute the factorial moment $E[X(X-1)]$:
> $$E[X(X-1)]=\sum_{k=2}^{\infty}k(k-1)\frac{\lambda^k}{k!}e^{-\lambda}=\lambda^2 e^{-\lambda}\sum_{k=2}^{\infty}\frac{\lambda^{k-2}}{(k-2)!}=\lambda^2.$$
> Hence $E[X^2]=E[X(X-1)]+E[X]=\lambda^2+\lambda$, so
> $$\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2=\lambda^2+\lambda-\lambda^2=\lambda.$$

**Example 2.** Let $X\sim U(0,1)$ and $Y=X^2$. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ and the correlation $\rho_{XY}$.

> **Solution.** For $X\sim U(0,1)$: $E[X]=\dfrac12$, $E[X^2]=\dfrac13$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\dfrac13-\dfrac14=\dfrac{1}{12}$.
> Also $E[Y]=E[X^2]=\dfrac13$ and $E[XY]=E[X^3]=\dfrac14$, so
> $$\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]=\frac14-\frac12\cdot\frac13=\frac14-\frac16=\frac{1}{12}.$$
> Next $\operatorname{Var}(Y)$: $E[Y^2]=E[X^4]=\dfrac15$, $\operatorname{Var}(Y)=\dfrac15-\dfrac19=\dfrac{4}{45}$.
> $$\rho_{XY}=\frac{1/12}{\sqrt{1/12}\,\sqrt{4/45}}=\frac{1/12}{\sqrt{1/135}}=\frac{\sqrt{135}}{12}\approx 0.968.$$
> Close to $1$, indicating strong positive association (but not perfectly linear, so $\rho<1$).

**Example 3.** A component's lifetime $X$ (hours) has mean $\mu=1000$ and standard deviation $\sigma=100$. Use Chebyshev's inequality to bound $P(800<X<1200)$ from below.

> **Solution.** The interval $800<X<1200$ is $|X-1000|<200$, so take $\varepsilon=200=2\sigma$. By Chebyshev's inequality,
> $$P\big(|X-1000|<200\big)\ge 1-\frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}=1-\frac{100^2}{200^2}=1-\frac14=0.75.$$
> So the lifetime lies in $(800,1200)$ with probability at least $75\%$.

**Example 4.** (Indicator decomposition for the binomial mean and variance) Let $X\sim B(n,p)$. Write $X$ as a sum of independent indicators and find $E[X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)$.

> **Solution.** Let $X_i$ be the indicator of trial $i$ ($1$ for success, else $0$); then $X_i\sim B(1,p)$ are independent and $X=\sum_{i=1}^{n}X_i$. For a single indicator,
> $$E[X_i]=p,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X_i)=E[X_i^2]-(E[X_i])^2=p-p^2=p(1-p).$$
> By linearity of expectation (no independence needed),
> $$E[X]=\sum_{i=1}^{n}E[X_i]=np.$$
> By independence, variances add,
> $$\operatorname{Var}(X)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\operatorname{Var}(X_i)=np(1-p).$$
> Far cleaner than summing $\sum_k k\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ directly — indicator decomposition is the standard trick for "counting" variables.

**Example 5.** (Variance and covariance of linear combinations) Suppose $E[X]=1,\ E[Y]=2$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=4,\ \operatorname{Var}(Y)=9$, $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=-3$. Let $U=2X+Y$ and $V=X-Y$. Find $E[U]$, $\operatorname{Var}(U)$, and $\operatorname{Cov}(U,V)$.

> **Solution.** By linearity $E[U]=2E[X]+E[Y]=2\cdot1+2=4$.
> Expand the variance:
> $$\operatorname{Var}(U)=\operatorname{Var}(2X+Y)=4\operatorname{Var}(X)+\operatorname{Var}(Y)+2\cdot2\cdot\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=4\cdot4+9+4(-3)=16+9-12=13.$$
> Expand the covariance by bilinearity:
> $$\operatorname{Cov}(U,V)=\operatorname{Cov}(2X+Y,\,X-Y)=2\operatorname{Var}(X)-2\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)+\operatorname{Cov}(Y,X)-\operatorname{Var}(Y).$$
> With $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\operatorname{Cov}(Y,X)=-3$,
> $$\operatorname{Cov}(U,V)=2\cdot4-2(-3)+(-3)-9=8+6-3-9=2.$$

## Exercises

1. Let $X$ have mass function $P(X=-1)=0.2,\ P(X=0)=0.5,\ P(X=2)=0.3$. Find $E[X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
2. Let $X\sim \mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$. Find $E[X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)$.
3. Suppose $\operatorname{Var}(X)=4,\ \operatorname{Var}(Y)=9,\ \rho_{XY}=0.5$. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$, $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)$, and $\operatorname{Var}(2X-Y)$.
4. Let $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with MGF $M_X(t)=e^{\mu t+\frac12\sigma^2 t^2}$. Use $M_X$ to verify $E[X]=\mu$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\sigma^2$.
5. Let $X\sim U(a,b)$. Find $E[X]$ and $\operatorname{Var}(X)$ from the definition, and verify $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\dfrac{(b-a)^2}{12}$.
6. Let $X,Y$ be independent $N(0,1)$. Set $U=X+Y$ and $V=X-Y$. Find $\operatorname{Cov}(U,V)$ and explain why $U,V$ are uncorrelated (and in fact independent).

**Answers.**
1) $E[X]=(-1)(0.2)+0(0.5)+2(0.3)=0.4$; $E[X^2]=1\cdot0.2+0+4\cdot0.3=1.4$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=1.4-0.16=1.24$.
2) $E[X]=\int_0^\infty x\lambda e^{-\lambda x}dx=\dfrac1\lambda$; $E[X^2]=\dfrac{2}{\lambda^2}$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\dfrac{2}{\lambda^2}-\dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda^2}$.
3) $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=\rho\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)\operatorname{Var}(Y)}=0.5\cdot\sqrt{36}=3$; $\operatorname{Var}(X+Y)=4+9+2\cdot3=19$; $\operatorname{Var}(2X-Y)=4\cdot4+9-2\cdot2\cdot3=13$.
4) $M_X'(t)=(\mu+\sigma^2 t)M_X(t)$, so $M_X'(0)=\mu=E[X]$; $M_X''(t)=\big[(\mu+\sigma^2t)^2+\sigma^2\big]M_X(t)$, so $M_X''(0)=\mu^2+\sigma^2=E[X^2]$, giving $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\mu^2+\sigma^2-\mu^2=\sigma^2$.
5) $E[X]=\int_a^b \dfrac{x}{b-a}dx=\dfrac{a+b}{2}$; $E[X^2]=\int_a^b\dfrac{x^2}{b-a}dx=\dfrac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}$, $\operatorname{Var}(X)=\dfrac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}-\dfrac{(a+b)^2}{4}=\dfrac{(b-a)^2}{12}$.
6) $\operatorname{Cov}(U,V)=\operatorname{Cov}(X+Y,X-Y)=\operatorname{Var}(X)-\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)+\operatorname{Cov}(Y,X)-\operatorname{Var}(Y)=1-0+0-1=0$, so uncorrelated; since $(U,V)$ is a linear transform of a normal vector, uncorrelated implies independent.
