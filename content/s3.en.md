## Core Notes

### The Idea of Hypothesis Testing

**Hypothesis testing** uses a sample to decide whether to "reject" or "not reject" a claim about a population.

The claim is written as a pair of opposing hypotheses: the **null hypothesis** $H_0$ and the **alternative hypothesis** $H_1$. Usually "the status quo / no effect / the cautious conclusion to protect" is taken as $H_0$, and "a difference / an effect" as $H_1$. For example, to test whether a process mean is still $\mu_0$:
$$H_0:\mu=\mu_0\quad\text{vs}\quad H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0.$$

**The basic logic (proof by contradiction + small-probability principle).** Assume $H_0$ holds and construct a **test statistic** with known distribution; if the value computed from the sample falls in a region that is "almost impossible under $H_0$" (the **rejection region**), then a small-probability event has occurred, so we **reject $H_0$**; otherwise we **do not reject $H_0$**. "Almost impossible" is quantified by the **significance level** $\alpha$ (e.g. $0.05, 0.01$): the probability of the rejection region (under $H_0$) is exactly $\alpha$.

**p-value.** The probability, assuming $H_0$, of observing data "at least as extreme" as what was seen is the **p-value**. Rule: reject $H_0$ if $p\le\alpha$, do not reject if $p>\alpha$. The smaller the p-value, the stronger the evidence against $H_0$.

**One- vs two-sided.** $H_1:\mu\ne\mu_0$ is **two-sided** with rejection region in both tails; $H_1:\mu>\mu_0$ (or $<$) is **one-sided** with the region in one tail.

### Two Error Types and Power

A decision can be wrong in two ways:

**Type I error (false rejection).** $H_0$ is actually true but is rejected. Its probability
$$\alpha=P(\text{reject}\ H_0\mid H_0\ \text{true})$$
is exactly the significance level.

**Type II error (false acceptance).** $H_0$ is actually false but is not rejected. Its probability is
$$\beta=P(\text{do not reject}\ H_0\mid H_1\ \text{true}).$$

| | $H_0$ true | $H_0$ false |
|---|---|---|
| Reject $H_0$ | Type I error ($\alpha$) | correct |
| Do not reject $H_0$ | correct | Type II error ($\beta$) |

**Power.** $1-\beta$ is the **power** of the test: the probability of correctly rejecting $H_0$ when $H_1$ is true; larger is better.

**Trade-off.** For a fixed sample size, decreasing $\alpha$ increases $\beta$; they move in opposite directions and cannot both be made arbitrarily small. In practice one fixes $\alpha$ (controlling false rejection), then reduces $\beta$ and raises power by increasing the sample size $n$. This is why "do not reject $H_0$" is not "$H_0$ is true" — the sample may simply be too small to detect a difference.

### Tests for the Mean (z/t Tests)

Let $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$; test whether $\mu$ equals a known constant $\mu_0$ at level $\alpha$.

**(1) $\sigma^2$ known: z test.** The statistic
$$Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\overset{H_0}{\sim} N(0,1).$$
Rejection region: two-sided $|Z|>z_{\alpha/2}$; right-sided ($H_1:\mu>\mu_0$) $Z>z_\alpha$; left-sided $Z<-z_\alpha$.

**(2) $\sigma^2$ unknown: t test.** Replace $\sigma$ by $S$,
$$T=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{S/\sqrt n}\overset{H_0}{\sim} t(n-1).$$
Rejection region: two-sided $|T|>t_{\alpha/2}(n-1)$; right-sided $T>t_\alpha(n-1)$; left-sided $T<-t_\alpha(n-1)$.

**Paired / two-sample.** To compare two normal means via $\mu_1-\mu_2$: known variances use a two-sample $z$ test; unknown but equal variances use the pooled variance $S_w^2=\dfrac{(n_1-1)S_1^2+(n_2-1)S_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}$ to form a $t(n_1+n_2-2)$ statistic; paired data use a one-sample $t$ test on the differences.

**Procedure.** State $H_0,H_1$ → choose a statistic → set the rejection region (quantiles from $\alpha$) → plug in the data → reject if it falls in the region, and conclude.

### Tests for Variance (χ²/F Tests)

**(1) One-population variance: $\chi^2$ test.** Let $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$; test $H_0:\sigma^2=\sigma_0^2$. The statistic
$$\chi^2=\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma_0^2}\overset{H_0}{\sim}\chi^2(n-1).$$
Two-sided rejection region: $\chi^2>\chi^2_{\alpha/2}(n-1)$ or $\chi^2<\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2}(n-1)$. One-sided takes one tail per $H_1$.

**(2) Ratio of two variances: $F$ test.** For two independent normal samples, test $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$. Under $H_0$,
$$F=\frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}\overset{H_0}{\sim}F(n_1-1,\,n_2-1).$$
Two-sided rejection region: $F>F_{\alpha/2}(n_1-1,n_2-1)$ or $F<F_{1-\alpha/2}(n_1-1,n_2-1)$, where $F_{1-\alpha/2}(m,n)=1/F_{\alpha/2}(n,m)$. The $F$ test is often used before a two-sample $t$ test to check equality of variances.

### Goodness-of-Fit and Independence Tests

The above concern parameters of a normal population; the **$\chi^2$ test** can also test the "shape of a distribution" and "whether two attributes are independent" — nonparametric methods.

**Goodness-of-fit test (Pearson $\chi^2$).** Test whether a sample comes from a specified distribution. Group the data into $k$ classes, with $O_i$ the **observed frequency** of class $i$ and $E_i=n p_i$ the **expected frequency** under $H_0$ ($p_i$ the theoretical class probability). The statistic
$$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^{k}\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}\overset{H_0}{\sim}\chi^2(k-1-r),$$
where $r$ is the number of parameters estimated from the data. A large $\chi^2$ signals a big discrepancy; the rejection region is $\chi^2>\chi^2_\alpha(k-1-r)$ (right tail). Typically each $E_i\ge5$.

**Independence test (contingency table).** For an $r\times c$ table, test whether two attributes are independent. Expected frequencies $E_{ij}=\dfrac{\text{row total}\times\text{column total}}{n}$, and the statistic
$$\chi^2=\sum_{i,j}\frac{(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}\overset{H_0}{\sim}\chi^2\big((r-1)(c-1)\big).$$
A large $\chi^2$ rejects the independence hypothesis.

### Summary

- $H_0$ vs $H_1$; small-probability principle: if a rare event occurs under $H_0$, reject $H_0$.
- Two error types: Type I $\alpha=P(\text{reject }H_0\mid H_0)$; Type II $\beta=P(\text{not reject}\mid H_1)$; power $1-\beta$. With fixed $n$, $\alpha\downarrow\Rightarrow\beta\uparrow$.
- Mean: $\sigma$ known $Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\sim N(0,1)$; $\sigma$ unknown $T=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{S/\sqrt n}\sim t(n-1)$.
- Variance: one population $\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma_0^2}\sim\chi^2(n-1)$; two populations $\frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}\sim F(n_1-1,n_2-1)$.
- Goodness-of-fit / independence: $\chi^2=\sum\frac{(O-E)^2}{E}$, df $k-1-r$ and $(r-1)(c-1)$; reject in the right tail.
- p-value: reject if $p\le\alpha$, else not; "not reject" $\ne$ "true".

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** ($\sigma$ known, two-sided z test) A part diameter $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with target $\mu_0=10$ mm and $\sigma=0.1$ mm known. A sample of $n=25$ gives $\bar X=10.05$ mm. Does the mean diameter deviate from target ($\alpha=0.05$)? (Use $z_{0.025}=1.96$.)

> **Solution.** Set $H_0:\mu=10$, $H_1:\mu\ne10$ (two-sided). With $\sigma$ known, use the $z$ test:
> $$Z=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n}=\frac{10.05-10}{0.1/\sqrt{25}}=\frac{0.05}{0.02}=2.5.$$
> Rejection region $|Z|>z_{0.025}=1.96$. Since $|2.5|>1.96$, it **falls in the region**, so reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ the mean diameter differs significantly from the target.

**Example 2.** ($\sigma$ unknown, one-sided t test) A bulb's lifetime $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma$ unknown. The maker claims a mean life $\ge1000$ hours. A sample of $n=16$ gives $\bar X=960$, $S=80$. Can we conclude the mean life is below $1000$ hours ($\alpha=0.05$)? (Use $t_{0.05}(15)=1.753$.)

> **Solution.** To test "below", set $H_0:\mu\ge1000$, $H_1:\mu<1000$ (left-sided). With $\sigma$ unknown, use the $t$ test:
> $$T=\frac{\bar X-\mu_0}{S/\sqrt n}=\frac{960-1000}{80/\sqrt{16}}=\frac{-40}{20}=-2.0.$$
> Left-sided rejection region $T<-t_{0.05}(15)=-1.753$. Since $-2.0<-1.753$, reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ the mean life is significantly below $1000$ hours.

**Example 3.** (One-population variance, $\chi^2$ test) An instrument's reading $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. The spec requires $\sigma\le0.5$ (i.e. $\sigma^2\le0.25$). A sample of $n=10$ gives $S^2=0.45$. Is the variance out of spec ($\alpha=0.05$)? (Use $\chi^2_{0.05}(9)=16.92$.)

> **Solution.** Set $H_0:\sigma^2\le0.25$, $H_1:\sigma^2>0.25$ (right-sided). The statistic
> $$\chi^2=\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma_0^2}=\frac{9\times0.45}{0.25}=\frac{4.05}{0.25}=16.2.$$
> Right-sided rejection region $\chi^2>\chi^2_{0.05}(9)=16.92$. Since $16.2<16.92$, it **does not fall in the region**, so do not reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ we cannot yet conclude the variance is out of spec.

**Example 4.** (Two-variance F test) Two machines make the same part with normal diameters. Samples of $n_1=9$ and $n_2=7$ give $S_1^2=0.36$ and $S_2^2=0.10$. Do the machines' precisions (variances) differ significantly ($\alpha=0.10$)? (Use $F_{0.05}(8,6)=4.15$, $F_{0.95}(8,6)=1/F_{0.05}(6,8)=1/3.58\approx0.279$.)

> **Solution.** Set $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$, $H_1:\sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2$ (two-sided, $\alpha=0.10$, $0.05$ per tail). The statistic
> $$F=\frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}=\frac{0.36}{0.10}=3.6\overset{H_0}{\sim}F(8,6).$$
> Rejection region $F>F_{0.05}(8,6)=4.15$ or $F<F_{0.95}(8,6)\approx0.279$. Since $0.279<3.6<4.15$, it does not fall in the region, so do not reject $H_0$: at level $0.10$ the precisions do not differ significantly.

**Example 5.** (Goodness-of-fit $\chi^2$ test) A die is rolled $120$ times, with face counts: $1{:}\,18$, $2{:}\,23$, $3{:}\,16$, $4{:}\,21$, $5{:}\,18$, $6{:}\,24$. Is the die fair ($\alpha=0.05$)? (Use $\chi^2_{0.05}(5)=11.07$.)

> **Solution.** Set $H_0$: the die is fair, each $p_i=1/6$; $H_1$: not fair. Expected frequencies $E_i=np_i=120\times\frac16=20$ (each face). Compute
> $$\chi^2=\sum_{i=1}^{6}\frac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i}=\frac{(18\!-\!20)^2+(23\!-\!20)^2+(16\!-\!20)^2+(21\!-\!20)^2+(18\!-\!20)^2+(24\!-\!20)^2}{20}.$$
> The numerator $=4+9+16+1+4+16=50$, so $\chi^2=50/20=2.5$. Degrees of freedom $k-1=6-1=5$ (no estimated parameters, $r=0$).
> Right-sided rejection region $\chi^2>\chi^2_{0.05}(5)=11.07$. Since $2.5<11.07$, do not reject $H_0$: at level $0.05$ the die may be regarded as fair.

## Exercises

1. Explain in words the Type I and Type II errors, write the probability definitions of $\alpha,\beta$, and say why they cannot both be reduced at a fixed sample size.
2. A product's net weight $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, nominal $\mu_0=500$ g, with $\sigma=8$ g known. A sample of $n=16$ gives $\bar X=496$ g. At $\alpha=0.05$ test whether the net weight equals $500$ g (two-sided $z$ test). (Use $z_{0.025}=1.96$.)
3. Exam scores $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma$ unknown. A sample of $n=9$ gives $\bar X=78$, $S=12$. At $\alpha=0.05$ test $H_0:\mu=72$ vs $H_1:\mu\ne72$ ($t$ test). (Use $t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$.)
4. A process requires variance $\sigma^2=4$. A sample of $n=16$ gives $S^2=7.5$. At $\alpha=0.05$ test $H_0:\sigma^2=4$ vs $H_1:\sigma^2\ne4$ (two-sided $\chi^2$ test). (Use $\chi^2_{0.025}(15)=27.49$, $\chi^2_{0.975}(15)=6.26$.)
5. Compare two processes' variances. A: $n_1=11$, $S_1^2=8.0$; B: $n_2=9$, $S_2^2=2.0$. At $\alpha=0.10$ test $H_0:\sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2$ vs $H_1:\sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2$ ($F$ test). (Use $F_{0.05}(10,8)=3.35$, $F_{0.95}(10,8)\approx0.326$.)
6. In a genetics experiment the four phenotype counts are $315,108,101,32$ (total $556$), with theoretical ratio $9:3:3:1$. At $\alpha=0.05$ perform a goodness-of-fit test. (Use $\chi^2_{0.05}(3)=7.815$.)

**Answers.**
1) Type I error = reject $H_0$ when true (false rejection), $\alpha=P(\text{reject }H_0\mid H_0\text{ true})$; Type II error = not reject $H_0$ when false (false acceptance), $\beta=P(\text{not reject }H_0\mid H_1\text{ true})$; with $n$ fixed, shrinking the rejection region (smaller $\alpha$) enlarges the non-rejection region, raising $\beta$ — they trade off.
2) $Z=\frac{496-500}{8/\sqrt{16}}=\frac{-4}{2}=-2.0$; $|Z|=2.0>1.96$, falls in the region, reject $H_0$: the net weight differs significantly from $500$ g.
3) $T=\frac{78-72}{12/\sqrt9}=\frac{6}{4}=1.5$; $|T|=1.5<t_{0.025}(8)=2.306$, does not fall in the region, do not reject $H_0$.
4) $\chi^2=\frac{(16-1)\times7.5}{4}=\frac{112.5}{4}=28.125$; rejection region $\chi^2>27.49$ or $<6.26$; since $28.125>27.49$, reject $H_0$: the variance differs significantly from $4$.
5) $F=\frac{S_1^2}{S_2^2}=\frac{8.0}{2.0}=4.0\sim F(10,8)$; rejection region $F>3.35$ or $F<0.326$; since $4.0>3.35$, reject $H_0$: the variances differ significantly.
6) Expected frequencies $E_i=556\times\{\frac9{16},\frac3{16},\frac3{16},\frac1{16}\}=\{312.75,104.25,104.25,34.75\}$; $\chi^2=\frac{(315-312.75)^2}{312.75}+\frac{(108-104.25)^2}{104.25}+\frac{(101-104.25)^2}{104.25}+\frac{(32-34.75)^2}{34.75}\approx0.016+0.135+0.101+0.218=0.470$; df $4-1=3$; since $0.470<7.815$, do not reject $H_0$: consistent with $9:3:3:1$.
