## Core Notes

### Structure of Solutions (Homogeneous Solution + Particular Solution)

A general $n$-th order **linear ODE** has the form
$$y^{(n)}+a_{n-1}(x)y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_1(x)y'+a_0(x)y=f(x).$$
When $f(x)\equiv 0$ it is **homogeneous**, otherwise **non-homogeneous**. Writing the left side as a linear operator $L[y]$, the equation is $L[y]=f$; $L$ satisfies $L[c_1y_1+c_2y_2]=c_1L[y_1]+c_2L[y_2]$, i.e. **linearity**.

**Superposition principle**: any linear combination of two solutions of $L[y]=0$ is again a solution. An $n$-th order homogeneous equation has exactly $n$ **linearly independent** solutions $y_1,\dots,y_n$, forming a **fundamental set**, with general solution
$$y_h=C_1y_1+C_2y_2+\cdots+C_ny_n.$$
Linear independence can be tested by the **Wronskian**
$$W(y_1,\dots,y_n)=\begin{vmatrix} y_1 & \cdots & y_n\\ y_1' & \cdots & y_n'\\ \vdots & & \vdots\\ y_1^{(n-1)} & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)}\end{vmatrix}.$$
If $W\neq 0$ at some point, the solutions are linearly independent.

**Structure theorem for non-homogeneous equations**: the general solution of $L[y]=f$ equals
$$y=y_h+y_p,$$
the **general solution of the associated homogeneous equation** $y_h$ plus **any one particular solution** $y_p$. This is the master plan for higher-order linear equations.

### Constant-Coefficient Homogeneous Equations and the Characteristic Equation

When the coefficients $a_i$ are all constant, we get a **constant-coefficient homogeneous linear equation**
$$y^{(n)}+a_{n-1}y^{(n-1)}+\cdots+a_1y'+a_0y=0.$$
Take $n=2$: $y''+py'+qy=0$. **Trying** $y=e^{\lambda x}$ and substituting gives $(\lambda^2+p\lambda+q)e^{\lambda x}=0$; since $e^{\lambda x}\neq 0$, $\lambda$ must satisfy the **characteristic equation**
$$\lambda^2+p\lambda+q=0.$$
In general the constant-coefficient $n$-th order equation has characteristic equation
$$\lambda^n+a_{n-1}\lambda^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1\lambda+a_0=0.$$
The roots determine the fundamental set, as follows.

**Case 1: distinct real roots** $\lambda_1\neq\lambda_2$. The fundamental solutions are $e^{\lambda_1 x},e^{\lambda_2 x}$, and
$$y=C_1e^{\lambda_1 x}+C_2e^{\lambda_2 x}.$$

### Complex and Repeated Roots

**Case 2: a complex conjugate pair** $\lambda=\alpha\pm\beta i$ ($\beta\neq 0$). The formal solutions $e^{(\alpha\pm\beta i)x}$ are complex; by Euler's formula $e^{i\beta x}=\cos\beta x+i\sin\beta x$, taking real and imaginary parts gives **real-valued** fundamental solutions, so
$$y=e^{\alpha x}\big(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x\big).$$

**Case 3: repeated roots.** If $\lambda_1$ is a root of multiplicity $k$, it contributes $k$ linearly independent solutions
$$e^{\lambda_1 x},\ x e^{\lambda_1 x},\ x^2 e^{\lambda_1 x},\ \dots,\ x^{k-1}e^{\lambda_1 x}.$$
For example a double real root $\lambda$ of a second-order equation gives $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{\lambda x}$. If a complex pair $\alpha\pm\beta i$ has multiplicity $k$, the corresponding solutions are $x^{j}e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x,\ x^{j}e^{\alpha x}\sin\beta x\ (j=0,\dots,k-1)$.

Collecting the solutions contributed by all roots yields $n$ linearly independent solutions whose combination is the homogeneous general solution.

### The Method of Undetermined Coefficients

To find one **particular solution** $y_p$ of $L[y]=f(x)$ when $f(x)$ has a **special type** (polynomials, exponentials, sines/cosines, and their products), guess the form of $y_p$ and substitute to fix the coefficients. Common correspondences:

- $f(x)=P_m(x)e^{\alpha x}$: try $y_p=x^s Q_m(x)e^{\alpha x}$, where $Q_m$ is an undetermined degree-$m$ polynomial and $s$ is the **multiplicity** of $\alpha$ as a characteristic root ($s=0$ if it is not a root).
- $f(x)=e^{\alpha x}\big[P(x)\cos\beta x+R(x)\sin\beta x\big]$: try $y_p=x^s e^{\alpha x}\big[\tilde P(x)\cos\beta x+\tilde R(x)\sin\beta x\big]$, with $s$ the multiplicity of $\alpha+\beta i$ as a characteristic root.

The factor $x^s$ is the **resonance correction** introduced to avoid overlap with the homogeneous solution. If $f$ is a sum of several terms, find a particular solution for each and add them (superposition).

### Variation of Parameters

Undetermined coefficients works only for special right sides. For a general continuous $f(x)$, use **variation of parameters**. For second order, with homogeneous solution $y_h=C_1y_1+C_2y_2$, replace the constants by unknown functions:
$$y_p=u_1(x)y_1+u_2(x)y_2.$$
Imposing the side condition $u_1'y_1+u_2'y_2=0$ and substituting yields the system
$$\begin{cases} u_1'y_1+u_2'y_2=0,\\[2pt] u_1'y_1'+u_2'y_2'=f(x).\end{cases}$$
Its coefficient determinant is precisely the Wronskian $W=W(y_1,y_2)\neq 0$, so by Cramer's rule
$$u_1'=-\frac{y_2 f}{W},\qquad u_2'=\frac{y_1 f}{W}.$$
Integrate for $u_1,u_2$, giving $y_p=u_1y_1+u_2y_2$. The general solution is $y=y_h+y_p$.

### Summary

- The operator $L$ obeys superposition; an $n$-th order homogeneous equation has $n$ independent solutions, with $W\neq 0$ testing independence.
- Non-homogeneous general solution = homogeneous solution $y_h$ + one particular solution $y_p$.
- Constant coefficients: solve the characteristic equation. **Distinct real roots** $\to e^{\lambda x}$; **complex** $\alpha\pm\beta i\to e^{\alpha x}(\cos\beta x,\sin\beta x)$; **root of multiplicity $k$** $\to e^{\lambda x},xe^{\lambda x},\dots,x^{k-1}e^{\lambda x}$.
- **Undetermined coefficients**: for $f$ of polynomial $\times$ exponential $\times$ trig type, guess the form with an $x^s$ resonance correction and substitute.
- **Variation of parameters**: for general $f$, $y_p=u_1y_1+u_2y_2$ with $u_1'=-y_2f/W,\ u_2'=y_1f/W$ integrated.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the general solution of $y''-5y'+6y=0$.

> **Solution.** The characteristic equation is $\lambda^2-5\lambda+6=0$, i.e. $(\lambda-2)(\lambda-3)=0$, giving distinct real roots $\lambda_1=2,\ \lambda_2=3$. Hence
> $$y=C_1e^{2x}+C_2e^{3x}.$$

**Example 2.** Solve $y''+4y=0$ with $y(0)=1,\ y'(0)=2$.

> **Solution.** Characteristic equation $\lambda^2+4=0$, roots $\lambda=\pm 2i$, i.e. $\alpha=0,\ \beta=2$. The general solution is
> $$y=C_1\cos 2x+C_2\sin 2x.$$
> From $y(0)=1$, $C_1=1$. Differentiating, $y'=-2C_1\sin 2x+2C_2\cos 2x$, and $y'(0)=2$ gives $2C_2=2$, so $C_2=1$. Hence
> $$y=\cos 2x+\sin 2x.$$

**Example 3.** Find the general solution of $y''-4y'+4y=0$ (repeated root).

> **Solution.** Characteristic equation $\lambda^2-4\lambda+4=0$, i.e. $(\lambda-2)^2=0$, a double root $\lambda=2$. The double root contributes $e^{2x}$ and $xe^{2x}$, so
> $$y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{2x}.$$

**Example 4.** Use undetermined coefficients to solve $y''-3y'+2y=e^{3x}$.

> **Solution.** The characteristic equation $\lambda^2-3\lambda+2=0$ has roots $\lambda=1,2$, so $y_h=C_1e^{x}+C_2e^{2x}$.
> The right side $f=e^{3x}$ has $\alpha=3$, not a characteristic root ($s=0$), so try $y_p=Ae^{3x}$. Then $y_p'=3Ae^{3x},\ y_p''=9Ae^{3x}$, and substituting:
> $$(9-9+2)A=1\Rightarrow A=\tfrac12.$$
> Thus $y_p=\tfrac12 e^{3x}$, and
> $$y=C_1e^{x}+C_2e^{2x}+\frac12 e^{3x}.$$

**Example 5.** Use variation of parameters to solve $y''+y=\sec x$ on $-\tfrac\pi2<x<\tfrac\pi2$.

> **Solution.** The homogeneous equation $y''+y=0$ has roots $\lambda=\pm i$, fundamental solutions $y_1=\cos x,\ y_2=\sin x$, $y_h=C_1\cos x+C_2\sin x$. The Wronskian is
> $$W=\begin{vmatrix}\cos x & \sin x\\ -\sin x & \cos x\end{vmatrix}=\cos^2x+\sin^2x=1.$$
> With $f=\sec x$:
> $$u_1'=-\frac{y_2 f}{W}=-\sin x\sec x=-\tan x,\qquad u_2'=\frac{y_1 f}{W}=\cos x\sec x=1.$$
> Integrating: $u_1=\ln|\cos x|,\ u_2=x$. Hence
> $$y_p=\cos x\,\ln|\cos x|+x\sin x,$$
> and the general solution is
> $$y=C_1\cos x+C_2\sin x+\cos x\,\ln|\cos x|+x\sin x.$$

## Exercises

1. Find the general solution of $y''+y'-6y=0$.
2. Find the general solution of $y''+2y'+5y=0$ (complex roots).
3. Solve $y''+6y'+9y=0$ with $y(0)=0,\ y'(0)=1$ (repeated root).
4. Use undetermined coefficients to find a particular solution of $y''-y=x$ and write the general solution.
5. Use undetermined coefficients to find a particular solution of $y''+y=\cos x$ (mind the resonance).
6. Use variation of parameters to solve $y''-2y'+y=\dfrac{e^{x}}{x}$ for $x>0$.

**Answers.** 1) roots $\lambda=2,-3$, $y=C_1e^{2x}+C_2e^{-3x}$; 2) $\lambda^2+2\lambda+5=0\Rightarrow\lambda=-1\pm2i$, $y=e^{-x}(C_1\cos 2x+C_2\sin 2x)$; 3) $(\lambda+3)^2=0$, double root $-3$, $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{-3x}$; $y(0)=0$ gives $C_1=0$, and $y'=(C_2-3C_2x)e^{-3x}$ with $y'(0)=C_2=1$, so $y=xe^{-3x}$; 4) $y_h=C_1e^{x}+C_2e^{-x}$; with $f=x$ try $y_p=ax+b$, $y_p''=0$, so $-(ax+b)=x\Rightarrow a=-1,b=0$, $y_p=-x$, hence $y=C_1e^{x}+C_2e^{-x}-x$; 5) $\lambda=\pm i$, so $\cos x$ resonates with the homogeneous solution, $s=1$; try $y_p=x(A\cos x+B\sin x)$, substitution gives $A=0,B=\tfrac12$, $y_p=\tfrac{x}{2}\sin x$; 6) $(\lambda-1)^2=0$, $y_1=e^{x},y_2=xe^{x}$, $W=e^{2x}$, $f=e^x/x$; $u_1'=-\dfrac{xe^x\cdot e^x/x}{e^{2x}}=-1$, $u_2'=\dfrac{e^x\cdot e^x/x}{e^{2x}}=\dfrac1x$, integrating $u_1=-x,\ u_2=\ln x$, $y_p=-xe^x+xe^x\ln x$, so $y=(C_1+C_2x)e^{x}+xe^x\ln x$ (absorbing $-xe^x$ into the $C_2xe^x$ term).
