## Core Notes

### Two-Variable Equations and Systems

A polynomial equation with **two** unknowns, each of degree $1$, is a **linear equation in two variables**, written in general as $ax+by=c$ (with $a,b$ not both zero). A pair of values that makes the two sides equal is a **solution** of the equation, recorded as $\begin{cases}x=\dots\\ y=\dots\end{cases}$.

A single two-variable equation usually has **infinitely many** solutions (fix any $x$ and the matching $y$ follows). Putting two such equations together gives a **system of linear equations in two variables**:

$$\begin{cases} a_1x+b_1y=c_1, \\ a_2x+b_2y=c_2. \end{cases}$$

A **solution of the system** is a pair $(x,y)$ that satisfies **both** equations at once.

**Intuition:** each two-variable linear equation is a **straight line** in the coordinate plane, and the solution of the system is the **point where the lines meet**. Two lines crossing at one point → a **unique solution**; parallel lines → **no solution**; coincident lines → **infinitely many solutions**.

**Pitfall:** a solution of the system must satisfy **both** equations simultaneously; meeting just one of them is not enough.

### Solving by Substitution

The idea of **substitution** is to turn a "two-variable" problem into a **one-variable** one: solve one equation for an unknown and **substitute** it into the other, eliminating that unknown. Steps:

1. From the simpler equation, express one unknown **in terms of the other** (e.g. $y=\dots$ or $x=\dots$).
2. **Substitute** this expression into the **other** equation, obtaining a linear equation in a single unknown.
3. **Solve** for that unknown.
4. **Back-substitute** the value into the expression from step 1 to find the other unknown.
5. Write the solution $\begin{cases}x=\dots\\ y=\dots\end{cases}$.

**Intuition:** "which to swap for which" — prefer to solve for the unknown whose coefficient is $\pm1$, so substitution produces no fractions and the work is simplest.

**Pitfall:** substitute into the **other** equation (not back into the one you used, which only yields an identity), and wrap the whole substituted expression in **brackets** to avoid sign errors.

### Solving by Elimination

When an unknown has the **same coefficient, or opposite coefficients**, in the two equations, **adding or subtracting** the equations removes it directly — this is **elimination**. Steps:

1. Multiply one (or both) equations by suitable constants so that one unknown has **equal absolute value** of its coefficient.
2. **Subtract** the equations when those coefficients are **equal**, **add** them when they are **opposite**, eliminating that unknown.
3. Solve the remaining one-variable equation.
4. Back-substitute for the other unknown and write the solution.

For example, for $\begin{cases}2x+3y=12\\ 2x-y=4\end{cases}$, subtracting eliminates $x$: $(2x+3y)-(2x-y)=12-4$, giving $4y=8$, so $y=2$.

**Intuition:** make the "two copies" of one unknown cancel. **Rule of thumb:** same sign subtract, opposite sign add.

**Pitfall:** when subtracting, change the sign of **every term** (you are subtracting the whole equation); when scaling an equation, multiply **both sides and every term** by the same number.

### Three-Variable Systems

A system with **three** unknowns in which every equation is of the first degree is a **three-variable linear system**:

$$\begin{cases} a_1x+b_1y+c_1z=d_1, \\ a_2x+b_2y+c_2z=d_2, \\ a_3x+b_3y+c_3z=d_3. \end{cases}$$

The guiding idea is again **elimination** — reduce three variables to two, then to one:

1. Pick the unknown easiest to remove (say $z$) and eliminate it from **two pairs** of equations, using substitution or elimination, to get **two** equations in the remaining two unknowns.
2. Combine those two two-variable equations into a system and solve it as before.
3. **Back-substitute** the two values into any original equation to find the third unknown.

**Intuition:** "three → two → one", stepping down one unknown at a time, like descending a staircase.

**Pitfall:** in step 1 eliminate the **same** unknown each time, and use **all three** equations (otherwise there is too little information); the two resulting two-variable equations must be **independent**.

### Applications of Systems

Many real problems with **several unknown quantities** are handled more naturally by setting up two (or three) unknowns and a system than by using a single variable. The procedure mirrors single-variable modeling, but you must find **as many independent equalities as there are unknowns** (one equation per unknown).

Classic models:

- **Chickens and rabbits in a cage:** let there be $x$ chickens and $y$ rabbits. The **head** relation is $x+y=\text{total heads}$; the **leg** relation is $2x+4y=\text{total legs}$ (a chicken has $2$ legs, a rabbit $4$).
- **Mixture/proportion problems:** let the amounts of two items be $x,y$. One equation gives the **total amount**, the other the **total of some ingredient** or the **total cost**.
- **Part-whole / digit problems:** use two unknowns for two quantities and write down both a "sum (difference)" and a "multiple (ratio)" relation.

**Intuition:** as many independent facts as the problem supplies, that many equations you can write; when the **number of unknowns equals the number of independent equations**, the solution is usually unique.

### Summary

- A two-variable equation $ax+by=c$ has infinitely many solutions; a **system** is solved by the $(x,y)$ satisfying both, geometrically the **intersection** of two lines.
- **Substitution:** solve for one unknown → substitute into the other → reduce to one variable; prefer a coefficient of $\pm1$.
- **Elimination:** match the absolute value of one coefficient → subtract for same sign, add for opposite sign.
- **Three-variable system:** eliminate step by step "three → two → one", removing the same unknown from **all equations**.
- Modeling: find as many **independent equalities** as unknowns; classics are chicken-rabbit (head and leg equations) and mixtures (total and ingredient equations).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** (Substitution) Solve $\begin{cases} y=2x-1, \\ 3x+y=14. \end{cases}$

> **Solution.** The first equation already gives $y$ in terms of $x$, so **substitute** into the second:
> $$3x+(2x-1)=14.$$
> Remove brackets and combine: $5x-1=14$, $5x=15$, $x=3$.
> Back-substitute $y=2x-1=2\cdot3-1=5$.
> So the solution is $\begin{cases}x=3\\ y=5\end{cases}$.

**Example 2.** (Elimination) Solve $\begin{cases} 3x+2y=16, \\ 5x-2y=8. \end{cases}$

> **Solution.** The coefficients of $y$ are opposite ($+2$ and $-2$), so **add** the equations to remove $y$:
> $$(3x+2y)+(5x-2y)=16+8 \;\Rightarrow\; 8x=24 \;\Rightarrow\; x=3.$$
> Substitute $x=3$ into $3x+2y=16$: $9+2y=16$, $2y=7$, $y=\dfrac{7}{2}$.
> So the solution is $\begin{cases}x=3\\ y=\frac{7}{2}\end{cases}$.

**Example 3.** (Scaling, then elimination) Solve $\begin{cases} 2x+3y=12, \\ 3x-2y=5. \end{cases}$

> **Solution.** To eliminate $y$, multiply the first equation by $2$ and the second by $3$, making the coefficients of $y$ equal to $+6$ and $-6$:
> $$\begin{cases} 4x+6y=24, \\ 9x-6y=15. \end{cases}$$
> Add: $13x=39$, $x=3$. Substitute into $2x+3y=12$: $6+3y=12$, $3y=6$, $y=2$.
> So the solution is $\begin{cases}x=3\\ y=2\end{cases}$.

**Example 4.** (Three-variable system) Solve $\begin{cases} x+y+z=6, \\ x-y+z=2, \\ 2x+y-z=1. \end{cases}$

> **Solution.** First eliminate $y$. Subtract the second equation from the first: $(x+y+z)-(x-y+z)=6-2$, giving $2y=4$, so $y=2$.
> Substitute $y=2$ into the first and third equations:
> $$\begin{cases} x+z=4, \\ 2x-z=-1. \end{cases}$$
> Add to remove $z$: $3x=3$, $x=1$; then $x+z=4$ gives $z=3$.
> So the solution is $\begin{cases}x=1\\ y=2\\ z=3\end{cases}$.

**Example 5.** (Chickens and rabbits) A cage holds $35$ chickens and rabbits in all, with $94$ legs total. How many of each are there?

> **Solution.** Let there be $x$ chickens and $y$ rabbits. The **head** and **leg** relations give
> $$\begin{cases} x+y=35, \\ 2x+4y=94. \end{cases}$$
> From the first, $x=35-y$; substitute into the second: $2(35-y)+4y=94$, i.e. $70+2y=94$, $2y=24$, $y=12$.
> Back-substitute $x=35-12=23$. Check: $23+12=35$ heads and $2\cdot23+4\cdot12=46+48=94$ legs — correct.
> **Answer:** $23$ chickens and $12$ rabbits.

## Exercises

1. Solve by substitution: $\begin{cases} x=y+2, \\ 2x+y=10. \end{cases}$
2. Solve by elimination: $\begin{cases} x+2y=7, \\ x-2y=-1. \end{cases}$
3. Solve $\begin{cases} 4x+3y=10, \\ 3x-2y=1. \end{cases}$
4. Solve the three-variable system $\begin{cases} x+y+z=12, \\ 2x-y+z=9, \\ x+2y-z=5. \end{cases}$
5. (Ticket prices) Adult tickets cost \$30 each and child tickets \$15 each. A group buys $40$ tickets for a total of \$900. How many of each were bought?
6. (Mixture) Candy A costs \$20 per kg and candy B \$12 per kg. They are mixed into $10$ kg of assorted candy costing \$15 per kg. How many kg of each are needed?

**Answers.**
1) Substitution gives $2(y+2)+y=10$, $3y=6$, $y=2$, $x=4$; solution $\begin{cases}x=4\\ y=2\end{cases}$.
2) Adding gives $2x=6$, $x=3$; subtracting gives $4y=8$, $y=2$; solution $\begin{cases}x=3\\ y=2\end{cases}$.
3) Multiply the first by $2$ and the second by $3$: $8x+6y=20$, $9x-6y=3$; adding, $17x=23$, $x=\dfrac{23}{17}$, $y=\dfrac{10-4x}{3}=\dfrac{26}{51}$.
4) Subtracting the second from the first gives $-x+2y=3$; subtracting the third from the first gives $-y+2z=7$. With $x+y+z=12$ this yields $x=4,\ y=3,\ z=5$.
5) Let $x$ adult and $y$ child tickets: $\begin{cases}x+y=40\\ 30x+15y=900\end{cases}$, giving $x=20,\ y=20$.
6) Let $x$ kg of A and $y$ kg of B: $\begin{cases}x+y=10\\ 20x+12y=150\end{cases}$, giving $x=\dfrac{15}{4}=3.75,\ y=\dfrac{25}{4}=6.25$.
