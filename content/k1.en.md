## Core Notes

Optimization studies the problem of "making an objective as good as possible (smallest or largest) subject to constraints." **Convex optimization** matters for one fundamental reason: **every local optimum is a global optimum**, and there exist mature, efficient algorithms. Whether a problem is "convex" hinges on whether its feasible set is a **convex set** and its objective a **convex function**. This chapter lays those two cornerstones. Convention: throughout we write optimization as **minimization** ($\min$), since $\max f$ is equivalent to $\min(-f)$.

### Convex Sets and Convex Combinations

Let $C\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n$. If for every $x,y\in C$ and every $\theta\in[0,1]$ we have

$$\theta x+(1-\theta)y\in C,$$

then $C$ is a **convex set**. Geometrically, the **line segment** joining any two points of the set **lies entirely inside the set** — there are no "dents" or "holes."

A point $\theta_1 x_1+\cdots+\theta_k x_k$ with $\theta_i\ge 0$ and $\sum_i\theta_i=1$ is a **convex combination** of $x_1,\dots,x_k$. One can show $C$ is convex **if and only if** it is closed under convex combinations of any finite collection of its points. The smallest convex set containing $C$ — the set of all convex combinations of points of $C$ — is its **convex hull**, written $\operatorname{conv} C$.

**Common convex sets:** the whole space $\mathbb{R}^n$ and singletons; **hyperplanes** $\{x:a^\top x=b\}$ and **halfspaces** $\{x:a^\top x\le b\}$; **balls** $\{x:\lVert x-x_0\rVert\le r\}$; **polyhedra** (intersections of finitely many halfspaces) $\{x:Ax\le b\}$; and the cone $\mathbb{S}^n_+$ of symmetric positive-semidefinite matrices.

### Convex Functions (Jensen's Inequality)

Let $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ have a convex domain $\operatorname{dom}f$. If for all $x,y\in\operatorname{dom}f$ and $\theta\in[0,1]$,

$$f\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)\le \theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y),$$

then $f$ is a **convex function**. Geometrically, the **chord** joining any two points of the graph **lies on or above the curve**. If the inequality is strict for $x\ne y$ and $\theta\in(0,1)$, $f$ is **strictly convex**. If $-f$ is convex, $f$ is **concave**.

Extending the definition to several points gives **Jensen's inequality**: for convex $f$, with $\theta_i\ge 0$ and $\sum_i\theta_i=1$,

$$f\Big(\sum_{i}\theta_i x_i\Big)\le \sum_{i}\theta_i f(x_i).$$

Taking expectations over a probability distribution yields $f(\mathbb{E}X)\le\mathbb{E}f(X)$, the source of countless inequalities in probability and information theory.

### First-Order and Second-Order Conditions

When $f$ is differentiable, convexity admits checkable differential characterizations.

**First-order condition.** If $f$ is differentiable on a convex open set, then $f$ is convex **if and only if** for all $x,y\in\operatorname{dom}f$,

$$f(y)\ge f(x)+\nabla f(x)^\top(y-x).$$

This says the graph **lies everywhere above its tangent plane**; the first-order (linear) approximation is a **global underestimator** of $f$. A powerful consequence: a stationary point of a convex function ($\nabla f(x^*)=0$) is automatically a **global** minimizer, since then $f(y)\ge f(x^*)$ for all $y$.

**Second-order condition.** If $f$ is twice continuously differentiable, then $f$ is convex **if and only if** its **Hessian** is positive semidefinite everywhere:

$$\nabla^2 f(x)\succeq 0,\qquad \forall x\in\operatorname{dom}f.$$

Here $\nabla^2 f$ is the symmetric matrix of second partials $\big[\partial^2 f/\partial x_i\partial x_j\big]$, and $\succeq 0$ means positive semidefinite (all eigenvalues $\ge 0$, i.e. $v^\top\nabla^2 f\,v\ge 0$ for all $v$). If the Hessian is everywhere **positive definite** $\nabla^2 f\succ 0$, then $f$ is strictly convex (the converse can fail, e.g. $f(x)=x^4$). In one variable this is the familiar $f''(x)\ge 0$.

### Operations that Preserve Convexity

Returning to the definition each time is tedious; **convexity-preserving operations** let us build complicated convex functions from simple ones like LEGO bricks:

- **Nonnegative weighted sum:** if each $f_i$ is convex and $w_i\ge 0$, then $\sum_i w_i f_i$ is convex.
- **Affine composition:** if $f$ is convex, then $g(x)=f(Ax+b)$ is convex.
- **Pointwise supremum:** if each $f_\alpha$ is convex, then $\sup_\alpha f_\alpha$ is convex (so a piecewise-linear $\max_i(a_i^\top x+b_i)$ is convex).
- **Composition with a monotone convex outer function:** if $h$ is convex and nondecreasing and $g$ is convex, then $h\circ g$ is convex.

Correspondingly, convex sets are preserved by **intersection** ($\bigcap_\alpha C_\alpha$), **affine images and preimages**, and **Cartesian products** (but **unions** generally are not).

### Standard Form of a Convex Optimization Problem

Write an optimization problem in standard form

$$\min_{x}\ f_0(x)\quad \operatorname{s.t.}\ f_i(x)\le 0\ (i=1,\dots,m),\ \ h_j(x)=0\ (j=1,\dots,p).$$

It is a **convex optimization problem** if and only if the objective $f_0$ and all inequality-constraint functions $f_i$ are **convex**, and the equality constraints $h_j(x)=0$ are **affine** ($h_j(x)=a_j^\top x-b_j$). Then the feasible set $\{x:f_i(x)\le 0,\ h_j(x)=0\}$ is convex (an intersection of convex sublevel sets with an affine set).

The fundamental payoff: **every local optimum is a global optimum.** Sketch: if $x^*$ were locally optimal yet some $y$ had $f_0(y)<f_0(x^*)$, then by convexity $f_0$ strictly decreases along $\theta y+(1-\theta)x^*$, and points near $\theta=0$ stay feasible and arbitrarily close to $x^*$ — contradicting local optimality. Note: constraints must be written as $f_i(x)\le 0$ (so sublevel sets are convex), never $f_i(x)\ge 0$.

### Summary

- Convex set: $x,y\in C,\theta\in[0,1]\Rightarrow\theta x+(1-\theta)y\in C$; equivalently closed under convex combinations.
- Convex function: $f(\theta x+(1-\theta)y)\le\theta f(x)+(1-\theta)f(y)$; Jensen: $f(\sum\theta_i x_i)\le\sum\theta_i f(x_i)$.
- First-order: $f(y)\ge f(x)+\nabla f(x)^\top(y-x)$ $\Rightarrow$ a stationary point of a convex $f$ is a global minimizer.
- Second-order: $\nabla^2 f\succeq 0$ (and $\nabla^2 f\succ 0\Rightarrow$ strictly convex).
- Preserved by: nonnegative weighted sums, affine composition, pointwise sup, composition with a nondecreasing convex function; sets by intersection/affine image.
- Convex standard form: $f_0,f_i$ convex and $h_j$ affine $\Rightarrow$ local optimum = global optimum.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Show the halfspace $H=\{x\in\mathbb{R}^n:a^\top x\le b\}$ is convex.

> **Solution.** Take any $x,y\in H$, so $a^\top x\le b$ and $a^\top y\le b$. For $\theta\in[0,1]$,
> $$a^\top\big(\theta x+(1-\theta)y\big)=\theta\,a^\top x+(1-\theta)\,a^\top y\le \theta b+(1-\theta)b=b.$$
> Hence $\theta x+(1-\theta)y\in H$, so $H$ is convex. (The same argument shows a hyperplane $a^\top x=b$ is convex.)

**Example 2.** Using the second-order condition, show $f(x)=x^\top Q x$ (with $Q$ symmetric) is convex if and only if $Q\succeq 0$.

> **Solution.** The gradient is $\nabla f(x)=2Qx$, and the Hessian is
> $$\nabla^2 f(x)=2Q,$$
> independent of $x$. By the second-order condition, $f$ is convex $\iff \nabla^2 f\succeq 0 \iff 2Q\succeq 0\iff Q\succeq 0$. For instance $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2+x_2^2$ has $Q=I\succ 0$, so it is strictly convex.

**Example 3.** Show the exponential $f(x)=e^{ax}$ (one variable, $a\in\mathbb{R}$) is convex.

> **Solution.** Differentiate: $f'(x)=a e^{ax}$ and $f''(x)=a^2 e^{ax}$. Since $a^2\ge 0$ and $e^{ax}>0$, we have $f''(x)\ge 0$ everywhere, so by the second-order condition $f$ is convex. When $a\ne 0$, $f''(x)>0$, so $f$ is strictly convex.

**Example 4.** Use Jensen's inequality to prove the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality: for $x_1,\dots,x_n>0$,
$$\frac{x_1+\cdots+x_n}{n}\ge \sqrt[n]{x_1\cdots x_n}.$$

> **Solution.** Let $f(x)=-\ln x$. Its second derivative is $f''(x)=1/x^2>0$, so $f$ is convex on $(0,\infty)$. With weights $\theta_i=\tfrac1n$, Jensen gives
> $$-\ln\Big(\frac1n\sum_i x_i\Big)\le \frac1n\sum_i(-\ln x_i)=-\ln\big(x_1\cdots x_n\big)^{1/n}.$$
> Multiplying by $-1$ flips the inequality, and exponentiating (an increasing operation) yields $\dfrac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i\ge(x_1\cdots x_n)^{1/n}$.

**Example 5.** Decide whether $g(x)=\max\{2x+1,\ -x+4,\ x^2\}$ is convex, with justification.

> **Solution.** Examine the three pieces: $2x+1$ and $-x+4$ are affine, hence both convex (and concave); $x^2$ has second derivative $2>0$, hence convex. By the **pointwise-supremum rule**, the pointwise maximum of finitely many convex functions is convex. Therefore $g(x)=\max\{2x+1,-x+4,x^2\}$ is convex.

## Exercises

1. Decide whether each set is convex: (a) $\{(x,y):x^2+y^2\le 1\}$; (b) $\{(x,y):y\ge 1/x,\ x>0\}$; (c) $\{(x,y):x^2+y^2=1\}$.
2. Use the second-order condition to decide whether $f(x)=x^4$ is convex; is it strictly convex? Where does $f''$ vanish?
3. Decide whether $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2-x_1x_2+x_2^2$ is convex (write the Hessian and test for positive semidefiniteness).
4. Let $f$ be convex and $a,b$ constants. Show $g(x)=f(ax+b)$ is convex.
5. Using the first-order condition, show that if $f$ is differentiable and convex with $\nabla f(x^*)=0$, then $x^*$ is a global minimizer of $f$.
6. Rewrite "$\max\ 3x_1+2x_2$ subject to $x_1+x_2\le 4,\ x_1\ge 0,\ x_2\ge 0$" in standard $\min$ form as a convex problem, and explain why it is convex.

**Answers.** 1) (a) convex (closed unit disk); (b) convex — it is the region above the graph of the convex function $1/x$ on $x>0$, i.e. an epigraph, hence convex; (c) **not** convex (the unit circle; a chord leaves the curve). 2) $f''(x)=12x^2\ge 0$, so **convex**; $f$ is in fact strictly convex (verify from the definition) even though $f''(0)=0$; $f''$ vanishes only at $x=0$. 3) Hessian $\nabla^2 f=\begin{bmatrix}2&-1\\-1&2\end{bmatrix}$, with leading minors $2>0$ and $\det=3>0$, so positive definite and $f$ is **strictly convex**. 4) Put $u=ax_1+b,\ v=ax_2+b$; for $\theta\in[0,1]$, $g(\theta x_1+(1-\theta)x_2)=f(\theta u+(1-\theta)v)\le\theta f(u)+(1-\theta)f(v)=\theta g(x_1)+(1-\theta)g(x_2)$, so $g$ is convex. 5) By the first-order condition, for any $y$: $f(y)\ge f(x^*)+\nabla f(x^*)^\top(y-x^*)=f(x^*)+0=f(x^*)$, so $x^*$ is a global minimizer. 6) $\min\ -3x_1-2x_2$, $\operatorname{s.t.}\ x_1+x_2-4\le 0,\ -x_1\le 0,\ -x_2\le 0$; the objective and all three constraint functions are affine (hence convex) with no equality constraints, so the problem is convex (in fact a linear program).
