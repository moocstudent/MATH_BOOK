## Core Notes

### The Complex Derivative

Let $w=f(z)$ be defined on a neighbourhood of $z_0$. If the limit

$$f'(z_0)=\lim_{\Delta z\to0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}$$

exists (as a finite complex number), then $f$ is **differentiable** at $z_0$, and the limit is its **derivative** there.

Formally this matches the real derivative, but it is **much stronger**: $\Delta z$ is a complex increment in the plane that may approach $0$ along **any direction**, and the limit must be independent of direction. This stringent requirement makes complex differentiability a very "rigid" condition. Differentiability implies continuity (otherwise the difference quotient blows up). The sum, product, quotient, chain, and power rules hold exactly as in the real case, e.g. $(z^n)'=nz^{n-1}$ and $(e^z)'=e^z$.

**A cautionary example:** $f(z)=\bar z$ is continuous everywhere yet **differentiable nowhere**. Indeed the difference quotient $\dfrac{\overline{z_0+\Delta z}-\bar z_0}{\Delta z}=\dfrac{\overline{\Delta z}}{\Delta z}=e^{-2i\theta}$ (with $\Delta z=re^{i\theta}$) varies with the direction $\theta$, so the limit fails to exist. Differentiation is a far finer sieve for complex functions than for real ones.

### The Cauchy–Riemann Equations

Direction-independence of the derivative translates into partial differential equations. Write $f(z)=u(x,y)+iv(x,y)$. Letting $\Delta z\to0$ first along the real axis ($\Delta z=\Delta x$) and then along the imaginary axis ($\Delta z=i\Delta y$), the two difference-quotient limits must agree, giving

$$\boxed{\;u_x=v_y,\qquad u_y=-v_x\;}$$

the **Cauchy–Riemann equations** (C-R equations). They are a **necessary** condition for $f$ to be differentiable at a point.

**Sufficient condition:** if $u,v$ are differentiable at $(x_0,y_0)$ (partials exist and are continuous) and satisfy the C-R equations, then $f$ is differentiable at $z_0$. The derivative is then expressible from either direction:

$$f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=v_y-iu_y.$$

The C-R equations also have a polar form: with $z=re^{i\theta}$ and $f=u(r,\theta)+iv(r,\theta)$,

$$u_r=\frac{1}{r}v_\theta,\qquad v_r=-\frac{1}{r}u_\theta.$$

### Analytic Functions: Definition and Tests

If $f$ is differentiable at $z_0$ **and throughout some neighbourhood of it**, then $f$ is **analytic** (holomorphic) at $z_0$. A function analytic at every point of a region $D$ is an **analytic function** on $D$.

**Differentiable vs. analytic:** analyticity demands differentiability on a **whole neighbourhood**, not merely at a single point. For example $f(z)=|z|^2=x^2+y^2$ is differentiable only at $z=0$ (where $u_x=v_y$ and $u_y=-v_x$ happen to hold), but since no neighbourhood makes it everywhere differentiable, $f$ is **nowhere analytic**.

**Combined test:** on a region $D$, $f=u+iv$ is analytic $\iff$ $u,v$ have continuous partials on $D$ and satisfy the C-R equations everywhere. Sums, differences, products, quotients (nonzero denominator), and compositions of analytic functions are analytic. If an analytic function on a region has derivative identically zero, it is constant. Analyticity brings startling rigidity: we will later show analytic functions are **infinitely differentiable** and expandable in power series.

### Harmonic and Conjugate-Harmonic Functions

If a real function $\varphi(x,y)$ has continuous second-order partials and satisfies **Laplace's equation**

$$\Delta\varphi=\varphi_{xx}+\varphi_{yy}=0,$$

then $\varphi$ is a **harmonic function**.

**Key theorem:** if $f=u+iv$ is analytic on a region $D$, then both $u$ and $v$ are harmonic on $D$. The proof differentiates the C-R equations once more: from $u_x=v_y$ and $u_y=-v_x$ we get $u_{xx}=v_{yx}$ and $u_{yy}=-v_{xy}$, which sum to $u_{xx}+u_{yy}=0$; likewise $v$ is harmonic.

Of such a pair $(u,v)$ satisfying the C-R equations, $v$ is called the **conjugate harmonic** of $u$ (note the **order**: $u+iv$ is analytic, but $v+iu$ generally is not). **Recovering $v$ from $u$** is a standard task: from $v_x=-u_y$ and $v_y=u_x$, integrate in one variable, then use the other equation to fix the "constant of integration" function, and finally assemble the analytic $f(z)$.

### Elementary Analytic Functions

Series or the C-R equations extend the real elementary functions to the plane, where they are analytic on their domains.

**Complex exponential** $e^z=e^{x+iy}=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)$. It is analytic on the whole plane (an entire function), with $(e^z)'=e^z$, and it is periodic with period $2\pi i$: $e^{z+2\pi i}=e^z$.

**Trigonometric functions** are defined via Euler's formula,

$$\cos z=\frac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2},\qquad \sin z=\frac{e^{iz}-e^{-iz}}{2i}.$$

These are entire too; the derivative rules $(\sin z)'=\cos z$, $(\cos z)'=-\sin z$ and the identity $\sin^2z+\cos^2z=1$ still hold. But $|\sin z|,|\cos z|$ can be **unbounded** (since $\cos(iy)=\cosh y\to\infty$), unlike the real case.

**Complex logarithm** $\ln z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z$ is the principal branch of the multivalued $\operatorname{Ln}z$. Each branch is analytic on the plane cut along a ray from the origin (the **branch cut**, taken along the negative real axis for the principal branch), with $(\ln z)'=1/z$. The origin and the negative real axis are its branch point and line of discontinuity.

### Summary

- Complex derivative $f'(z_0)=\lim_{\Delta z\to0}\frac{f(z_0+\Delta z)-f(z_0)}{\Delta z}$, requiring the **same limit along every direction**; differentiable $\Rightarrow$ continuous.
- Cauchy–Riemann $u_x=v_y,\ u_y=-v_x$: **necessary** for differentiability; with $u,v$ differentiable it is also **sufficient**. Derivative $f'=u_x+iv_x$.
- Analytic = differentiable throughout a **whole neighbourhood**; $f$ analytic on $D\iff u,v$ have continuous partials and satisfy C-R. $|z|^2$ is differentiable only at $0$ and nowhere analytic.
- Analytic $\Rightarrow u,v$ harmonic ($\Delta u=\Delta v=0$); $v$ is the **conjugate harmonic** of $u$ (ordered); from $u$ the C-R equations recover $v$ and $f$.
- Elementary analytic functions: $e^z=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)$ (period $2\pi i$), $\cos z,\sin z$ via Euler (can be unbounded), $\ln z=\ln|z|+i\operatorname{Arg}z$ (cut along the negative real axis).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Use the C-R equations to verify that $f(z)=z^2$ is analytic everywhere, and find $f'(z)$.

> **Solution.** With $z=x+iy$, $z^2=(x^2-y^2)+i(2xy)$, so $u=x^2-y^2$ and $v=2xy$. The partials are
> $$u_x=2x,\quad u_y=-2y,\quad v_x=2y,\quad v_y=2x.$$
> Thus $u_x=2x=v_y$ and $u_y=-2y=-v_x$; the C-R equations hold everywhere with continuous partials, so $f$ is analytic on the whole plane. The derivative is
> $$f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=2x+i\,2y=2(x+iy)=2z,$$
> matching $(z^2)'=2z$.

**Example 2.** Show that $f(z)=|z|^2$ is differentiable only at $z=0$, hence nowhere analytic.

> **Solution.** Here $|z|^2=x^2+y^2$, so $u=x^2+y^2$, $v=0$. The partials are
> $$u_x=2x,\quad u_y=2y,\quad v_x=0,\quad v_y=0.$$
> The C-R equations require $u_x=v_y$, i.e. $2x=0$, and $u_y=-v_x$, i.e. $2y=0$, holding only at $x=y=0$. So $f$ is differentiable only at $z=0$. Since no neighbourhood makes it everywhere differentiable, $f$ is nowhere analytic.

**Example 3.** Given the harmonic function $u=x^2-y^2+2x$, find its conjugate harmonic $v$ and the analytic function $f(z)=u+iv$.

> **Solution.** First check $u_{xx}+u_{yy}=2+(-2)=0$, so $u$ is harmonic. By C-R,
> $$v_y=u_x=2x+2.$$
> Integrating in $y$: $v=2xy+2y+g(x)$ with $g$ to be determined. Now use $v_x=-u_y$:
> $$v_x=2y+g'(x),\qquad -u_y=-(-2y)=2y,$$
> so $g'(x)=0$, i.e. $g(x)=C$. Hence
> $$v=2xy+2y+C.$$
> Therefore $f=u+iv=(x^2-y^2+2x)+i(2xy+2y+C)$. Recognising $x^2-y^2+2x+i(2xy+2y)=(x+iy)^2+2(x+iy)=z^2+2z$, we get $f(z)=z^2+2z+iC$.

**Example 4.** Find $\cos i$ and $\sin i$, and explain why $|\cos z|$ can be unbounded.

> **Solution.** From $\cos z=\dfrac{e^{iz}+e^{-iz}}{2}$ with $z=i$,
> $$\cos i=\frac{e^{i\cdot i}+e^{-i\cdot i}}{2}=\frac{e^{-1}+e^{1}}{2}=\cosh 1\approx1.543.$$
> Similarly $\sin i=\dfrac{e^{-1}-e^{1}}{2i}=\dfrac{-2\sinh 1}{2i}=i\sinh 1\approx1.175\,i$. In general, for $z=iy$ we have $\cos(iy)=\cosh y$, which tends to $\infty$ as $y\to\infty$, so $|\cos z|$ is **unbounded** — unlike the real cosine bounded by $1$.

**Example 5.** Determine whether $f(z)=e^{x}(\cos y+i\sin y)$ is analytic, and find its derivative.

> **Solution.** Here $u=e^x\cos y$, $v=e^x\sin y$. The partials are
> $$u_x=e^x\cos y,\quad u_y=-e^x\sin y,\quad v_x=e^x\sin y,\quad v_y=e^x\cos y.$$
> Checking C-R: $u_x=e^x\cos y=v_y$ ✓ and $u_y=-e^x\sin y=-v_x$ ✓, holding everywhere with continuous partials, so $f$ is analytic on the whole plane. The derivative is
> $$f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=e^x\cos y+ie^x\sin y=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)=f(z).$$
> This is exactly $f(z)=e^z$ with $(e^z)'=e^z$.

## Exercises

1. Use the C-R equations to decide whether $f(z)=z^3$ is analytic, and find $f'(z)$.
2. Determine at which points $f(z)=2x+ixy^2$ (with $z=x+iy$) is differentiable.
3. Verify that $u=e^x\cos y$ is harmonic, and find its conjugate harmonic $v$ and the analytic function $f(z)$.
4. Let $f(z)=u+iv$ be analytic on a region $D$ with $u\equiv\text{const}$. Prove $f$ is constant on $D$.
5. Compute $\sin\!\big(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+i\big)$ in the form $a+bi$.
6. Find the principal value $\ln(-e)$ and all values of $\operatorname{Ln}(-e)$.

**Answers.**
1) $z^3=(x^3-3xy^2)+i(3x^2y-y^3)$; $u_x=3x^2-3y^2=v_y$, $u_y=-6xy=-v_x$, so C-R holds everywhere, $f$ is entire, and $f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=3x^2-3y^2+i\,6xy=3(x+iy)^2=3z^2$.
2) $u=2x$, $v=xy^2$; $u_x=2$, $u_y=0$, $v_x=y^2$, $v_y=2xy$. C-R needs $2=2xy$ and $0=-y^2$; the latter forces $y=0$, which makes the former $2=0$, a contradiction. So $f$ is **nowhere differentiable**.
3) $u_{xx}+u_{yy}=e^x\cos y-e^x\cos y=0$, harmonic. From $v_y=u_x=e^x\cos y$, integrate to $v=e^x\sin y+g(x)$; then $v_x=e^x\sin y+g'(x)=-u_y=e^x\sin y$ gives $g'(x)=0$, so $v=e^x\sin y+C$ and $f(z)=e^z+iC$.
4) If $u$ is constant then $u_x=u_y=0$; by C-R, $v_y=u_x=0$ and $v_x=-u_y=0$, so $v$ is constant too. Hence $f=u+iv$ is constant.
5) $\sin\!\big(\tfrac{\pi}{2}+i\big)=\sin\tfrac{\pi}{2}\cos i+\cos\tfrac{\pi}{2}\sin i=1\cdot\cosh 1+0=\cosh 1\approx1.543$ (real, i.e. $\cosh1+0\,i$).
6) $-e=e\cdot e^{i\pi}$, $|-e|=e$, $\operatorname{Arg}(-e)=\pi$, so the principal value is $\ln(-e)=\ln e+i\pi=1+\pi i$; all values are $\operatorname{Ln}(-e)=1+(2k+1)\pi i$, $k\in\mathbb{Z}$.
