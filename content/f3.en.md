## Core Notes

The triangle is the most basic closed figure and the "building block" of geometry — any polygon can be split into triangles for study. This chapter first pins down the iron laws relating a triangle's sides and angles, then learns to decide when two triangles are "exactly the same" (congruent), meets two special families of triangles, and finally focuses on the most famous theorem about right triangles — the **Pythagorean theorem**. All figures are described with vertex labels, side lengths, and angles.

### Sides, angles and the angle sum

The figure bounded by three non-collinear points $A$, $B$, $C$ and the segments joining them is a **triangle**, written $\triangle ABC$. Its sides are $AB$, $BC$, $CA$, and its interior angles are $\angle A$, $\angle B$, $\angle C$.

**Triangle angle-sum theorem.** The three interior angles of a triangle sum to $180^\circ$:
$$\angle A+\angle B+\angle C=180^\circ.$$
Intuitively, tearing off the three angles and fitting them together makes a straight angle. An immediate consequence: the two acute angles of a right triangle are complementary (they sum to $90^\circ$).

**Triangle inequality.** The sum of any two sides exceeds the third, and the difference of any two sides is less than the third:
$$AB+BC>CA, \qquad |AB-BC|<CA.$$
This is why "a straight segment is the shortest path between two points".

**Side–angle ordering.** Within one triangle, **the longer side faces the larger angle, and the larger angle faces the longer side** — the longer a side, the larger the angle opposite it.

A triangle also has an important **exterior-angle property**: an exterior angle equals the sum of the two **non-adjacent** interior angles.

### Congruence tests (SSS/SAS/ASA/AAS/HL)

If two triangles can be made to **coincide exactly** (corresponding sides equal, corresponding angles equal), they are **congruent**, written $\triangle ABC\cong\triangle A'B'C'$. Congruent triangles have the same size and shape, differing only in position.

You need not check all six elements; the tests below give shortcuts (letters denote a pattern of Side / Angle):

- **SSS (side-side-side).** Three pairs of equal sides force congruence.
- **SAS (side-angle-side).** Two sides and their **included angle** equal forces congruence. The angle must be the one between the two sides.
- **ASA (angle-side-angle).** Two angles and their **included side** equal forces congruence.
- **AAS (angle-angle-side).** Two angles and the side **opposite** one of them equal forces congruence.
- **HL (hypotenuse–leg).** For **right triangles** only: an equal hypotenuse and one equal leg force congruence.

Two warnings: **SSA generally fails** (two sides and a non-included angle do not force congruence), and **AAA fails too** (three equal angles force only the same shape — similarity — not the same size). The main use of congruence is "to prove corresponding sides or angles equal": typically you first find (or build) a pair of congruent triangles, then read off the equality you need.

### Isosceles and equilateral triangles

**Isosceles triangle.** A triangle with two equal sides; the equal sides are the **legs** and the third side is the **base**. Its core properties:

- **Equal sides face equal angles.** Equal legs force equal **base angles** (i.e. $AB=AC\Rightarrow\angle B=\angle C$); the converse "equal angles face equal sides" also holds.
- **Three lines coincide.** In an isosceles triangle the **bisector** of the apex angle, the **median** to the base, and the **altitude** to the base are one and the same segment.

**Equilateral triangle.** A triangle with all three sides equal (also called regular). It is a special isosceles triangle with all angles equal, so each angle is
$$\frac{180^\circ}{3}=60^\circ.$$
Conversely, a triangle with all angles $60^\circ$ must be equilateral.

### The Pythagorean theorem

In a right triangle the three side lengths satisfy an exceptionally elegant relation. The longest side, opposite the right angle, is the **hypotenuse**; the two sides forming the right angle are the **legs**.

**Pythagorean theorem.** In a right triangle the sum of the squares of the two legs equals the square of the hypotenuse. With legs $a$, $b$ and hypotenuse $c$,
$$a^2+b^2=c^2.$$

It converts the angular fact "right angle" into a numerical relation among side lengths, so knowing any two sides yields the third. The most familiar integer-sided right triangle is "$3,4,5$" (since $3^2+4^2=9+16=25=5^2$) and its scalings, such as $6,8,10$ and $5,12,13$; such triples are called **Pythagorean triples**.

### The converse and applications

The Pythagorean theorem also runs in reverse, giving a way to detect a "right angle" by computation.

**Converse of the Pythagorean theorem.** If the three sides $a$, $b$, $c$ of a triangle satisfy
$$a^2+b^2=c^2,$$
then the triangle is a **right triangle**, with the right angle opposite $c$.

**Typical applications.**

- **Testing for a right angle.** Given three side lengths, check whether the square of the longest equals the sum of the squares of the other two.
- **Finding distances and heights.** Model a real situation (a ladder against a wall, the width of a river, the height of a pole) as a right triangle and solve for the unknown side with $a^2+b^2=c^2$.
- **Distance between two points.** For $P(x_1,y_1)$ and $Q(x_2,y_2)$ in the plane, the horizontal gap $|x_2-x_1|$ and vertical gap $|y_2-y_1|$ are the legs, and the hypotenuse is the distance:
$$PQ=\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}.$$

### Summary

- Angle sum $\angle A+\angle B+\angle C=180^\circ$; triangle inequality "sum of two sides exceeds the third"; longer side faces larger angle; exterior angle = sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles.
- Congruence tests: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, plus HL for right triangles; SSA and AAA do **not** prove congruence. Congruence proves corresponding sides/angles equal.
- Isosceles: equal sides face equal angles, three lines coincide; equilateral: each angle $60^\circ$.
- Pythagoras: in a right triangle $a^2+b^2=c^2$ (with $c$ the hypotenuse); common triples $3,4,5$ and $5,12,13$.
- Converse: sides with $a^2+b^2=c^2$ form a right triangle; used to test right angles and find distances; distance $\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** In $\triangle ABC$, $\angle A=70^\circ$ and $\angle B=2\angle C$. Find $\angle B$ and $\angle C$.

> **Solution.** By the angle sum $\angle A+\angle B+\angle C=180^\circ$, we get $\angle B+\angle C=180^\circ-70^\circ=110^\circ$. Substituting $\angle B=2\angle C$:
> $$2\angle C+\angle C=110^\circ \;\Rightarrow\; 3\angle C=110^\circ.$$
> So $\angle C=\dfrac{110^\circ}{3}\approx 36.7^\circ$ and $\angle B=2\angle C\approx 73.3^\circ$.

**Example 2.** Given $AB=AC$, with $AD$ bisecting the apex angle $\angle BAC$ and meeting $BC$ at $D$, prove $\triangle ABD\cong\triangle ACD$.

> **Solution.** In $\triangle ABD$ and $\triangle ACD$: $AB=AC$ (given), $\angle BAD=\angle CAD$ ($AD$ bisects $\angle BAC$), and $AD=AD$ (common side). These form a side-angle-side pattern, so by **SAS**
> $$\triangle ABD\cong\triangle ACD.$$
> (This also confirms the isosceles "three lines coincide": $AD$ is at once the bisector, the median to $BC$, and the altitude.)

**Example 3.** An isosceles triangle has apex angle $40^\circ$. Find its two base angles.

> **Solution.** Let each base angle be $x$. The two base angles are equal, so by the angle sum
> $$40^\circ+x+x=180^\circ \;\Rightarrow\; 2x=140^\circ \;\Rightarrow\; x=70^\circ.$$
> So both base angles are $70^\circ$.

**Example 4.** A 13 m ladder leans against a vertical wall, with its foot 5 m from the base of the wall. How high up the wall does the ladder reach?

> **Solution.** The wall, the ground, and the ladder form a right triangle: the ladder is the hypotenuse $c=13$, the foot-to-wall distance is one leg $a=5$, and the height sought is the other leg $b$. By $a^2+b^2=c^2$:
> $$b^2=c^2-a^2=13^2-5^2=169-25=144,$$
> so $b=\sqrt{144}=12$. The ladder reaches 12 m up the wall.

**Example 5.** A triangle has sides $9$, $12$, $15$. Decide whether it is a right triangle.

> **Solution.** Use the converse. The longest side is $15$; check whether the sum of the squares of the other two equals its square:
> $$9^2+12^2=81+144=225, \qquad 15^2=225.$$
> They are equal, $9^2+12^2=15^2$, so it **is** a right triangle, with the right angle opposite the side of length $15$.

## Exercises

1. Two interior angles of a triangle are $50^\circ$ and $60^\circ$. Find the third.
2. Decide whether segments of lengths $4$, $5$, $10$ can form a triangle, with a reason.
3. What is each interior angle of an equilateral triangle, and why?
4. The legs of a right triangle are $6$ and $8$. Find the hypotenuse.
5. Decide whether a triangle with sides $7$, $24$, $25$ is a right triangle.
6. In the coordinate plane, find the distance between $A(1,2)$ and $B(4,6)$.

**Answers.** 1) $180^\circ-50^\circ-60^\circ=70^\circ$; 2) no, since $4+5=9<10$ — the two shorter sides do not exceed the longest, violating the triangle inequality; 3) each angle is $60^\circ$: equal sides force equal angles, and $180^\circ\div3=60^\circ$; 4) hypotenuse $c=\sqrt{6^2+8^2}=\sqrt{36+64}=\sqrt{100}=10$; 5) check $7^2+24^2=49+576=625=25^2$, equal, so it is a right triangle; 6) $AB=\sqrt{(4-1)^2+(6-2)^2}=\sqrt{3^2+4^2}=\sqrt{9+16}=\sqrt{25}=5$.
