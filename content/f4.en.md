## Core Notes

"Same shape, different size" is everywhere in daily life — a map versus the real terrain, a photo enlarged or shrunk. **Similarity** is the tool that makes this relation precise. And the **circle**, the most symmetric of plane figures, hides a string of beautiful theorems. This chapter covers similar triangles, then moves to the basic properties of circles, inscribed angles and tangents, and finally gives the formulas for arc length and sector area. All figures are described in words using vertices, radii, and angles.

### Tests and properties of similar triangles

Two triangles with the **same shape** (equal corresponding angles and proportional corresponding sides) are **similar**, written $\triangle ABC\sim\triangle A'B'C'$. The ratio of corresponding sides is the **ratio of similarity** $k$.

**Tests.**

- **Two equal angles (AA).** If two pairs of corresponding angles are equal, the triangles are similar. This is the most-used test — just find two equal pairs of angles.
- **Two proportional sides with equal included angle (SAS similarity).** If two pairs of corresponding sides are proportional and the included angles are equal, the triangles are similar.
- **Three proportional sides (SSS similarity).** If all three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional, the triangles are similar.

**Properties.** Let the ratio of similarity be $k$ (the ratio of corresponding sides). Then:

- **Corresponding sides are proportional and corresponding angles equal.** This is the definition, and the main basis for finding unknown lengths.
- **The ratio of perimeters equals $k$.** Ratios of corresponding altitudes, medians, and bisectors also equal $k$.
- **The ratio of areas equals $k^2$.**
$$\frac{S_{\triangle ABC}}{S_{\triangle A'B'C'}}=k^2.$$
Intuitively: scaling lengths by $k$ scales (two-dimensional) area by $k^2$.

Note that congruence is a special case of similarity (similarity with $k=1$).

### Proportional segments from parallels

**Proportional-segments theorem.** Three parallel lines $l_1\parallel l_2\parallel l_3$ cut two transversals into proportional corresponding segments. If they cut $AB$, $BC$ on the first transversal and $DE$, $EF$ on the second, then
$$\frac{AB}{BC}=\frac{DE}{EF}.$$

A key corollary inside a triangle follows:

**Proportional division in a triangle (basic similarity).** A line parallel to one side of a triangle cuts the other two sides (or their extensions) into proportional segments. If $DE\parallel BC$ in $\triangle ABC$ with $D$ on $AB$ and $E$ on $AC$, then
$$\frac{AD}{DB}=\frac{AE}{EC}, \qquad \triangle ADE\sim\triangle ABC.$$
This "A-shaped similarity" is the starting point for many later proportion calculations.

### Basic concepts and properties of circles

A **circle** is the set of all points in a plane at a fixed distance from a fixed point. The fixed point is the **center**, the fixed distance the **radius** $r$. A portion of the circle between two points is an **arc**; a segment joining two points on the circle is a **chord**; a chord through the center is a **diameter**, the longest chord, of length $2r$.

The circle's high symmetry yields many properties; the most useful is the perpendicular-from-center theorem:

**Perpendicular-diameter theorem.** A diameter perpendicular to a chord **bisects that chord** and bisects the two arcs it subtends. Conversely, a diameter bisecting a chord (not itself a diameter) is perpendicular to it.

This theorem ties "radius, half-chord, and distance from center to chord" into a **right triangle**: if the chord has length $2a$, the center-to-chord distance is $d$, and the radius is $r$, then
$$a^2+d^2=r^2.$$
So knowing any two yields the third — a powerful tool for length calculations in circles.

### The inscribed-angle theorem

An angle with vertex at the center and sides along two radii is a **central angle**; an angle with vertex on the circle and both sides meeting the circle is an **inscribed angle**.

**Inscribed-angle theorem.** The **inscribed angle** subtending an arc equals **half the central angle** subtending the same arc. That is, an inscribed angle on a given arc is $\frac{1}{2}$ of the central angle on that arc.

Two common corollaries follow at once:

- **Inscribed angles on the same arc are equal.** Each is half of the same central angle, so they are equal to one another.
- **An angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle.** The central angle on a diameter is the straight angle $180^\circ$, half of which is $90^\circ$. Conversely, a $90^\circ$ inscribed angle subtends a diameter. This is a standard way to detect or construct a right angle.

### Tangents, arc length and sector area

**Tangent.** A line meeting a circle in **exactly one point** is a tangent, and the common point is the point of tangency. Its test and property are paired: a line through the outer end of a radius and perpendicular to that radius is a tangent; conversely, **a tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency**. The two tangents drawn from an external point have **equal tangent lengths**.

**Arc length.** The circumference is $2\pi r$, corresponding to a central angle of $360^\circ$. An arc with central angle $n^\circ$ occupies the fraction $\frac{n}{360}$ of the circumference, so its length is
$$l=\frac{n\pi r}{180}.$$
(This simplifies from $\dfrac{n}{360}\times 2\pi r$.)

**Sector area.** The figure bounded by two radii and the arc between them is a **sector**. The whole disk has area $\pi r^2$, and a sector with central angle $n^\circ$ takes the fraction $\frac{n}{360}$, so
$$S=\frac{n\pi r^2}{360}.$$
A handy equivalent form is $S=\frac{1}{2}lr$ (half the arc length times the radius), echoing the triangle area $\frac12\times$ base $\times$ height.

### Summary

- Similarity $\triangle ABC\sim\triangle A'B'C'$: equal corresponding angles, proportional sides; tests AA, SAS, SSS; perimeter ratio $=k$, **area ratio $=k^2$**.
- Proportional segments from parallels; $DE\parallel BC\Rightarrow\triangle ADE\sim\triangle ABC$ (A-shape).
- Circle: radius $r$, diameter $2r$, chord, arc; the perpendicular-diameter theorem gives $a^2+d^2=r^2$ (half-chord, center distance, radius).
- Inscribed-angle theorem: inscribed angle = half the central angle on the same arc; inscribed angles on one arc are equal; an angle in a semicircle is $90^\circ$.
- A tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency, and tangent lengths from a point are equal; arc length $l=\frac{n\pi r}{180}$, sector area $S=\frac{n\pi r^2}{360}=\frac12 lr$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** In $\triangle ABC$, $DE\parallel BC$ with $D$ on $AB$ and $E$ on $AC$. Given $AD=2$, $DB=3$, $AE=2.4$, find $EC$.

> **Solution.** Since $DE\parallel BC$, proportional division gives $\dfrac{AD}{DB}=\dfrac{AE}{EC}$:
> $$\frac{2}{3}=\frac{2.4}{EC} \;\Rightarrow\; EC=\frac{2.4\times 3}{2}=3.6.$$

**Example 2.** Two similar triangles have ratio of similarity $2:3$, and the smaller has area $8$. Find the area of the larger.

> **Solution.** The area ratio equals the square of the similarity ratio:
> $$\frac{S_{\text{small}}}{S_{\text{large}}}=\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2=\frac{4}{9}.$$
> So $\dfrac{8}{S_{\text{large}}}=\dfrac{4}{9}$, giving $S_{\text{large}}=\dfrac{8\times 9}{4}=18$.

**Example 3.** A circle has radius $r=5$ and a chord of length $8$. Find the distance from the center to the chord.

> **Solution.** By the perpendicular-diameter theorem, the perpendicular from the center bisects the chord, giving a half-chord $a=\dfrac{8}{2}=4$. The half-chord, the center distance $d$, and the radius form a right triangle with $a^2+d^2=r^2$:
> $$d^2=r^2-a^2=5^2-4^2=25-16=9 \;\Rightarrow\; d=3.$$
> The distance from the center to the chord is $3$.

**Example 4.** An arc subtends a central angle of $80^\circ$. Find the inscribed angle on that arc; and if $AB$ is a diameter with $C$ on the circle, find $\angle ACB$.

> **Solution.** By the inscribed-angle theorem, the inscribed angle is half the central angle on the same arc, so it is
> $$\frac{80^\circ}{2}=40^\circ.$$
> Also $AB$ is a diameter, subtending a straight central angle of $180^\circ$, so $\angle ACB=\dfrac{180^\circ}{2}=90^\circ$ (an angle in a semicircle is a right angle).

**Example 5.** A circle has radius $r=6$ and central angle $n=120^\circ$. Find the arc length and the area of the corresponding sector.

> **Solution.** Arc length $l=\dfrac{n\pi r}{180}$:
> $$l=\frac{120\times\pi\times 6}{180}=\frac{720\pi}{180}=4\pi.$$
> Sector area $S=\dfrac{n\pi r^2}{360}$:
> $$S=\frac{120\times\pi\times 6^2}{360}=\frac{120\times 36\pi}{360}=12\pi.$$
> (Check with $S=\tfrac12 lr=\tfrac12\times 4\pi\times 6=12\pi$.)

## Exercises

1. Given $\triangle ABC\sim\triangle DEF$ with $AB=6$, $DE=9$, and $BC=8$, find $EF$.
2. Two similar polygons have ratio of similarity $3:5$. Find their ratio of perimeters and of areas.
3. In $\triangle ABC$, $DE\parallel BC$ with $AD=4$, $AB=6$, $AE=6$. Find $AC$.
4. A circle has radius $13$ and a chord whose distance from the center is $5$. Find the length of the chord.
5. An inscribed angle on an arc is $35^\circ$. Find the central angle on that arc.
6. For a sector of radius $9$ and central angle $60^\circ$, find the arc length and the area.

**Answers.** 1) similarity ratio $\dfrac{AB}{DE}=\dfrac{6}{9}=\dfrac{2}{3}$, so $\dfrac{BC}{EF}=\dfrac23$ and $EF=\dfrac{8\times3}{2}=12$; 2) perimeter ratio equals the similarity ratio $3:5$, area ratio equals its square $9:25$; 3) $DE\parallel BC$ gives $\triangle ADE\sim\triangle ABC$, so $\dfrac{AD}{AB}=\dfrac{AE}{AC}$, i.e. $\dfrac{4}{6}=\dfrac{6}{AC}$, $AC=\dfrac{6\times6}{4}=9$; 4) half-chord $a=\sqrt{r^2-d^2}=\sqrt{13^2-5^2}=\sqrt{169-25}=\sqrt{144}=12$, so the chord is $2a=24$; 5) central angle $=2\times35^\circ=70^\circ$; 6) $l=\dfrac{60\pi\times9}{180}=3\pi$, and $S=\dfrac{60\pi\times9^2}{360}=\dfrac{60\times81\pi}{360}=\dfrac{27\pi}{2}$ (or $S=\tfrac12 lr=\tfrac12\times3\pi\times9=\tfrac{27\pi}{2}$).
