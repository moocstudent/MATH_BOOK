## Core Notes

This chapter treats **unconstrained optimization** $\min_{x\in\mathbb{R}^n} f(x)$: finding a point that minimizes a smooth objective $f$ over the whole space. We first give criteria for "which points can possibly be minimizers," then present **iterative algorithms** that march toward a minimizer — gradient descent, Newton's method, and quasi-Newton methods — and discuss what governs how fast they converge. This is the mathematical core of training machine-learning models, parameter estimation, and much more.

### Optimality Conditions

Let $f$ be twice continuously differentiable and let $x^*$ be a local minimizer. Then:

**First-order necessary condition:** $\nabla f(x^*)=0$. A point satisfying this is a **stationary point** (or critical point). Intuition: at a minimizer the directional derivative is nonnegative in every direction, which together force the gradient to vanish.

**Second-order necessary condition:** $\nabla^2 f(x^*)\succeq 0$ (the Hessian is positive semidefinite).

**Second-order sufficient condition:** if $\nabla f(x^*)=0$ and $\nabla^2 f(x^*)\succ 0$ (positive definite), then $x^*$ is a **strict local minimizer**.

In practice, read off the signs of the eigenvalues of $\nabla^2 f(x^*)$: all positive $\Rightarrow$ minimum; all negative $\Rightarrow$ maximum; mixed signs $\Rightarrow$ **saddle point** (no extremum). In particular, **if $f$ is convex, the first-order condition $\nabla f(x^*)=0$ is both necessary and sufficient, and $x^*$ is a global minimizer** — the hallmark of convex optimization.

### Gradient Descent

Iterative methods start from an initial guess $x_0$ and step along a "descent direction" toward a minimizer. The **negative gradient** $-\nabla f(x_k)$ is the direction of **steepest descent** at $x_k$, giving **gradient descent**:

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha\,\nabla f(x_k),$$

where $\alpha>0$ is the **step size** (learning rate). Each step moves a little along the locally steepest downhill direction. The method stops when $\lVert\nabla f(x_k)\rVert$ is small enough. It uses **only first derivatives**, has low per-step cost, and is simple to implement — the workhorse for large-scale problems — but can converge slowly near a minimizer (see the conditioning section).

### Step Size and Line Search (Armijo)

Choosing $\alpha$ is critical: too large may overshoot the minimizer or even diverge, too small converges painfully slowly. Beyond a fixed step, **line search** adaptively picks $\alpha$ at each step.

**Armijo rule (sufficient-decrease condition).** Fix $c_1\in(0,1)$ (often $10^{-4}$) and require the step to satisfy

$$f(x_k-\alpha\nabla f(x_k))\le f(x_k)-c_1\,\alpha\,\lVert\nabla f(x_k)\rVert^2.$$

This guarantees the objective **actually decreases enough** (by at least a fixed fraction of the predicted linear decrease). **Backtracking line search** uses it to choose the step: start from some $\alpha=\alpha_0$, and while Armijo fails set $\alpha\leftarrow\beta\alpha$ (shrink factor $\beta\in(0,1)$, e.g. $0.5$), halving repeatedly until it holds. This avoids overlong steps while still exploiting the largest acceptable one.

### Newton's Method and Quasi-Newton Methods

Gradient descent uses only first-order information; **Newton's method** also exploits second-order information (the Hessian), forming a quadratic model of $f$ at $x_k$ and jumping to the minimizer of that model:

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\big[\nabla^2 f(x_k)\big]^{-1}\nabla f(x_k).$$

When $\nabla^2 f\succ 0$ and the iterate is close enough to a minimizer, Newton's method enjoys **quadratic convergence** (the error roughly squares each step — extremely fast); for a **quadratic function** it reaches the minimizer in a single step. The cost is forming and solving with the Hessian each step (expensive at large scale), and far from the minimizer it need not decrease $f$, so it is usually paired with a line search.

**Quasi-Newton methods** (e.g. BFGS) approximate the Hessian (or its inverse) from accumulated gradient differences. Writing the approximation $B_k\approx\nabla^2 f(x_k)$,

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-B_k^{-1}\nabla f(x_k),$$

combining Newton-like speed with gradient-like cost (no explicit second derivatives). They are a standard choice for medium-scale smooth optimization.

### Convergence and the Condition Number

For a **convex** smooth objective, fixed-step gradient descent with a suitable $\alpha$ converges to the global minimizer; the **rate** is governed by the "shape" of the objective, captured by the **condition number** of the Hessian,

$$\kappa=\frac{\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 f)}{\lambda_{\min}(\nabla^2 f)}\ (\ge 1).$$

The larger $\kappa$, the more elongated and flat the level sets, and the more gradient descent **zig-zags** across the valley and crawls (for a quadratic the contraction factor is about $\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}$); when $\kappa=1$ (circular level sets) one step suffices. Improving conditioning (**preconditioning**, feature scaling) accelerates it markedly. Newton's method, by correcting with $[\nabla^2 f]^{-1}$, effectively rescales coordinates to the $\kappa\approx 1$ case and is therefore **insensitive to conditioning**.

### Summary

- Optimality: first-order necessary $\nabla f(x^*)=0$; second-order sufficient $\nabla f(x^*)=0$ and $\nabla^2 f(x^*)\succ 0$; for convex $f$ the first-order condition gives global optimality.
- Gradient descent: $x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha\nabla f(x_k)$, along steepest descent, first derivatives only.
- Step size: Armijo $f(x_k-\alpha\nabla f)\le f(x_k)-c_1\alpha\lVert\nabla f\rVert^2$; backtracking halves $\alpha$ until it holds.
- Newton: $x_{k+1}=x_k-[\nabla^2 f(x_k)]^{-1}\nabla f(x_k)$, quadratic convergence near the minimizer, one step on a quadratic; quasi-Newton (BFGS) approximates the Hessian from gradient differences.
- Condition number $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$: larger means slower gradient descent ($\sim\frac{\kappa-1}{\kappa+1}$); Newton is insensitive to $\kappa$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Find the stationary point of $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2+2x_2^2-2x_1-8x_2+9$ and classify it via the second-order condition.

> **Solution.** The gradient is $\nabla f=(2x_1-2,\ 4x_2-8)^\top$. Setting it to zero: $2x_1-2=0,\ 4x_2-8=0$, so $x^*=(1,2)^\top$.
> The Hessian $\nabla^2 f=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&4\end{bmatrix}\succ 0$ (eigenvalues $2,4>0$), so by the sufficient condition $x^*=(1,2)$ is a strict local (here also global) minimizer, with minimum $f(1,2)=1+8-2-16+9=0$.

**Example 2.** For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^2$ with $x_0=4$ and fixed step $\alpha=0.5$, write the first three gradient-descent iterates $x_1,x_2,x_3$ and discuss convergence.

> **Solution.** Here $f'(x)=x$, so $x_{k+1}=x_k-0.5\,x_k=0.5\,x_k$. Thus
> $$x_1=0.5\cdot4=2,\quad x_2=0.5\cdot2=1,\quad x_3=0.5\cdot1=0.5.$$
> In general $x_k=4\cdot(0.5)^k\to 0$, converging to the unique minimizer $x^*=0$ (the error halves each step — linear convergence).

**Example 3.** For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^2$ with $x_0=4$, perform one Newton step and verify it reaches the minimizer in one step.

> **Solution.** Here $f'(x)=x$ and $f''(x)=1$. The Newton iteration gives
> $$x_1=x_0-\frac{f'(x_0)}{f''(x_0)}=4-\frac{4}{1}=0.$$
> One step lands on the minimizer $x^*=0$, illustrating "Newton converges in one step on a quadratic" (the quadratic model is the function itself).

**Example 4.** Let $f(x)=x_1^2+x_1x_2+x_2^2$. At $x_0=(1,1)^\top$, use **backtracking line search** (initial $\alpha_0=1$, $\beta=0.5$, $c_1=10^{-4}$) to choose the first gradient-descent step.

> **Solution.** $\nabla f=(2x_1+x_2,\ x_1+2x_2)^\top$, so $\nabla f(x_0)=(3,3)^\top$, $\lVert\nabla f(x_0)\rVert^2=18$, and $f(x_0)=1+1+1=3$.
> Try $\alpha=1$: the new point $x_0-\nabla f(x_0)=(-2,-2)$ gives $f=4+4+4=12$. The Armijo right side is $3-10^{-4}\cdot1\cdot18\approx2.998$. Since $12>2.998$, Armijo fails; shrink $\alpha\leftarrow0.5$.
> Try $\alpha=0.5$: the new point $(-0.5,-0.5)$ gives $f=0.25+0.25+0.25=0.75$. The right side is $3-10^{-4}\cdot0.5\cdot18\approx2.999$. Since $0.75\le2.999$, Armijo holds. So take $\alpha=0.5$, landing at $(-0.5,-0.5)$.

**Example 5.** Find the stationary point of $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2-x_2^2$ and classify it; describe gradient descent's behavior here.

> **Solution.** $\nabla f=(2x_1,\ -2x_2)^\top=0$ gives the unique stationary point $(0,0)$. The Hessian $\nabla^2 f=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&-2\end{bmatrix}$ has eigenvalues $2$ and $-2$ (mixed signs), so $(0,0)$ is a **saddle point**, neither a max nor a min. The function is unbounded below ($f\to-\infty$ as $x_2\to\infty$), and gradient descent generally will not stop at the saddle but descends along $x_2$ and diverges — showing that unconstrained minimization is only meaningful when the objective is bounded below.

## Exercises

1. Find the stationary point and minimum of $f(x_1,x_2)=x_1^2+x_2^2-4x_1+6x_2+13$, and verify it is a minimum via the second-order condition.
2. Write the gradient-descent iteration and state the geometric meaning of $-\nabla f(x_k)$ (which direction is it?).
3. For $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^2$ with $x_0=8$ and fixed step $\alpha=0.25$, find $x_1,x_2$ and a closed form for $x_k$.
4. Explain the role of the Armijo rule; if the initial step always fails Armijo in backtracking, how should $\alpha$ be adjusted?
5. For $f(x)=\tfrac14 x^4$, perform one Newton step at $x_0=2$ to find $x_1$; does it reach the minimizer $x^*=0$ in one step? Why or why not?
6. For the quadratic $f(x)=\tfrac12 x^\top A x$ with $A=\begin{bmatrix}100&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix}$, find the condition number $\kappa$ and explain why gradient descent is slow here while Newton's method is unaffected.

**Answers.** 1) $\nabla f=(2x_1-4,\ 2x_2+6)=0\Rightarrow x^*=(2,-3)$; $\nabla^2 f=\begin{bmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{bmatrix}\succ0$, so it is a minimum with value $f(2,-3)=4+9-8-18+13=0$. 2) $x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha\nabla f(x_k)$; $-\nabla f(x_k)$ is the **steepest-descent direction** at $x_k$ (the direction of fastest decrease of $f$). 3) $x_{k+1}=x_k-0.25 x_k=0.75 x_k$, so $x_1=6,\ x_2=4.5$ and $x_k=8\cdot(0.75)^k$. 4) Armijo guarantees a sufficient decrease each step, preventing overlong steps that fail to decrease $f$ or diverge; if the initial step fails, **shrink $\alpha$ by the factor $\beta$ repeatedly (backtrack)** until it holds. 5) $f'(x)=x^3,\ f''(x)=3x^2$; $x_1=2-\dfrac{2^3}{3\cdot2^2}=2-\dfrac{8}{12}=2-\dfrac23=\dfrac43\approx1.33$. It does **not** reach $0$ in one step, because $f$ is not quadratic; Newton only converges in one step on quadratics (though it converges quadratically near the minimizer). 6) $\kappa=\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}=100/1=100\gg1$; the level sets are extremely elongated, so gradient descent zig-zags along the steep direction and crawls along the flat one, with contraction factor about $\frac{99}{101}\approx0.98$ (shrinking the error only ~2% per step). Newton corrects with $A^{-1}$, equivalent to rescaling coordinates to be isotropic ($\kappa=1$), reaching the minimizer in one step regardless of conditioning.
