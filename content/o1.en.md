## Core Notes

### Basic Concepts: Order, Solution, General and Particular Solutions

An equation involving an unknown function and its derivatives is a **differential equation**. If the unknown depends on a single independent variable and only ordinary derivatives appear, it is an **ordinary differential equation (ODE)**. The **order** of the equation is the order of the highest derivative present. For instance $y'=xy$ is first-order and $y''+y=0$ is second-order.

A general first-order ODE can be written $F(x,y,y')=0$; when solved for the highest derivative it takes the **explicit** form
$$y'=f(x,y).$$

A function $y=\varphi(x)$ defined on an interval $I$ is a **solution** on $I$ if substituting it makes the equation hold identically on $I$. The solution curve $y=\varphi(x)$ of a first-order ODE is called an **integral curve**.

A solution $y=\varphi(x,C)$ containing one **independent arbitrary constant** $C$ and capturing "almost all" solutions is a **general solution**. Imposing an **initial condition** $y(x_0)=y_0$ pins down $C$ and yields a **particular solution**. Finding the solution satisfying an initial condition is the **initial value problem (Cauchy problem)**:
$$y'=f(x,y),\qquad y(x_0)=y_0.$$

Geometrically, $y'=f(x,y)$ assigns to each point $(x,y)$ a slope $f(x,y)$, forming a **direction field**; integral curves are exactly the curves tangent to this field everywhere. The **existence-uniqueness theorem** (Picard) states: if $f$ and $\partial f/\partial y$ are continuous near $(x_0,y_0)$, then the initial value problem has a unique solution near $x_0$.

### Separable Equations

If a first-order equation can be written
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=g(x)\,h(y),$$
i.e. the right side factors into a function of $x$ times a function of $y$, it is **separable**. Solve by **separating variables** and integrating both sides: where $h(y)\neq 0$,
$$\frac{dy}{h(y)}=g(x)\,dx\ \Longrightarrow\ \int\frac{dy}{h(y)}=\int g(x)\,dx+C.$$

Note: any constant $y\equiv y_*$ making $h(y)=0$ is also a solution — a **singular** (equilibrium) solution that is "divided out" during separation and must be checked separately.

For example $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=ky$ separates to $\dfrac{dy}{y}=k\,dx$, integrating to $\ln|y|=kx+C_1$, i.e. $y=Ce^{kx}$ — the exponential growth/decay model.

### Homogeneous Equations (set $u=y/x$)

If the right side depends only on the ratio $y/x$, i.e.
$$\frac{dy}{dx}=F\!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right),$$
the (first-order) equation is **homogeneous**. Test: replace $x,y$ by $tx,ty$ in $f(x,y)$; if $f(tx,ty)=f(x,y)$ for all $t$, the equation is homogeneous.

Solve by the **substitution** $u=\dfrac{y}{x}$, i.e. $y=ux$. Differentiating, $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=u+x\dfrac{du}{dx}$, and substitution gives
$$u+x\frac{du}{dx}=F(u)\ \Longrightarrow\ x\frac{du}{dx}=F(u)-u.$$
This is **separable** in $u$:
$$\frac{du}{F(u)-u}=\frac{dx}{x}.$$
Integrate to relate $u$ and $x$, then back-substitute $u=y/x$ for the general solution.

### First-Order Linear Equations and the Integrating Factor $\mu=e^{\int P\,dx}$

An equation of the form
$$\frac{dy}{dx}+P(x)\,y=Q(x)$$
is a **first-order linear ODE**. When $Q(x)\equiv 0$ it is **homogeneous linear** (and separable); otherwise **non-homogeneous**.

The key is the **integrating factor**
$$\mu(x)=e^{\int P(x)\,dx}.$$
Multiplying through by $\mu$, the left side becomes the derivative of a product:
$$\mu\frac{dy}{dx}+\mu P y=\frac{d}{dx}\big(\mu y\big)=\mu Q.$$
Integrating gives the **general solution formula**
$$y=\frac{1}{\mu(x)}\left(\int \mu(x)\,Q(x)\,dx+C\right)=e^{-\int P\,dx}\left(\int e^{\int P\,dx}Q\,dx+C\right).$$

Thus general solution = **homogeneous solution** $Ce^{-\int P\,dx}$ + **one particular solution** — a structure that persists for higher-order linear equations.

### Bernoulli Equations and Initial Value Problems

An equation
$$\frac{dy}{dx}+P(x)\,y=Q(x)\,y^{n}\qquad(n\neq 0,1)$$
is a **Bernoulli equation**. It is not linear but becomes linear under a substitution. Divide by $y^{n}$:
$$y^{-n}\frac{dy}{dx}+P(x)\,y^{1-n}=Q(x).$$
Let $z=y^{1-n}$, so $\dfrac{dz}{dx}=(1-n)y^{-n}\dfrac{dy}{dx}$, giving a **first-order linear** equation in $z$:
$$\frac{dz}{dx}+(1-n)P(x)\,z=(1-n)Q(x).$$
Solve by the integrating factor, then back-substitute $z=y^{1-n}$.

**Initial value problems**, general procedure: find the general solution with its arbitrary constant, substitute $y(x_0)=y_0$ to solve for the constant, then write the particular solution — always checking it is defined on the required interval.

### Summary

- Explicit first-order ODE $y'=f(x,y)$; IVP $y'=f(x,y),\ y(x_0)=y_0$; the general solution has one constant $C$, fixed by the initial data.
- **Separable**: $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y)\Rightarrow\int\dfrac{dy}{h(y)}=\int g\,dx+C$; do not forget singular solutions where $h(y)=0$.
- **Homogeneous**: $y'=F(y/x)$; set $u=y/x$ to get separable $\dfrac{du}{F(u)-u}=\dfrac{dx}{x}$.
- **Linear**: $y'+Py=Q$, integrating factor $\mu=e^{\int P\,dx}$, $y=\dfrac1\mu\!\left(\int\mu Q\,dx+C\right)$.
- **Bernoulli**: $y'+Py=Qy^n$; set $z=y^{1-n}$ to linearize.
- Recognition order: try separable → homogeneous → linear → Bernoulli.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Solve the separable equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{2x}{y}$ and find the particular solution with $y(0)=3$.

> **Solution.** Separate: $y\,dy=2x\,dx$. Integrate both sides,
> $$\int y\,dy=\int 2x\,dx\ \Longrightarrow\ \frac{y^2}{2}=x^2+C_1.$$
> So the general solution is $y^2=2x^2+C$ (with $C=2C_1$). Substituting $x=0,\ y=3$: $9=0+C$, so $C=9$.
> Hence $y^2=2x^2+9$. Since $y(0)=3>0$, take the positive root:
> $$y=\sqrt{2x^2+9}.$$

**Example 2.** Find the general solution of the homogeneous equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{x+y}{x}$.

> **Solution.** The right side $=1+\dfrac{y}{x}$ is a function of $y/x$, so the equation is homogeneous. Let $u=\dfrac{y}{x}$, i.e. $y=ux$, so $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=u+x\dfrac{du}{dx}$. Substituting:
> $$u+x\frac{du}{dx}=1+u\ \Longrightarrow\ x\frac{du}{dx}=1.$$
> This is separable: $du=\dfrac{dx}{x}$, integrating to $u=\ln|x|+C$. Back-substitute $u=y/x$:
> $$\frac{y}{x}=\ln|x|+C\ \Longrightarrow\ y=x\ln|x|+Cx.$$

**Example 3.** Find the general solution of the linear equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac{1}{x}\,y=x^2$ for $x>0$.

> **Solution.** Here $P(x)=\dfrac1x,\ Q(x)=x^2$. The integrating factor is
> $$\mu=e^{\int \frac1x\,dx}=e^{\ln x}=x.$$
> Multiplying by $\mu=x$: $x y'+y=x^3$, whose left side is $\dfrac{d}{dx}(xy)$. Thus
> $$\frac{d}{dx}(xy)=x^3\ \Longrightarrow\ xy=\int x^3\,dx=\frac{x^4}{4}+C.$$
> The general solution is
> $$y=\frac{x^3}{4}+\frac{C}{x}.$$

**Example 4.** Solve the IVP $\dfrac{dy}{dx}-2y=e^{x},\ \ y(0)=1$.

> **Solution.** Linear with $P=-2,\ Q=e^x$. Integrating factor $\mu=e^{\int(-2)\,dx}=e^{-2x}$. Multiplying:
> $$\frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-2x}y\big)=e^{-2x}e^{x}=e^{-x}.$$
> Integrate: $e^{-2x}y=-e^{-x}+C$, i.e. $y=-e^{x}+Ce^{2x}$. Substituting $y(0)=1$: $1=-1+C$, so $C=2$.
> The particular solution is
> $$y=2e^{2x}-e^{x}.$$

**Example 5.** Solve the Bernoulli equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y=x y^{2}$ by substitution.

> **Solution.** Here $n=2$. Divide by $y^2$: $y^{-2}y'+y^{-1}=x$. Let $z=y^{1-n}=y^{-1}$, so $z'=-y^{-2}y'$, i.e. $y^{-2}y'=-z'$. Substituting:
> $$-z'+z=x\ \Longrightarrow\ z'-z=-x.$$
> This is linear with integrating factor $\mu=e^{-x}$:
> $$\frac{d}{dx}\big(e^{-x}z\big)=-x e^{-x}.$$
> By parts, $\int -xe^{-x}\,dx=xe^{-x}+e^{-x}+C$, so $e^{-x}z=xe^{-x}+e^{-x}+C$, i.e. $z=x+1+Ce^{x}$. Back-substitute $z=1/y$:
> $$\frac{1}{y}=x+1+Ce^{x}\ \Longrightarrow\ y=\frac{1}{x+1+Ce^{x}}.$$

## Exercises

1. Find the general solution of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=-\dfrac{x}{y}$ and describe the integral curves geometrically.
2. Find the solution of the separable equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y\cos x$ with $y(0)=2$.
3. Using $u=y/x$, solve the homogeneous equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{y}{x}+\dfrac{x}{y}$.
4. Find the general solution of the linear equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2xy=x$.
5. Solve the IVP $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+y\tan x=\sec x,\ \ y(0)=0$ on $-\tfrac\pi2<x<\tfrac\pi2$.
6. Linearize and solve the Bernoulli equation $\dfrac{dy}{dx}+\dfrac{1}{x}y=y^{2}$ for $x>0$.

**Answers.** 1) Separating $y\,dy=-x\,dx$ gives $x^2+y^2=C$ ($C>0$); the integral curves are a family of **concentric circles** centered at the origin; 2) $\dfrac{dy}{y}=\cos x\,dx\Rightarrow\ln|y|=\sin x+C_1$, and $y(0)=2$ gives $y=2e^{\sin x}$; 3) with $u=y/x$: $u+xu'=u+\dfrac1u$, so $u\,du=\dfrac{dx}{x}$, integrating $\dfrac{u^2}{2}=\ln|x|+C_1$, hence $y^2=2x^2\ln|x|+Cx^2$; 4) $\mu=e^{\int 2x\,dx}=e^{x^2}$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}(e^{x^2}y)=xe^{x^2}$, giving $e^{x^2}y=\tfrac12 e^{x^2}+C$, so $y=\tfrac12+Ce^{-x^2}$; 5) $\mu=e^{\int\tan x\,dx}=\sec x$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}(y\sec x)=\sec^2 x$, so $y\sec x=\tan x+C$, i.e. $y=\sin x+C\cos x$; $y(0)=0$ gives $C=0$, hence $y=\sin x$; 6) $n=2$, set $z=y^{-1}$ to get $z'-\dfrac1x z=-1$, $\mu=e^{-\int\frac1x dx}=\dfrac1x$, $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{z}{x}\right)=-\dfrac1x$, so $\dfrac{z}{x}=-\ln x+C$, giving $z=x(C-\ln x)$, i.e. $y=\dfrac{1}{x(C-\ln x)}$.
