## Core Notes

### Equations and Properties of Equality

An **equation** is an equality that contains an unknown. A value of the unknown that makes the two sides equal is a **solution** (when there is a single unknown it is also called a **root**), and finding all such values is called **solving the equation**. If an equation contains exactly **one** unknown raised to the first power, it is a **linear equation in one variable**, and it can always be rearranged into the standard form

$$ax+b=0\quad(a\ne0).$$

Everything we do when solving rests on **two properties of equality**:

- **Property 1 (add/subtract):** adding (or subtracting) the same number or expression to both sides keeps the equality. If $a=b$, then $a\pm c=b\pm c$.
- **Property 2 (multiply/divide):** multiplying both sides by the same number, or dividing by the same **nonzero** number, keeps the equality. If $a=b$, then $ac=bc$; and if $c\ne0$, then $\dfrac{a}{c}=\dfrac{b}{c}$.

**Intuition:** picture an equation as a **balance scale**. As long as you do **exactly the same** thing to both sides, the scale stays level. This is precisely why moving a term across and "making the coefficient $1$" are legal.

**Pitfalls:** in Property 2 the divisor must **never be zero**, and you must multiply **both** sides by the same number — scaling just one side breaks the balance.

### Solving a Linear Equation

To reduce a linear equation to the form $x=\text{a number}$, follow a fixed sequence of **five steps**:

1. **Clear denominators:** multiply both sides by the **least common multiple** of all denominators, turning fractions into whole expressions (this uses Property 2).
2. **Remove brackets:** expand with the distributive law $a(b+c)=ab+ac$. When a **minus sign** stands before a bracket, **every term inside changes sign** as the bracket is removed.
3. **Transpose:** move terms with the unknown to one side and constants to the other. **A term changes sign when it moves** — this is just Property 1 in action.
4. **Combine like terms:** bring the equation to the form $ax=b$.
5. **Make the coefficient $1$:** divide both sides by the coefficient $a$ ($a\ne0$) to get $x=\dfrac{b}{a}$.

**Intuition:** each step "peels the onion" — undoing, one layer at a time, the operations wrapped around $x$ until $x$ stands alone.

**Pitfalls:** when clearing denominators, **do not forget to multiply terms that have no denominator** (such as constants); when transposing, change the sign of **only the term that moves**. Finally, substitute the solution back to **check** it.

### Equations with Denominators

When the unknown appears in a **denominator**, or the equation contains more involved fractions, the key first step is still to **clear denominators**: multiply both sides by the least common multiple of all denominators to get a polynomial equation, then solve.

But when an unknown sits in a denominator there is one extra requirement: **check the roots**. Clearing denominators amounts to multiplying both sides by an expression containing the unknown, and that expression **might happen to equal zero** — which is forbidden (Property 2 needs a nonzero divisor). If a value found makes **any denominator of the original equation zero**, it is an **extraneous root** and must be discarded.

For instance, to solve $\dfrac{5}{x-2}=\dfrac{3}{x}$: multiply both sides by $x(x-2)$ to get $5x=3(x-2)$, i.e. $2x=-6$, so $x=-3$. Checking, $x-2=-5\ne0$ and $x=-3\ne0$, so $x=-3$ is genuinely a solution.

**Pitfall:** after finding a value you **must** verify it does not zero out a denominator of the original equation; for any equation with an unknown in a denominator, checking roots is mandatory and cannot be skipped.

### Modeling with Equations

To solve a word problem with an equation, the heart of the matter is translating **words into mathematics**. The general procedure:

1. **Read carefully:** identify the known quantities, the unknowns, and the **equal relationship** between them.
2. **Choose a variable:** let $x$ stand for a convenient unknown (express other quantities in terms of $x$ if needed).
3. **Set up the equation:** capture the **equality** stated in the problem — this is the crucial step.
4. **Solve** the equation.
5. **Check and answer:** make sure the result fits the situation (ages or head-counts should be positive whole numbers), then state the answer.

**Intuition:** decide "what is $x$, and what equals what" — first pick the most convenient quantity to call $x$, then find the hidden sentence "… equals …". Common equalities come from: total = sum of parts, after a change = before $\pm$ change, or two different ways of counting give the same result.

### Rate, Work and Part-Whole Problems

These three families appear most often, each with its own core formula:

- **Rate (motion) problems:** the basic relation is $\text{distance}=\text{speed}\times\text{time}$, i.e. $s=vt$. In a "meeting" problem the **sum** of the two distances equals the total gap; in a "catch-up" problem the **difference** of the distances equals the initial gap.
- **Work problems:** treat the whole job as the unit $1$, so the **work rate** $=\dfrac{1}{\text{time to finish alone}}$. If A alone needs $a$ days and B alone needs $b$ days, together they do $\dfrac{1}{a}+\dfrac{1}{b}$ per day, finishing in $\dfrac{1}{\frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b}}$ days.
- **Part-whole (sum/difference/multiple) problems:** turn the words "sum", "difference", "times" into an equality. "A is $3$ times B" becomes $\text{A}=3\cdot\text{B}$; "two numbers add to $20$" becomes $x+(20-x)=20$, or simply let the numbers be $x$ and $20-x$.

**Pitfalls:** keep units consistent (do not mix km/h with minutes); use the **sum** for meeting and the **difference** for catch-up — do not swap them; and in work problems the "rate" is the **fraction** of the job done per unit time, not an absolute amount.

### Summary

- Linear equation in one variable: rearrange to $ax+b=0\ (a\ne0)$; solving rests on the **two properties of equality** (add/subtract; multiply or divide by a nonzero number).
- Five steps: **clear denominators → remove brackets → transpose → combine → make the coefficient $1$**; a term changes sign when transposed.
- Equation with an unknown in a denominator: after clearing denominators you **must check roots**, discarding any **extraneous root** that zeroes a denominator.
- Five modeling steps: read → choose a variable → set up the **equality** → solve → check and answer.
- Three applications: rate $s=vt$ (sum for meeting, difference for catch-up), work (whole job $=1$, rate $=\frac1a$), part-whole (translate sum/difference/multiple directly).

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** Solve $3(x-2)+5=2x-1$.

> **Solution.** Remove brackets: $3x-6+5=2x-1$, i.e. $3x-1=2x-1$.
> Transpose ($2x$ to the left, $-1$ to the right, each changing sign): $3x-2x=-1+1$.
> Combine like terms: $x=0$.
> Check: left side $=3(0-2)+5=-1$, right side $=2\cdot0-1=-1$; the two agree, so $x=0$.

**Example 2.** Solve $\dfrac{2x-1}{3}-\dfrac{x+2}{2}=1$.

> **Solution.** The least common multiple of the denominators is $6$; multiply both sides by $6$ (clear denominators — note the $1$ on the right is multiplied too):
> $$2(2x-1)-3(x+2)=6.$$
> Remove brackets: $4x-2-3x-6=6$.
> Combine: $x-8=6$.
> Transpose: $x=14$.
> So the solution is $x=14$.

**Example 3.** Solve $\dfrac{3}{x-1}=\dfrac{2}{x+1}$.

> **Solution.** Multiply both sides by $(x-1)(x+1)$ to clear denominators:
> $$3(x+1)=2(x-1).$$
> Remove brackets: $3x+3=2x-2$.
> Transpose and combine: $x=-5$.
> **Check roots:** $x-1=-6\ne0$ and $x+1=-4\ne0$, so no denominator vanishes; hence $x=-5$ is a genuine solution.

**Example 4.** (Rate problem) Towns A and B are $300$ km apart. A fast train and a slow train set out at the same time toward each other, the fast one at $80$ km/h and the slow one at $40$ km/h. After how many hours do they meet?

> **Solution.** Let them meet after $x$ hours. At the meeting the **sum** of the two distances equals the total $300$ km.
> The fast train covers $80x$ km and the slow one $40x$ km, so
> $$80x+40x=300.$$
> Combine: $120x=300$; make the coefficient $1$: $x=\dfrac{300}{120}=2.5$.
> This fits the situation. **Answer:** they meet after $2.5$ hours.

**Example 5.** (Work problem) Crew A alone finishes a job in $10$ days and crew B alone in $15$ days. Working together, how many days do they need?

> **Solution.** Treat the whole job as $1$. Each day A does $\dfrac{1}{10}$ and B does $\dfrac{1}{15}$. Let them finish together in $x$ days; the work done in $x$ days totals $1$:
> $$\left(\frac{1}{10}+\frac{1}{15}\right)x=1.$$
> Since $\dfrac{1}{10}+\dfrac{1}{15}=\dfrac{3}{30}+\dfrac{2}{30}=\dfrac{5}{30}=\dfrac{1}{6}$, we get $\dfrac{1}{6}x=1$, so $x=6$.
> **Answer:** together they need $6$ days.

## Exercises

1. Solve $5x-3=2x+9$.
2. Solve $2(3x-1)-4=3(x+2)$.
3. Solve $\dfrac{x+1}{2}-\dfrac{2x-3}{5}=2$.
4. Solve $\dfrac{4}{x+3}=\dfrac{1}{x}$ and check the root.
5. (Part-whole) A father's age this year is $4$ times his son's, and their ages add to $50$. Find each age this year.
6. (Catch-up) A starts at $6$ km/h; one hour later B sets out along the same route at $9$ km/h to catch up. How many hours after B starts does B catch A?

**Answers.**
1) Transpose and combine: $3x=12$, $x=4$.
2) Remove brackets $6x-2-4=3x+6$, i.e. $6x-6=3x+6$; transpose $3x=12$, $x=4$.
3) Multiply by $10$: $5(x+1)-2(2x-3)=20$, i.e. $5x+5-4x+6=20$, $x+11=20$, $x=9$.
4) Multiply by $x(x+3)$: $4x=x+3$, $3x=3$, $x=1$; check $x+3=4\ne0,\ x=1\ne0$, so $x=1$.
5) Let the son be $x$, the father $4x$; $x+4x=50$, $5x=50$, $x=10$; father $40$, son $10$.
6) Let B catch up $x$ hours after starting. A has a head start of $6\times1=6$ km; equal distances give $6+6x=9x$, $3x=6$, $x=2$; B catches A $2$ hours after starting.
