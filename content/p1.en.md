## Core Notes

### Sample Space and Events

The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment $E$ is the **sample space**, denoted $\Omega$; each outcome $\omega$ is a **sample point**. Any subset of $\Omega$ is a **random event** (or simply **event**), usually written $A,B,C$.

- Event $A$ **occurs** if and only if the realized outcome satisfies $\omega\in A$.
- An event consisting of a single sample point is an **elementary event**.
- $\Omega$ itself is the **certain event** (always occurs); the empty set $\varnothing$ is the **impossible event**.

Since events are sets, their relations and operations obey set algebra:

- **Inclusion** $A\subset B$: if $A$ occurs, then $B$ occurs.
- **Union** $A\cup B$: at least one of $A,B$ occurs.
- **Intersection** $A\cap B$ (written $AB$): both $A$ and $B$ occur.
- **Difference** $A\setminus B=A\bar B$: $A$ occurs but $B$ does not.
- **Mutually exclusive (disjoint)**: $AB=\varnothing$.
- **Complement** $\bar A=\Omega\setminus A$: satisfies $A\cup\bar A=\Omega$ and $A\bar A=\varnothing$.

Union and intersection are commutative, associative and distributive, and obey **De Morgan's laws** (extending to any number of events):

$$\overline{A\cup B}=\bar A\,\bar B,\qquad \overline{A\cap B}=\bar A\cup\bar B,\qquad \overline{\bigcup_i A_i}=\bigcap_i \bar A_i.$$

**Intuition.** Translating a verbal event into set operations is the first step of any problem. Common correspondences: "at least one occurs" $=A\cup B$; "both occur" $=AB$; "neither occurs" $=\bar A\,\bar B=\overline{A\cup B}$; "exactly one occurs" $=A\bar B\cup\bar A B$; "not both occur" $=\overline{AB}=\bar A\cup\bar B$.

**Probability & statistics notation (used throughout this module):**

| Symbol | Read as / name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $\Omega$ | sample space | the set of all outcomes |
| $\omega$ | sample point | a single outcome |
| $\varnothing$ | impossible event | the empty set |
| $P(A)$ | probability of $A$ | a number between $0$ and $1$ |
| $P(A\mid B)$ | conditional probability | probability of $A$ given $B$ |
| $A,B$ independent | independence | $P(AB)=P(A)P(B)$ |
| $X\sim F$ | $X$ follows distribution $F$ | e.g. $X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)$ (p2) |
| $E[X]$ | expectation | the "average" of $X$ (p3) |
| $\operatorname{Var}(X)$ | variance | spread, $=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2$ (p3) |
| $\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)$ / $\rho$ | covariance / correlation | strength of association (p3) |

(For number sets and set symbols, see F1 ch. 1 and "Sets & Relations".)

### Axiomatic Definition of Probability

Let $\Omega$ be a sample space and $\mathscr F$ a field of events (a $\sigma$-algebra) on it. A set function $P:\mathscr F\to\mathbb R$ is a **probability** if it satisfies **Kolmogorov's axioms**:

1. **Non-negativity**: $P(A)\ge 0$ for every event $A$.
2. **Normalization**: $P(\Omega)=1$.
3. **Countable additivity**: if $A_1,A_2,\dots$ are pairwise disjoint, then
$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}A_i\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P(A_i).$$

Useful consequences follow from the axioms:

- $P(\varnothing)=0$ and $P(\bar A)=1-P(A)$.
- **Monotonicity**: $A\subset B\Rightarrow P(A)\le P(B)$, with $P(B\setminus A)=P(B)-P(A)$.
- **Addition rule**: $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(AB)$;
$$P(A\cup B\cup C)=P(A)+P(B)+P(C)-P(AB)-P(AC)-P(BC)+P(ABC).$$

**Key derivation (complement rule).** Since $A\cup\bar A=\Omega$ with $A\bar A=\varnothing$, the two are disjoint, so finite additivity and normalization give $P(A)+P(\bar A)=P(\Omega)=1$, hence $P(\bar A)=1-P(A)$. For "at least ..." probabilities, switching to the complement is usually simplest.

The addition rule follows from a disjoint decomposition: split $A\cup B$ into the disjoint pieces $A\bar B$, $AB$, $\bar A B$, sum and recombine to get $P(A)+P(B)-P(AB)$; the three-event case unfolds this **inclusion–exclusion** step by step.

### Classical and Geometric Probability (Counting)

The **classical model** requires a finite sample space whose elementary events are **equally likely**. If $\Omega$ has $n$ elementary events and $A$ contains $k$ of them, then
$$P(A)=\frac{k}{n}.$$

Counting uses permutations and combinations: the number of ordered selections of $k$ from $n$ distinct objects is $A_n^k=\dfrac{n!}{(n-k)!}$, and the number of unordered selections is $\binom{n}{k}=\dfrac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}$. Two handy identities: $\binom{n}{k}=\binom{n}{n-k}$ and Pascal's rule $\binom{n}{k}=\binom{n-1}{k-1}+\binom{n-1}{k}$. When modeling, fix "ordered/unordered" and "with/without replacement" consistently, counting numerator and denominator the same way.

The **geometric model** applies when the sample space is a region (segment, plane, or solid) and outcomes are "uniformly" spread. The probability of an event equals the ratio of measures:
$$P(A)=\frac{\mu(A)}{\mu(\Omega)},$$
where $\mu$ denotes length, area, or volume. Here each single point has probability $0$, so "$P(A)=0$" no longer means "$A$ is impossible".

### Conditional Probability and the Multiplication Rule

For $P(B)>0$, the **conditional probability** of $A$ given $B$ is
$$P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(AB)}{P(B)}.$$
It is itself a probability (satisfying all three axioms) — think of it as restricting the sample space to $B$ and renormalizing. The definition immediately yields the **multiplication rule**:
$$P(AB)=P(B)\,P(A\mid B)=P(A)\,P(B\mid A),$$
which extends to $n$ events (assuming the conditional probabilities are defined):
$$P(A_1A_2\cdots A_n)=P(A_1)\,P(A_2\mid A_1)\,P(A_3\mid A_1A_2)\cdots P(A_n\mid A_1\cdots A_{n-1}).$$
The multiplication rule fits "draw step by step / occur in sequence": compute each stage conditioned on what came before, then multiply.

### Total Probability and Bayes' Formula

If events $B_1,\dots,B_n$ are pairwise disjoint, satisfy $\bigcup_i B_i=\Omega$, and have $P(B_i)>0$, they form a **partition** of $\Omega$ (a complete system of events). For any event $A$, the **law of total probability** states:
$$P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i).$$
It computes $P(A)$ as a weighted sum over the possible "causes" $B_i$ — especially useful when $A$ is hard to compute directly but easy under each $B_i$.

**Derivation.** The partition gives $A=A\Omega=\bigcup_i AB_i$ with the $AB_i$ pairwise disjoint; additivity yields $P(A)=\sum_i P(AB_i)$, and applying the multiplication rule $P(AB_i)=P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)$ to each term completes it.

If moreover $P(A)>0$, **Bayes' formula** recovers the posterior probability of cause $B_k$ given the "effect" $A$:
$$P(B_k\mid A)=\frac{P(B_k)\,P(A\mid B_k)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i)}.$$
Here $P(B_k)$ is the **prior** (belief about causes before the experiment) and $P(B_k\mid A)$ the **posterior** (revised after observing $A$). Bayes' formula is the foundation of "reasoning from effect to cause" in statistical inference and machine-learning classification.

### Independence of Events

Events $A,B$ are **independent** if $P(AB)=P(A)P(B)$. When $P(B)>0$ this is equivalent to $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$: whether $B$ occurs does not change the probability of $A$.

Note that **mutual exclusivity** and **independence** are distinct: if $A,B$ are disjoint with $P(A),P(B)>0$, then they are **not** independent, since $P(AB)=0\ne P(A)P(B)$. Intuitively, disjoint means "incompatible" (one occurring forces the other not to — strong dependence), while independent means "no influence".

Three events $A,B,C$ are **mutually independent** if they are pairwise independent **and**
$$P(ABC)=P(A)P(B)P(C);$$
pairwise independence alone does not imply mutual independence. If $A,B$ are independent, so are each of the pairs $A,\bar B$; $\bar A,B$; $\bar A,\bar B$. Independence is the engine of "independent repeated trials": the probability that $n$ independent events **all** occur is the product of their probabilities, while **at least one** occurs is $1-\prod_i\big(1-P(A_i)\big)$.

### Summary

- Event algebra: "at least one" $=A\cup B$, "both" $=AB$, "neither" $=\overline{A\cup B}=\bar A\,\bar B$; De Morgan $\overline{A\cup B}=\bar A\,\bar B$.
- Three axioms: non-negativity, $P(\Omega)=1$, countable additivity; consequences $P(\bar A)=1-P(A)$, addition rule $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(AB)$.
- Classical $P(A)=k/n$; geometric $P(A)=\mu(A)/\mu(\Omega)$.
- Conditional $P(A\mid B)=P(AB)/P(B)$; multiplication $P(AB)=P(A)P(B\mid A)$.
- Total probability $P(A)=\sum_i P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)$; Bayes $P(B_k\mid A)=\dfrac{P(B_k)P(A\mid B_k)}{\sum_i P(B_i)P(A\mid B_i)}$.
- Independence $P(AB)=P(A)P(B)$; independent $\ne$ disjoint.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** A lot has $100$ items, of which $5$ are defective. Three are drawn at random without replacement. Find the probability of "exactly one defective" and of "at least one defective".

> **Solution.** This is the classical model; total selections $\binom{100}{3}$.
> Let $A=\{\text{exactly one defective}\}$: choose $1$ of $5$ defectives and $2$ of $95$ good items,
> $$P(A)=\frac{\binom{5}{1}\binom{95}{2}}{\binom{100}{3}}=\frac{5\cdot 4465}{161700}=\frac{22325}{161700}\approx 0.138.$$
> Let $C=\{\text{at least one defective}\}$; use the complement "all good":
> $$P(C)=1-\frac{\binom{95}{3}}{\binom{100}{3}}=1-\frac{138415}{161700}\approx 0.144.$$

**Example 2.** In a population, $0.5\%$ of adults have a certain disease. A test is positive for a sick person with probability $0.98$ and falsely positive for a healthy person with probability $0.05$. A person tests positive; find the probability that they actually have the disease.

> **Solution.** Let $D=\{\text{diseased}\}$ and $+=\{\text{test positive}\}$. We are given
> $$P(D)=0.005,\quad P(+\mid D)=0.98,\quad P(+\mid \bar D)=0.05.$$
> By the law of total probability,
> $$P(+)=P(D)P(+\mid D)+P(\bar D)P(+\mid \bar D)=0.005\cdot 0.98+0.995\cdot 0.05=0.0547.$$
> By Bayes' formula,
> $$P(D\mid +)=\frac{P(D)P(+\mid D)}{P(+)}=\frac{0.0049}{0.0547}\approx 0.0896.$$
> So a positive test means only about a $9\%$ chance of disease — a low base rate dominates, the classic Bayesian caution.

**Example 3.** (Meeting problem, geometric model) Two people each arrive at a random time in $[0,60]$ minutes; whoever arrives first waits $20$ minutes and then leaves. Find the probability that they meet.

> **Solution.** Let their arrival times be $x,y$; the sample space is the square
> $$\Omega=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 60,\ 0\le y\le 60\},\quad \mu(\Omega)=60^2=3600.$$
> They meet iff $|x-y|\le 20$, whose region is the square minus two right triangles with legs $40$:
> $$\mu(A)=60^2-2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot 40^2=3600-1600=2000.$$
> Therefore
> $$P(A)=\frac{2000}{3600}=\frac{5}{9}\approx 0.556.$$

**Example 4.** (Multiplication rule, draw without replacement) A bag has $6$ white and $4$ red balls. Two are drawn in succession without replacement. Find the probability that "both are white" and that "the second is white".

> **Solution.** Let $A_i=\{\text{$i$-th draw is white}\}$. By the multiplication rule,
> $$P(A_1A_2)=P(A_1)\,P(A_2\mid A_1)=\frac{6}{10}\cdot\frac{5}{9}=\frac{30}{90}=\frac13.$$
> For $P(A_2)$, use total probability with the first draw as the partition:
> $$P(A_2)=P(A_1)P(A_2\mid A_1)+P(\bar A_1)P(A_2\mid \bar A_1)=\frac{6}{10}\cdot\frac{5}{9}+\frac{4}{10}\cdot\frac{6}{9}=\frac{30+24}{90}=\frac{54}{90}=\frac35.$$
> Note $P(A_2)=\dfrac35=P(A_1)$: in drawing without replacement, the (unconditional) probability of white on **any** given draw equals the initial proportion — the "fairness of drawing lots".

**Example 5.** (Independent repeated trials) A system has $3$ independent components in parallel, working with probabilities $0.9,0.8,0.7$. A parallel system works if **at least one** component works. Find the probability the system works.

> **Solution.** Let $A_i=\{\text{component $i$ works}\}$, independent, with $P(A_1)=0.9,\ P(A_2)=0.8,\ P(A_3)=0.7$. The system works iff $A_1\cup A_2\cup A_3$; use the complement ("all three fail"):
> $$P(A_1\cup A_2\cup A_3)=1-P(\bar A_1\,\bar A_2\,\bar A_3)=1-(1-0.9)(1-0.8)(1-0.7).$$
> Hence
> $$P=1-0.1\times 0.2\times 0.3=1-0.006=0.994.$$
> Parallelism sharply boosts reliability: the best single component is only $0.9$, yet the parallel system reaches $0.994$.

## Exercises

1. Roll two fair dice. Find the probability that the sum equals $7$, and the probability of "at least one $6$".
2. Suppose $P(A)=0.6,\ P(B)=0.5,\ P(A\cup B)=0.8$. Find $P(AB)$ and $P(A\mid B)$, and decide whether $A,B$ are independent.
3. Three machines produce the same part, contributing $25\%,35\%,40\%$ of output with defect rates $5\%,4\%,2\%$. A part is chosen at random. (a) Find the probability it is defective; (b) given it is defective, find the probability it came from the third machine.
4. Let $A,B$ be independent with $P(A)=0.4,\ P(B)=0.7$. Find $P(A\cup B)$ and $P(\bar A\,\bar B)$.
5. Four letters are dropped at random into $3$ mailboxes. Find the probability that "the first mailbox receives exactly $2$ letters".
6. Three people independently try to break the same code, succeeding with probabilities $\tfrac12,\tfrac13,\tfrac14$. Find the probability that the code is broken (at least one succeeds).

**Answers.**
1) There are $6$ ordered outcomes summing to $7$, so $P=\dfrac{6}{36}=\dfrac16$; "at least one $6$" $=1-\left(\dfrac{5}{6}\right)^2=\dfrac{11}{36}$.
2) $P(AB)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cup B)=0.6+0.5-0.8=0.3$; $P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{0.3}{0.5}=0.6$; since $P(AB)=0.3=P(A)P(B)=0.6\times0.5$, $A,B$ are **independent**.
3) (a) $P=0.25\cdot0.05+0.35\cdot0.04+0.40\cdot0.02=0.0125+0.014+0.008=0.0345$; (b) $P=\dfrac{0.008}{0.0345}\approx 0.232$.
4) $P(A\cup B)=0.4+0.7-0.4\cdot0.7=0.82$; $P(\bar A\,\bar B)=(1-0.4)(1-0.7)=0.6\times0.3=0.18$.
5) Each letter lands independently and equally likely in $3$ boxes, total $3^4=81$. Choose $2$ of $4$ letters for box $1$ and put the other $2$ into the remaining $2$ boxes: $\binom{4}{2}\cdot 2^2=6\cdot4=24$, so $P=\dfrac{24}{81}=\dfrac{8}{27}\approx 0.296$. Equivalently, by the binomial: $\binom{4}{2}\left(\tfrac13\right)^2\left(\tfrac23\right)^2=\dfrac{8}{27}$.
6) Use the complement ("all three fail"): $P=1-\left(1-\tfrac12\right)\left(1-\tfrac13\right)\left(1-\tfrac14\right)=1-\dfrac12\cdot\dfrac23\cdot\dfrac34=1-\dfrac14=\dfrac34$.
