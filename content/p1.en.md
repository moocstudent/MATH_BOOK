## Core Notes

### Sample Space and Events

The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment $E$ is the **sample space**, denoted $\Omega$; each outcome $\omega$ is a **sample point**. Any subset of $\Omega$ is a **random event** (or simply **event**), usually written $A,B,C$.

- Event $A$ **occurs** if and only if the realized outcome satisfies $\omega\in A$.
- An event consisting of a single sample point is an **elementary event**.
- $\Omega$ itself is the **certain event** (always occurs); the empty set $\varnothing$ is the **impossible event**.

Since events are sets, their relations and operations obey set algebra:

- **Inclusion** $A\subset B$: if $A$ occurs, then $B$ occurs.
- **Union** $A\cup B$: at least one of $A,B$ occurs.
- **Intersection** $A\cap B$ (written $AB$): both $A$ and $B$ occur.
- **Difference** $A\setminus B=A\bar B$: $A$ occurs but $B$ does not.
- **Mutually exclusive (disjoint)**: $AB=\varnothing$.
- **Complement** $\bar A=\Omega\setminus A$: satisfies $A\cup\bar A=\Omega$ and $A\bar A=\varnothing$.

Union and intersection are commutative, associative and distributive, and obey **De Morgan's laws**:

$$\overline{A\cup B}=\bar A\,\bar B,\qquad \overline{A\cap B}=\bar A\cup\bar B.$$

### Axiomatic Definition of Probability

Let $\Omega$ be a sample space and $\mathscr F$ a field of events (a $\sigma$-algebra) on it. A set function $P:\mathscr F\to\mathbb R$ is a **probability** if it satisfies **Kolmogorov's axioms**:

1. **Non-negativity**: $P(A)\ge 0$ for every event $A$.
2. **Normalization**: $P(\Omega)=1$.
3. **Countable additivity**: if $A_1,A_2,\dots$ are pairwise disjoint, then
$$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty}A_i\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{\infty}P(A_i).$$

Useful consequences follow from the axioms:

- $P(\varnothing)=0$ and $P(\bar A)=1-P(A)$.
- **Monotonicity**: $A\subset B\Rightarrow P(A)\le P(B)$, with $P(B\setminus A)=P(B)-P(A)$.
- **Addition rule**: $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(AB)$;
$$P(A\cup B\cup C)=P(A)+P(B)+P(C)-P(AB)-P(AC)-P(BC)+P(ABC).$$

### Classical and Geometric Probability (Counting)

The **classical model** requires a finite sample space whose elementary events are **equally likely**. If $\Omega$ has $n$ elementary events and $A$ contains $k$ of them, then
$$P(A)=\frac{k}{n}=\frac{\text{outcomes in }A}{\text{total outcomes}}.$$

Counting uses permutations and combinations: the number of ordered selections of $k$ from $n$ distinct objects is $A_n^k=\dfrac{n!}{(n-k)!}$, and the number of unordered selections is $\binom{n}{k}=\dfrac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}$.

The **geometric model** applies when the sample space is a region (segment, plane, or solid) and outcomes are "uniformly" spread. The probability of an event equals the ratio of measures:
$$P(A)=\frac{\mu(A)}{\mu(\Omega)},$$
where $\mu$ denotes length, area, or volume.

### Conditional Probability and the Multiplication Rule

For $P(B)>0$, the **conditional probability** of $A$ given $B$ is
$$P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(AB)}{P(B)}.$$
It is itself a probability (satisfying all three axioms). The definition immediately yields the **multiplication rule**:
$$P(AB)=P(B)\,P(A\mid B)=P(A)\,P(B\mid A),$$
which extends to $n$ events (assuming the conditional probabilities are defined):
$$P(A_1A_2\cdots A_n)=P(A_1)\,P(A_2\mid A_1)\,P(A_3\mid A_1A_2)\cdots P(A_n\mid A_1\cdots A_{n-1}).$$

### Total Probability and Bayes' Formula

If events $B_1,\dots,B_n$ are pairwise disjoint, satisfy $\bigcup_i B_i=\Omega$, and have $P(B_i)>0$, they form a **partition** of $\Omega$ (a complete system of events). For any event $A$, the **law of total probability** states:
$$P(A)=\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i).$$
It computes $P(A)$ as a weighted sum over the possible "causes" $B_i$.

If moreover $P(A)>0$, **Bayes' formula** recovers the posterior probability of cause $B_k$ given the "effect" $A$:
$$P(B_k\mid A)=\frac{P(B_k)\,P(A\mid B_k)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(B_i)\,P(A\mid B_i)}.$$
Here $P(B_k)$ is the **prior** probability and $P(B_k\mid A)$ the **posterior** probability.

### Independence of Events

Events $A,B$ are **independent** if $P(AB)=P(A)P(B)$. When $P(B)>0$ this is equivalent to $P(A\mid B)=P(A)$: whether $B$ occurs does not change the probability of $A$.

Note that **mutual exclusivity** and **independence** are distinct: if $A,B$ are disjoint with $P(A),P(B)>0$, then they are **not** independent, since $P(AB)=0\ne P(A)P(B)$.

Three events $A,B,C$ are **mutually independent** if they are pairwise independent **and**
$$P(ABC)=P(A)P(B)P(C);$$
pairwise independence alone does not imply mutual independence. If $A,B$ are independent, so are each of the pairs $A,\bar B$; $\bar A,B$; $\bar A,\bar B$.

## Worked Examples

**Example 1.** A lot has $100$ items, of which $5$ are defective. Three are drawn at random without replacement. Find the probability of "exactly one defective" and of "at least one defective".

> **Solution.** This is the classical model; total selections $\binom{100}{3}$.
> Let $A=\{\text{exactly one defective}\}$: choose $1$ of $5$ defectives and $2$ of $95$ good items,
> $$P(A)=\frac{\binom{5}{1}\binom{95}{2}}{\binom{100}{3}}=\frac{5\cdot 4465}{161700}=\frac{22325}{161700}\approx 0.138.$$
> Let $C=\{\text{at least one defective}\}$; use the complement "all good":
> $$P(C)=1-\frac{\binom{95}{3}}{\binom{100}{3}}=1-\frac{138415}{161700}\approx 0.144.$$

**Example 2.** In a population, $0.5\%$ of adults have a certain disease. A test is positive for a sick person with probability $0.98$ and falsely positive for a healthy person with probability $0.05$. A person tests positive; find the probability that they actually have the disease.

> **Solution.** Let $D=\{\text{diseased}\}$ and $+=\{\text{test positive}\}$. We are given
> $$P(D)=0.005,\quad P(+\mid D)=0.98,\quad P(+\mid \bar D)=0.05.$$
> By the law of total probability,
> $$P(+)=P(D)P(+\mid D)+P(\bar D)P(+\mid \bar D)=0.005\cdot 0.98+0.995\cdot 0.05=0.0547.$$
> By Bayes' formula,
> $$P(D\mid +)=\frac{P(D)P(+\mid D)}{P(+)}=\frac{0.0049}{0.0547}\approx 0.0896.$$
> So a positive test means only about a $9\%$ chance of disease — a low base rate dominates, the classic Bayesian caution.

**Example 3.** (Meeting problem, geometric model) Two people each arrive at a random time in $[0,60]$ minutes; whoever arrives first waits $20$ minutes and then leaves. Find the probability that they meet.

> **Solution.** Let their arrival times be $x,y$; the sample space is the square
> $$\Omega=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 60,\ 0\le y\le 60\},\quad \mu(\Omega)=60^2=3600.$$
> They meet iff $|x-y|\le 20$, whose region is the square minus two right triangles with legs $40$:
> $$\mu(A)=60^2-2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot 40^2=3600-1600=2000.$$
> Therefore
> $$P(A)=\frac{2000}{3600}=\frac{5}{9}\approx 0.556.$$

## Exercises

1. Roll two fair dice. Find the probability that the sum equals $7$, and the probability of "at least one $6$".
2. Suppose $P(A)=0.6,\ P(B)=0.5,\ P(A\cup B)=0.8$. Find $P(AB)$ and $P(A\mid B)$, and decide whether $A,B$ are independent.
3. Three machines produce the same part, contributing $25\%,35\%,40\%$ of output with defect rates $5\%,4\%,2\%$. A part is chosen at random. (a) Find the probability it is defective; (b) given it is defective, find the probability it came from the third machine.
4. Let $A,B$ be independent with $P(A)=0.4,\ P(B)=0.7$. Find $P(A\cup B)$ and $P(\bar A\,\bar B)$.

**Answers.**
1) There are $6$ ordered outcomes summing to $7$, so $P=\dfrac{6}{36}=\dfrac16$; "at least one $6$" $=1-\left(\dfrac{5}{6}\right)^2=\dfrac{11}{36}$.
2) $P(AB)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cup B)=0.6+0.5-0.8=0.3$; $P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{0.3}{0.5}=0.6$; since $P(AB)=0.3=P(A)P(B)=0.6\times0.5$, $A,B$ are **independent**.
3) (a) $P=0.25\cdot0.05+0.35\cdot0.04+0.40\cdot0.02=0.0345$; (b) $P=\dfrac{0.008}{0.0345}\approx 0.232$.
4) $P(A\cup B)=0.4+0.7-0.4\cdot0.7=0.82$; $P(\bar A\,\bar B)=(1-0.4)(1-0.7)=0.18$.
